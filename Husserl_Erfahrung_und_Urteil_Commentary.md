# Husserl, *Erfahrung und Urteil* — Paragraph-by-Paragraph Commentary

## Introduction. Sense and Delimitation of the Investigation

### §1. The Predicative Judgment as the Central Theme in the Genealogy of Logic

**Paragraph 1.** The opening paragraph announces the task of the entire work: these investigations address a problem of origins (*Ursprungsproblem*). Their aim is a clarification of origins (*Ursprungsklärung*) of the predicative judgment, which is to serve as a contribution to the genealogy of logic as a whole. Husserl immediately distances this project from two potential misunderstandings: it is neither a question belonging to the "history of logic" in the ordinary historiographic sense, nor a problem of genetic psychology. What is at stake is rather the elucidation of the essence (*Wesen*) of the formation (*Gebilde*) whose origin is being interrogated. The task is therefore an essential clarification (*Wesensklärung*) of the predicative judgment by way of an investigation into its origin. The word *Ursprung* here does not mean temporal or causal genesis but rather the uncovering of the essential structures and founding relations that make the predicative judgment possible — a genealogical inquiry in the phenomenological sense.

**Paragraph 2.** Husserl explains why the clarification of origins of the predicative judgment in particular can advance the genealogy of logic as a whole. The reason is that at the center of formal logic, as it has historically developed, stands the concept of the predicative judgment — the *apophansis*. Formal logic is at its core apophantic logic: a doctrine of judgment and its "forms." This paragraph establishes the historically given centrality of the judgment as the point of departure that justifies concentrating the genetic investigation here.

**Paragraph 3.** Having named apophantic logic as the kernel of formal logic, Husserl immediately broadens the picture. Formal logic, when fully developed as a formal *mathesis universalis* that encompasses formal mathematics, contains not only formal apophantics (the doctrine of judgment-forms) but also a formal ontology: the doctrine of "something in general" (*Etwas überhaupt*) and its derivative forms — concepts like object, property, relation, multiplicity, and so on. Husserl notes that the tradition has always treated questions from both domains without clearly distinguishing them. He flags, but sets aside, the difficult problems concerning the relationship between formal apophantics and formal ontology — their correlative belonging-together, indeed inner unity, in view of which their separation proves to be merely provisional and based not on differences of domain but only on differences of attitude (*Einstellung*). The reference is to the detailed treatment in the *Formal and Transcendental Logic*, I. Part, Chs. 4 and 5. Despite setting these problems aside, Husserl makes the decisive point: all the categorial forms that constitute the subject matter of formal ontology accrue to objects *in judging*. Even the empty concept "something in general," under which objects are logically thought, occurs nowhere other than in judgment. The same holds for its derivative forms: property, as a form that first appears unselfstandingly in judgment and yields the substrate-form "property" when nominalized; the plural, which arises in plural judging and, when nominalized — transformed into the distinguished sense of substrate, of "object-about-which" — yields the set (*Menge*). This could be shown for all the other concepts appearing in formal ontology. The consequence is that the doctrine of judgment occupies a central position in the entirety of formal-logical problematics, and this centrality rests on both historical and substantive grounds.

**Paragraph 4.** Husserl cautions that this observation about the centrality of judgment should not be taken as a premature essential determination of what "logic" and "logical" mean in the widest and most comprehensive sense. Rather, this comprehensive essential concept can only be the final result of the phenomenological clarification and origin-research into the logical, as begun and discussed in its principial questions in the *Formal and Transcendental Logic* and carried out in a particular piece in the present investigation. Phenomenological origin-illumination (*Ursprungserhellung*) of the logical discovers that the domain of the logical is far larger than what traditional logic has treated, and it simultaneously discovers the hidden essential grounds from which this narrowing stems — precisely by going back, above all, to the origins of the "logical" in its traditional sense. In doing so, it finds not only that logical achievement (*logische Leistung*) is already present in strata where the tradition did not see it, and that the traditional logical problematic only begins at a relatively high level, but moreover that precisely in those lower strata lie the hidden presuppositions on the basis of which alone the sense and right of the logician's higher-level evidences can ultimately be made intelligible. Only through this does a confrontation with the entire logical tradition become possible, and — as a further long-range goal of the phenomenological clarification of logic — the attainment of that comprehensive concept of logic and *logos*.

**Paragraph 5.** Since the domain of the logical cannot be demarcated in advance, its phenomenological clarification requires a preliminary concept (*Vorbegriff*) to give it direction. This preliminary concept cannot be arbitrarily chosen; it is simply the traditionally given concept of logic and "logical." And at its center stands the problematic of the predicative judgment. This paragraph brings the methodological circle into focus: phenomenology must start from the traditional pre-understanding of logic precisely in order to disclose the deeper layers that the tradition overlooked, and it is the predicative judgment — the traditional centerpiece — that provides the guiding thread.

### §2. The Traditional Determination and Privileged Position of the Predicative Judgment and Its Problems

**Paragraph 1.** The section opens by noting that "judgment" (*Urteil*) or *apophansis* in the traditional sense is itself a title encompassing much. A more precise determination of the theme is needed, along with a look at what problems the tradition has bequeathed. Only then can we begin to characterize the genetic method to be pursued (§3 ff.).

**Paragraph 2.** Through the entire tradition, the most manifold "forms" of judgments have been distinguished, and what "judgment" itself is has been fixed in the most various ways. But what has been established from the beginning — from the Aristotelian founding of our logical tradition — is that the predicative judgment is universally characterized by a two-membered structure (*Zweigliedrigkeit*): a "what lies under" (*hypokeimenon*), about which something is predicated, and that which is predicated of it (*kategoroumenon*); distinguished from another direction, in terms of linguistic form, as *onoma* and *rhēma*. Every assertoric sentence must consist of these two members. This entails that every judging presupposes that an object is there, pregiven to us, about which predication takes place. This gives an "archetypal model" (*Urmodell*) of what is to be interrogated as to its origin. Husserl emphasizes that it must remain entirely open at this stage whether we are really dealing with the most original logical formation. Only the origin-illumination of this traditionally determined formation can answer this and all further connected questions.

**Paragraph 3.** Husserl now enumerates the questions that the traditional two-membered determination of judgment opens up. In what sense is the predicative judgment the preferred and central theme of logic, such that logic is necessarily at its core apophantic logic, a doctrine of judgment? What is the nature of the connection of the two members that have always been distinguished in judgment — in what sense is judgment synthesis and diairesis in one? This has been a constant embarrassment for logicians and remains unsatisfactorily solved. What is it that is "combined" and "separated" in judgment? Further: which of the manifold traditionally distinguished judgment-forms is the most original — the one that, as lowest and founding all others, must be presupposed and thought of as already given in order for other, "higher-level" forms to be built upon it? Is there one basic form or several equally basic ones standing side by side, and if only one, how can all others be traced back to it? For example, are affirmative and negative judgments two equally original basic forms, or does one have priority?

**Paragraph 4.** Beyond these questions that arise from the traditional determination of judgment, further questions remain that cannot be straightforwardly answered on the path of origin-illumination of the traditionally pregiven judgment but would already be matters of a confrontation with the entire tradition exceeding the scope of this investigation. Husserl nonetheless indicates some of these problems. Since Aristotle, the basic schema of judgment has been taken to be the copulative judgment, reducible to the form "S is p." Every judgment of other composition — e.g., the verbal sentence form — can according to this view be transformed without change of logical sense into the copulative form: "the man walks" is logically equivalent to "the man is walking." The "is" stands as part of the *rhēma* in which "time is always co-designated," thereby resembling the verb. A precise insight into what occurs in this copulative connection — its essence and origin — is needed before one can take a position on whether this transformability legitimately holds and whether the difference is merely a difference of linguistic form pointing to no difference of logical sense-achievement (*Sinnesleistung*). If the latter should turn out to be the case, the problem would arise how the two forms — copulative sentence and verbal sentence — relate to each other: are they equally original sense-achievements, or is one, and which, the more original? Does the copulative form "S is p" really represent, in the sense of the tradition, the basic schema of judgment?

**Paragraph 5.** Furthermore, the question of the originality of this schema should be raised in view of the fact that in it the subject is taken for granted in the form of the third person. This harbors the presupposition that the first and second persons — judgments in the form "I am..." or "you are..." — express no logical sense-achievement that differs from the one expressed in the privileged basic schema "it is..." This presupposition would itself need examination and would show the question of the originality of the traditional basic schema "S is p" in a new light. Husserl here registers a deep question about the implicit depersonalization built into the traditional logical schema — a point that resonates with his later life-world analyses and with questions about the constituting subject's place in logic.

### §3. The Two-Sidedness of the Logical Thematic. The Problem of Evidence as the Starting Point of Subjectively Directed Questioning and Its Bypassing in the Tradition

**Paragraph 1.** The judgment, to which all these questions attach, is first pregiven to the logician in its linguistic form as an assertoric sentence — that is, as a kind of objective formation (*objektives Gebilde*), as something that he can investigate for its forms and modes of relation much as he investigates any other existing thing. Cognition with its "logical" procedures has always already done its work when we engage in logical reflection; we have always already made judgments, formed concepts, drawn inferences, which are now our cognitive possessions (*Erkenntnisbesitz*), pregiven to us as such. This means: the interest the beginning logician takes in these formations is not merely an interest in some formations of a certain form, but an interest in formations that claim to be the precipitate of cognitions. The judgments he investigates for their forms present themselves as putative cognitions (*prätendierte Erkenntnisse*). Implicit in this: prior to all logical reflection, we already know about the difference between judgments that are genuine cognition — to which truth belongs — and those that are merely supposed, merely putative cognition. Before all logical reflection, we already know about the differences between the true judgment and the one that is at first putatively true and then perhaps turns out to be false, the correct inference and the fallacy, etc.

**Paragraph 2.** If the logician is really directed toward a logic in the comprehensive and serious sense, his interest in the laws of the form-building of judgments — the principles and rules of formal logic — is not an interest in them as mere rules of a game, but as rules that form-building must satisfy if cognition is to be possible at all. They hold for judgments purely according to their form, entirely apart from the material content of what is inserted as judgment-object, judgment-substrate into the empty form. They thus contain, so to speak, merely negative conditions of possible truth: a judging that violates them can never have truth — or, subjectively speaking, evidence — as its result; it can never be evident judging. But on the other hand, even if it satisfies the demands of these laws, it need not for that reason already have truth. Formal-logical lawfulness is a necessary but not sufficient condition for truth. This paragraph establishes the double-sidedness of the logical thematic: on the one hand, the objective side of judgment-forms and their laws; on the other, the subjective side, the question of evidence — the consciousness in which judgments achieve fulfillment and truth.

**Paragraph 3.** Husserl now drives toward the key methodological point. The logician who investigates the forms of judgments and their laws does so in an attitude directed toward objective formations. But these formations, as putative cognitions, inherently refer back to the subjective acts in which they originate. Every judgment, as an objective formation, is the precipitate of a subjective process of judging. To understand the judgment fully, we must go back to the judging activity itself — to the evidence in which it is originally given. The problem of evidence is thus the starting point for subjectively directed questioning. The tradition, however, has systematically bypassed (*übersprungen*) this subjective dimension. Traditional logic has dealt with judgments as ready-made formations, investigating their formal properties, without asking about the subjective conditions under which they can be given in evidence, nor about the genesis of their senses out of pre-predicative experience. This bypassing of the evidence-problem constitutes the decisive limitation of the tradition that phenomenological genealogy aims to overcome.

**Paragraph 4.** The tradition's bypassing of the evidence-problem is not accidental but stems from a deeply rooted tendency toward what Husserl calls objectivism: the natural inclination to attend exclusively to the objects and formations of cognition rather than to the subjective acts in which they are constituted. The logical tradition has taken judgments as objective items and classified them according to their forms without inquiring into the experiential processes from which these forms arise. The phenomenological approach reverses this tendency by making the subjective life of consciousness — the acts of judging, perceiving, explicating, relating — thematic in its own right. Only by doing so can the origin of the logical formations in the pre-predicative strata of experience be uncovered.

**Paragraph 5.** Husserl clarifies that the subjectively directed questioning he advocates is not a psychologistic reduction of logic to psychology. The phenomenological inquiry into the subjective origins of logical formations is an essential (*wesensmäßige*) inquiry: it asks about the essential structures of the experiential acts in which logical formations originate, not about empirical-psychological facts. The evidence in question is not a psychological feeling but the intentional achievement in which something is given as itself — self-givenness (*Selbstgegebenheit*). This is the evidence-problem as Husserl understands it, and it serves as the methodological point of departure for the genetic investigations that follow.

### §4. The Levels of the Evidence-Problem. Objectual Evidence as a Precondition for Possible Evident Judging

**Paragraph 1.** Husserl now introduces the idea that the evidence-problem has levels (*Stufen*). Not all evidence is of the same type or rank. The evidence that pertains to judging — the evidence of a judgment's truth — is not a simple, unstructured phenomenon. Rather, it is built up from more fundamental kinds of evidence. Just as complex judgments presuppose simpler ones, the evidence of complex judgments presupposes simpler, more basic forms of evidence.

**Paragraph 2.** The decisive point is this: the evidence of judgment (*Urteilsevidenz*) presupposes the evidence of the objects about which the judgment is made. This is what Husserl calls objectual or gegenständliche evidence — the evidence in which the objects themselves, the substrates of judgment, are given. If a judgment is to be evident — not merely made, but given in its truth — then the objects about which it judges must themselves be given in evidence. They must be there, present to consciousness in an experiential mode that makes them accessible for predicative determination. The objectual evidence is the lower level; the judgment-evidence is the higher level that presupposes it.

**Paragraph 3.** This stratification means that the investigation of evidence cannot start with the judgment itself. It must first descend to the lower level and ask: how are the objects that serve as judgment-substrates given in evidence? What is the character of the experiential access to them? How are they pregiven before any predication takes place? These questions point beyond the traditional domain of logic — which begins with the ready-made judgment — down into the pre-predicative domain of experience. The objectual evidence of which Husserl speaks is the evidence of experience (*Erfahrung*), above all the evidence of perception, in which individual objects are given in the flesh (*leibhaft*).

**Paragraph 4.** Husserl emphasizes that this is not merely a psychological observation about the temporal precedence of perception over judgment. It is a claim about essential founding relations. The evidence of the predicative judgment is essentially founded upon (*fundiert in*) the evidence of the objects that serve as its substrates. This founding relation is a structural, essential one: it belongs to the essence of evident judging that it draws upon a pre-predicative givenness of its objects. Without this pre-predicative givenness, predicative determination would be empty — it would have no substrate upon which to operate.

**Paragraph 5.** This paragraph draws the programmatic consequence: the investigation of the origins of the predicative judgment must proceed downward, from the higher-level evidence of judgment to the lower-level evidence of the objects — from judgment-evidence to objectual evidence, from the predicative to the pre-predicative. The genealogy of logic thus becomes, in its first and fundamental part, a theory of pre-predicative experience. This is the decisive turn of the investigation: the origin of the predicative judgment is to be sought not in the judgment itself, not in the ready-made logical formation, but in the experiential subsoil out of which it grows.

### §5. The Regress from Judgment-Evidence to Objectual Evidence

#### a) Mere Judging as an Intentional Modification of Evident Judging

**Paragraph 1.** Husserl begins by deepening the distinction between evident judging and mere judging. Not every judging is evident judging. In our ordinary cognitive life, we constantly make judgments that are not given in evidence — judgments based on hearsay, habitual convictions, vague anticipations, and the like. These are "mere" judgments (*bloße Urteile*): they have the form of judgments, they posit their contents, but they are not accompanied by the self-givenness of the state of affairs they assert. Evident judging, by contrast, is judging in which the state of affairs is given as it is in itself — given in the mode of "it itself" (*es selbst*).

**Paragraph 2.** Mere judging is to be understood as an intentional modification of evident judging. This is a crucial phenomenological point. The relationship between mere judging and evident judging is not that they are two separate kinds of activity; rather, mere judging is a derivative, modified mode of the original, evidently fulfilled judging. Every mere judgment implicitly refers back to a possible fulfillment — a possible evident judging in which the same content would be given in the mode of self-givenness. This is what Husserl calls the intentional implication (*intentionale Implikation*) of every judgment: every judgment, even one made emptily, carries within itself a reference to possible fulfillment, to a possible return to the things themselves.

**Paragraph 3.** The notion of intentional modification is essential to the entire genetic project. It means that the "higher" or "emptier" forms of consciousness always implicitly presuppose the "lower" or "fuller" forms. Empty judgment presupposes the possibility of filled, evident judgment; and filled judgment presupposes the objectual givenness of the substrates. The genetic regress is a tracing back of these intentional modifications to their fulfilling originals.

#### b) Mediate and Immediate Evidences and the Necessity of the Regress to the Plainly Immediate Cognitions

**Paragraph 4.** Within the domain of evident judgments themselves, there is a further distinction: some evidences are mediate, others immediate. A mediate evidence is one that rests on other evidences — for example, the evidence of a conclusion rests on the evidences of its premises. An immediate evidence is one that does not rest on any other evidence but is given originally, on its own basis. The regress from judgment-evidence to objectual evidence is, more precisely, a regress from mediate to immediate evidences, and ultimately to the most plainly (*schlicht*) immediate cognitions.

**Paragraph 5.** The plainly immediate cognitions are those in which objects are given not through any mediating judgmental activity but in the straightforward manner of direct acquaintance — paradigmatically, in perception. These are the "ultimate" evidences upon which all higher-level evidences rest. The regress to them is the regress to the bedrock of cognitive life. It is these plainly immediate cognitions that constitute the domain of pre-predicative experience.

#### c) The Immediate, "Ultimate" Judgments Relating to Individuals as Ultimate Objects-About-Which (Ultimate Substrates)

**Paragraph 6.** The lowest-level, most immediate judgments are those that relate to individual objects — to spatiotemporal particulars given in perceptual experience. These are the "ultimate" judgment-substrates (*letzte Substrate*). Every higher-level judgment, no matter how abstract or formal, can in principle be traced back — through chains of founding — to judgments about individuals. The individual object given in experience is the ultimate "object-about-which" (*Gegenstand-worüber*) of all judging.

**Paragraph 7.** This means that the investigation of origins must start from the most basic case: the case of a judgment about an individual perceptual object. How does such an object become available as a judgment-substrate? What are the pre-predicative experiential processes through which it is constituted as something determinable, something about which predications can be made? These questions define the initial terrain of the investigation that occupies the first part of the work.

### §6. Experience as Evidence of Individual Objects. Theory of Pre-Predicative Experience as the First Piece of the Genetic Theory of Judgment

**Paragraph 1.** The question about the character of objectual evidence is now identified as a question about the evident givenness of individuals. And the evidence of individual objects constitutes, in the widest sense, the concept of experience (*Erfahrung*). Experience in the first and most pregnant sense is thereby defined as direct relation to what is individual. Accordingly, the first judgments in themselves — as judgments with individual substrates, judgments about individuals — are experience-judgments (*Erfahrungsurteile*). The evident givenness of individual objects of experience precedes these judgments: their pre-predicative givenness. The evidence of experience is thus the ultimately original evidence sought, and thereby the point of departure for the origin-clarification of the predicative judgment. The theory of pre-predicative experience — that experience which provides the most original substrates in objectual evidence — is the first piece of the phenomenological theory of judgment. The investigation must begin with pre-predicative experience-consciousness and proceed upward from there, following the genesis of the higher-level evidences.

**Paragraph 2.** The concept of experience must be taken broadly enough to include not only the self-givenness of individual existence in straightforward certainty (*schlichte Gewißheit*), but also the modalizations of this certainty — probability, likelihood, and so on. Beyond these, it must also encompass experience in the mode of "as if" (*als ob*): the givenness of something individual in phantasy, which, through a corresponding freely possible change of attitude, becomes positional experience of a possible individual. This broadening of the concept of experience is critical for the later analyses, particularly in Part III on universal judgments and eidetic intuition, which rely on phantasy-variation.

**Paragraph 3.** This general and still relatively empty concept of experience does not yet suffice to understand the sense of the demanded regress, nor especially to understand in what way such an origin-clarification — which seeks the founding of predicative evidences in experiential evidences — is not a question of psychological genesis and in principle cannot be one. Moreover, the logician will still have objections to this regress. Even if he grants an evidence of experience and admits the broadening of the concept of evidence, the evidence of judgment will naturally appear to him as the better one — the one at which alone knowledge and cognition in the proper sense can be spoken of. What good is the regress from the domain of episteme into that of doxa, into a domain of vague experience with its "deceptive appearance"? Does not predicative judging alone remain the seat of knowledge, of genuine and proper evidence? Even granting experience a kind of evidence and conceding its genetic priority, is its evidence not of lesser quality? What, then, can an origin-clarification of judgment accomplish that leads from its evidence back into a dimension of apparently lesser rank? How can the essence of the better be clarified by regress to the lesser?

### §7. World as Universal Ground of Belief Pregiven for Every Experience of Individual Objects

**Paragraph 1.** To answer these questions requires deeper insight into the essence and structure of pre-predicative experience. The concept of experience as self-givenness of individual objects was taken so broadly as to include not only self-givenness in the mode of straightforward certainty, but also modifications of this certainty, and even the as-if modifications of actual experience. Even so, experience in the mode of certainty of being (*Seinsgewißheit*) has a distinctive priority. Not only does every phantasy-experience present itself as a modification and transformation of prior experiences and genetically refers back to them, but the modalizations of straightforward belief-certainty into probability, likelihood, and so forth are themselves modifications of an original, straightforward belief-consciousness (*schlichtes Glaubensbewußtsein*) in which all beings of experience are at first simply pregiven for us — as long as the further course of experience does not give occasion for doubt or modalization of any kind.

**Paragraph 2.** Prior to any onset of cognitive activity, objects are always already there for us, pregiven in straightforward certainty. Every beginning of cognitive doing already presupposes them. They are there for us in straightforward certainty: that is, as putatively being and being thus-and-so, as already counting for us before cognition, in various ways. As thus straightforwardly pregiven, they are the starting point and stimulus for cognitive activity, in which they receive form and the character of legitimacy, becoming the enduring core of cognitive achievements whose goal is called "truly being object" — the object as it is in truth. Before the onset of the movement of cognition, we have "putative objects," straightforwardly believed in certainty of belief, until the further course of experience or the critical activity of cognition shakes this belief-certainty, modifying it into "not so, but otherwise," "presumably so," and so on, or confirms the putative object as "really being so" and "truly being."

**Paragraph 3.** We can also say: prior to every movement of cognition, the object of cognition is already there as *dynamis* that is to become *entelecheia*. With this prior-being-there is meant: it affects as entering our field of consciousness from the background, or it is already in the foreground, even already apprehended, but only then awakens the "cognitive interest" (*Erkenntnisinteresse*) that is distinguished over against all other interests of life-praxis. But prior to apprehension there always lies affection, which is not an affecting by an isolated single object. To affect means to stand out from a surrounding that is always co-present, to draw interest — eventually cognitive interest — to itself. The surrounding is co-present as a domain of pregivenness, a passive pregivenness — one that, without any doing on our part, without any turning of the apprehending gaze, without any awakening of interest, is always already there. This domain of passive pregivenness is presupposed by all cognitive activity, by every apprehending turn toward a single object. What affects from its field is an object, a being among others, already pregiven in a passive doxa, in a field that itself constitutes a unity of passive doxa.

**Paragraph 4.** We can also say: prior to all cognitive activity, a respective world lies as universal ground; and that means, initially, a ground of universal passive belief in being (*passiver Seinsglaube*), which every single act of cognition already presupposes. Everything that is a being-object, a goal of cognition, is a being on the ground of the world that self-evidently counts as being. Individual putative beings within it may turn out to be non-being; cognition may in particular cases bring correction of beliefs about being; but that only means that instead of being thus, it is otherwise — otherwise on the ground of the world that in the whole is a being-world. This universal ground of world-belief is presupposed by all praxis, both the praxis of life and the theoretical praxis of cognition. The being of the world as a whole is the self-evidence that is never doubted and not itself first acquired through judicative activity, but rather already forms the presupposition for all judging.

**Paragraph 5.** World-consciousness (*Weltbewußtsein*) is consciousness in the mode of belief-certainty, acquired not through a special act of positing being or an act of apprehending something as existent, much less through a predicative existential judgment. All such acts presuppose world-consciousness in belief-certainty. In a concrete example: when I apprehend in my perceptual field — say, looking at a book on the table — some object, I apprehend a being that was already being for me, already "there," "in my study," even when I was not yet directed toward it. And likewise the whole study, with all its perceptually delimited objects, was already there for me, together with the unseen side of the room and its familiar things, with the sense "room of my apartment" in the familiar street, street in my city, and so on. Thus everything that is a being that affects us affects on the ground of the world; it gives itself as putatively being. And cognitive, judicative activity aims to examine whether it is truly as it presents itself, as it is pre-believed to be. The world as being-world is the universal passive pregivenness of all judicative activity, of all theoretic interest that sets in.

**Paragraph 6.** Even though it belongs to the distinctive character of consistently operative theoretical interest that it is ultimately directed toward cognition of the totality of beings — meaning of the world — this is already something later and derivative. The world as a whole is always already passively pregiven in certainty, and genetically more original than the direction toward its cognition as a whole is the direction toward individual beings, to know them — whether because their being or being-so has become doubtful and requires critical examination through cognitive doing, or because, undoubted in their being, they demand closer scrutiny for the purposes of praxis.

### §8. The Horizon-Structure of Experience; Typical Pre-Acquaintance with Every Individual Object of Experience

**Paragraph 1.** That every apprehension of an individual object and every further cognitive activity takes place on the ground of the world means still more than the dependence of cognitive activity on a domain of what is passively pregiven in certainty. A cognitive achievement is never performed on individual objects of experience as though they were given for the first time as still entirely indeterminate substrates. The world is always already for us a world in which cognition has already done its work in the most manifold ways. It is beyond doubt that there is no experience in the primarily simple sense of a thing-experience that, in first apprehending this thing, in taking cognizance of it, does not already "know" more of it than comes to cognizance in this moment.

**Paragraph 2.** Every experience, whatever it properly experiences, whatever it gets to see as the thing itself, has *eo ipso*, has necessarily, a knowing and co-knowing (*Mitwissen*) concerning this very thing — namely, of what belongs to it that it has not yet gotten to see. This fore-knowledge (*Vorwissen*) is indeterminate or incompletely determined in content, but never completely empty; and if it did not co-hold, the experience would not be experience of a thing at all — of this thing. Every experience has its experience-horizon; every one has its core of actual and determinate cognizance, its content of immediately self-given determinacies, but beyond this core of determinate being-so, beyond what is properly given as "itself there," it has its horizon.

**Paragraph 3.** This means: every experience refers to the possibility — and, from the ego's side, an "I-can" — not only of explicating the thing given in a first glimpse step by step according to what is properly self-given, but also of gaining further and further new determinations of the same thing through experience. Every experience is expandable into a continuity and explicative chain of individual experiences, synthetically unified as one single experience — an openly endless experience of the same. One may have enough of what has actually been experienced for one's present purposes, but then one "breaks off" with a "that is enough." But one can convince oneself that no determination is the last, that what has actually been experienced always has, endlessly, a horizon of possible experience of the same. And this horizon, in its indeterminacy, is co-held in advance as a range of possibilities, prescribing a course of closer determination that only actual experience decides for the determinate possibility, actualizing it over against other possibilities.

**Paragraph 4.** Thus every experience of an individual thing has its inner horizon; and "horizon" here means the induction belonging essentially and inseparably to every experience within every experience itself. The word is useful because it foreshadows (itself an "induction") induction in the ordinary sense of a mode of inference and the fact that this latter, in its really comprehending clarification, ultimately leads back to the original and primordial anticipation. From this, a genuine "theory of induction" must be built up. But this is said only in passing; what matters here is only the horizon-structure of experience.

**Paragraph 5.** This original "induction" or anticipation proves to be a modification-mode of originally instituting cognitive activities — a mode of "intentionality," one that intends (*meint*) beyond a core of givenness, anticipating. But intending-beyond not only in the manner of anticipating determinations that are now expected to come to light in this experienced object, but also, on another side, intending beyond this thing itself with all its anticipated possibilities of further determination — intending toward the other objects co-conscious with it, at first only in the background. That is, every experienced thing has not only an inner horizon but also an openly endless outer horizon of co-objects — a second-level horizon, referring to the first and implying it — objects that I am not turned toward at the moment but can turn to at any time, as different from the currently experienced one or as resembling it in some typology. But despite all anticipatorily conscious possible difference of the other objects, one thing is common to them all: all simultaneously anticipated or even only co-conscious background reals are conscious as real objects from the world, as being in the one spatiotemporal horizon.

**Paragraph 6.** This holds first and immediately for the world of plain, sensuous experience — for pure nature. But mediately it also holds for everything worldly: for human and animal subjects as subjects of the world, for cultural goods, utensils, artworks, and so on. Everything worldly has a share in nature. The naturalization of spirit is not an invention of philosophers — it is, when falsely interpreted and exploited, a fundamental error, but only then. It has its ground and its right in the fact that mediately or immediately in the spatiotemporal sphere everything that is worldly-real has its place.

**Paragraph 7.** Existence of a real thing thus never has any other sense than in-existence (*Inexistenz*) — being in the universe, in the open horizon of spatiotemporality, the horizon of already known and not merely now actually conscious, but also unknown, possibly coming to experience and future acquaintance, reals. The individual apperceptions make individual reals conscious, but inescapably with a sense-content that reaches beyond them, beyond the entire stock of individually apperceived items. In progressing from any given stock of posited individual apperceptions to a new stock, synthetic unity prevails; the newly apperceived possesses the previously still empty, content-indeterminate horizon of pre-validity that is already predelineated but not yet particularized and determined.

**Paragraph 8.** Thus to every individual apperception, to every given total stock of individual apperceptions, there attaches a sense-transcendence (*Sinnestranszendenz*): on the one hand, with respect to the constantly anticipated potentiality of possible new individual reals to be experienced from the world in the process of coming-to-consciousness; on the other hand, as inner horizon in every already appearing real, with respect to the stock of not yet apperceived features. Everything newly entering experience stands in the world-horizon and as such has its inner horizon. In thematic perception, it becomes known as it continuously presents itself during the stretch of experiencing, laying itself out into its individual features, its what-moments; these in turn are also conscious as self-presenting, but precisely with the sense of moments in which the real shows itself as what it is.

**Paragraph 9.** Everything that shows itself, and that was already implicitly there before the explication of the perceived, essentially counts as what of the real properly comes to perception in this perceiving. The thing itself is more than what comes to actual cognizance and has already come: it has the sense that its "inner horizon" constantly communicates to it. The seen side is only a side insofar as it has unseen sides that are anticipatingly sense-determined. We can thematically direct ourselves toward them, we can ask about them, we can make them intuitively present for ourselves. Every such fore-intuitioning (*Vorveranschaulichung*) of what belongs to this real "a priori" has the essential character of indeterminate generality. That is: if, for example, we fore-intuition the visual back side of a thing, we gain an intuitive presentation (similar to a memory), but not a fixed one, not an individually binding determinacy as in a genuine recollection. As soon as we really progress to inner determinacy, we become conscious of the arbitrariness of the color that results and must now be maintained as the thing's color. Every fore-intuitioning is carried out in a co-conscious fluid variability, in the consciousness of being able to fix variants — for example, a determinate color, but as a free variant, for which we could equally well substitute another.

**Paragraph 10.** On the other hand, the arbitrariness is not without limits. In the wavering of fore-intuitioning, in the transition from one variant to another, we remain in the unity of the anticipation — namely, the anticipation of the back side's color — which is, as anticipation, indeterminately general, anticipating what is determinate in a typical manner as typically pre-familiar. In the explication of this typical generality into determinate "possibilities" open for the actual being of this color, the range of possibilities emerges as the explicit "extension" of the indeterminate generality of the anticipation. Insofar as the thing entering into experience only has a sense of being as that of a given inner horizon, although only a core of what-nesses has entered into factual and proper cognizance, the thing — every real thing as experienceable — has its general "a priori," a pre-acquaintance (*Vorbekanntheit*), as an indeterminate but constantly self-identical identifiable generality of an a priori type, belonging to a range of a priori possibilities.

**Paragraph 11.** Obviously the type also encompasses the features that have entered into actual cognizance, if we take the type as total. In the alternation of what-nesses entering and departing, the real is constantly conscious as one and identifiable, and to this unity belongs the total type as total horizon of typical generality, into which everything that becomes actually known is incorporated as a particularizing, more or less completely fulfilling determination. As for the outer horizon that sense-determiningly belongs to any given individual real, it lies in the consciousness of a potentiality of possible experiences of individual reals, each having its own a priori as its typology in which it is necessarily anticipated, and which remains invariant through every fulfillment in the form of this or that possibility within the invariant range.

**Paragraph 12.** All particular typology — that of particular reals and constellations of reals — is surrounded by the totality-typology belonging to the whole world-horizon in its infinity. In the streaming of world-experience, of the concretely full world-consciousness in its particular present, the sense of being "world" remains invariant, and with it the structural makeup of this sense of being from invariant types of individual realities. Thus a fundamental structure of world-consciousness — or, correlatively, of the world as horizon of all experienceable individual reals — is the structure of acquaintance and non-acquaintance (*Bekanntheit und Unbekanntheit*), with its thoroughgoing relativity and its equally thoroughgoing relative distinction of indeterminate generality and determinate particularity.

**Paragraph 13.** The horizonally conscious world, in its constant validity as being, has the subjective character of familiarity in general — as a horizon of beings that is known in general but not in individual particularities. To everything that comes to particular validity as a being, this indeterminately general familiarity is distributed; each has its own as a known form within which all further distinctions between acquaintance and non-acquaintance run.

**Paragraph 14.** These rough indications must suffice for now, so that we may gain a concept of the essence and achievement of pre-predicative experience, of everything that is already in play in the experience of an object — this apparent ultimacy and originality of a primitive apprehension. It becomes clear how on the one hand it is correct that a truly being object is first the product of our cognitive activity, but also how for all cognitive activity, wherever it sets in, this producing of the truly being object does not mean bringing it forth from nothing. Objects are always already pregiven; an objectual surrounding world (*gegenständliche Umwelt*) is always already pregiven for us in advance.

**Paragraph 15.** From the very outset, everything affecting us from the background is conscious in an "objectual apprehension" (*gegenständliche Auffassung*), anticipatorily conscious as what belongs to every moment of life: the perceptual field is from the outset a field of "objects" apprehended as unities of "possible experience," that is, as possible substrates of acts of taking cognizance. What affects us from the passively pregiven background field is not a completely empty something, not some datum (we have no right word) without any sense, a datum of absolute non-acquaintance. Rather, non-acquaintance is always at the same time a mode of acquaintance. At the very least, what affects us is known in advance insofar as it is at all a something with determinations; it is conscious in the empty form of determinability, hence equipped with an empty horizon of determinations ("certain" indeterminate, unknown ones).

**Paragraph 16.** But still more. Not only the general apprehension as "object," "explicable in general," is predelineated for the developed consciousness, but already a determinate typification of all objects. With every novel, (genetically speaking) first-time constituted object, a new object-type is permanently predelineated, according to which other objects similar to it are apprehended from the outset. Thus our pregiven surrounding world is already "pregiven" as multiply formed — formed according to its regional categories and typified according to manifold special genera, species, and so on. The implication is that what affects from the background and is apprehended in the first active grasp is already known in a much more far-reaching sense: it is already in the background passively apprehended not merely as "object," experienceable, explicable, but as thing, as human being, as human artifact, and so on in further particularities. It accordingly has its empty horizon of a known non-acquaintance — the universal horizon "object" with particular inscriptions or rather predelineations for a style of explication to be carried out with explicates of a corresponding style. This horizon is nonetheless an empty one, a horizon of indeterminacies, of unknowns as determinables. Occasionally, something affecting may lack particular typification, but at the very least it is apprehended as an object, as a spatial object if it is a sensuous datum — even within the most general and absolutely necessary form "object in general."

### §9. The World as Horizon of All Possible Judgment-Substrates. The Character of Traditional Logic as World-Logic Conditioned Thereby

**Paragraph 1.** However much in formal logic the "terms" in judgments — the "S" and "p" — are thought of as formalized, the interchangeability of what can be inserted into these empty places (which, when judgments are considered purely according to their form, can be thought of as entirely arbitrary in its what) has its limits. It is not entirely freely arbitrary what can be inserted here. There remains the never explicitly stated presupposition that this something that is inserted is a being that enters into the unity of experience — correlatively, into the unity of the world as the totality of the experienceable in general — not merely into the unity of factual experience but also of all phantasy-possible experience: it is a being, if not of the actual world, then of a possible world. Accordingly, everything that can become a substrate, an object of judicative activity in free arbitrariness, has a belonging-together, a common structure, on the basis of which alone it can come to meaningful judgments at all. It is bound to the condition that it is a something, an identicum in the unity of our experience, and therefore something that must become accessible in objectual evidence in the unity of our experience.

**Paragraph 2.** With this, a limit is set to the free variability of the cores — a limit through which logic, without this ever having been expressed in it or made visible as its basic presupposition, is precisely logic of the world, of worldly beings. Thus the reduction of predicative evidence to the evidence of pre-predicative experience and the demonstration of the genesis of predicative judgment from pre-predicative world-experience does not represent a restriction that would put in question the universality of the exemplary value of this reduction. One could not object that there are predicative judgments not reducible to the pre-predicative evidence of experience. Rather, since we are asking about the genesis of what is traditionally pregiven as logical, we have herewith in fact demonstrated its genesis in universal generality, because it is the tacit presupposition of this traditional logic that everything that can enter as substrate into its judgments is something belonging together in the unity of our experience, and therefore traceable to a basic type — that of beings as worldly beings — as the universal style and invariant framework into which everything must fit.

**Paragraph 3.** With this, what was already asserted earlier (§5) becomes fully intelligible: the demonstration of the founding of predicative evidences in pre-predicative ones represents not merely the genealogy of determinate kinds of predications and predicative evidences, but the genealogy of logic itself in a fundamental piece — because all evidences, including those of the logician himself, have their sense-foundation in the conditions to which the possible making-evident of the ultimate substrates of judging is subject.

### §10. The Regress to the Evidence of Experience as Regress to the Life-World. Dismantling the Idealizations That Conceal the Life-World

**Paragraph 1.** All predicative evidences are ultimately to be grounded in the evidences of experience. The task of the origin-clarification of the predicative judgment — to demonstrate this founding relation and to follow the genesis of predicative evidences from those of experience — proves, after the now-achieved clarification of the essence of experience, to be the task of regressing to the world as it is pregiven as the universal ground of all individual experiences — as world of experience, immediately and prior to all logical achievements. The regress to the world of experience is a regress to the "life-world" (*Lebenswelt*) — the world in which we always already live and which provides the ground for all cognitive achievements and for all scientific determination.

**Paragraph 2.** From what has already been said, it follows that in the stream of our world-experience, as it is related to the always already pregiven world, we will by no means straightforwardly find those sought-for ultimately original experiential evidences — that genuine original institution of pre-predicative evidence building upon entirely original, originally instituted experiential evidence. It does not suffice simply to go back from individual judgments to the manner of pregivenness of their substrate-objects, as though the regress to an ultimately original experiential evidence could be undertaken from any arbitrary judgment taken as an example. Rather, in order to envision the structure of an entirely original acquisition of cognitions, we must always keep in view the experiential horizon in which every such experiential achievement inextricably stands.

**Paragraph 3.** The world in which we live and in which we engage in cognitive-judicative activity, from which everything that becomes a substrate of possible judgments affects us, is always already pregiven to us as pervaded with the precipitate of logical achievements. It is never given to us otherwise than as a world at which we or others — whose experiential acquisitions we take over through communication, learning, tradition — have already been logically judicatively, cognitively active. And this refers not only to the typically determined sense with which every object stands before us as familiar, in a horizon of typical familiarity, but also to the horizon-predelineation — the sense with which it is pregiven to us as an object of possible cognition, as a determinable in general. The sense of this pregivenness is determined by the fact that to the world, as it is pregiven to us, adult human beings of our time, belongs everything that modern natural science has achieved in the way of determinations of beings. Even if we ourselves are not interested in natural science and know nothing of its results, the beings are in advance at least so far determinately pregiven to us that we apprehend them as in principle scientifically determinable.

**Paragraph 4.** In other words, for this world pregiven to us, the idea has become self-evident on the basis of the tradition of modernity: that the infinite totality of beings as such is in itself a rational totality that, correlatively, is to be mastered through a universal science, indeed exhaustively. This idea of the world as a universe of being that is masterable by exact methods — those of mathematical-physical natural science — as an in-itself-determinate universe whose factual determinations science then has to ascertain, is so self-evident to us that we understand every individual datum of our experience in its light. Even where we do not acknowledge the universal validity and universal applicability of "exact" natural-scientific methods and ideals of cognition, the style of this mode of cognition has become so exemplary that the conviction prevails in advance that the objects of our experience are determinate in themselves, and that the achievement of cognition is precisely to discover these in-themselves-existing determinations in an approximation, to ascertain them "objectively," as they are in themselves — and "objectively" means "once and for all" and "for everyone."

**Paragraph 5.** This idea of the determinacy of beings "in themselves" is so self-evident that for even the layperson, wherever he reflects on the achievement of cognition, its "objectivity" is self-evident from the outset. Self-evidently presupposed thereby is that the space of our world and the time in which beings are experienced are precisely the space and time that are then to be grasped exactly as existing in themselves — the task of mathematical-physical natural science. Equally self-evidently presupposed is that the causal connection of beings, as given in experience, is the very same one that is then determined exactly and objectively in objective science.

**Paragraph 6.** With this, the world of our experience is from the outset interpreted with the help of an "idealization" — and it is no longer even seen that this idealization, which leads to the exact space of geometry, the exact time of physics, the exact causal law, and leads to viewing our world of experience as in-itself determined in this manner, is itself already an achievement of cognitive methods that grounds itself in the pregivennesses of our immediate experience. And this experience in its immediacy knows no exact space, no objective time and causality. Even though all theoretically scientific determination of beings is ultimately referred back to experience and its givennesses, experience does not simply give the objects in such a way that thought operating on the self-experienced objects — explicating, collecting, disjoining, relating, concept-forming, deducing, inducing — would straightforwardly lead to objects in the sense of true theory, of science. The objects of science are not the objects of experience, as they are purely experienced and determined on the basis of pure experience in the categorial operations that find their adequate expression in predicative sentences as finished formations of these operations.

**Paragraph 7.** Husserl draws the crucial distinction: "experience-judgments" — more precisely, judgments gained purely from the original productions in categorial acts, purely on the basis of experience — are not final judgments, not judgments of science in the pregnant sense, which works under ideas of finality. What separates the logical activities of idealization and mathematization (which presupposes idealization) from other categorial activities — to be expressed generally as "geometrization" — is precisely this.

**Paragraph 8.** The mathematization of nature, prepared by the creation of Euclidean geometry and paradigmatic since Galileo, has become so self-evident that from the very beginning in its Galilean creation the exact world was substituted for the world of our experience, and the question was entirely neglected of going back to the original sense-giving achievements through which the space of intuition, with its vague and fluid typology, becomes the exact space of geometry. Such a reconsideration would have shown that no phantasy-refashioning of the intuitive spatial shapes leads to the exact shapes of geometry, but only a method of idealization of the intuitively given; and similarly for all natural-scientific determinations that are attributed to beings as their determinations in themselves. What was thereby overlooked is that this method of idealization ultimately achieves nothing other than an infinitely extended foresight of what is to be expected in experience. And what is always overlooked is that this universe of determinations in themselves, in which exact science grasps the universe of beings, is nothing but "a garment of ideas thrown over the world of immediate intuition and experience, over the life-world" — so that every result of science has its sense-foundation in this immediate experience and world of experience and is referred back to it.

**Paragraph 9.** This garment of ideas makes us take for true being what is a method; and we always already understand the world of our experience in terms of the garment of ideas thrown over it, as if it were "in itself" this way. Therefore, every recollection of "pure experience" — including the reflections familiar to positivism — stops at the already idealized nature. And the same holds for the logician when he once asks about the experiential foundations of cognition; and no less for the psychologist, who from the outset views experiencing as standing in correlation with the true being of things, which he believes he can find in its objective determinacy and determinability.

**Paragraph 10.** Thus the logician too always sees the sense of cognitive achievements in the attainment of this "in-itself," of "objective" cognition; he forgets to ask whether that is the sense of cognitive achievement as such, the norm by which all judicative-cognitive doing may be measured — whether the achievement of cognition must not rather be measured against the aim of original experience, the goals arising from it, of which exact cognition forms only one possible one. He cannot do so precisely because he cannot gain any concept of an original experience, owing to the overlay of the originally given with the (no longer understood as such) idealization stemming from exact science.

**Paragraph 11.** If we wish to go back to experience in the ultimately original sense we are seeking, it can only be the original life-worldly experience — the one that does not yet know any of these idealizations, but is their necessary foundation. And this regress to the original life-world is not one that simply takes the world of our experience as it is given to us, but rather traces back the historicity already deposited in it to its origin — a historicity in which the world first accrued the sense of an "in itself" being world of objective determinability, on the basis of original intuition and experience.

**Paragraph 12.** Because the logician does not inquire behind this overlay of the world of original experience with idealizations, because he believes that the originality of experience can be straightforwardly restored at any time, he also measures cognition against this ideal of exactness, of episteme as exact, "objective" knowledge. Over against this, the regress to pre-predicative experience and the insight into what the deepest and ultimately original stratum of pre-predicative experience is means a justification of the doxa (*Rechtfertigung der Doxa*) — which is the domain of ultimately original evidences, not yet exact and not mathematically-physically idealized. The domain of doxa thereby proves itself to be not a domain of evidences of lesser rank than that of episteme, of judicative knowing and its precipitates, but rather the domain of ultimate originality to which, in terms of sense, exact cognition goes back, and whose character as a mere method — and not as a cognitive path mediating an "in-itself" — must be seen through.

**Paragraph 13.** No devaluation of exact cognition is thereby intended, nor of the logician's own apodictic evidences. What is meant is only an illumination of the path by which the higher-level evidences are to be reached, and of the hidden presuppositions on which they rest and that determine and delimit their sense. They themselves are not put in question in their content. It remains the case that cognition terminates in them, that it essentially belongs to its path to ascend from doxa to episteme — only that above the final goal, the origin and the proper right of the lower levels must not be forgotten.

### §11. The Origin-Clarification of Judgment and Genealogy of Logic in the Overall Horizon of the Transcendental, Phenomenological-Constitutive Problematic

**Paragraph 1.** For the same reason, the necessary regress to the most original evidences of experience cannot be accomplished with the means of psychology. Even a pure psychology — one directed purely at the purely lived and consciously given as such, carried out as pure inner psychology — could at best trace back from the pregiven typology of logical forms to the subjective achievements essentially belonging to such forms, in which formations of this form arise as evident formations. But even where it seriously takes up the evidence-problem and traces the founding of judgment-evidences in pre-predicative evidences, it would necessarily trace back to the experiences of evidence of subjects that are already subjects of our world — a world already overlaid with idealizations and apperceived in the sense of this overlay.

**Paragraph 2.** In its reflection on logicizing activity, from which all judgment-evidences arise, psychology would stop at an experience that is quite self-evidently thought of as related to an idealized world. The dismantling (*Abbau*) of these idealizations, the breaking through to their hidden sense-foundation in ultimately original experience, is no longer a problem that any psychology, however broadly and purely carried out, can set for itself. For psychological reflection on experiences, as they are accessible in inner perception, cannot lead to the genesis of this garment of ideas thrown over the world from original life-worldly experience. It takes experiences as individual, distinguishable occurrences in our consciousness, which it can investigate individually as to their genesis; but every such psychological reflection will lead to experiences as they are precisely experiences, experiences of the world — of a world that for this subject already stands there as finished, meaning as the one at which modern science has already done its work of exact determination.

**Paragraph 3.** What belongs to the experiences as the psychologist quite self-evidently finds them is this world, as correlate of the experiences he investigates, always already co-given. And from the experiences backward, he has no path to the origin of this world itself — which, admittedly, is itself no other than one arising from subjective achievements, activities of cognition, of exercising scientific methods, through which the world stands before us as thus and so determined and in principle further determinable in infinitum regarding its true being. These too are precipitates of subjective achievements, intentional achievements — but of an intentionality that does not lie open to the gaze of reflection, but is only implied (*impliziert*) in the precipitates that refer back to it.

**Paragraph 4.** To unveil these intentional implications and thereby the history of the world itself — in which the subject of psychology already finds itself in a finished world — thus also means regress to something subjective, through whose intentional achieving the world has gained this shape. But it is regress to a concealed subjectivity (*verhüllte Subjektivität*) — concealed because not actually demonstrable in reflection in its intentional achieving, but only indicated by the precipitates of this achieving in the pregiven world. The inquiry back to the most original evidences is thus also a subjective one, but directed to a subjectivity in a more radical sense than psychology's subjectivity can ever be. It is a dismantling of all the sense-precipitates that are already present in the world of our current experience, a tracing back from these sense-precipitates to the subjective sources from which they have come — and thereby to an achieving subjectivity (*leistende Subjektivität*) that is not the subjectivity of the subject that, reflecting psychologically, already confronts this finished world. It is rather the subjectivity through whose sense-achievements the world, as pregiven to us — our world — has become what it is for us.

**Paragraph 5.** In this unveiling of intentional implications, in the questioning of the sense-precipitates of the world as to their origin from intentional achievements, we understand ourselves as transcendental subjectivity — where "transcendental" means nothing other than the original Cartesian motif of inquiring back to the ultimate source of all cognition-formations, of the cognizer's reflecting upon himself and his cognitive life.

**Paragraph 6.** More precisely, the regress to the transcendental subjectivity constituting this pregiven world proceeds in two stages: (1) the regress from the pregiven world with all its sense-precipitates, with its science and scientific determination, to the original life-world; (2) the inquiry back from the life-world to the subjective achievements from which it itself arises. For the life-world too is nothing simply pregiven; it too is a formation that can be questioned as to the manners of its constitutive formation. Here too we already find logical sense-achievements — logical not in the sense of our traditional logic, which always already has the idealization of in-itself-being and in-itself-determinacy as its basis, but in the sense of an original logical achieving directed first at determination, cognition, in the restricted and relative horizons of life-worldly experience. But the logical sense-achievements are only one part of what contributes to the constitution of our experiential world. There belong to it also practical and emotional experiences, experience in willing, valuing, and hands-on doing. And the entire achievements of sensory experience belong to it, through which a world-time and a space and spatial things and co-subjects come to be constituted at all.

**Paragraph 7.** The whole interplay of conscious achievements that leads to the constitution of a possible world — a possible world: that is, what is at issue is the essential form of world as such and not our factual, actual world — to clarify this is the comprehensive task of constitutive phenomenology. The origin-clarification of the predicative judgment takes its place within this framework as the fundamental task of a genealogy of logic that, understood in this overall horizon and taken in its full and comprehensive sense, becomes transcendental logic.

**Paragraph 8.** Here, however, the task is a more modest one. We do not yet have at our disposal this comprehensive concept of the logical; we must start from the traditional concept and trace the incorporation and the share of what has already come into the tradition's view as logic and logical achievement, in the constitution of our experiential world, as well as the level it occupies in this constitution. Thus the task of transcendental logic — taken in the indicated comprehensive and ideally perfected sense — separates on one side from the task of constitutive phenomenology as a whole, and on the other from the task of a genealogy, that is, origin-analysis and subjective grounding of the traditional formal logic. Only the latter task is pursued here, in a fundamental piece: the origin-clarification of the predicative judgment.

### §12. The Approach of the Individual Analyses. The Distinction between Plain and Founded Experiences and the Necessity of Regressing to the Plainest Experiences

**Paragraph 1.** These indications must suffice for understanding the sense of the regress from predicative evidence to pre-predicative, objectual evidence — which is a regress to the evidence of life-worldly experience. What matters now is to draw from these general insights the consequences for the correct choice of the approach for the concrete individual analyses, and to seek out in the total domain of life-worldly experience those pre-predicative evidences in which the origin of the predicative judgment can be shown.

**Paragraph 2.** Even though we have already gained a concept of experience as objectual evidence of individual objects, such experience — even after all idealizations overlaying its originality are dismantled — is still manifold in itself. Our life-world, in its originality (to be restored only through the dismantling of those sense-layers), is not only a world consisting of logical achievements, not only a domain of pregivenness of objects as possible judgment-substrates and possible themes of cognitive activity. It is the field of experience in the entirely concrete sense that is everyday associated with the word "experience." And this everyday sense by no means refers purely to cognitive comportment, but in its greatest generality to a habituality that gives the person possessing it — the "experienced" person — security of deciding and acting in the situations of life. This everyday, concrete sense of "experience" points much more to practically active and valuing comportment than specifically to cognitive-judicative comportment.

**Paragraph 3.** From all this concrete fullness of experiencing, we initially abstract and go back directly from predicative judging and its evidence to the domain of passive belief in being — the consciousness of the pregivenness of judgment-substrates — a ground of belief that has been shown to be that of the world, through which every individual experience is experience in the horizon of the world. But this world-horizon is one determined not only by the familiarities of beings stemming from cognitive praxis, but above all by those stemming from the everyday life-praxis of hands-on doing. Despite this, the restriction to the narrower concept of experience is justified by the fact that every worldly comportment — practical doing as well as purely cognitive — is founded in experience in this sense.

**Paragraph 4.** We must therefore distinguish between plain (*schlichte*) and founded (*fundierte*) experiences. The world, as passively pregiven in belief-certainty and providing the ground of belief for all individual judging, is pregiven in plain experience, as a world of plainly, sensuously apprehensible substrates. Every plain experience, or every experience with the sense of being of a plain substrate, is sensory experience. The being substrate — a body maintaining itself in the consistency of experience as counting as truly being — in sensory experience, in universal consistency, has a unity of being, a unity of being-experienced. The beings of this universal experience constitute all-nature, the universe of all bodies. Nature in the world of our experience is the lowest, all-other-things-founding stratum; beings in their plainly experienceable properties as nature are what underlies all other ways of experiencing as substrate, what our valuing and practical doing operates on, and what lies at the base as invariant through all changing relativity of valuations and of usefulness for certain purposes.

**Paragraph 5.** If this experience is originarily giving, we call it perception, and specifically outer perception (*äußere Wahrnehmung*). Everything in the outer world is perceived as bodily existing in the spatiotemporal all-nature. Where we encounter animals and humans and cultural objects (utensils, artworks, and whatever else), there we have not mere nature, but expression of spiritual meaning-content (*geistiger Seinssinn*); there we are led beyond the domain of the plainly, sensuously experienceable. Perception purely as sensory perception is directed toward mere corporeality, plainly and straightforwardly. Over against it stands the perception of what is perceivable only through understanding of expression — as the understanding of a tool in its referring "memory" of humans who made it for a purpose, or again, immediately, the expression of a physical body as that of a human being. Both presuppose a sensory perception of the expression-founding corporeal and from there the transition into a reflection that mediately or immediately brings a co-being of human-personal (egoic) or animal subjectivity to final certainty — in this founded manner, a being that is not merely corporeal but at once therewith and in relation to it, subjective.

**Paragraph 6.** In order to reach the truly ultimate and original evidences of pre-predicative experience, we must regress from these founded experiences to the plainest, and for this purpose put all expression out of function. For every experience that finds beings determined otherwise than according to their natural properties — that ascertains them as tools or whatever — refers back to an understanding of expression. We thus leave in validity purely sensory perception and then experience in general, consider the world purely as world of perception, and abstract from everything it bears in the way of familiarities and determinations that do not stem from purely perceptual comportment but from our valuative as well as others' comportment — all those determinations that accrue to it from one's own or others' personal conduct. We thereby gain pure universal nature as passively pregiven experiential ground.

**Paragraph 7.** In this abstractive restriction of experience to the domain of what holds only for me, the person reflecting, there is already contained the exclusion of all idealizations — the exclusion of the presupposition of objectivity, the validity of our judgments "for everyone," which traditional logic, oriented toward the ideal of exact determination in the sense of scientific finality, tacitly always presupposed as belonging to the essence of judging. For as soon as we abstract from the others, there are also no validities referring to the cognitive activity of others, no sense-precipitates that make our world already understood as the one determined under ideas of finality through the achievements of science.

**Paragraph 8.** Admittedly, a certain idealization is already present in judgments on the basis of experience, in that we designate the substrates taken as examples with universal names, so that the objects so designated count as known at least for the relevant speech-community and the judgments as valid for it. The objectification even of natural experience is one to whose sense it belongs to count already for all — all who belong to the relevant lifeworld and community. But to arrive at an original judging, at the pursuit of the entirely original achievement that is accomplished in judicative determination, we must also abstract from this and proceed as though the achievements, without any such predelineation of an already co-given community, were in each case my entirely original acquisitions. Difficulties arise from the fact that the expressions of our language are necessarily expressions of universal, communicative sense, so that with the use of any object-designations at least this first idealization — that of counting for a speech-community — is always suggested, and it always takes renewed effort to keep this obtruding sense of the expressions at bay.

**Paragraph 9.** We thus pursue judging as though it were judging only for me, with acquisitions only for me, and we entirely abstract from the communicative function of judging and from the fact that it always already presupposes prior communication in the manner of how it has its objects pregiven. Only then do we arrive at the most primitive building blocks of logical achievement from which our world is built up. The objects that thus function as substrates are objects not thought of as being for all, not even as being for everyone of a limited community, but objects only for me; and the world from which they affect must be thought of as a world only for me. This methodical restriction to the domain of what is in each case one's own is necessary in order to get logical achievement in its ultimate originality, in which it is precisely the achievement of a single subject, into view.

### §13. The General Concept of Judgment and of the Object. Judgment as Determination

**Paragraph 1.** When one abstracts from all these overlays of the world of our experience and of our own experience, it becomes apparent that judging, already at this lowest level — where it is judging purely on the basis of experience and, still more restrictedly, of one's own experience — has structures that coincide with those of judgments under the idea of finality. Not only does the domain of the logical reach in this way into regions where there is not yet the scientific aim; with the expression "judgment," a universal essence is designated that is, in its basic structure, the same at all levels of logical achievement in which it occurs. What is exhibited as the structure of the predicative judgment in the restricted domain of experience-judgments simultaneously has its exemplary function for insight into the essence of judgment at higher levels.

**Paragraph 2.** If we wish to make precise the most general concept of judgment and the corresponding concept of object, of judgment-substrate, we must go back to the connection in which judging stands with life-experience in the entirely concrete sense. All experience in this concrete sense rests at bottom on the plain, ultimate, plainly apprehensible substrates-giving original doxa (*Urdoxa*). The natural bodies pregiven in it are ultimate substrates for all further determinations — cognitive, practical, and evaluative alike. These all attach to these plainly apprehensible substrates. But this domain of the original doxa is a domain of the flowing. Passively pregiven identity-unity is not yet identity apprehended and retained as such. Rather, the apprehension — for instance, the perceptual contemplation of the pregiven sensory substrate — is already an activity, a cognitive achievement of the lowest level. So too in mere perceiving, in which we let the gaze wander over the pregiven, affecting object. It then shows itself as "the same object from different sides," and in reflection the gaze is directed to the fact that it is given to us in perspectives, in adumbrations (*Abschattungen*), in which it shows itself as this one and selfsame, on which our attention is directed.

**Paragraph 3.** Even the mere perceptual contemplation of a pregiven substrate thus proves to be our achievement, a doing, not a mere undergoing of impressions. The naive consciousness, directed through all perspectives and adumbrations toward the object itself in its identity, only ever has the result of this doing in view — the object as perceptually explicating itself thus and so. It does not become aware that this givenness of the object, in these sensory properties, is itself already its achievement, a cognitive achievement of the lowest level. Hence it will be inclined to regard perceiving and contemplating as an undergoing, as passive comportment, and to contrast with this passivity of taking-in of pregiven objects only praxis in the narrower sense — the hands-on reshaping of pregiven things, as well as the producing of predicative sentences that then stand there as objective formations, products.

**Paragraph 4.** Thus the division between active comportment and passive taking-in, undergoing, runs for the naive, straightforwardly object-directed consciousness at a different place than for the gaze of reflection, which finds even in that taking-in of the pregiven, in its contemplative apprehension, a moment of activity, and over against it must gain a more radical concept of passivity than that of naive consciousness. This concept is that of purely affective pregivenness, of passive belief in being, in which there is nothing yet of cognitive achievement: the mere "stimulus" (*Reiz*) that proceeds from something environmentally being, like the barking of a dog "that just reaches our ear," without our having yet paid it attention and turned to it as a thematic object. Wherever there is attention, there is egoic activity of the lowest level.

**Paragraph 5.** Every apprehending turn-toward that takes part in the flow of sensory experience, attending to it, penetrating its properties contemplatively, is already an achievement, a cognitive activity of the lowest level, for which we can already speak of a judging. The being is admittedly already passively pregiven, constituted in identity, but only in apprehension is it held fast as this identical unity — which, however, need not yet involve anything of predicating.

**Paragraph 6.** Under "judgment" in the sense of traditional logic, to be sure, the predicative judgment is always understood — the judgment that finds its linguistic precipitate in the *apophansis*, in the assertoric sentence. Indeed, wherever a thing is designated by a name, even in the context of practical dealings, not merely a pre-predicative apprehension but already a predicative judging is at play, or presupposed as an already accomplished sense-achievement. But in order to delimit a widest concept of judgment over against this narrowest and most proper one — the predicative — we can abstract from all this and establish that already in every pre-predicative objectifying turn-toward a being, we must speak of judging in a wider sense. For example, a perceptual consciousness in which an object stands before us as being, as what is meant by us, is judging in this wider sense.

**Paragraph 7.** If we further consider that pre-predicative consciousness has its different modes of clarity and confusion, and that predicative judging in turn has its own distinctions of clarity and distinctness, then the widest concept of judgment encompasses all these modes, both predicative and pre-predicative. Judgment in this sense is the title for the totality of objectifying (objectivating) ego-acts — in the language of the *Ideas*, the doxic ego-acts. How pre-predicative judging, as a lower level of egoic activity (the level of receptivity) — of perceptual contemplating, explicating, and so on — is distinguished from the higher level, the spontaneity of predicative judging, will soon occupy us at length.

**Paragraph 8.** Judging in this widest sense of egoic activity of lower or higher level must not be confused with the passive *belief* that Hume and the positivism following him assumes as a datum on the consciousness-table. Brentano's concept of judgment also means such a datum — at least, as his doctrine of inner consciousness shows, not an activity radiating from the ego-pole. Every pregiven object affecting from the passive background has its passive doxa. It is the way it is pregiven, regardless of whether a gaze of perceptual, objectifying apprehension is directed toward this pregivenness, or whether it immediately becomes the theme of practical doing.

**Paragraph 9.** Already the passive constitution of a datum standing out in the background as a unity in immanent temporality has a passive doxa. This is nothing other than the belief-certainty belonging to the passive concordance (*Zusammenstimmung*) of intentionalities into a synthetic unity, which enters into all reproduction in a modified way, but always as passive certainty. Everything that constitutes itself in intentional concordance as a unity has a doxa. But only the activity of objectification, of cognizing — the egoic activity of lower and higher level that is not merely passive doxa — creates objects of cognition and judgment.

**Paragraph 10.** Objectification is thus always an active achievement of the ego, a being-conscious of the consciously given something; and this something is, through the continuous extension of the "I know it" in its duration, one thing, continuously the same. In distinguished acts that come to synthesis, it is conscious as identified, as synthetically the same, and always again recognizable as the same — including in freely repeatable recollections or freely producible perceptions. Precisely this identity, as correlate of an identification accomplishable in openly endless and free repetition, constitutes the pregnant concept of the object. As every other praxis has its practical goal, so the being object as being is the goal of the doxic, cognitive action — the action of explication of the being into its modes of being that are called determinations. This determination (*Feststellung*) of the being, as it is and what it is — which constitutes the function of judicative objectification — becomes a determination to which one can always return, and which as such is lasting cognitive possession, properly only at the higher level, in predicative judging, which finds its precipitate in the assertoric sentence. This sentence, as precipitate of a cognitive possession, is freely available, storable, and communicable.

**Paragraph 11.** Predicative judging first creates cognitive possession and cognitive objects in the pregnant sense — not yet the merely receptive, judicative contemplating, although this too already creates knowledge (*Kenntnis*) that is habitually retained. Every predicative judgment is a step in which lasting cognitive possession is created. It is in itself — and how, the further analyses will show — a completed step of determination and the original cell (*Urzelle*) of thematic determination.

**Paragraph 12.** Admittedly, not all cognizing-judicative objectification, not even everywhere it is predicative and finds its precipitate in assertoric sentences, is guided by this tendency toward determination "once and for all," toward "objective" determination. It can also be a matter of determining that serves only transitory practical purposes, valid only for a determinate situation or a plurality of typically similar situations. And all determination of value-properties and practical properties of things has this relativity to the situation. But even in this relativity, what is the distinguishing mark of all cognitive intention, of all judicative objectification, is preserved: that it strives to create, beyond the momentary situation, a possession of cognition that is communicable and usable in the future.

### §14. The Necessity of Starting the Analyses from Outer Perception and the Perceptual Judgment, and the Delimitation of the Investigation

**Paragraph 1.** Having gained a view of the connection of logical achievement with the stream of world-experience and its function in it, it must now also become clear where we must begin in order to follow in analytical detail the genesis of predicative judgment-forms from pre-predicative experience. Since we are looking for what is most elementary and foundational, it must be the judging grounded in the plainest and most immediate experience. The plainest experience is that of the sensory substrates, of the nature-stratum of the whole concrete world. We shall therefore orient ourselves by the judgment on the basis of outer perception — bodily perception — in order to study exemplarily the structures of predicative judging as such and its constitution upon pre-predicative achievements.

**Paragraph 2.** Judging on the basis of sensory perception and the explication into which perception mostly immediately transitions, and this perception itself, already presuppose a living-out of a purely contemplative interest in the pregiven affecting ultimate substrates, the bodies. What is first pursued in the pre-predicative sphere is thus the consistent working-out of the perceptual interest. This is not to say that in the context of concrete world-experience, it must always immediately come to such interest. Rather, the rule will be the transition from *aisthēsis*, from plain sensory awareness, to doing, valuing, and so on — to apprehending things as enjoyable, useful, and the like — before it can, for special reasons, come to such purely contemplative interest. The ego, living concretely in its surrounding world, given over to its practical purposes, is by no means above all a contemplating ego.

**Paragraph 3.** But the subsequent philosophical reflection on the structure of the world of immediate experience, our life-world, shows that contemplative perception has a distinction in that it uncovers and makes thematic structures of the world that everywhere underlie all practical comportment as well, although they are not throughout thematic. The contemplatively-perceptually lived-out interest is the activation of the fundamental *aisthēsis*, the passive original doxa — that fundamental stratum that underlies all experiencing in the concrete sense. Outer perception and the contemplative-perceptual interest thus have the advantage that in them the things are so apprehended that the tendency of judging toward determination can most readily be fulfilled. For it is the objects of pure perception, the plainly-sensuously apprehensible substrates — the nature-things, the things according to their fundamental stratum as natural bodies — that are irrelative, maintaining themselves as abiding objectual identities through all relativities of environmental dealings, and that can be determined and judged as such.

**Paragraph 4.** Perceiving and judging on the basis of perception is thus related not only to an invariant in all change and relativity of environments, but is at the same time, as a consistently lived-out contemplative interest (in a modification that includes the mentioned idealizations), the very attitude that underlies theoretical science and enables a determining with the aim of objectivity, of validity "once and for all" and "for everyone." It is therefore at the same time the very mode of pre-predicative evidence on which predicative judging grounds itself as traditional logic has it in view.

**Paragraph 5.** The preference for perception has a further ground in its greater simplicity. It is a methodological precept to begin such analyses with the simpler and only then ascend to the more complex. In this domain of merely contemplative perceiving, the constitution of predicative judging upon pre-predicative perceptual experiencing is most easily demonstrated; here there are objectual evidences that can straightforwardly be made visible as pre-predicative — the evidence of contemplative perceiving and explicating that is founded in no other evidence. The syntheses demonstrated here thereby gain the significance of exemplary ones. Investigating the more complicated transition to predicating from practical comportment and in its context, and the kind of syntheses in question there, would be the subject of separate investigations.

**Paragraph 6.** For the same reason of greater simplicity and transparency, the analyses will orient themselves above all by the perception of resting, unmoved objects, and will not bring in the much more difficult analysis of the perception of movement and judging about moving beings. It must remain open what modifications would result, though a basic structure of synthesis and explication and the predicative synthesis building upon it should prove to be pervasive.

**Paragraph 7.** It is further grounded in the exemplary character of the following investigations that they will confine themselves to the categorical judgment. It would be the task of further investigations to carry out a genetic derivation for the other judgment-forms as well. The theme is thus the categorical judgment on the basis of perception. And therein lies a further restriction: in perception, objects are given as actually being, in contrast to phantasy. Phantasy too has its manner of givenness of objectualities, but these are not actual but quasi-actual objects, objects in the as-if. Setting the domain of positional experience against that of neutrality, the exclusion of phantasy-experiences is at the same time the restriction of the investigation to the domain of positionality — at least for the start. At a later point, phantasy-experiences and judgments on the basis of phantasy will also need to be taken into account.

**Paragraph 8.** The analyses thus delimited will move, under abstraction from the co-being of the others, in the domain of being-only-for-me, in which there can be no talk of any of the idealizations, the garment of ideas thrown over the world of pure experience. Here we must seek out the most original, ultimately founding evidences from which predicative judging arises. With this originality, it is by no means said that these investigations, placed into the overall constitution of the phenomenological-constitutive systematics, concern an entirely elementary stratum in it. Even though they begin with an analysis of the perception of spatial-thingly individual objects, their theme is not the constitution of the perceptual thing and further of a spatial-thingly external world; rather, the structures of perceiving are drawn upon only so far as is necessary to understand how logical achievements build upon sensory-perceptual experience, how on the basis of perception categorial objects — states of affairs and general objectualities — are produced through logical spontaneity.

**Paragraph 9.** Already presupposed at the point where our analyses set in are manifold constitutive strata and achievements: it is presupposed that a field of spatial-thingly pregivennesses is already constituted, and with it the entire stratum of constitutive investigations concerning the constitution of thing-perception in all its levels — the constitutive formation of individual sense-fields, the cooperation of individual sense-fields and domains of sensibility for the perception of a fully concrete thing, the kinaestheses, the relation to a normally functioning body of the perceiver, and so on through the constitution of the sense-thing as resting and finally as standing in causal connection with other things. Also presupposed is the already accomplished constitution of the thing as temporal, as temporally extended, and on another side the constitution of the individual acts in which spatial-thingly reality is constituted, in inner time-consciousness. All these are dimensions of constitutive investigations that lie still deeper than those carried out here, and to which reference must be made so that clarity obtains about the place our investigations occupy in the overall constitutive systematics.


## Part I. Pre-Predicative (Receptive) Experience

### Chapter I. The General Structures of Receptivity

### §15. Transition to the Analysis of Outer Perception

**Paragraph 1.** The transition from the Introduction to Part I proper is marked by §15, which specifies the exemplary domain of analysis: the acts of outer perception, understood as the consciousness of the bodily presence (*leibhafte Gegenwart*) of individual spatial-thingly objects. These acts will serve as the exemplary field for studying the essence of pre-predicative experiential achievement and for showing how predicative syntheses build upon it. Husserl immediately emphasizes that the distinctions to be drawn in this domain of perceiving — between passive pregivenness and active ego-turning, between interest, receptivity, and spontaneity — are not restricted to the domain of perceiving or even of doxic experiences in general. Rather, they are structures to be found in all other domains of consciousness in the same way. There is not only an original passivity of sensory givennesses ("sense-data") but also of feeling; and correspondingly, not only an objectifying active turning-toward (as in perception) but also in valuing, in being pleased; and there too there are analogues of evidence and of perception as original self-givenness — self-givenness of values, of purposes, and so on.

**Paragraph 2.** Perceiving — the perceptual turning-toward individual objects, their contemplation and explication — is already an active achievement of the ego. As such, it presupposes that something is already pregiven to which we can turn in perception. And what is pregiven is not merely individual objects in isolation, but always a field of pregivenness from which individual items stand out and, so to speak, "incite" us to perception, to perceptual contemplation. What incites us to perception is pregiven in our surrounding world, affecting us from it. But in accordance with the introductory methodological restrictions, we abstract from the fact that it is always perceiving of objects of the world, of our environment. We presuppose only, within the indicated restriction, that there is a field of prominences (*Abgehobenheiten*) for me, toward which I turn perceptually. The constitution of this field itself would be the theme of its own very comprehensive analyses; within the framework of this investigation, some brief indications must suffice.

### §16. The Field of Passive Pregivennesses and Its Associative Structure

**Paragraph 1.** Let us take the field of passive pregivennesses in its — admittedly only abstractively attainable — originality: that is, let us abstract from all the acquaintance-qualities and familiarities with which everything affecting us already stands before us, on the basis of prior experiences. Let us take it as it is before egoic activity has exercised any sense-giving achievements upon it. In the strict sense, it is not yet a field of objectualities. For an object is, as noted, the product of an objectifying, egoic achievement — in the pregnant sense, of a predicatively judging achievement. But this field is nonetheless not a mere chaos, not a mere "buzzing" of "data," but rather a field of determinate structure, of prominences and articulated particulars. A sense-field — a field of sensory givennesses, for example optical ones — is the simplest model at which we can study this structure.

**Paragraph 2.** Even though a sense-field, an articulated unity of sensory givennesses (colors, for instance), is not immediately given as an object in the experience in which colors are already "apprehended" as colors of concrete things, surface-colors, "spots" on an object, and so on, the abstractive turn of the gaze is always possible in which we make this apperceptive sub-stratum itself into an object. The abstractively exhibited sensory givennesses are themselves already unities of identity that appear in manifold how's and can then become thematic objects. The now-seeing of white color in this illumination, and so on, is not the white color itself. Thus the sensory givennesses are already products of a constitutive synthesis that presupposes at the lowest level the achievements of synthesis in inner time-consciousness. The latter are the lowest-level syntheses that necessarily bind together all others. Time-consciousness is the primordial site of the constitution of identity-unity as such. But it is only a consciousness establishing a universal form. What the constitution of time achieves is only a universal ordering-form of succession and a form of coexistence of all immanent givennesses. But form is nothing without content. An enduring immanent datum is only enduring as datum of its content. Thus the syntheses that establish the unity of a sense-field are already, so to speak, a higher level of constitutive achievements.

**Paragraph 3.** Let us now consider a unitary sense-field as it is given in an immanent present, and ask: how is consciousness of a prominent individual possible in it at all? Further, what essential conditions must be fulfilled for consciousness of a prominent plurality of equals or similars to come about? Every such sense-field is a unity for itself, a unity of homogeneity. To every other sense-field it stands in the relation of heterogeneity. Something individual in it is prominent by virtue of contrasting against something: for example, red spots on a white background. The red spots contrast against the white surface, but among themselves they are merged without contrast — not in the manner of flowing into each other, but in a kind of distant fusion (*Fernverschmelzung*); they can be brought into coincidence with each other as equals. Yet even in every contrast there remains something of kinship and fusion: the red spots and the white surface are originally related to each other as visual givennesses. And this homogeneity is distinguished from the heterogeneity of givennesses of another kind, such as acoustic ones.

**Paragraph 4.** Thus the most general content-syntheses of prominent sense-givennesses, which are unified in the living present of a consciousness, are those according to kinship (homogeneity) and foreignness (heterogeneity). Kinship or similarity can have various degrees, with the limit of most perfect kinship being distanceless equality. Wherever no perfect equality obtains, contrast goes hand in hand with similarity: the standing-out of the unequal from a ground of the common. When we pass from equality to equality, the new equal gives itself as repetition; it comes into perfect distanceless coincidence with the first in regard to its content. This is what we call fusion (*Verschmelzung*). When we pass from similar to similar, a kind of coincidence also occurs, but only partial — with simultaneous conflict of the non-equal. What in a purely static description gives itself as equality or similarity is thus itself already to be regarded as the product of one or another kind of coincidence-synthesis — a coincidence-synthesis that we designate, with the traditional expression but under a transformation of its sense, as association. The phenomenon of associative genesis governs this sphere of passive pregivenness, built up upon the syntheses of inner time-consciousness.

**Paragraph 5.** The title "association" in this context designates a form of lawfulness of immanent genesis belonging essentially to consciousness as such. That it can become a general theme of phenomenological description and not merely of objective psychology lies in the fact that the phenomenon of indication (*Anzeige*) is something phenomenologically demonstrable. (This insight, already worked out in the *Logical Investigations*, already formed there the germ of genetic phenomenology.) Every conception of association and its lawfulness as a kind of psychophysical natural lawfulness to be won through objective induction must therefore be excluded here. Association comes into question here exclusively as the purely immanent nexus of "something reminds of something," "one points to the other." This phenomenon can be concretely seen only where we have individual prominences, individual givennesses standing out from a field: one reminds of the other. And this relation is itself phenomenologically demonstrable. It gives itself in itself as genesis: the one member is consciously characterized as waking, the other as wakened. Not always is association originarily given in this way; there are also cases of mediate association with skipping of intermediate members. But all immediate association is association according to similarity. It is essentially not possible otherwise than through similarity of varying degree up to the limit of complete equality.

**Paragraph 6.** Thus all original contrast also rests on association: the unequal stands out from the ground of the common. Homogeneity and heterogeneity are therefore the result of two different fundamental modes of associative unification. A further mode, distinct from these, is the unification of what is present and what is not present. The unity of a sense-field is thus unity only through associative fusion (homogeneous association), just as its order and articulation and every formation of groups and equalities within it is produced through the operation of association: the similar is wakened by the similar and enters into contrast with the dissimilar. This can first be demonstrated in the structure of a homogeneous sense-field; it then holds in the same way for all givennesses, including the more complex ones. And what we call the perceptual field — the field of passive pregivenness to which perceptual apprehension turns, apprehending individual items from it as perceptual objects — is already a "field" of much more complicated structure, constituted through the synthetic unification and cooperation of several sense-fields.

### §17. Affection and Ego-Turning. Receptivity as the Lowest Level of Egoic Activity

**Paragraph 1.** All prominences in the field, its articulation according to equalities and differences and the resulting group-formation, the standing-out of individual members from the homogeneous ground, are the product of manifold associative syntheses. But these are not simply passive processes in consciousness; these coincidence-syntheses have their affective force. We say, for example, that what stands out through its dissimilarity from the homogeneous ground "catches the eye" (*fällt auf*); and this means it unfolds an affective tendency toward the ego. The syntheses of coincidence — whether coincidence in distanceless fusion or coincidence under conflict of the non-equal — have their affective force, exercise upon the ego a stimulus (*Reiz*) toward turning, whether or not it follows the stimulus.

**Paragraph 2.** When it comes to the apprehension of a sensory datum in the field, this always occurs on the basis of such prominence. It stands out through its intensity from a plurality of affecting items. For example, in the sensory sphere, a tone, a noise, a color is more or less obtrusive. They lie in the perceptual field and stand out from it, exercising upon the ego, not yet apprehended, a stronger or weaker stimulus. Likewise, an emerging thought can be obtrusive, or a wish, a desire, can address us from the background with obtrusiveness. The obtruding is conditioned by more or less sharp prominence — in the sensory sphere, by contrasts, qualitative discontinuities of considerable distance, and the like. In the domain of non-sensory givennesses, there is no talk of such qualitative discontinuities; yet there is something analogous: among various obscure thought-stirrings that move us, one thought, for example, stands out above all others, has a sensitive effect on the ego by pressing itself upon it.

**Paragraph 3.** We must distinguish those discontinuities (in the sensory sphere, primarily qualitative or intensive discontinuities) that "cause" an obtruding, and whatever else is similarly a condition of obtruding, from the obtruding itself. Obtrusiveness has gradual degrees, and the obtruding item comes nearer to or remains farther from the ego. We thus distinguish what obtrudes and the ego upon which it obtrudes. According to the intensity of obtrusiveness, the obtruding item has greater ego-nearness or ego-distance (*Ichnähe* or *Ichferne*). These differences of obtrusiveness and of the corresponding stimuli upon the ego can be quite well ascertained in the field of consciousness through retrospection — they are phenomenologically demonstrable data.

**Paragraph 4.** Something new enters when the ego follows the stimulus. The stimulus of the intentional object, directed toward the ego, draws it with a stronger or weaker pull, and the ego yields. A gradual tendency connects the phenomena — a tendency of the transition of the intentional object from the status of the ego-background into that of the ego-counterpart (*Ichgegenüber*); a transformation that is correlatively one of the whole intentional background-experience into a foreground-experience: the ego turns toward the object. The turning-toward itself is first an intermediary process; the turning ends with the ego's being-at (*Sein bei*) the object and its touching apprehension of it. With this yielding of the ego, a new tendency has appeared — a tendency from the ego toward the object. We must therefore distinguish: (1) the tendency before the cogito — the tendency as stimulus of the intentional background-experience, with its various degrees of strength; and (2) the turning-toward as yielding to the tendency — the transformation of the tendency-character of the intentional background-experience, through which it becomes an actual cogito.

**Paragraph 5.** The ego is now turned toward the object, from itself tendentiously directed toward it. Thus, generally speaking, every cogito, every specific ego-act, is a striving carried out from the ego, with its various forms of working-out. It can work itself out unhindered or hindered, more or less completely. This tendency too has various degrees of tension-strength. The ego can be more or less vividly drawn to an affecting item, at varying tempos of intensity-increase. Correspondingly, the manner and tempo of following can have similar differences, without being determined by this alone. The ego need not wholly give itself over to a strong stimulus; it can engage with it at various intensities.

**Paragraph 6.** The carrying-out of the turning is what we designate as wakefulness (*Wachsein*) of the ego. More precisely, there is to be distinguished wakefulness as actual carrying-out of ego-acts and wakefulness as potentiality, as the state of being-able-to-carry-out acts, which forms the presupposition for their actual carrying-out. Awakening is directing the gaze at something. Being-awakened means undergoing an effective affection; a background becomes "alive," intentional objects come nearer to the ego from there, this or that one effectively draws the ego to itself. It is at it when it turns toward it.

**Paragraph 7.** Insofar as the ego, in turning-toward, takes up what is pregiven to it through the affecting stimuli, we can here speak of the receptivity of the ego. This phenomenologically necessary concept of receptivity by no means stands in exclusive opposition to the activity of the ego — under which title all acts specifically proceeding from the ego-pole are to be comprised. Rather, receptivity is to be regarded as the lowest level of activity. The ego lets what comes in be pleasing to it and takes it up. Thus we distinguish, for example, under the title "perceiving": on the one hand, the mere being-conscious in original appearances (which present objects in original bodily presence) — in this way an entire perceptual field is placed before our eyes, already in pure passivity; on the other hand, under the same title "perceiving" stands active perception as active apprehending of objects that stand out in the perceptual field extending beyond them.

### §18. Attention as Ego-Tendency

**Paragraph 1.** What specifically concerns the domain of objectifying experiences — the doxic ones, in which "what is" is conscious to us, even if only in the manner of background — it is in general the corresponding doxic turning-toward that the psychologists usually have in view under the title of attention. But someone wholly given over to the beauty of a picture, living in pleasure in it and not in the belief of being, in an intention toward what is, and likewise someone in the carrying-out of a productive action who lives wholly in the act of willing — both are called "attentive" in ordinary life: attentive to the beauty, attentive to the productive happening. In these cases, doxic doing and valuative or productive doing are often interwoven.

**Paragraph 2.** In general, attention is a tending of the ego toward the intentional object — toward the unity "appearing" in the constant change of modes of givenness — belonging to the essential structure of a specific ego-act, and specifically as a carrying-out-tending. The carrying-out commencing with the turning-toward, the starting-point of the act-carrying-out, is the beginning of an ongoing carrying-out-directedness of the ego toward the object. The beginning prefigures a direction of a further, synthetically unitary carrying-out-process in which, phase by phase, the tendency that has accrued to it from the starting-point and the previous carrying-outs is fulfilled, but at the same time tendentiously extends itself further and points ahead to new stages of fulfillment. So up to the "end" or to the breaking-off. The beginning thus has an intentional horizon; it points beyond itself in an empty manner that becomes intuitive only in subsequent actualizations.

**Paragraph 3.** To all intentional experiences belongs this essential difference in the mode of tendency: either "in" the experience the ego actively lives, directed toward the intentional objectuality, occupied with it; or this is not the case — in which at best, from the experience (as background-experience), or from the intentional object therein, a waking pull goes toward the ego, a waking stimulus in varying affective force upon the (in the case of wakefulness, otherwise already active) ego.

### §19. The Experiencing Ego-Tendency as "Interest" in What Is Experienced and Its Working-Out in the "Doing" of the Ego

**Paragraph 1.** A special case of attentive intentional experiences, of ego-acts in the mode of carrying-out, are the doxic ones — acts directed toward what is (in eventual modalization: what possibly is, presumably is, is not). Among these belong the intuitive ones in different modes of intentional immediacy or mediacy, and ultimately the evident ones — the experiences that give what is, itself (strictly speaking: the doxic experiences). When we further speak of attention, specifically of perception and recollection, the doxic acts are always meant.

**Paragraph 2.** The onset of the turning-toward, of the attending to what is, sets a tendentious comportment in play — a striving. It is an actualizing striving, a doing in its various forms of conclusion or breaking-off. The beginning of the perceptual act with the turning-toward is already a consciousness of being-at the object itself — perceiving is consciousness of the apprehension of the object in its bodily presence, so to speak. But the tendency from the ego has not yet come to its conclusion with the onset of the turning. It is directed toward the object, but initially merely aiming at it. We can say: with it, an interest in the perceptual object as being has awakened. We are continuously directed toward it, carrying out the continuous consciousness of its experiencing. But in this firm direction toward the object, in the continuity of its experiencing, there lies an intention that tends beyond the given and its momentary mode of givenness toward an ongoing *plus ultra*. It is not only an ongoing being-conscious, but a striving-onward to new consciousness as an interest in the enrichment of the objectual "self" that comes with further apprehending. Thus the tendency of the turning goes further as a tendency toward complete fulfillment.

**Paragraph 3.** What affects initially draws the gaze of the ego upon itself in an undifferentiated unity. But this unity immediately begins to come apart into its constituting moments; they begin to stand out; while one is in the focal point, the others, as belonging to the object, are thematically drawn into the intentional unity of the same and exercise their stimuli. Likewise, horizons are awakened with everything actually given. For example, when I see the resting thingly object from the front, the unseen back side is conscious in horizon; the tendency that aims at the object is now also aimed at making it accessible from the other side as well. Only in this enrichment of givenness — the penetration into the particulars and the being-given "from all sides" — does the tendency pass from the initial mode of aiming (*Abzielung*) into the mode of attainment (*Erzielung*), which again has its various stages.

**Paragraph 4.** Thus the tendency works itself out in a manifold "doing" of the ego. It aims to transform the appearance (presentation) that the ego has of the external object into other and yet other "appearances of the same object." It moves in the closed manifold of "possible appearances." It strives for ever new alterations of appearance in order to bring the object to givenness. It is directed thereby at the one identical object that "presents itself" in all these appearances — the same object from this side and that side, from nearer and farther. The tendency aims to transform the something in the how of one mode of appearance into the same something in the how of other modes of appearance. It aims at the "production" of ever new modes of appearance, which we can also call "images" (*Bilder*) — a concept of image that of course has nothing to do with depiction, but is quite usual in language.

**Paragraph 5.** In this sense, every object of outer perception is given in an "image," and it constitutes itself in the synthetic transition from image to image, in which the images come into synthetic coincidence as images (appearances) of the same. Every perception that presents the object to me in this orientation practically leaves open the transitions into other appearances of the same object, in certain groups of appearances; the transition-possibilities are practical possibilities. There is a freedom of running through such that I move my eyes, move my head, alter my bodily posture, walk around, keeping the gaze directed at the object, and so on. We call these movements, which belong to the essence of perception and serve to bring the perceptual object to givenness from all sides as far as possible, kinaestheses. They are the working-out of the tendencies of perception, in a certain sense "activities," although not voluntary actions. I carry them out (in general) without performing voluntary acts. Involuntarily I move my eyes and so on, without "thinking about my eyes." The relevant kinaestheses have the character of active subjective processes; hand in hand with them, motivated by them, goes a running-off of the changing "associated" visual or tactual "images," while yet the object is "given" to me in its resting duration or in its alteration.

**Paragraph 6.** I am on the one hand receptive with respect to the object and on the other hand nevertheless productive. The occurrence of images is "in my power"; I can also let the series break off, I can, for example, close my eyes. But beyond my power is that, when I let the kinaestheses run off, a different image occurs; with respect to it I am merely receptive. Thus perceiving, commencing with the first turning of the ego, is animated by perceptual tendencies — tendencies of continuous transition from apperceptions to new apperceptions, tendencies to run through kinaesthetic manifolds and thereby to bring the "images" into their course. A division is thus to be made between (1) a doing that is not an "I-do" — a doing before the turning-toward; and (2) the I-do, which, however, need not yet include anything of voluntary action.

### §20. Narrower and Wider Concept of Interest

**Paragraph 1.** We also spoke of an interest that awakened with the turning toward the object. Now it becomes apparent that this interest has nothing yet to do with a specific act of will. It is no interest that would generate something like intentions and voluntary actions. It is merely a moment of striving belonging to the essence of normal perception. That we speak of interest here has its ground in the fact that hand in hand with this striving goes a feeling — a positive feeling, not to be confused with a pleasure in the object. It may indeed be that the object itself touches our feeling, that it is valuable to us, and that we therefore turn toward it and linger with it. But equally it may be that it is a disvalue and precisely through its repulsiveness awakens our interest. The feeling that belongs to interest is a quite peculiarly directed one. In every case, the sensory content of the object will necessarily enrich itself, partly through its mere intuitive continuation in perception, partly through the consequent awakening of its obscure horizons, related to possibilities and expectations of ever new enrichments. Attached to this is a feeling of satisfaction in this enrichment, and with reference to this horizon of widening and heightening enrichment, a striving to "come ever closer" to the object, to appropriate its self ever more completely.

**Paragraph 2.** From the concept of interest developed here, another, wider concept is to be distinguished. This striving to enter into the object and the satisfaction in the enrichment of its self does not occur when I am merely turned toward the object in general, but only when I am turned toward it in the specific sense of a theme. Theme in this pregnant sense and object of the ego-turning do not always coincide. I can be thematically occupied with something — for example, a scientific work — and be disturbed by a noise from the street. It presses in upon me, and I turn toward it for a moment. Yet my previous theme is not thereby dropped; it has merely retreated into the background for the moment. It remains my theme, to which I return as soon as the disturbance is past. With reference to this, a wider concept of interest can be formed, encompassing not only the acts in which I am thematically turned toward an object but every act of the (transitory or lasting) ego-turning, of the being-there (*inter-esse*) of the ego.

### §21. The Inhibition of Tendencies and the Origin of the Modalizations of Certainty

**Paragraph 1.** Let us return to interest in the first and proper sense. In the working-out of its tendentious striving-onward to ever new modes of givenness of the same object, the concrete perception comes about. These tendencies can work themselves out unhinderdly or be inhibited. This means: the tendencies are not merely blind striving-onward to ever new modes of givenness of the object; they go hand in hand with expectation-intentions, with protentional expectations relating to what will come to givenness in the further course of perceptual contemplation — expectations, for example, regarding the hitherto unseen back side. Every perceptual phase is a system of rays of actual and potential expectation-intentions. In the normal case of perception — the unhindered continuous running-off of phases — a continuous process of actualizing arousal and then steady fulfillment of expectations takes place, whereby fulfillment is at the same time closer determination of the object. The satisfaction of the interest, the fulfillment of the tendencies in striving-onward from phase to phase, is at once fulfillment of the expectation-intentions. This is the normal case of the unhindered running-off of intentions; the object then stands before us in straightforward belief-certainty as being and being-so.

**Paragraph 2.** The opposite case is that the tendencies are inhibited. Then it stays perhaps at one image of the object; it comes to givenness not from all sides but only "from this side." Then perceiving breaks off — whether because the object disappears from the perceptual field, is covered by another object pushing before it, or because although it still stands perceptibly before our eyes, another stronger interest makes itself felt, giving occasion for other thematic occupation and displacing the interest in the object without its tendency's having come to full working-out and fulfillment. The interest remains more or less unsatisfied.

#### a) The Origin of Negation

**Paragraph 3.** But inhibitions can also occur in the fulfillment-course of the tendencies in another way: the perceptual interest in the object can persist; it is further contemplated, is further given so that it can be contemplated. Yet instead of fulfillment of the expectation-intentions, disappointment (*Enttäuschung*) occurs. For example, a uniformly red sphere is seen; for a stretch, the perceptual course has run off such that this apprehension fulfills itself consistently. But now in the progress of perceiving, part of the hitherto unseen back side gradually shows itself, and contrary to the original predelineation — "uniformly red, uniformly spherical" — the consciousness of otherwise (*Anders*), disappointing the expectation, occurs: "not red, but green," "not spherical, but dented." A certain measure of continuing fulfillment is presupposed throughout, and correlatively, a certain unity of the objectual sense must maintain itself through the flow of changing appearances. Only one part of the predelineating expectation-intention is affected, and the corresponding part of the objectual sense receives the character of "not so, rather otherwise." A conflict (*Widerstreit*) occurs between the still living intentions and the sense-contents appearing in newly instituted originality. But not only conflict: the newly constituted objectual sense in its bodily presence (*Leibhaftigkeit*) throws its opponent, as it were, out of the saddle, overwhelming it with its bodily fullness.

**Paragraph 4.** This describes the original phenomenon of negation, of nullity or "cancellation" (*Aufhebung*), of "otherwise." What was analyzed at the example of outer perception holds analogously for every other positional (being-positing) consciousness and its objectualities. Negation is thus not first a matter of predicative judging but already appears in its primal form in the pre-predicative sphere of receptive experience. Essential for negation is always the overlay of a new sense upon an already constituted one together with the displacement of the latter; and correlatively, on the noetic side, the formation of a second apprehension that lies not alongside the first, displaced one, but above it, in conflict with it. Belief conflicts with belief — belief of one sense-content and intuitive mode with that of another sense-content in its intuitive mode.

**Paragraph 5.** Two results follow: (1) Negation in its originality presupposes the normal original constitution of the object — normal perception, normally unhindered working-out of the perceptual interest. This must be there in order to be originarily modified. Negation is a consciousness-modification that in its own essence announces itself as such. It is always partial cancellation on the ground of a belief-certainty that maintains itself throughout, ultimately on the ground of the universal world-belief. (2) The original constitution of a perceptual object takes place in intentions that essentially can at any time take on a modification through the disappointment of the protentional expectation-belief; this occurs in one with the overlay of oppositely directed intentions that essentially enters here.

#### b) The Doubt-Consciousness and the Possibility-Consciousness

**Paragraph 6.** Not only the original phenomenon of negation is already to be found in the pre-predicative sphere; the so-called modalities of judgment, which form a core piece of traditional formal logic, also have their origin and their foundation in occurrences of pre-predicative experience. Not always must a smooth break of a normally running perception simply occur, a smooth disappointment of one of its expectation-intentions. In place of simple cancellation, a mere becoming-doubtful can also occur, without a hitherto straightforwardly valid perceptual apprehension's being straightforwardly cancelled. Doubt represents a transitional mode toward negating cancellation, but it can also appear as a lasting state. We see, for instance, a figure standing in a shop window, which we at first take for an actual person — perhaps an employee working there. Then we become uncertain whether it might not be a mere mannequin. The doubt can resolve in one direction or the other upon closer looking, but a hovering state of doubt — whether person or mannequin — can also be maintained for a time.

**Paragraph 7.** Two perceptual apprehensions overlap. One maintains itself in the normally running perception with which we began; for a while we saw a person there, consistently and without dispute. Then, not a smooth break in the form of decided disappointment occurs; rather, the full concrete content of actual appearance receives a second content pushing over it: the visual appearance — the color-filled spatial shape — was previously equipped with a halo of apprehension-intentions giving the sense "human body" and indeed "person"; now the sense "clothed mannequin" pushes over it. Nothing changes in what is actually seen; indeed, common to both is still more — clothes, hair, and the like apprehended in both cases, but in one case flesh and blood, in the other perhaps painted wood. One and the same stock of sensation-data is the common foundation of two superimposed apprehensions. Neither is cancelled during the doubt; they stand in mutual conflict, each has its force, its motivation, its demand. But demand stands against demand; one contests the other and receives the same from it. There remains, in doubt, an undecided conflict.

**Paragraph 8.** The division is thus not truly a doubling of perceptions, although the fundamental character of perception — the bodily-presence consciousness — obtains on both sides. The consciousness-mode has changed: instead of being conscious as simply-there, as in the normal, uni-directional and simultaneously consistent perception, it is now conscious as questionable, as doubtful, as contested — contested by another, by something bodily given in another perceptual phase, interpenetrating with it in conflict.

**Paragraph 9.** We can also express this by saying that the consciousness that makes things conscious originarily and bodily has not only the mode of bodily presence (distinguishing it from presentifying and empty consciousness) but also a changeable mode of being or validity (*Geltungsmodus*). The original, normal perception has the primal mode: being, valid simply; it is naive, straightforward certainty. The appearing object stands in undisputed and unbroken certainty. The "undisputed" points to possible disputings or even breaks — those now described — with which, in becoming bifurcated, a modification of the validity-mode takes place. In doubt, the two conflicting bodily-presences have the same validity-mode: "questionable" — and each questionable item is a contested one, contested by another.

**Paragraph 10.** All this holds not only for the momentary perceptual situation in the now-phase but, just as with negation, the conflict essentially radiates back into the already elapsed phases. In them too, the uni-directional consciousness breaks apart into a multi-directional one: the becoming-bifurcated with its apperceptive overlay extends into the retentional consciousness. In this case of becoming-doubtful too, just as with negation, an inhibition in the fulfillment-course of the tendentious perceptual interest is present. The ego is, so to speak, pulled back and forth between inclinations to believe (*Glaubensneigungen*) without, in the case of doubt, being able to decide for one. Each of the anticipatory expectation-intentions belonging to the perception gives an ambiguous rather than unambiguous predelineation.

**Paragraph 11.** Thus the normal ego-act of perception with its straightforward belief-certainty becomes modalized in what we call belief-suggestions (*Glaubensanmutungen*). With regard to the noematic side, we speak of being-suggestions (*Seinsanmutung*): from the object, affection goes out — it suggests itself, just as its hostile partner, as being and being-so, to the ego. This suggestive quality we call (considered apart from the relation to the ego) possible. In this conflict of inclinations to believe, correlatively of being-suggestions, a concept of possibility has its origin. Possible-being, possibility, is a phenomenon that, like negation, already occurs in the pre-predicative sphere and is most originally at home there. These are in this case problematic possibilities, lying in conflict with one another. We can also call them questionable possibilities. The most characteristic expression is, however, suggestive possibility (*anmutliche Möglichkeit*). Only in this case of possibilities for which something speaks can there also be talk of probability: for one of the two sides, the inclination to believe may be greater from the total perceptual situation, for the other lesser.

#### c) Problematic Possibility and Open Possibility

**Paragraph 12.** The distinctive nature of problematic possibility, arising from the doubt-situation, becomes still clearer when we contrast it with another kind of possibility that we designate as open possibility, whose occurrence is grounded in the structure of the unhindered, unbroken perceptual course. What is intentionally predelineated in the apperceptive horizon of a perception is not possible but certain. And yet possibilities, indeed ranges of manifold possibilities, are always contained in such predelineations. The predelineation given in the perception of a thing from the front side for the unseen sides is an indeterminately general one. This generality is a noetic character of the emptily pointing consciousness, and correlatively, for what is predelineated, a character of its objectual sense. For example, the color of the thing's back side is not predelineated as a quite determinate color if the thing is still unknown to us. Yet "a color" is predelineated, and possibly more. This pre-pointing has the mode of naive certainty — but it has this mode according to what it makes conscious and in the manner in which it makes it conscious: certain is "some color or other," the indeterminate generality.

**Paragraph 13.** It belongs to the general essence of every empty intention, including such indeterminate foreshadowings, that it is explicable in the form of presentifications. We can freely form illustrating presentifications of the unseen — for example, by imagining walking around the object. The resulting intuitions present quite determinate colors. But we can obviously freely vary these colors within the framework of indeterminacy. What falls within this range is equally implicitly encompassed and yet not positively motivated, not positively predelineated. It is a member of an open range of closer determinations that are insertable into the framework but beyond that entirely uncertain. This constitutes the concept of open possibility — a totally different kind of modalization from problematic possibility, because the modalizing consciousness is in both cases of fundamentally different origin. In problematic possibility, there are inclinations to believe motivated by the perceptual situation, in conflict with each other — a possibility for which something speaks, which has its weight. In open possibility, there is no talk of weight. There are no alternatives; within the determinate framework of generality, all possible particularizations are equally open.

#### d) The Double Sense of Talk of Modalization

**Paragraph 14.** There is also another sense in which one can speak of modalization. A renewed consideration of the doubt-phenomenon makes this clear. It belongs to the essence of doubt that it admits of the possibility of resolution and eventual active decision. Doubt itself is called, by contrast, indecision, the consciousness an undecided consciousness. In the domain of perception, the decision takes place necessarily in the form (as the most original form of decision) that in the progress to new appearances, a fitting, expected fullness inserts itself into one of the conflicting empty horizons. We step closer, we perhaps also grasp by touch, and the hitherto doubtful intention toward wood (instead of human body) receives the certainty-priority. It receives it through the consistent transition into new appearances that do not accord with the person-apprehension in its unfulfilled horizons and negate it through the fulfilling force of bodily presence.

**Paragraph 15.** In a certain way, then, the confirming "yes" — like the "no" — is, although it gives certainty of belief and being, a modification over against the entirely original, entirely unmodified primal mode of certain validity in which the simple constitution of the perceptual object takes place uni-directionally and entirely without conflict. Thus "modalization" receives a double meaning. On the one hand, it can mean every change of the validity-mode over against the original, so to speak naive certainty — not broken through by becoming-bifurcated, by doubt. On the other hand, it can mean a change of the validity-mode of certainty whereby it ceases to be certainty (the modalizations by possibility, probability, etc.). The primal mode is certainty, but in the form of simplest certainty. When, through the passage through doubt, affirming or negating decision occurs, we have restoration of certainty. And yet the consciousness is now changed. The passage through doubt to decision gives consciousness the character of the deciding, and its noematic sense the corresponding character, which then expresses itself in "yes," "indeed," "really so."

**Paragraph 16.** When we speak of decision in the proper sense, however, we are already led beyond the sphere of receptivity into the domain of spontaneous position-takings of the ego. In receptive perception, by contrast, we deal only with passively running syntheses that maintain themselves consistently, or break apart in conflict, or — through the passage through the wavering of apprehensions — lead back to consistency and resolution of the "doubt." All these phenomena then occasion, at the higher level, the formation of judgment-modalities in the usual sense — modalized predicative judgments. This will be pursued at a later point. The doctrine of judgment-modalities hangs in the air if developed only with a view to the predicative judgment, as is the case in the tradition, without seeking the origin of all these phenomena of modalization in the pre-predicative sphere. And here we grasp the modalizations as inhibitions in the course of the original perceptual interest. In such origin-clarification, it shows itself that the simplest belief-certainty is the primal form, and that all other phenomena — negation, possibility-consciousness, restoration of certainty through affirmation or negation — first arise through modalization of this primal form and do not stand as equally ranked side by side.

**Paragraph 17.** From this kind of inhibited working-out of the perceptual interest — inhibition as modalization — the first-mentioned kind is to be distinguished: inhibition of the tendencies as breaking-off of the perceptual course, whether because the breaking-off has its ground in the mode of givenness of the object (disappearing, being covered, etc.) or in the displacement of the interest in the still perceptually given by another stronger interest. Both kinds of inhibition can cooperate and reciprocally condition each other.

### Chapter II. Simple Apprehension and Explication

### §22. The Levels of Contemplative Perception as the Theme of the Further Analyses

**Paragraph 1.** Let us restrict ourselves in what follows to the modes of unhindered course of perception — perceptions in which neither modalization nor inhibition through breaking-off of the course occurs. Even here there are achievements of different levels, some of which were already in view during the analysis of modalizations, although not yet expressly named. For if something individual is to be affected by modalization, it must already have been singled out, apprehended as individual — and this apprehending is itself already an achievement that admits of different levels. At the lowest level stands the simple apprehension (*schlichte Erfassung*) — the mere apprehending grasp of an object singled out from the field. Beyond this lies the explicating contemplation (*explizierende Betrachtung*) — the penetrating into the inner determinations of the object. And still higher are the relating acts that bring different objects into relation. These three levels — simple apprehension, explication, and relating — correspond to the three basic types of pre-predicative synthesis.

**Paragraph 2.** In the following analyses, Husserl proceeds systematically through these levels, beginning with simple apprehension (§23), then moving to explication (§24 ff.), and finally to relating contemplation (§33 ff.). Each level presupposes the preceding one and adds a new dimension of cognitive achievement. The movement through these levels recapitulates, in the pre-predicative sphere, the very structure that will be shown to underlie the predicative judgment-forms analyzed in Part II.

### §23. Simple Apprehension and Contemplation

#### a) Perception as Immanent-Temporal Unity. The Still-Holding-in-Grasp as Passivity in the Activity of Apprehending

**Paragraph 1.** The first and simplest level of active perceptual engagement is the simple apprehension — the mere grasping of an object that has affected us from the background field. In apprehending, we direct the ray of attention toward the object, and it now stands before us as "grasped," as the theme of our attending gaze. This apprehension is already an egoic activity — the lowest level of activity that we have called receptivity. But it is an activity that lives within the passive flow of time-consciousness. The object, as perceived, is a unity in immanent time: it endures through the streaming phases of perception, continuously identified as the same.

**Paragraph 2.** As the perception proceeds — even in simple apprehension — the object is continuously given in an ever-new "now," while the just-past phases sink into retention and the upcoming ones are protentionally anticipated. What was just given is "still held in grasp" (*noch im Griff*). This still-holding-in-grasp is a form of passivity within the activity of apprehending. The object does not simply vanish when a new phase appears; it is retained in a modified manner, still belonging to the ongoing perception as a just-past phase of the same object. This retentional consciousness is not an active achievement but a passive one: it happens by itself as an essential structural moment of time-consciousness. It is, as it were, the passivity within the activity of perceiving.

**Paragraph 3.** The simple apprehension can also be characterized as a simple, uni-directional (*einstrahlig*) grasping. In it, the ego is directed toward the object as a whole, not yet toward its parts or properties. The object stands there as an undifferentiated unity — not yet analyzed, not yet explicated. It is "this here" — a concrete individual given in bodily presence — but it is not yet determined as being such-and-such. This simple apprehension is the foundation upon which all further cognitive activity builds.

#### b) Different Modes of Holding-in-Grasp and Its Difference from Retention

**Paragraph 4.** The still-holding-in-grasp that characterizes the ongoing apprehension is to be distinguished from the mere retentional consciousness that characterizes the passive sinking of just-past phases into the background. In retention, the just-past is still conscious but no longer actively held; it is, so to speak, sinking away. In the still-holding-in-grasp, by contrast, the just-past is still actively maintained as part of the ongoing apprehension — it is held, not merely retained. This difference is crucial for understanding how the apprehension provides the basis for explication: in explication, the ego moves from one part or property of the object to another, and the already-explicated parts must be held in grasp while new ones are attended to. This active holding-in-grasp is not yet explication, but it is the condition for the possibility of explication.

### §24. Explicating Contemplation and the Explicative Synthesis

#### a) The Explicative Synthesis as the Place of Origin of the Categories "Substrate" and "Determination" and the Task of Its Analysis

**Paragraph 1.** In the transition from simple apprehension to explicating contemplation, a decisive new step is taken. In explication, the ego does not merely grasp the object as a whole but penetrates into it, bringing its inner determinations — its properties, parts, moments — to givenness. The object is no longer an undifferentiated "this here" but begins to unfold as a "this which is such-and-such." This unfolding is the explicative synthesis (*explikative Synthesis*). It is the place of origin of the most fundamental logical-categorial distinction: that between substrate (*Substrat*) and determination (*Bestimmung*). In explication, the object functions as substrate — the identical thing that persists through the explication — and what is brought to givenness in each phase of explication functions as a determination of this substrate.

**Paragraph 2.** The task is to analyze this synthesis precisely — not merely to note that it occurs, but to describe its essential structure, its modes of running-off, and its conditions. This analysis will show that the categories "substrate" and "determination," which formal logic takes as primitive and unexplained, have their origin in the pre-predicative operation of explication. They are not categories imposed upon experience from outside but rather categories arising from the essential structure of the explicative engagement with objects.

#### b) Explicative Coincidence as a Special Mode of Synthesis of Overlapping

**Paragraph 3.** The explicative synthesis is a special mode of the synthesis of overlapping (*Überschiebung*). When the ego moves from the object as a whole to one of its properties — for example, from the perceived thing to its color — a peculiar coincidence takes place. The object as a whole and the property that is now thematized overlap: the property is given not as something separate from the object but as belonging to it, as a moment of it. The ego is simultaneously directed toward the object as substrate and toward the property as determination. This double directedness is the essence of the explicative coincidence: the substrate and the determination are given in a synthesis of partial coincidence — they are not identical, but neither are they merely associated or juxtaposed. The determination is given as inhering in the substrate.

**Paragraph 4.** This synthesis of overlapping is to be distinguished from other kinds of synthesis — for instance, from the synthesis of identification in which one and the same object is recognized in different acts, or from the synthesis of association in which different objects are connected by similarity. The explicative overlapping is a synthesis in which the substrate remains thematic while the determination emerges within it — a synthesis of penetration, in which the ego goes into the substrate and brings out what belongs to it.

#### c) The Holding-in-Grasp in Explication as Distinguished from the Holding-in-Grasp in Simple Apprehension

**Paragraph 5.** The holding-in-grasp that operates in explication is qualitatively different from that in simple apprehension. In simple apprehension, the object is held as a whole, as an undifferentiated unity. In explication, the substrate-object must be held in grasp while the attention moves to one of its determinations, and then this determination must be held while the attention moves to the next. There is thus a layered holding-in-grasp: the substrate is continuously held as the underlying theme, while the determinations that have been traversed are held in a secondary manner, forming a chain of already-explicated moments. This layered structure is the pre-predicative precursor of the subject-predicate structure of the predicative judgment.

#### d) Explication and Plurality-Apprehension

**Paragraph 6.** Explication is to be distinguished from the apprehension of a plurality. In the apprehension of a plurality, the ego grasps several objects in succession, each one standing on its own as an independent substrate. In explication, by contrast, the ego remains directed toward one substrate and brings its inner determinations to givenness. The two operations can of course combine: one can explicate several objects in succession, or one can, within the explication of a single object, encounter parts that are themselves independent objects (as in the explication of a machine into its components). But the fundamental distinction remains: explication is an operation of penetration into one thing; plurality-apprehension is an operation of grasping several things.

### §25. The Habitual Precipitate of Explication. The Impressing-Upon-Oneself

**Paragraph 1.** The result of explication is not merely a transitory act that vanishes without trace. The explicative engagement with an object leaves a habitual precipitate (*habitueller Niederschlag*): the object is now known in a way it was not known before. What has been brought to givenness through explication is retained as an acquired determination — not merely in the passive manner of retention, but as a habitual possession (*habitueller Besitz*) of knowledge. The next time the ego encounters this object or a similar one, the already-explicated determinations will function as part of the horizon of acquaintance with which the object is pregiven.

**Paragraph 2.** This habitual precipitate is what Husserl calls the "impressing-upon-oneself" (*Sich-einprägen*). Through repeated explication, the determinations of the object become firmly established as its known properties. The object acquires the character of being "such-and-such" — not yet in the form of a predicative judgment, but in the form of a habitual acquaintance. This is the pre-predicative precursor of cognitive possession: the knowledge that, at the predicative level, finds its expression and fixation in the assertoric sentence.

### §26. Explication as Making-Distinct of What Is Anticipatorily Given in the Horizon, and Its Difference from Analytic Clarification

**Paragraph 1.** Explication operates within the horizon-structure of experience described in §8. Every object is given with a horizon of anticipated but indeterminate determinations. Explication is the process of making these anticipated determinations distinct — of transforming the indeterminately anticipated into the determinately given. In this sense, explication is a fulfillment of the horizonal anticipation: what was "known in advance" in an indeterminate way becomes "known" in a determinate way through the explicative engagement.

**Paragraph 2.** This making-distinct through explication is to be sharply distinguished from what might be called "analytic clarification." Analytic clarification is an operation performed upon already-constituted logical formations — upon judgments, concepts, and the like. It consists in making explicit what is implicitly contained in these formations. Explication, by contrast, operates in the pre-predicative domain: it does not clarify logical formations but rather brings the objective determinations themselves to givenness for the first time. The two operations are analogous in structure — both involve a transition from the indeterminate to the determinate — but they operate at different levels: explication at the pre-predicative level of experience, analytic clarification at the predicative level of logic.

### §27. Original and Non-Original Modes of Explication. Explication in Anticipation and in Memory

**Paragraph 1.** The paradigmatic case of explication is explication in the mode of perceptual presence: the ego is perceptually directed toward the object and penetrates into its properties as they present themselves in bodily presence. This is original explication — explication in which the determinations are given in their most originary form. But there are also non-original modes of explication. One can explicate an object in anticipation — running through its anticipated properties before they are actually given in perception. And one can explicate an object in memory — recalling its previously perceived properties. In both cases, the explicative structure (the articulation into substrate and determinations) is the same, but the mode of givenness of the determinations is different: anticipatory in the first case, reproductive in the second.

**Paragraph 2.** These non-original modes of explication are founded upon original explication. Anticipatory explication operates within the horizon of what has been previously experienced; it is possible only because the type of the object — its typical determinations — is already known from prior original explications. Similarly, memorial explication draws upon the habitual precipitate of prior perceptual explications. The hierarchy of originality corresponds to the hierarchy of evidence: original perceptual explication provides the highest evidence; memorial and anticipatory explication provide derived, secondary evidence.

### §28. Multi-Layered Explication and the Relativization of the Distinction between Substrate and Determination

**Paragraph 1.** Explication need not stop at a single level. The determinations brought to givenness through explication can themselves become substrates of further explication. The color of a thing, for instance, first brought to givenness as a determination of the thing, can itself be explicated: its shade, its brightness, its saturation can be brought to givenness as determinations of the color. This is multi-layered or iterated explication. It shows that the distinction between substrate and determination is relative: what functions as a determination at one level can function as a substrate at the next.

**Paragraph 2.** This relativization is of the greatest importance for understanding the structure of the predicative judgment. The predicative forms "S is p" and "p is q" correspond to different levels of explication: in the first, S is the substrate and p the determination; in the second, the former determination p has become a substrate and q its new determination. The iteration of explication thus prefigures the possibility of iterating predication — the possibility of making what was predicated into a new subject of further predication.

### §29. Absolute Substrates and Absolute Determinations, and the Threefold Sense of This Distinction

**Paragraph 1.** Despite the relativization of substrate and determination, there is a sense in which some substrates and some determinations are "absolute." An absolute substrate is one that cannot itself be a determination of anything else — an independently existing individual thing, for instance. An absolute determination is one that cannot function as a substrate — a simple qualitative moment, for instance. These absolute cases represent the endpoints of the possible iteration of explication: the absolute substrate is where explication ultimately begins, and the absolute determination is where it ultimately terminates.

**Paragraph 2.** Husserl identifies a threefold sense in which the distinction between substrate and determination can be made: (1) in the relative sense, where what is substrate or determination depends on the level of explication; (2) in the absolute sense just described; and (3) in the sense of the functional roles that terms play within the predicative judgment — the logical subject and the logical predicate. These three senses are related but not identical, and their clarification is one of the tasks of the genetic investigation.

### §30. Self-Standing and Non-Self-Standing Determinations. The Concept of the Whole

**Paragraph 1.** Within the domain of determinations brought to givenness through explication, a fundamental distinction emerges: between self-standing (*selbständige*) and non-self-standing (*unselbständige*) determinations. A self-standing determination is one that can be apprehended on its own, as an independent entity — a part of a thing, for instance, which could in principle be separated from the thing and encountered on its own. A non-self-standing determination is one that cannot be apprehended on its own, that can exist only as a moment of something else — a color, for instance, which cannot exist apart from a colored surface. This distinction between the self-standing (pieces or parts) and the non-self-standing (moments or abstract properties) is one of the most fundamental distinctions in Husserl's ontology, first elaborated in the Third Logical Investigation and here given its genetic grounding.

**Paragraph 2.** The concept of the whole (*Ganzes*) is correlative with this distinction. A whole is what has parts — and these parts can be self-standing (pieces) or non-self-standing (moments). The explication of a whole into its self-standing parts yields a different structure than the explication into its non-self-standing moments. The former yields parts that are themselves potential substrates of independent existence; the latter yields moments that are essentially dependent — that can exist only as belonging to their substrate.

### §31. The Apprehension of Pieces and of Non-Self-Standing Moments

**Paragraph 1.** The apprehension of pieces — self-standing parts of a whole — is a special case of plurality-apprehension combined with the consciousness of belonging-together. When we explicate a whole into its pieces, each piece stands before us as something that could in principle be encountered on its own, but that in fact belongs to this whole. The pieces are given in a synthesis of conjunction: they are together, they form the whole, but each one has its own independence.

**Paragraph 2.** The apprehension of non-self-standing moments — abstract properties, qualities, relations — is, by contrast, essentially tied to the substrate. A color cannot be apprehended apart from a colored surface; it is essentially a moment of its substrate. In apprehending a non-self-standing moment, we remain directed toward the substrate and apprehend the moment as inhering in it. This inherence is given in the explicative synthesis itself: the moment is given as a "how" of the substrate, not as a "what" alongside it.

### §32. Non-Self-Standing Moments as Connections and as Properties

#### a) Mediate and Immediate Properties

**Paragraph 1.** Among non-self-standing moments, a further distinction must be drawn between those that function as connections (*Verbindungen*) and those that function as properties (*Eigenschaften*). A connection is a moment that relates the parts of a whole to one another — for example, the spatial arrangement of parts. A property is a moment that qualifies the substrate itself — for example, its color, shape, or texture. Properties can be immediate or mediate: an immediate property is one that belongs directly to the substrate; a mediate property is one that belongs to a part or moment of the substrate and thereby indirectly to the substrate itself.

#### b) The Pregnant Concept of Property and Its Difference from Connection

**Paragraph 2.** In the pregnant sense, a property is a non-self-standing moment that qualifies the substrate as a whole and can be brought to givenness in the explicative synthesis as a determination of the substrate. A connection, by contrast, is a non-self-standing moment that holds between parts of the substrate and can be brought to givenness only in a different kind of synthesis — one that moves between the parts rather than from the substrate to a qualifying moment. This distinction will prove crucial for understanding the different forms of predicative judgment: property-predication ("S is p") corresponds to the explication of properties, while relational predication ("S stands in relation R to T") corresponds to the apprehension of connections.

### Chapter III. The Apprehension of Relations and Its Foundations in Passivity

### §33. Horizon-Consciousness and Relating Contemplation

**Paragraph 1.** We now turn from the explication of a single object to the relating contemplation (*beziehendes Betrachten*) that brings different objects into relation. This is a new level of pre-predicative achievement, founded upon the previous levels. In the transition from explication to relating contemplation, the ego's gaze expands from one object to another, bringing both into the unity of a single contemplation. The foundation for this lies in the horizon-structure of experience: every object is given with an outer horizon that includes other objects. These co-given objects exercise their affective pull, drawing the ego's attention from one to the other. The relating contemplation begins when the ego, instead of simply turning from one object to another, holds both in grasp simultaneously and brings them into an explicit relation.

### §34. General Characterization of Relating Contemplation

#### a) and b)

**Paragraph 1.** In relating contemplation, two (or more) objects are held together in the ego's grasp and compared or connected. The ego is directed toward both, but not in the manner of simple plurality-apprehension (where each object is grasped independently); rather, the objects are grasped together, in their togetherness, and the focus is on what holds between them. The fundamental distinction within relating contemplation is between relations of connection (*Verbindungsbeziehungen*) and relations of comparison (*Vergleichungsbeziehungen*). Relations of connection are those that hold between objects by virtue of a real or experienced bond — spatial proximity, temporal succession, causal connection, and the like. Relations of comparison are those that hold by virtue of the similarity or dissimilarity of the objects — their agreement or disagreement in properties.

**Paragraph 2.** In both cases, the relating contemplation involves a distinctive synthesis — different from the explicative synthesis. In explication, the ego moves from a substrate to its determinations, remaining within the unity of one object. In relating contemplation, the ego moves between objects, bringing them into the unity of a relational whole. The relating synthesis is thus a between-synthesis rather than a within-synthesis.

### §35. The Question of the Essence of the Relation-Founding Unity

**Paragraph 1.** What founds the possibility of bringing different objects into relation? What is the essential unity that makes relating contemplation possible? Husserl argues that this unity lies ultimately in the unity of consciousness itself — specifically, in the temporal unity of conscious life. All objects that can be brought into relation must be given within the unity of one consciousness, within one temporal stream of experience. This temporal unity is the most universal foundation for all relating; it makes possible the bringing-together of any two objects whatsoever. But beyond this universal foundation, there are more specific ones: the unity of a single perceptual field, the unity of a memory, the unity of a phantasy — each of these provides a more specific framework within which objects can be related.

### §36. The Passive (Temporal) Unity of Perception

**Paragraph 1.** The most basic relation-founding unity is the passive temporal unity of perception. In every perceptual present, a plurality of objects is co-given — the objects of the perceptual field. They are given together, in one temporal now, and this togetherness is not an active achievement of the ego but a passive pregivenness. This passive co-givenness provides the foundation for the simplest kind of relating: the relating of objects that are perceptually co-present. The ego can turn from one to the other and bring them into an explicit relation precisely because they are already passively co-given in one perceptual present.

### §37. The Unity of Memory and Its Separation from Perception

**Paragraph 1.** The question now turns to further kinds of intuitive unity beyond the immediate unity of perception. Remaining for now within positional consciousness, the next question concerns the connection of perception with memory (*Erinnerung*) as positing presentification (*setzende Vergegenwärtigung*), and the kind of their intuitive unity — a unity that can also obtain when the objects related and unified are not simultaneously given in a perception but partly in perceptions and partly in presentifications.

**Paragraph 2.** An example: I see a table perceptually before me and simultaneously remember intuitively another table that formerly stood at this place. Even if I can, so to speak, "transpose" the remembered table alongside the perceived one, it does not stand in the unity of an actual duration beside it; it is in a certain way separated from it. The world of perception and the world of memory are separated worlds. On the other hand, there is, as will be shown in more than one sense, a unity — insofar as I have both tables intuitively before my eyes in one presence. In what sense is there separation here, and in what sense unity?

**Paragraph 3.** Certainly the talk of the separatedness of the perceived and the remembered has its good right. If I live in the memory, I have a unity of the memorial intuition; the remembered is therein, prior to all comparing, distinguishing, and relating, a "sensuous" and in flowing parts "intuitive" unity, self-enclosed — as long as I live in a memory-intuition that remains unbroken, as long as I do not "jump" from memory to memory in a disorder of "occurrences." Every unitary memory is in itself continuously unitary and constitutes within itself a conscious unity of objectualities — a unity that is intuitive-sensuous unity: intuitive in flowing parts. That is, running through a longer-lasting process in memory has exactly the same structure as its apprehension in original perception. As in the latter only one phase is ever originally intuitively conscious, immediately replaced by the next and, held in grasp, synthetically united with it, so in the memory of the process the entire process is unitarily intuitive, namely the one meant in all its phases, although at any time only a flowing temporal stretch of it is "properly intuitive."

**Paragraph 4.** The principle of the closedness of memory is of course exactly the same as what was established for perception: it rests on a unity of temporal duration. It is a unity not only in respect of the singling out and thematic contemplation of a perceived individual thing or process, but already in respect of the unity-founding phenomenon of "impression" in which a unity of objectuality (however many-membered it may be) is sensuously pregiven to us, passively there for us. It is an originally constituted, flowing formation. This formation — whether of perception (of the originally sensuous givenness) or of memory — is in each case self-enclosed, and only the horizon-intentions give it connection with the objectivity reaching beyond it, the objective world of which it is a component.

**Paragraph 5.** In such memory, on the basis of these horizon-intentions, what we call continuous running-through in memory can occur — for instance, running through a nearer past up to the originary present. The initially isolated emerging memory can be "freely" led further; we advance into the memorial horizon toward the present, progressing continuously from memory to memory; all the memories emerging along the way are now flowing stretches of a connected, unitary memory, running into one another. The process thereby normally undergoes a coarsening and shortening (compression) through the skipping of inessential memorial parts.

**Paragraph 6.** We must therefore distinguish: (1) the unity of the respective (constantly flowing) memorial field, which is an intuitive unity in the narrower sense — the running through of a longer-lasting process in memory is one memory insofar as in each phase of this memorial experience the intuited content of the preceding phase (the earlier past) is "still" intuitive, still held in grasp, while what newly emerges in it just comes to "primary" intuitiveness; (2) the total intuitive memorial field in the wider sense, which includes the continuum of properly intuitive memorial fields that has been "run through" in a unity of consciousness — those that are no longer properly intuitive but still have retentional liveliness and are not "sunken" — and also everything contained in the not newly re-remembered horizons of the past, contained as a mere potentiality for bringing the intentions to fulfillment in the form of re-memories.

**Paragraph 7.** These total unities of re-memory are (unless they are led back in specially structured processes to originary perception, or connected with one another in continuous connection to a connected unity of one re-memory) separated from one another. What appears in them as sensuous unities, objects and connections, is separated from one another and also from what appears in the respective perceptual world. We cannot therefore speak of the givennesses here and there entering into an improper or proper "intuitive," "sensuous" connection. A perceptual object — the fountain pen I now perceive lying on the table here — stands in no intuitive connection to the book that lay at the same spot on this table a year ago and which I now remember. It does not lie "beside" the fountain pen; it has no spatial unity-relation to it at all — for which unity of what is intuited in one temporal duration would be required. Such relations and the relating contemplation directed toward them — relations of spatial position to one another — are thus not possible for objects of such separated intuitions.

### §38. The Necessary Connection of the Intentional Objects of All Perceptions and Positional Presentifications of an Ego and an Ego-Community on the Basis of Time as the Form of Sensibility

**Paragraph 1.** Nonetheless, despite all separation, a unity and unity-relations grounded upon it do obtain here. What kind they are will become clear when we recall the already mentioned horizon-intentions. Every perception, as consciousness meaning actual objectuality, has its horizon of the before and after. It points back to what was previously perceived, which can be presentified in memories, even where these do not directly connect with the respective perception but are separated from it by obscure, unremembered stretches. Apart from the connection (to be discussed later) that every perceived item "reminds" of something past, similar or equal, even when temporally separated from it — a relation of equality and similarity — there also obtains here a deeper-lying kind of unity: when I am led back through memories, starting from a perception, into my own past, this past is precisely mine, the past of the same present and living subject. And the past and now remembered surrounding world belongs to the same world as this world in which I now live, only presentified in a piece of its past.

**Paragraph 2.** Likewise, to bring in intersubjectivity, when another person tells me about his past experiences, communicates his memories, what is remembered in them belongs to the same objective world as what is given in my and our common living present. Despite all differences between remembered environments, all these are pieces from the one and same objective world. This world is, in the most comprehensive sense as life-world for a humanity standing in a community of possible mutual understanding, our earth, which encloses all these various environments with their changes and pasts. In this one world, everything that I now originally sensuously perceive, that I have ever perceived and can now recall, or about which others can report to me as their perceived or remembered, has its place. It has its unity through having its fixed time-position, its position in objective time.

**Paragraph 3.** This holds for every object of perception as such — that is, as meant, as putatively actually existing. In the sphere of living presence there is conflict (*Widerstreit*), there is the springing over of one perception into a second interpenetrating it in conflict. In sensibility itself (thus prior to all activity) conflict arises. But what must be noted is that the intentional time — the time belonging to the meant objectual as such — is insofar not affected, since the intentionally conflicting and interpenetrating objects do not conflict with respect to the time-moment itself. The originally passively arising sensuous conflict necessarily brings two objects of the same time-determination and presupposes this sameness of time-determination.

**Paragraph 4.** Thus the sensuously constituted time-series is under all circumstances a single one, into which — apart from otherwise constituted or to-be-constituted unity-characters and independence-characters — everything intentional as such is ordered that is sensuously constituted (originally appears). Hence everything originally appearing, even if it appears in conflict, has its determinate time-position — not only an immanent phenomenal time in the intentional objectuality as such, but also its fixed position in the one objective time.

**Paragraph 5.** We now understand the inner truth of the Kantian thesis: time is the form of sensibility, and therefore it is the form of every possible world of objective experience. Prior to all questions about objective actuality stands the fact of the essential peculiarity of all "appearances," whether true or shown to be null: that they are time-giving, and indeed so that all given times fit into one time. Thus all perceived and ever perceivable individuals have the common form of time. It is the first and fundamental form, the form of all forms, the presupposition of all other unity-founding connections. "Form" means here from the outset the character that necessarily precedes everything else in the possibility of an intuitive unity. Temporality as duration, coexistence, and succession is the necessary form of all unitarily intuitable objects and, insofar, their form of intuition.

**Paragraph 6.** At the same time, the word "form of intuition" has a second sense: everything individual intuited in the unity of an intuition is given in a temporal orientation that is the form of givenness of everything present in a presence. Further, all concrete individuals conscious in initially unconnected intuitions belong to the unity of a (though not intuitive, yet intuitively achievable) time. The one time is the one single form of all individual objectualities that an ego has given or may have given in initially unconnected intuitions — for example, in perceptions and memories separated from them.

**Paragraph 7.** This continues in empathy (*Einfühlung*). In it, an intersubjectively common objective time is constituted, into which everything individual — in experiences and in intentional objectualities — must be able to be ordered. This can be traced back to the fact that for every ego, empathy is nothing other than a special group of positional presentifications alongside memories and expectations, and that it can unify these intuitions like all positional intuitions in the indicated manner.

**Paragraph 8.** When we asked about the connection that makes possible the unity between all perceptions and positional presentifications of an ego, the time-connection has been brought out as this connection. It is established in the sphere of passivity — that is, in sensibility. Arbitrary perceptions within one ego-consciousness necessarily have connection, whether the ego actively synthesizes them, relatingly refers them to others, or does not live in them at all and is occupied with whatever other objects — in themselves they have connection, they constitute an all-encompassing connection of their intentional objects. Every perception has its retentional horizon and provides the possibility of penetrating into this horizon and unfolding it in memories.

**Paragraph 9.** Thus all non-intuitive connection within the unity of a perception points back to chainings of connections in the unity of actual intuition — that is, to possible continuous re-memories that intuitively reproduce the chaining. Conversely, what is actually intuitive points forward to new actual intuitions, and this pointing-forward is anticipation. It belongs to the essence of the perceptions of an ego that they occur only in continuous chaining. The unity of an ego reaches so far and can only reach so far as we have unity of an inner consciousness; and so far must all intentional objects of perceptions constituted therein constitute a time-connection that coincides with that of the immanent time of the acts.

**Paragraph 10.** Every perception and every re-memory as reproduction of a perception must therefore yield for their objects a time-relation that is in principle intuitively achievable. As related to actual or putatively actual objects within a world, they stand in connection. This connection provides the ground for a kind of relations: the relations of temporal position of all perceived, in perceptions as actually being meant, objectualities. Formulated as a law: all perceptions and experiences of an ego stand, with respect to their intentional objects, in connection — they refer (even where they enter into conflict) to one time. And likewise: all perceptions and experiences of all mutually understanding ego-subjects stand, with respect to their intentional objects, in connection — in the connection of an objective time constituting itself in all their subjective times, and of an objective world constituting itself in it.

### §39. Transition to Quasi-Positionality. The Disconnectedness of Phantasy Intuitions

**Paragraph 1.** Having considered only the possibilities of intuitive unity within positional consciousness — the unity of perceptions among themselves and of perceptions with positing presentifications — we now transition to quasi-positionality: perceptive or reproductive phantasy. We ask what possibilities of intuitive unity obtain within phantasy itself (among its intentional objects), as well as between phantasy and the intentional objects of positional experiences.

**Paragraph 2.** Among the experiences of perceptual meaning of actual world-objects, phantasy experiences can appear without connection to them — directed toward ficta, toward objectualities meant as fictive. They are without connection with the perceptions: while all perceptions, with respect to the objectualities meant in them, close together into a unity, related to the unity of one world, the phantasy-objectualities fall outside this unity; they do not join with the perceptual objectualities in this way into the unity of one meant world.

**Paragraph 3.** Admittedly, the phantasies of an ego do have connection among themselves and with its perceptions as experiences, like all experiences of inner consciousness. They are ordered as experiences, like all acts, into its unity — which means that inner consciousness constitutes an intentional connection. But they do not have connection in their objectual reference, neither among themselves nor with the perceptions. The centaur I now fictively imagine, and a hippopotamus I previously imagined, and again the table I now perceive, have no connection among themselves — that is, they have no temporal position relative to one another. The centaur is neither earlier nor later than the hippopotamus or than the table I now perceive.

**Paragraph 4.** In a certain sense, admittedly, every phantasy-objectuality has its time; it is conscious as a unity of temporal duration. So here too time functions as unity-forming, exactly as was shown for a self-enclosed perception or memory. The phantasy-object is conscious as temporal and time-determined, enduring in time — but its time is a quasi-time. Take the phantasy of a red-colored triangle as it hovers before me. I can describe it and come thereby also to its duration. It is a temporal object, it has its time. And yet it is in no time. That is: the time-duration of the triangle is, with all its time-points, modified in the same sense as the quasi-coloring it has over against the coloring of an actual red triangle. The phantasy-color is the intentional correlate of phantasy and as such has the mode of the as-if.

**Paragraph 5.** Nonetheless, it makes good sense to say that the same thing that is merely imagined could also be actual, or indeed might not be actual — that a non-actual thing, given or hovering in a presentation and regularly exhibiting itself, might conform point for point, determination for determination, to an actual thing. Conversely, for every item legitimately given in normal perception, we could construct a pure phantasy that imagined exactly the same object in exactly the same manner of presentation. But one thing is necessarily absent in mere fiction — what distinguishes actually existing objects: the absolute temporal position, the "actual" time, as the absolute, serious uniqueness of the individual content given in time-form. Time is indeed imagined, even intuitively imagined, but it is a time without actual and proper positional location — precisely a quasi-time.

**Paragraph 6.** Of course, phenomenal places and relative position-relations, distances, are intuitively given also in phantasy. But it offers us no positions that can be identified in the sense of an "in-itself" and correspondingly distinguished. We can imagine a red-colored triangle in arbitrarily many disconnected phantasies in complete equality, in completely equal duration: each is then distinguished from every other as the content of a different phantasy-consciousness, but by no means already distinguished as an individual object. If the phantasies are truly disconnected, every possibility of speaking here of several objects or even of the same one object that is merely repeatedly represented is absent.

**Paragraph 7.** Thus the sense of the disconnectedness of phantasy-intuitions has become clear. Phantasy-objectualities lack the absolute temporal position, and so they cannot, like perceptual objects, have unity of a time among themselves, a single time-order — insofar as we speak, as hitherto, of phantasies that do not among themselves form a consciously co-meant connection, a unity of phantasy. Such possible unity-formation is, however, extrinsic to phantasies. It does not belong to their essence that they must occur in continuous chaining that constitutes the continuity of a phantasy. Separated phantasies have a priori no necessary connection and also in the rule in our factual experience none. It therefore makes no sense in such cases to ask whether the object of one is before or after that of another. Every disconnected phantasy has its imagination-time, and there are as many mutually incomparable ones as there are or can be such phantasies — infinitely many. No absolute position of one can be identical with that of another.

### §40. Temporal Unity and Connection in Phantasy through the Joining of Phantasies into the Unity of a Phantasy-World. Individuation Possible Only within the World of Actual Experience

**Paragraph 1.** Despite the essential disconnectedness of all phantasy-intuitions, unity is in a certain way possible even here — namely, insofar as in all phantasies (spoken in terms of the neutrality-modification) a single quasi-world constitutes itself, partly intuited, partly meant in empty horizons. Admittedly, it lies in our freedom to let the indeterminacy of these horizons be quasi-fulfilled by phantasies arbitrarily. But this changes nothing about the fact that, so far as this is the case, all these phantasies have connection in the unity of an encompassing object-consciousness, an actual and possible one. "Unity of a phantasy" is evidently nothing other than unity of a possible experience, or neutrality-modification of an experiential unity. And this provides the ground for the essence of unity of experience.

**Paragraph 2.** So there is a unity-formation of all free phantasies belonging to a fairy tale, which we think of as free from all relation to the actual world in order to have a pure phantasy. Whether we phantasy the fairy tale through in one go or in separated stretches: every new stretch connects through an obscure but unfoldable horizon to the earlier one. Within such a phantasy-world, we have for every individual phantasy-object (as quasi-actuality) an "individual" singularization for every time-point and time-duration. We have such, first, in the narrowest unity of a phantasy, namely within a presence: equals therein are individually distinguished.

**Paragraph 3.** But what if we now pass from one phantasy-world into another, unrelated to it? It does not lie in the essence of any two arbitrary phantasies that they demand unification into one phantasy. Within a phantasy, as we move intentionally in a phantasy-world, there is consistency and contradiction, incompatibility, and all the relations of spatial and temporal position are possible that we demonstrated for objects within an actual world — all now transposed into the quasi. Between the formations of disconnected phantasies, there is no such thing. For the "things," the events, the "actualities" of one world have "nothing to do" with those of another. More precisely: the fulfillments and disappointments of the intentions constitutive for one phantasy-world can never reach into those constitutive for another phantasy-world.

**Paragraph 4.** It makes no sense, for example, to ask whether the Gretel in one fairy tale and a Gretel in another fairy tale is the same Gretel, whether what is phantasied and asserted for the one accords or fails to accord with what is phantasied for the other, whether they are related, etc. I can stipulate it — and to assume it is already a stipulation — but then both fairy tales refer to the same world. Within a fairy tale, I can ask such questions, since there we have from the outset a phantasy-world; but even there the question ceases where the phantasy ceases, where it has not more closely determined. It remains reserved for the elaboration of the phantasy in the sense of continuation of the unity of a phantasy to make determinations arbitrarily.

**Paragraph 5.** In the actual world, nothing remains open: it is as it is. The phantasy-world "is" and is so-and-so insofar as it has been phantasied by the grace of phantasy; no phantasy is at an end and would not leave a free elaboration in the sense of a new determination open. But on the other hand, in the essence of the connection that constitutes the "unity" of a phantasy, there lies a wealth of essential restrictions that must not be overlooked. They find their expression in the fact that in the — even if openly free — continuation of the unity of a phantasy, a unity of a "possible world" constitutes itself with an encompassing form of the associated phantasy-time.

**Paragraph 6.** In what has been set forth lies the implication that individuation and identity of the individual, as well as the identification grounded therein, is possible only within the world of actual experience on the basis of the absolute temporal position. Phantasy-experience accordingly yields no individual objects in the proper sense at all, but only quasi-individual objects and quasi-identity, namely within the maintained unity of a phantasy-world. Herewith the right of our initial exclusion of the sphere of neutrality for the purpose of grounding a theory of judgment is demonstrated, insofar as a theory of judgment must begin with the experience of individuals as the ultimate evidence-giving experience, and such experience of individuals does not take place in phantasy and in neutral consciousness generally.

### §41. The Problem of the Possibility of Intuitive Unity between Perceptual and Phantasy Objects of an Ego

**Paragraph 1.** If nonetheless phantasy-experience has been drawn into the range of our consideration, this has its ground in the fact that it is more than a merely indifferent parallel to actual experience and the determination accomplished in it. It does not suffice to simply transpose everything established in the domain of positionality into the quasi. Rather, despite the disconnectedness of perceptual objects and phantasy-objects, an intuitive unity of a kind is still possible here that can contribute to the (relative) determination of individual objects given in experience.

**Paragraph 2.** Pursuing this question of the unity still possible here will bring us to a widest concept of unity of intuition — wider than those brought out so far — and to the most comprehensive kind of relations, namely the equality- and similarity-relations, which are possible between all objectualities unifiable in such unity of intuition, whether perceptual objects or phantasy-objects. The foundational function of these relations and thus also of free phantasy in the higher dimension of generality-consciousness and especially of essence-intuition is pointed to in advance. It will be discussed in detail in Part III. Here we remain in the sphere of experience of individuals and now ask: what kind is the unity of intuition that makes these relations possible, and upon what does it ground itself?

### §42. The Possibility of Establishing an Intuitive Connection between All Objectualities Constituted in One Stream of Consciousness through Association

#### a) The Temporal Unity of All Experiences of an Ego

**Paragraph 1.** The unity in question here cannot be a unity of objectualities in the absolute world-time as a unity of simultaneity or succession. For it was shown that phantasy-objects have no time-connection with perceptual objects or among themselves, and consequently no intuitive unity grounded therein. If the unity is therefore not one of the objectualities, it can only be a unity of the experiences constituting the objectualities — of the perceptual, memorial, and phantasy experiences.

**Paragraph 2.** All experiences of an ego have their temporal unity; they are constituted in the absolute flow of inner time-consciousness and have in it their absolute position and uniqueness, their unique occurrence in the absolute now, whereupon they retentionally fade and sink into the past. Of course, this time of the experiences is not the time of the intentional objectualities in the experiences. When, for example, while I perceive my thingly environment, a memory-occurrence comes over me and I even turn to it, the perceptual world does not disappear; however much it may lose its "actuality," recede "from me," it is perceptively still there, perceived in a wider sense. The memory in which I now live offers me a time for the remembered that is implicitly oriented toward the perceptual present. But the remembered is past and even "far removed" from the perceived (a character that is not an immediately intuitive time-character but points to an unfolding in chains of intuitions), while the memory as experience is simultaneous with the perceptual experience.

**Paragraph 3.** Thus between all experiences of an ego there obtains a temporal unity, which, however, is not yet a unity of intuition. For what is meant, intuited in the experiences — the perceived, remembered, phantasied objectualities — are separated from one another. And even though between all perceived and remembered individual objectualities of positional experiences there obtains the possibly intuitively achievable unity that they have on the basis of their absolute temporal position in the objective world, this possibility of connection drops away for phantasy-objectualities. Nonetheless, on the basis of their being-constituted-together in the flow of one inner time-consciousness, the possibility of establishing an intuitive connection between all objectualities constituted therein obtains.

#### b) The Double Function of Association for the Connection of Positional Consciousness

**Paragraph 4.** That such an intuitive connection, a unity of intuition between temporally separated intentional objects of one ego, is factually established — for this, the mere fact of their being-constituted-together in one ego-consciousness does not yet suffice. Time-consciousness is, after all, only a consciousness establishing a universal form (cf. §§16, 38). The factual awakening and thereby the factual intuitive unification of perceptions and memories, or of intentional objects of perception and memory, is an achievement of association — this manner of passive synthesis built up upon the lowest syntheses of time-consciousness.

**Paragraph 5.** We already had to go back to the lawfulnesses of association and affection in order to make intelligible the structure of a sense-field, a field of affectively operative pregivennesses together in one presence, and further the possibility of individual givennesses standing out from this field and prompting the ego to turn toward and objectually apprehend them (homogeneous association), as well as the possibility of the unification of data from different sense-fields given in one presence (heterogeneous association). But beyond this function of unification within a presence, association also has the further function: to unify what lies apart, insofar as it was ever constituted in one stream of consciousness, to unify what is present with what is not present, what is currently perceived with distant, separated memories, indeed also with phantasy-objects. The equal here reminds of the equal there, the similar of the similar. There thus takes place a peculiar referring-to-one-another — though in this sphere of passivity and the receptivity building upon it, not yet a relation in the logical sense, not a spontaneous, creative consciousness in which a relation as such is constituted.

**Paragraph 6.** So it is — confining ourselves for now to positional consciousness — the achievement of association to first make alive the connection that all perceptions of an ego, present and past, have on the basis of their being-constituted in one time-consciousness, to first establish a real conscious unity between them. Only on the basis of associative awakening can separated memories be referred to one another and, in stepping back link by link, be fitted into an intuitive connection of memory. That is, once memories are associatively awakened, they can then be ordered into the temporal connection; the before and after, "how it really was," and their time-position in the past can be determined. Thus associative awakening creates the presupposition for the constitution of the time-relations, of "earlier" and "later."

**Paragraph 7.** Through associative connection, even the lifeless memory-worlds gain a kind of being despite their separation from the perceptual present. And beyond the positional domain: the associative connection also reaches out to phantasy-objectualities. The similar here awakens the similar there, regardless of whether "there" is a perceptual, memorial, or phantasy-objectuality. This is the widest function of association: it produces the possibility of intuitive unity — and thereby of relating contemplation — between all objectualities constituted in one stream of consciousness, across the divide separating the positional from the neutral, the perceived from the phantasied. It is precisely this that will ground the possibility of comparison-relations between perceived and phantasied objects, and therewith — as will be shown in Part III — the possibility of free variation as the basis of essence-intuition.

### §43. Connection-Relations and Comparison-Relations

**Paragraph 1.** Having established the foundations of relating contemplation in the various unities of consciousness, Husserl now analyzes the two fundamental kinds of relation more closely. Connection-relations (*Verbindungsbeziehungen*) are those grounded in the real coexistence or co-occurrence of objects — spatial, temporal, or causal togetherness. Comparison-relations (*Vergleichungsbeziehungen*) are those grounded in the similarity or dissimilarity of objects — their agreement or disagreement in properties. The distinction cuts across the distinction between self-standing and non-self-standing: connection-relations can hold between self-standing objects (two things side by side) or between non-self-standing moments (two colors in the same field), and comparison-relations can likewise hold between objects of either kind.

**Paragraph 2.** Comparison-relations have a distinctive character: they presuppose that the compared objects have been at least partially explicated, so that the properties in respect of which they are to be compared are available for comparison. Comparing is thus founded upon explicating: one must have brought to givenness the relevant properties of each object before one can compare them. This founding relation between explication and comparison mirrors the founding relation between the predicative forms "S is p" and "S is similar to T in respect of p" — the latter presupposing the former.

### §44. Analysis of Comparative Contemplation

**Paragraph 1.** In comparative contemplation, the ego grasps two objects and brings them into a synthesis of overlapping (*Überschiebung*) in which their similarities and differences come to givenness. The overlapping is not a real fusion — the objects remain distinct — but an intentional operation in which the features of one are, as it were, laid over those of the other, and the degree of fit is assessed. Where the features coincide, similarity is given; where they do not, difference is given. The overlapping can be total (comparing the objects as wholes) or partial (comparing them in respect of specific features).

**Paragraph 2.** This analysis of comparative contemplation reveals its kinship with, and difference from, the associative synthesis described in §16. Association, as a passive synthesis, already constitutes a proto-comparison: the similar is passively wakened by the similar. But comparative contemplation is an active operation of the ego: the ego deliberately brings two objects together and compares them. It is, so to speak, the activation and thematization of what association already produces passively. This relationship between passive association and active comparison will prove important for understanding the genesis of the concept of similarity and, ultimately, of universals (Part III).

### §45. Total and Partial Similarity (Similarity in a Respect)

**Paragraph 1.** Similarity can be total — when two objects agree in all their experienceable properties — or partial — when they agree only in some. Partial similarity is similarity "in a respect" (*in einer Hinsicht*): the objects are similar with respect to color, for example, but different with respect to shape. The consciousness of similarity-in-a-respect presupposes the explication of both objects and the selective focus on the respect in which they agree. It is thus a multi-leveled achievement: explication of each object; comparison of the explicated features; and the selective foregrounding of the feature in respect of which similarity obtains.

### §46. Relational Determinations and Contrast Determinations ("Absolute Impressions")

**Paragraph 1.** Husserl distinguishes relational determinations — determinations that an object has only in relation to another (e.g., being-larger-than, being-to-the-left-of) — from what he calls "absolute impressions" — determinations that an object has on its own, without reference to another (e.g., its color, its shape). This distinction is not the same as the earlier distinction between connection-relations and comparison-relations; rather, it cuts across it. Some relational determinations are grounded in connection (spatial relations), others in comparison (similarity-relations). The "absolute impressions" are the properties that an object has independently of any relating — the properties that are given in pure explication, without reference to other objects.

**Paragraph 2.** The importance of this distinction lies in its implications for predication. Predicative judgments that ascribe relational determinations ("S is larger than T") have a different structure from those that ascribe absolute determinations ("S is red"). The former presuppose the relating contemplation as their pre-predicative foundation; the latter presuppose only explication. The genetic analysis of predication must therefore account for both kinds of judgment-form and trace each back to its respective pre-predicative source.

## Part II. Predicative Thinking and the Objectualities of the Understanding

### Chapter I. The General Structure of Predication and the Genesis of the Most Important Categorial Forms

### §47. The Cognitive Interest and Its Working-Out in the Predicative Achievements

**Paragraph 1.** Determination of the being as it is and what it is — this is the sense of all cognitive activity. It does not yet reach its goal in the previously investigated domain of receptivity. Objects as identical unities in a manifold of apprehending and explicating steps of turning-toward do constitute themselves already in receptivity. What affects is taken in, what is given in active turning is run through, what was previously run through is returned to in memory, set into relation with other items, and so on. But all these achievements are tied to the immediate — whether self-giving or reproductive — intuition of the substrates. Although nothing that was once given in experience and especially in intuition is lost in consciousness, since everything continues to operate by creating and further developing a horizon of familiarities and acquaintance-qualities, the experienced has not for all that yet become our possession, over which we dispose henceforth, which we can always retrieve, and of which we can give others notice.

**Paragraph 2.** The interest of perception that guides receptive experience is only the preliminary stage of the proper cognitive interest. It is a tendentious pull toward bringing the intuitively given object to givenness from all sides. But the will to cognition — whether for its own sake or in the service of a practical goal — aims at more. In receptivity, the ego is indeed actively turned toward what affects, but it does not make its cognition, nor the individual steps of cognition as means to its achievement, into an object of willing. In the proper cognitive interest, by contrast, a volitional participation of the ego is at play in an entirely new way: the ego wills to know the object, to hold fast what is known once and for all. Every step of cognition is guided by an active volitional impulse to maintain the known as this selfsame and as substrate of its determining features in the future course of life, to set it into relation, and so on. Cognition is action of the ego; the goal of willing is the apprehension of the object in its identical determinacy, the fixation of the result of contemplative perception "once and for all."

**Paragraph 3.** This achievement of cognition is an operation upon the pregiven objects, but in an entirely different way from the merely receptive activity of apprehending, explicating, and relating contemplation. Its result is cognitive possession. In the pregnant concept of the object as object of cognition, it is implied that it is something identical and identifiable beyond the time of its intuitive givenness — that what was once given in intuition must also, when the intuition is past, be capable of being preserved as a lasting possession, in formations that through initially empty indications can lead back to the intuitioning of the identical. Thus what is at issue here is objectifying achievements of a new kind: not merely operations upon pregiven and receptively apprehended objectualities, but in predicative cognition and its precipitate in the predicative judgment, new kinds of objectualities constitute themselves that can then in turn be apprehended and made thematic — the logical formations that we call, as arising from *kata apophansin* (assertoric judging), categorial objectualities, or (since judging is an achievement of the understanding) objectualities of the understanding (*Verstandesgegenständlichkeiten*).

**Paragraph 4.** This achievement of cognition, this higher level of activity in contrast to receptivity, is thus to be characterized as a creative, object-first-producing spontaneity. These are the objects that, as logical formations, have hitherto exclusively claimed the logicians' interest, without the manner of their original production and their arising from the lower level of cognitive achievement having been investigated. In these formations, cognition is so precipitated that it can first genuinely become a lasting possession — an object over which, as identical, I can not only myself dispose, but which is intersubjectively constituted in a way that, on the basis of the expressions connected with the logical achievements and their indications, what was initially given in my intuition can be intuited as identical by the other as well.

**Paragraph 5.** The entire stratum of expression, which is inseparably connected with predicative achievements — the entire set of questions about the connection of speaking and predicative thinking, whether and to what extent all predicative thinking is bound to words, how the syntactic articulation of the expression coheres with the articulation of the thought — all this must be set aside. The predicative achievements will be investigated purely as they present themselves, apart from all these connections, as subjective activity in phenomenal-experiential terms.

**Paragraph 6.** The main themes of the following investigations are thereby prefigured. They will first have to trace the structure of predicative activities in general, the manner in which they build upon the achievements of the lower level (Ch. I); then the structure and mode of being of the objectualities arising in them will have to be considered (Ch. II); and finally the fact of the detachability of these objectualities from their substratum will lead to the distinction of intuitive and empty judging, in which we will find the source of the modalities of the predicative judgment, and will grasp these themselves as modes of ego-decision from their constitutive origin (Ch. III).

### §48. Cognitive Action Paralleled with Practical Action

**Paragraph 1.** Before the transition to the special analyses, some general problems relating to predicative activity are discussed. Predicatively cognitive achievement was designated as an action, and this is justified by the fact that the general structures of action are demonstrable in it, while it nevertheless differs from action in the ordinary sense of the word. In the latter, we think primarily of an external doing, a producing of external objects (things) as self-given from other self-given objects. In cognitive action, new objectualities are indeed also preconstituted, but this production has an entirely different sense from that of things from things; and — what is above all important here — this production of categorial objectualities in cognitive action is not the final goal of this action. All cognitive activity is ultimately — notwithstanding the possibility of moving for long stretches within the domain of the produced objects, the logical formations, in the mere evidence of distinctness — related to the judgment-substrates. Not the production of objects is its goal, but a production of knowledge of a self-given object, hence its self-possession as something durably re-identifiable.

**Paragraph 2.** The cognitive striving has its analogies with desiring striving. Desiring striving leads to the realizing action that commences with a "fiat," and in the progress of action it fulfills itself more and more, passing from initial mere aiming to attaining. The path to the goal can be simple or composite, passing through intermediate goals. With the rising fulfillment during the course of action and the approach to the goal, a growing feeling of satisfaction sets in, and we must distinguish the fulfillment of the tendencies toward satisfaction from that of the volitional directedness toward the goal. In cognitive action, though we do not have such realization of external processes and objects, the structure has an exact analogy: the goal is knowledge, and we distinguish the completely unfulfilled aiming and its rising fulfillment in the cognitive action up to complete attainment, until the object stands before us as completely known.

**Paragraph 3.** This shows itself in the return to once-acquired cognitions, in the reproduction of intuitions in the form of memories or other presentifications. Such reproductions are then more than a mere memory of a previous intuition. We come back to the reproduced as to our actively, from an acquisitive will, produced acquisition. As such it is intentionally characterized. It is reproduced differently than in a mere memory: a volitional modification is involved, as with every acquisition. This gives it the character of the not merely previously volitionally apprehended, but of the still valid acquisition that we still hold in will — the will not simply repeated but in reproduced form "still" willing: I, the present ego, am still willing; therein is implied that I am in agreement with the past will, co-willing, holding it in co-validity as my present, presently willing ego. Thus what was once grasped in predicative judging in its truth, as "itself," is now a lasting possession, always available again as such, in that it can be apprehended, reproduced, in a repeating process.

**Paragraph 4.** The cognition of the true self is as goal-shape that toward which the whole process borne by the cognitive interest ultimately strives. Every step of cognition is determined by the fact that it means not merely fulfillment with clarity and intuitiveness but at the same time fulfillment of the cognitive striving and thereby its rising satisfaction. The satisfaction that goes hand in hand with the rising fulfillment of cognition is not satisfaction with the being of the object, or with its possession as in external action, but satisfaction with its mode of cognition, with the clarity of givenness. Cognition as action is thus an aiming activity directed toward a taking-possession of the true being and being-so of an object, its determining features, in the relevant states of affairs.

### §49. The Sense of the Level-Distinction of the Objectifying Achievements. Transition to the Constitutive Analyses

**Paragraph 1.** When we distinguish two levels of interest and correspondingly two levels of objectifying achievements — on the one hand, receptive experience, on the other, predicative spontaneity — this level-distinction must not be understood as though the various achievements were somehow separated from one another. Rather, what must be treated separately for purposes of analysis and is genetically recognized as belonging to different levels of objectification is factually as a rule closely interwoven. That receptivity lies before predicative spontaneity does not mean that it is factually something self-standing, as though a chain of receptive experiences always had to have run off before the proper cognitive interest awakens. Rather, we can from the outset make a pregiven object thematic in the cognitive interest — not only to look at it but to "determine" in lasting cognitions "how it is." Then immediately, hand in hand with the receptive apprehension goes the predicative forming and knowing, and what is genetically distinguished as belonging to different levels is factually inseparably interwoven in the concretion of one consciousness — always, to be sure, built up one upon the other: every step of predication presupposes a step of receptive experience and explication; originally predicated can be only what is originally intuitively given, apprehended, and explicated.

**Paragraph 2.** The same will hold when we later distinguish from the achievements of predicatively determining and relating thinking and its predicative forming, as a third and highest level, the comprehending thinking and the generality-forming accomplished in it. Here too it is a matter only — and to an even higher degree than in the distinction of the first two levels — of an abstractive separation. There is no predicative judging, no formation of predicative forms, that does not already at the same time enclose within itself a generality-formation. Just as every object of receptivity stands from the outset as an object of a somehow known type, so correspondingly in every predicative formation there already takes place a determination "as" such-and-such, on the basis of the expressions inseparably interwoven with every predication and the general meanings belonging to them.

**Paragraph 3.** The ordering of the following investigations is determined first by the course of the discussions of Part I. We began there from the explication of an object in perception. This leads on the predicative level to a perceptual judgment of the simplest form "S is p." With the pursuit of its constitution, general fundamental structures of predication will show themselves and thereby already more general insights into the essence of predicative forming as such and its relation to the processes in the lower stratum. If we then ascend further to the more complicated forms, the sequence will be determined solely by the viewpoint of lesser or greater complexity, proceeding from the simple to the ever more complex.

### §50. The Fundamental Structure of Predication

#### a) The Two-Memberedness of the Predicative Process

**Paragraph 1.** Let us take our departure from the simple perception and explication of a still undetermined substrate S, and let us restrict ourselves to its explication according to a non-self-standing inner determination, a moment that we will designate as p. The simplest case is that in which the explication does not proceed further at all to ever new moments. The contemplation pauses at once and leads only to p, whereupon transition to determination immediately follows. What is the new achievement when, on the basis of the explication, it comes to the predicative determination "S is p"?

**Paragraph 2.** We have seen that in the explication of a substrate S, a coincidence occurs between S and its determining moment p. The substrate has received a sense-accretion in this synthesis of transition from S to p as still-held-in-grasp substrate. But when we, holding S in grasp, pass over to its moment p — when we experience this coinciding, this "narrowing" of S into p — we have not thereby yet posited S as subject in a predicative judgment and determined it as having the moment in the manner "S is p." This is rather the achievement of a new kind of activity. Already in receptive apprehending and explicating, active steps took place: in an active turning, the substrate S was first apprehended in its undifferentiated unity, made thematic, and then in the explicative synthesis its determination p was actively apprehended. To that extent went the achievement of egoic activity. Beyond that, the explicative coincidence between the still-in-grasp substrate S and its determination p set itself in passively, and thereby the thematic substrate-object S, in this passive modification, received its sense-enrichment.

**Paragraph 3.** When the transition from S to p has taken place in this way, the interest of a higher level now arises in the substrate-object on the basis of the active contemplating — arising from it as an interest in holding fast the sense-accretion produced in contemplating, the S in its sense-enrichment. The S, which at the end of the process of contemplation is different from what it was at the beginning — having retreated, merely held in grasp, no longer standing "in the focal point" of interest — re-enters the focal point of interest as it now gives itself, as the sense-expanded. We go back to S, thus identify it with itself, which only means that in the return it stands "again" as S: in this new thematic apprehension, we have its sense-enrichment as a mere protention, in connection with the retention of the just-elapsed transition. The interest now operates in the direction of S in its sense-enrichment, which presupposes that we again pass over to p. But the passing-over is now guided by the cognitive will to hold fast S in its determination. An active intention aims at apprehending what was previously merely passive coincidence — at actively producing, in the active transition to p, what accrues to S. As active ego, I carry out the transition and the partial coincidence as free activity and thereby bring to fulfillment the determining intention — the intention upon S in its sense arising from transition and coincidence. I have S as substrate of a determination and actively determine it. The substrate-object takes on the form of the predicative subject; it is the subject-theme as *terminus a quo*, and the activity passes over to the predicate as the counter-terminus *ad quem*. Only now does the consciousness come about — produced in an activity that is not only synthetic activity in general but at the same time activity of the synthesis itself — that S through p receives determination in the manner "S is p."

**Paragraph 4.** We said that the distinctive feature of predicative synthesis consists in the active carrying-out of the synthetic transition from S to p, in the active carrying-out of the identity-unity between S and p. We are thus directed in a certain way toward the identity-unity. But this must not be understood as though we were (noetically) directed toward the process of identifying — toward the experiential manifold in which the synthetic unity between S and p is established. We are in that attitude now, as we phenomenologically clarify the predicative synthesis; but in its carrying-out itself we are objectually directed toward S in its partial identity with p. Nor does it mean that we explicate the result of the receptive explication — this successively constituted identity-unity that preconstituted itself in the explication. That would mean running through the succession anew, hence renewing the explication "in memory."

**Paragraph 5.** Rather: that the explication-substrate becomes subject and the explicates become predicates — this can only happen in that the gaze turns back toward the unity, in a certain way hidden, that was passively preconstituted within the receptive activity in the process of explication. To turn apprehendingly to this unity means to repeat the process in a changed attitude, to make an active synthesis out of a passive one. It is nothing that can be originally apprehended in a simple turning, in the way everything was apprehended in simple turnings at the lower level; it can only come into view in the repeating running-through. This takes place, as said, in a changed attitude: we do not again carry out a merely contemplative explication, but an activity of predicative identification, and this is an apprehending consciousness not of single-rayed but of multi-rayed (polythetic) activity. From the spontaneous apprehension of S as subjectification, the action goes as a determining identification to p: the apprehending gaze lives in the apprehension of the self-determining of the S through the p.

#### b) The Double Form-Building in Predication

**Paragraph 6.** The predicative synthesis thus involves a double form-building (*doppelte Formenbildung*). On the one hand, there is the form-building on the side of the substrate: the object S, which in receptive experience was not yet a "subject" in any logical sense, now takes on the logical form of the predicative subject. On the other hand, there is the form-building on the side of the determination: the moment p, which in explication was merely a moment "of" S, now takes on the logical form of the predicate. These two logical forms — subject-form and predicate-form — do not pre-exist in the objects themselves; they accrue to them only in the predicative activity. The same object that is here subject could, in a different predicative context, function as predicate, and vice versa. The logical forms are forms of function, not intrinsic properties of the objects.

**Paragraph 7.** This double form-building is the origin of the most fundamental categorial distinction in formal logic: that between subject and predicate. The analysis shows that this distinction does not rest on any intrinsic ontological difference between the objects that occupy these functional positions, but on the different roles they play in the predicative synthesis — roles that are assigned to them by the direction and articulation of the cognitive interest. The subject is the *terminus a quo* — what the ego starts from and holds in thematic grasp; the predicate is the *terminus ad quem* — what the ego arrives at through the explicative-predicative transition.

#### c) The Judgment as the Primordial Cell of the Thematic Nexus of Predicative Determination and the Sense of Its Self-Standingness

**Paragraph 8.** Each individual predicative judgment "S is p" is in itself a completed step of determination. It constitutes a self-standing whole — the primordial cell (*Urzelle*) of the thematic nexus of predicative determination. It has a beginning (the positing of the subject), a middle (the transition-synthesis), and an end (the positing of the predicate). The whole forms an articulated unity — not a mere succession of positing-acts but a synthetic unity in which the moments are held together by the identity-relation between subject and predicate.

**Paragraph 9.** The self-standingness of the predicative judgment is of the greatest importance. Each such judgment can stand on its own, can be detached from the context of the ongoing contemplation that gave rise to it, and can be preserved as a cognitive possession in its own right. This detachability is what makes it possible for judgments to function as the building-blocks of science: they can be stored, communicated, retrieved, and combined with other judgments in argumentation. The assertoric sentence, as the linguistic precipitate of the predicative judgment, is the vehicle of this detachability.

### §51. The Judgment-Forms Corresponding to Simply Progressive Explication

#### a) Continuing Determination

**Paragraph 1.** The simplest judgment-form "S is p" corresponds to the simplest case of explication: the explication of a single substrate according to a single determination. But explication normally does not stop at a single determination; it proceeds to further determinations of the same substrate. This progressive explication — running from S to p, then from S to q, then from S to r, and so on — gives rise to the form of continuing determination (*fortlaufendes Bestimmen*): "S is p; S is q; S is r; ..." Each step is a self-standing judgment, but they are connected by the identity of the substrate, which is held in thematic grasp throughout.

#### b) Determination in the Form of "And So Forth"

**Paragraph 2.** The continuing determination can take on the form of the "and so forth" (*und so weiter*): "S is p, and q, and r, ..." Here the individual determinations are linked by the copulative "and," and the whole forms a single, albeit open-ended, judgment. This corresponds to the case where the explication proceeds without break and without any change of the thematic substrate, running through one determination after another in a single continuous process.

#### c) Identifyingly Connecting Determination

**Paragraph 3.** A more complex case arises when, in the course of continuing determination, the ego returns to a previously made determination and connects it with a new one: "S, which is p, is also q." This is identifyingly connecting determination (*identifizierend anknüpfendes Bestimmen*): the ego explicitly identifies the S that is p with the S that is q, and the two determinations are brought into a single, more complex judgment. This form corresponds to the case where the contemplation does not simply run forward but loops back, connecting what was earlier determined with what is newly determined.

### §52. "Is"-Judgment and "Has"-Judgment

#### a) The Form of the "Has"-Judgment Corresponding to Explication According to Self-Standing Parts

**Paragraph 1.** The explication of a whole according to its self-standing parts (pieces) gives rise not to the form "S is p" but to a different form: "S has a, b, c, ..." This is the "has"-judgment (*Hat-Urteil*). While the "is"-judgment ascribes a non-self-standing determination (a property) to the substrate, the "has"-judgment attributes self-standing parts to a whole. The difference corresponds exactly to the distinction drawn in Part I (§§30–32) between the explication of non-self-standing moments (properties) and self-standing parts (pieces).

#### b) The Substantivization of Non-Self-Standing Determinations and the Transformation of the "Is"-Judgment into a "Has"-Judgment

**Paragraph 2.** A non-self-standing determination — a property — can be substantivized: instead of saying "S is red," we can say "S has redness." Through this substantivization, what was originally a predicate in an "is"-judgment becomes a quasi-substrate in a "has"-judgment. This transformation reveals an important feature of the predicative forms: the logical forms are not rigid but admit of transformations that preserve the cognitive content while altering the form. The same cognitive content can be expressed in different predicative forms, and the choice of form depends on the direction of the cognitive interest.

### §53. Judging on the Basis of Relating Contemplation. Absolute and Relative Adjectivity

**Paragraph 1.** The judgments considered so far arose from the explication of a single substrate. But the relating contemplation analyzed in Part I (Ch. III) also gives rise to predicative judgments — judgments that ascribe to a substrate not an "inner" determination but a relational determination: "S is larger than T," "S lies next to T," and so on. These are relational judgments, and the determinations ascribed in them are relative rather than absolute. Husserl introduces the distinction between absolute adjectivity (*absolute Adjektivität*) — the ascription of a property that the object has on its own — and relative adjectivity (*relative Adjektivität*) — the ascription of a property that the object has only in relation to another.

### §54. The Sense of the Distinction between Determining and Relating Judging

**Paragraph 1.** The distinction between determining judging (which arises from explication) and relating judging (which arises from relating contemplation) is not absolute but admits of transitions and ambiguities. A relational determination can be transformed into a determining one: "S is larger than T" can become "S has the property of being-larger-than-T," where the relational determination is substantivized and treated as a property of S. Conversely, what appears as an absolute property may, upon deeper analysis, turn out to involve a tacit relation. The distinction between determining and relating is thus relative to the level of analysis and to the direction of the cognitive interest.

### §55. The Origin of Attribution from the Unequal Distribution of Interest upon the Determinations

#### a) The Articulation into Main Clause and Subordinate Clause

**Paragraph 1.** Not all the determinations of a substrate receive equal attention in the judgment. The cognitive interest may be focused on some and merely acknowledge others in passing. This unequal distribution of interest gives rise to the attributive form: "S, which is p, is q." Here the determination p is attributed to S in a subordinate clause, while q is predicated in the main clause. The attribution of p is not an independent predicative act but a subsidiary one, serving only to further characterize the subject that is then determined in the main predication.

#### b) The Attributive Form as Modification of the Sentential Form

**Paragraph 2.** The attributive form "the p-ish S" (e.g., "the red ball") is a modification of the full sentential form "S is p." In the attributive form, the predicative synthesis is not carried out for its own sake but is, as it were, contracted into a single qualifying expression that attaches to the substrate-term. What was a full judgment has become an attribute — a modification that Husserl traces to the withdrawal of independent cognitive interest from the determination p, which now serves only to specify the substrate for the main predication.

#### c) Attributive Connection on the Determination-Side

**Paragraph 3.** Attribution can also occur on the determination-side: "S is p-ly q" — for example, "the surface is evenly red." Here the attribute qualifies not the subject but the predicate. This corresponds to a further articulation of the cognitive interest within the determination itself: the determination q is further specified by the subsidiary determination p. The resulting attributive structure on the predicate-side mirrors the attributive structure on the subject-side.

### §56. Constitution of Logical Sense as the Result of the Predicative Achievements for the Substrate-Object

**Paragraph 1.** Through the predicative achievements, the substrate-object acquires a new kind of determination that it did not have before: logical sense (*logischer Sinn*). Before predication, the object was given with its experiential properties — its colors, shapes, and so on — as determined through the receptive explication. Through predication, these experiential properties are taken up into logical forms: the object is now not merely red but "determined as red," "known as red." The logical sense is the form that the object's experiential content takes on through being made the subject of predicative determination. It is the precipitate of the predicative activity upon the object — what remains when the activity itself is past.

**Paragraph 2.** This constitution of logical sense is cumulative: each new predication adds to the logical sense of the object. The object that has been determined as "red," "spherical," "smooth," and "heavy" has a richer logical sense than one that has been determined only as "red." The totality of the predicative determinations constitutes the object's identity as an object of cognition — its identity not as an experiential thing (which it has already through receptive experience) but as a known thing, a thing determined through the activity of the understanding.

### §57. The Origin of the Identity-Judgment

**Paragraph 1.** A distinctive judgment-form arises when the predicative activity is directed not toward a new determination of the substrate but toward the identification of two substrates: "A is (identical with) B." This is the identity-judgment. It presupposes that the same object has been given under two different guises — for example, seen from two different angles, or encountered at two different times — and that the cognitive interest is directed toward establishing their sameness. The identity-judgment thus has a different genesis from the determining judgment: it arises not from explication but from identification — the bringing-together of two substrate-apprehensions and the recognition that they concern the same object.

**Paragraph 2.** The analysis of the identity-judgment reveals that identification is not a simple act but a complex synthesis: the two substrate-apprehensions must be brought into a coincidence that establishes their sameness despite the differences in their modes of givenness. The "is" in the identity-judgment has a different sense from the "is" in the determining judgment: it does not ascribe a property to a substrate but asserts the sameness of two substrates.

### Chapter II. The Objectualities of the Understanding and Their Origin from the Predicative Achievements

### §58. Transition to a New Level of Predicative Achievements. The Preconstitution of the State of Affairs as a Categorial Objectuality and Its "Extraction" through Substantivization

**Paragraph 1.** In the predicative judgment "S is p," a new kind of objectuality is preconstituted that is not identical with either the substrate S or the determination p: the state of affairs (*Sachverhalt*) — "that S is p." The state of affairs is the correlate of the judgment as a whole; it is what the judgment asserts, the "what" of its assertion. It is not a thing in the world; it is not a sensory object; it cannot be perceived. It is an objectuality of a new kind — a categorial objectuality — constituted not in receptive experience but in predicative spontaneity.

**Paragraph 2.** The state of affairs is "preconstituted" in the predicative judgment: it is implicitly present in every judgment but does not become an independent object until it is "extracted" through a new act of substantivization. When we pass from "S is p" to "that S is p" or "the being-p of S," we make the state of affairs into a substrate in its own right — a substrate that can then itself become the subject of further predications. This extraction through substantivization (or nominalization) is the fundamental operation by which categorial objectualities are constituted.

### §59. Plainly Givable Objects as "Sources" of States-of-Affairs (*Sachlagen*). *Sachlage* and *Sachverhalt*

**Paragraph 1.** Husserl introduces a crucial distinction between the *Sachlage* (state-of-things or situation) and the *Sachverhalt* (state of affairs). The *Sachlage* is the pre-predicative correlate: the objective situation as it obtains among the objects themselves, prior to and independently of any predicative forming. The *Sachverhalt*, by contrast, is the predicative correlate: the state of affairs as it is articulated and formed through the predicative judgment. The same *Sachlage* can give rise to different *Sachverhalte* depending on the direction and articulation of the predicative interest — for example, the same spatial situation can be articulated as "A is to the left of B" or as "B is to the right of A."

**Paragraph 2.** The plainly givable objects of receptive experience are the "sources" (*Quellen*) of states-of-affairs: it is from the pre-predicative relations and determinations obtaining among them that the predicative judgments draw their content. The *Sachverhalt* is thus not a free creation of the understanding but an articulation — in categorial form — of what is already pre-predicatively given in the *Sachlage*.

### §60. Distinction of *Sachverhalt* and Full Judgment-Sentence

**Paragraph 1.** The *Sachverhalt* is to be distinguished from the full judgment-sentence (*Urteilssatz*). The judgment-sentence includes not only the state of affairs it asserts but also the mode of assertion — the certainty, the modalization, the quality (affirmative or negative) with which it is asserted. The same *Sachverhalt* can be asserted with certainty, doubted, questioned, or denied. These different modes of assertion do not alter the *Sachverhalt* but alter the judgment as a whole. The *Sachverhalt* is the "matter" (*Materie*) of the judgment; the mode of assertion is its "quality" — a distinction that Husserl traces back to his earlier analyses in the *Logical Investigations*.

### §61. The Set (*Menge*) as a Further Example of an Objectuality of the Understanding; Its Constitution in Productive Spontaneity

**Paragraph 1.** The state of affairs is not the only kind of categorial objectuality. Another important example is the set or collection (*Menge*). When we bring together a plurality of objects in a collecting act — "a and b and c" — we constitute a new objectuality: the set {a, b, c}. This set is not itself a sensory object; it cannot be perceived; it is constituted only in the spontaneous, collecting activity of the understanding. Like the state of affairs, it is a categorial objectuality — an objectuality of a higher order, produced by the understanding on the basis of the pre-predicatively given objects.

**Paragraph 2.** The constitution of the set illustrates the productive character of the spontaneity in a particularly clear way. The elements of the set are given in receptive experience; but the set itself — the togetherness of the elements as a new, unified objectuality — is not given but produced. It is a genuine creation of the understanding, an objectuality that would not exist without the spontaneous act of collecting.

### §62. Objectualities of the Understanding as Sources of *Sachlagen* and *Sachverhalte*; Distinction of Syntactic and Non-Syntactic Connections and Relations

**Paragraph 1.** The categorial objectualities — states of affairs, sets, and others — can themselves become substrates of further predications and thus sources of new states of affairs. This iterability is essential to the life of the understanding: the products of one level of predicative activity become the substrates for the next. The understanding can thus build up ever higher levels of categorial complexity.

**Paragraph 2.** Husserl distinguishes between syntactic and non-syntactic connections and relations. Syntactic connections are those that arise from the predicative forming itself — the connection between subject and predicate in a judgment, the connection between the members of a conjunction, and so on. Non-syntactic connections are those that obtain among the objects themselves independently of any predicative forming — the connection of parts in a whole, the connection of cause and effect, and so on. The syntactic connections are categorial forms in the proper sense; the non-syntactic connections are the pre-categorial substrates upon which the syntactic forms are built.

### §63. The Difference in the Constitution of Objectualities of the Understanding and Objects of Receptivity

**Paragraph 1.** There is a fundamental difference between the constitution of objects of receptivity and that of objectualities of the understanding. Objects of receptivity — the things of perceptual experience — are constituted in passive synthesis and receptive apprehension. They are given, not produced; encountered, not created. Objectualities of the understanding — states of affairs, sets, and the like — are, by contrast, constituted in productive spontaneity. They are not given but produced; not encountered but created. The understanding does not find them in the world but generates them through its own activity.

**Paragraph 2.** This does not mean that the objectualities of the understanding are arbitrary inventions. They are produced on the basis of what is pre-predicatively given; they articulate and form what is already there in the experiential substratum. But the form they take — the specific categorial articulation — is the contribution of the understanding's spontaneous activity. The understanding is creative, but its creativity is guided and constrained by the pre-predicative material upon which it operates.

### §64. The Irreality of the Objectualities of the Understanding and Their Temporality

#### a) Immanent Time as the Form of Givenness of All Objectualities Whatsoever

**Paragraph 1.** All objectualities — whether objects of receptive experience or objectualities of the understanding — are given in immanent time. Every act of consciousness, whether perceptual, memorial, or productive, takes place in the flow of inner time-consciousness and has its position therein. The constituted objectualities are given as constituted at a certain time in the life of the ego.

#### b) The Temporality of Real Objectualities. Time of Givenness and Objective (Natural) Time

**Paragraph 2.** Real objectualities — the things of perceptual experience — have a double temporality. On the one hand, they are given at certain times in the life of the ego (their time of givenness). On the other hand, they have their own objective duration in the time of nature: they endure, change, come into being, and pass away. Their objective time is not identical with their time of givenness but is constituted through the latter.

#### c) The Time-Form of Irreal Objectualities as Omni-Temporality

**Paragraph 3.** The objectualities of the understanding — states of affairs, sets, propositions — do not have objective duration in the time of nature. They do not come into being and pass away in the manner of things. Yet they are not simply atemporal. Rather, they have the time-form of omni-temporality (*Allzeitlichkeit*): they hold "at all times" or "for all times." The state of affairs "2 + 2 = 4" does not begin or cease to hold; it holds always. This omni-temporality is the specific mode of temporality belonging to irreal objectualities — the objectualities of the understanding.

#### d) The Irreality of the Objectualities of the Understanding Does Not Mean Genus-Generality

**Paragraph 4.** The irreality and omni-temporality of the objectualities of the understanding must not be confused with genus-generality. The state of affairs "that S is p" is not a general concept or a universal; it is a particular state of affairs, though an irreal one. Its irreality means that it does not exist in the manner of a spatiotemporal thing, not that it is a general essence. The distinction between real and irreal objectualities cuts across the distinction between individual and general.

### §65. The Distinction of Real and Irreal Objectualities in Its Comprehensive Significance. The Objectualities of the Understanding Belonging to the Region of Sense-Objectualities (*Vermeintheiten*)

**Paragraph 1.** The distinction between real and irreal objectualities has a comprehensive significance for the whole of phenomenology and formal ontology. The real objectualities are the spatiotemporal things, events, and processes of the natural world. The irreal objectualities include not only the categorial objectualities of the understanding (states of affairs, sets, propositions) but also ideal objectualities of other kinds — meanings, essences, values, and the like. All irreal objectualities are characterized by their omni-temporality and their non-spatiotemporality.

**Paragraph 2.** The objectualities of the understanding belong specifically to the region of sense-objectualities (*Vermeintheiten*) — objectualities that are the correlates of meaning-acts, of acts of intending and asserting. They are "what is meant as such" — the noematic correlates of the noetic acts of the understanding. This classification places them within the broader framework of Husserl's phenomenology of meaning and intentionality.

### Chapter III. The Origin of the Modalities of Judgment

### §66. Introduction. The Modalities of the Predicative Judgment as Modes of Ego-Decision (Active Position-Taking)

**Paragraph 1.** The third chapter of Part II takes up the modalities of the predicative judgment. In Part I (§21), the modalizations of certainty — negation, doubt, possibility — were analyzed in their pre-predicative origin as inhibitions in the course of the perceptual interest. Now these are taken up again at the higher, predicative level. The modalities of the predicative judgment — certainty, negation, possibility, probability, questioning — are modes of ego-decision (*Ich-Entscheidung*), that is, modes of active position-taking (*aktive Stellungnahme*). Unlike the pre-predicative modalizations, which were largely passive occurrences, the predicative modalities involve an active ego that decides, affirms, denies, doubts, or questions.

**Paragraph 2.** Husserl begins with the broader sense of modalization (§21, d), which includes every modification of the original validity-mode of straightforward certainty, and only later (§§76 ff.) turns to the narrower sense (doubt, presumption, etc. as active position-takings). The chapter thus traces the genesis of all judgment-modalities from their pre-predicative origins through their predicative articulation.

### §67. The Empty Modifications of Judgment as Motives for Modalization

#### a) The Empty Modifications Grounded in the Anticipations of Experience and Their Modalizations

**Paragraph 1.** Not every judgment is carried out in full intuitive evidence. Many judgments are carried out "emptily" — without the intuitive givenness of what they assert. Empty judging is an intentional modification of evident judging: it retains the form and content of the judgment but lacks the fulfilling intuition. These empty modifications of judgment provide motives for modalization: an emptily held judgment can come into conflict with new experience, leading to doubt, modalization, or negation.

**Paragraph 2.** The empty modifications grounded in the anticipations of experience are of particular importance. Every experience carries with it anticipations of what further experience will bring. When these anticipations are taken up into predicative form, they become empty judgments — judgments about what is anticipated but not yet intuitively given. If subsequent experience fails to confirm them, the judgments are modalized: they become doubtful, probable, or are negated.

#### b) The Empty Modifications Arising from the Sedimentation of Originally Formed Judgments

**Paragraph 3.** A second source of empty modifications is the sedimentation (*Sedimentierung*) of originally formed judgments. Once a judgment has been formed on the basis of full intuitive evidence, it sediments into a habitual possession — a judgment that is "still held" but no longer carried out in full evidence. This sedimented judgment can be re-activated at any time, but in its sedimented state it is an empty modification of its originally evident form. The sedimented judgments form the "stock" of our cognitive convictions; they are the medium in which our knowledge is stored and transmitted. But precisely because they are empty, they can come into conflict with new experience and thereby become motives for modalization.

### §68. The Origin of the Judgment-Position-Takings from the Critique of Empty Presumptions. Critique Directed toward Confirmation (Adequation)

**Paragraph 1.** The modalized judgments that arise from the conflict between empty presumptions and new experience call for resolution. This resolution takes the form of a critique — an active examination of the empty presumption in light of the intuitive evidence. The critique is directed toward confirmation (*Bewährung*) or, as Husserl also says, adequation (*Adäquation*): it aims to determine whether the presumption accords with the evidence, whether the empty judgment can be filled by intuitive givenness. The outcome of the critique is an active position-taking: an affirming or denying judgment that resolves the modalization.

### §69. What Is Meant in the Judgment as Such and True State of Affairs. In What Sense the State of Affairs Is a Sense-Objectuality

**Paragraph 1.** In every judgment, something is "meant" — the judgment has a content, a "what" that it asserts. This "what is meant in the judgment as such" (*Urteilsvermeintes als solches*) is to be distinguished from the "true state of affairs" (*wahrer Sachverhalt*). The former is the noematic correlate of the judging act — what is intended, whether or not it is true. The latter is what actually obtains — the state of affairs as it truly is. The relationship between the two is the relationship between intending and fulfillment: the judgment is true when what it means accords with what truly obtains.

**Paragraph 2.** The state of affairs, whether as meant or as truly obtaining, is a sense-objectuality (*Sinngegenständlichkeit*). It is not a thing in the world but a correlate of meaning — an objectuality constituted in and through the activity of the understanding. This does not make it subjective or arbitrary; the true state of affairs is what it is independently of any particular judgment. But it is nonetheless an objectuality of a different kind from the things of perceptual experience.

### §70. The Evidence of the Givenness of States of Affairs Analogous to the Evidence of the Underlying Substrate-Objectualities

**Paragraph 1.** Just as the objects of receptive experience can be given in evidence — in the mode of self-givenness, of "the thing itself" — so too can states of affairs be given in evidence. The evidence of the state of affairs is the evidence of the judgment in which it is asserted: the judgment is evident when the state of affairs it asserts is given as itself. This evidence of the state of affairs is analogous to, but not identical with, the evidence of the underlying substrate-objectualities. The evidence of the substrate is perceptual evidence — the evidence of the bodily presence of the thing. The evidence of the state of affairs is judicative evidence — the evidence of the "being-so" of the thing as articulated in the judgment.

### §71. The Judgment-Position-Takings as Acknowledgment or Rejection. Acknowledgment as Appropriation and Its Significance for the Striving for Self-Preservation

**Paragraph 1.** The active position-takings of the ego in judgment can be characterized as acknowledgment (*Anerkennung*) or rejection (*Verwerfung*). When the ego affirms a judgment, it acknowledges the state of affairs it asserts; when it denies, it rejects. Acknowledgment is a form of appropriation (*Aneignung*): in affirming, the ego makes the judged content its own, incorporates it into its stock of convictions. This appropriation has a significance for the ego's striving for self-preservation (*Selbsterhaltung*): the ego strives to maintain a consistent, coherent system of convictions, and it affirms those judgments that accord with this system and rejects those that conflict with it.

### §72. The Problem of the "Quality" of the Judgment; the Negative Judgment Not a Basic Form

**Paragraph 1.** The tradition distinguishes between affirmative and negative judgments as two equally basic forms — the two "qualities" of the judgment. Husserl's genetic analysis shows that this equal ranking is mistaken. The negative judgment is not a basic form but a derived one: it arises only through the modalization of an originally affirmative judgment. The original, unmodalized form of the judgment is the straightforward affirmation; negation presupposes affirmation as its basis and is a modification of it. The negative judgment "S is not p" presupposes the prior (at least implicit) judgment "S is p," which is then negated. Negation is thus genetically secondary to affirmation.

### §73. Existential Judgment and Truth-Judgment as Judgment-Position-Takings of a Higher Level with Modified Judgment-Subject

**Paragraph 1.** Husserl now distinguishes two special forms of judgment-position-taking that operate at a higher level than ordinary predication: the existential judgment ("S exists") and the truth-judgment ("It is true that S is p"). In both cases, the judgment does not simply predicate a property of a substrate but takes a position regarding a judgment or a claim to existence. The existential judgment affirms or denies the existence of a substrate; the truth-judgment affirms or denies the truth of a previously held judgment. In both cases, the subject of the judgment is modified: in the existential judgment, the subject is an "entity-sense" (*Sinngegenständlichkeit*), not a perceived thing; in the truth-judgment, the subject is a proposition or judgment-content.

### §74. Distinction of Existential Predications from Actuality-Predications

#### a) The Origin of Actuality-Predication

**Paragraph 1.** Husserl distinguishes existential predications ("S exists") from actuality-predications ("S is actual" or "S is really so"). Actuality-predication arises from the critique of a previously doubtful or modalized judgment: when the doubt is resolved and the judgment is confirmed, the substrate receives the predicate "actual" or "really so." This predication of actuality is the expression of the ego's active confirmation of what had been in question.

#### b) Existential Predications Directed toward Senses, Actuality-Predications Directed toward Propositions as Subjects

**Paragraph 2.** Existential predications are directed toward senses (*Sinne*) — toward entity-senses that may or may not have existing correlates. Actuality-predications, by contrast, are directed toward propositions (*Sätze*) — toward judgment-contents that may or may not be true. The distinction corresponds to the difference between asking "Does S exist?" and asking "Is it true that S is p?"

### §75. Actuality-Predications and Existential Predications Not Determining Predications

**Paragraph 1.** Neither existential predications nor actuality-predications are determining predications in the sense of the "is"-judgment. They do not ascribe a property to a substrate in the manner of "S is p"; rather, they take a position regarding the being or the truth of something already constituted. "Existence" and "actuality" are not properties of objects alongside their other properties (such as color, shape, and size); they are modes of validity belonging to the judgment as a whole, not to the substrate as such. This is Husserl's version of the Kantian thesis that being is not a real predicate.

### §76. Transition to the Modalities in the Narrower Sense. Doubt and Presumption as Active Position-Takings

**Paragraph 1.** Having analyzed the broader modalities (negation, affirmation, existential and truth-judgments), Husserl now turns to the modalities in the narrower sense: doubt and presumption as active position-takings. In Part I (§21), doubt was analyzed as a pre-predicative phenomenon — a passive wavering between conflicting apprehensions. Now it is taken up at the predicative level as an active position-taking of the ego: the ego actively entertains doubt, actively withholds judgment, actively weighs the evidence for and against. Doubt at the predicative level is thus a transformed, higher-level version of the pre-predicative doubt.

**Paragraph 2.** Presumption (*Vermutung*) is the active position-taking in which the ego inclines toward one side of the doubt without fully resolving it: "presumably S is p." It is distinguished from full certainty by the residual openness that characterizes it — the ego's awareness that the matter is not fully settled. The presumption has a "weight" corresponding to the strength of the evidence in its favor.

### §77. The Modes of Certainty and the Concept of Conviction. Pure and Impure, Presumptive and Apodictic Certainty

**Paragraph 1.** Certainty itself admits of distinctions. Pure certainty is the unmodified, original mode in which the judgment is simply held. Impure certainty is certainty that has passed through doubt and been restored through confirmation — it carries the mark of having been contested and survived. Presumptive certainty is the certainty of presumption — a certainty that falls short of full evidence but goes beyond mere doubt. Apodictic certainty is the highest form: the certainty that the matter could not be otherwise — the certainty of necessity, of essential insight. These distinctions in the modes of certainty correspond to different experiential histories and different grades of evidence.

### §78. Question and Answer. Questioning as Striving for Judgment-Decision

**Paragraph 1.** Questioning is the active counterpart of doubt: it is the ego's active striving for judgment-decision in the face of unresolved modalization. The question "Is S p?" expresses the ego's active orientation toward resolving the doubt — its striving to arrive at an answer, whether affirmative or negative. The answer, when it comes, is the resolution of the question — the judgment-decision that terminates the questioning. Questioning and answering thus form a paired structure: the question opens a space of indecision; the answer closes it.

**Paragraph 2.** Questions can be simple or complex. A simple question asks about a single state of affairs: "Is S p?" A complex question asks about a relation or a plurality: "Which of these is larger?" The structure of the answer corresponds to the structure of the question: a simple question admits of a yes-or-no answer; a complex question may require a fuller, more articulated response.

### §79. The Distinction of Simple Questions and Justification-Questions

**Paragraph 1.** Husserl distinguishes simple questions (*schlichte Fragen*) from justification-questions (*Rechtfertigungsfragen*). A simple question asks about the being-so of a state of affairs: "Is S p?" A justification-question asks about the right or ground of a judgment: "Why is S p?" or "On what basis do you assert that S is p?" The justification-question is a higher-level question: it takes a judgment as its object and asks about its evidential basis. It presupposes the simple question and goes beyond it by thematizing the evidence-relations that underlie the judgment.

**Paragraph 2.** The distinction between simple questions and justification-questions corresponds to the distinction between judgments and judgments about judgments — between the first-order level of asserting states of affairs and the second-order level of reflecting on the grounds and evidence for such assertions. The justification-question is thus the entry-point into the domain of logic proper — the domain of the critique and grounding of knowledge. With it, the genetic investigation has traced the judgment from its pre-predicative origins in receptive experience, through its predicative articulation in the achievements of the understanding, up to the threshold of the reflective, critical activity that is the logician's proper concern.

## Part III. The Constitution of General Objectualities and the Forms of Judging-in-General (*Überhaupt*)

### §80. The Course of the Investigations

**Paragraph 1.** Part III turns to the highest level of logical activity: the constitution of general objectualities (universals, types, species, genera) and the judgment-forms that employ them. Husserl notes that the modification that general judging represents over against the previously analyzed forms is above all to be sought on the side of the judgment-cores (*Urteilskerne*): whereas the forms of predicative synthesis remain in their basic structure the same regardless of whether the S and the p are individual or general cores, the cores themselves undergo a decisive transformation. The analyses of predicative synthesis from Part II thus retain their universal validity; what changes is only the nature of the terms that enter into the synthesis.

**Paragraph 2.** The general objectualities constituted in the achievements of comprehending thinking — universalities, types, species, genera themselves as objects, as possible judgment-substrates — are of different levels. The initially most obvious generality, that of the empirical-presumptive type, will prove to be only one, and indeed a lower, level. Universalities can be constituted not only on the basis of what is already passively preconstituted in experience as a known but not yet apprehended type, but also freely produced in spontaneity. This leads at the highest level to the pure or essential universalities and, grounded upon them, to judgments that no longer arise from the thematization of the relation of objects to their empirical type of acquaintance, but from the thematization of the relation to their pure essence.

**Paragraph 3.** Only with these achievements of general judging does logical activity reach its *telos*. Objects are not only constituted as unities of identity through predicative forming, but at the same time comprehended and thereby cognized in a quite specific sense. Only general thinking leads to determinations that create an over-the-situation-available, also intersubjectively available, possession of cognitions. Predicative determining and relating of individual self-given substrates remains, as judging about individual "this-heres," always more or less bound to the situation of experience. Only the apprehension in the form of generality makes possible that detachment from the now and here of the experiential situation that is implied in the concept of objectivity of thinking.

**Paragraph 4.** The order of the investigations follows from this. We ascend from the lowest, constitutively simplest universalities to the highest, seeking all these forms in the originality of their production. The constitutively most original is not the nearest and most immediately presenting — as those empirical-presumptive types are. Genetically there precede them still more simply structured universalities (Ch. I). From them we must advance step by step to the highest, the pure universalities, whose constitution is independent of the preconstitution of such empirical types and rests on freely producing formation (Ch. II). Having thus traced the level-structure of general objectualities, only then can the constitution of the forms of judging-in-general (*Überhaupt*-Urteilen) be investigated as a further, highest spontaneous achievement (Ch. III), for which the constitution of general objectualities forms the presupposition.

### Chapter I. The Constitution of Empirical Universalities

### §81. The Original Constitution of the Universal

#### a) The Associative Synthesis of the Equal with the Equal as the Ground of the Standing-Out of the Universal

**Paragraph 1.** That all objects of experience are from the outset experienced as typically known has its ground in the sedimentation of all apperceptions and their habitual continued operation on the basis of associative awakening. Association originally passively establishes the synthesis of the equal with the equal, not only within a field of presence, but through the entire stream of experience and its immanent time and everything ever constituted in it. Syntheses of the equal with the equal are thus constituted, which can be associatively awakened and then brought into the unity of a presentifying intuition.

**Paragraph 2.** If we wish to seek the universal in its most original production, we must not initially draw on such syntheses of equality as lead to the empirical types, because what is brought together by association need not be self-given. We must restrict ourselves to what is self-given in the unity of a presence in a perception, in order to trace how, on its ground, universalities originally constitute themselves in self-givenness.

**Paragraph 3.** Each object affects from a plurality of co-given items in the field, and the plurality as such, as a multiplicity of separated objects, can act as an affective unity. It is not a mere togetherness of separated items; already in the passivity of its preconstitution, it essentially comprises a bond of inner affinity, insofar as the individual objects belonging to it have common features, on the basis of which they can then be taken together in the unity of a thematic interest. In the collecting running-through of the individual members, similarity-coincidence occurs according to their common features, and differentiation according to what is different.

**Paragraph 4.** On the basis of such equality-syntheses, the state-of-affairs form of the relational judgment can constitute itself ("A is equal to B"). But judging can also go in another direction: on the basis of the associative awakening of the equal by the equal, an object no longer merely affects on its own but in community with its kindred, and any judgment that holds for the object on its own can enter into connection with judgments holding for kindred objects. The distinctive feature of the synthesis of the equal with the equal is that it looks very much like an identity-synthesis but is not one. In the transition from equal to equal, we often straightforwardly say "that is the same thing." But the equals are two separated objects and not one and the same. And yet in every such duality and in every arbitrary manifold of equals, a unity and selfsameness in the strictest sense truly lies. It originally comes forth in the synthesis of equality-coincidence; it is through this originally preconstituted as an object.

#### b) The Universal Constituted in Productive Spontaneity. Individual Judgment and General Judgment

**Paragraph 5.** When the thematically determining interest concentrates on S and then wanders over to the co-affecting S' (co-affecting on the ground of a completely equal moment p as its individual moment), and when S' is predicatively determined through its moment p in the same manner as S was before, then the passive synthesis of coincidence between S and S' can be actively apprehended: we say "S and S' is the same — is p." But S had its moment p and S' had its moment p; like the substrates, their features are separated. Yet in the thematic transition they coincide, and an active identification takes place. In this transition from equal to equal moment with the coincidence, a unity comes forth — a unity in the separated and connected duality — and it comes forth again and again as totally identical the same when we proceed to a new member S'', then to S''', and so on. This unity constitutes itself first on the basis of the passive equality-coincidence of the individual moments, and it can then be apprehended for itself in a returning.

**Paragraph 6.** We must therefore distinguish the first series of judgments, in which each substrate is predicated of its individual moment ("S' is p', S'' is p''"), from the judgments in which the everywhere-equal p is predicated as the universal — the identically one in them all. The unity preconstitutes itself in the passive equality-coincidence of the p', p'', etc., as the unity of the species p. Thereupon a judging of a new direction is possible: we determine the S' not through p' as its individual moment but through p as identically the same in S, S', S'', etc. Thus instead of through the fleeting and changing moment, the substrate is determined through an ideally absolutely identical item that passes through all the individuated objects and their moments — which multiply in the manner of repetition or resemblance — as an ideal unity.

**Paragraph 7.** Different judgment-forms have thus separated from one another. The judgment "S is p'" (where p' indicates the individual moment at the individual object S) is an entirely different judgment from "S is p" (where p indicates the universal, the *eidos*). In the one case, an identification takes place between the substrate and its individual moment; in the other, a universal is predicated of the substrate — it is determined as being of the kind p. We call the former an individual judgment, the latter a general (*generelles*) judgment. The difference of the cores entails a modified form of identity-synthesis over against the simple explicative synthesis.

#### c) Participation in the Identity of the Universal and Mere Equality

**Paragraph 8.** The relation of participation (*Methexis*) is not to be confused with mere equality. Equality is only a correlate of the identity of a universal, which in truth can be discerned as one and selfsame and as the "counterpart" of the individual. This identical item "singularizes" itself first at two items and then at arbitrarily many. All these singularizations have through reference to the identical a relation to one another and are then called equal. In a transferred manner of speaking, the concrete objects that have such singularizations in themselves are called "equal with respect to the red," and can in an improper sense themselves be regarded as singularizations of the universal.

### §82. The Empirical Universalities and Their Extension. The Ideality of the Concept

**Paragraph 1.** The universal was initially thought of as given through the comparing of two substrates. Comparison can go further — from A to B, then to C, to D, and so on — and with each new step the universal gains a greater extension. On the basis of this equality-coincidence, new state-of-affairs forms arise as plurals: "A and B are red," "A and B and C are red," where "red" indicates the species. As the experience widens and leads to new equal objects, the universalities gain an infinite extension and lose their binding to just those individuals from which they were first abstracted. Moreover, we can go beyond experience into free phantasy and generate as many fictional equal instances as we wish. To every concept there thus belongs an infinite extension of purely possible instances.

**Paragraph 2.** The concept in its ideality is to be grasped as an objectuality that has a purely ideal being — a being that does not presuppose the actual existence of corresponding instances. It is what it is even if corresponding instances were only pure possibilities. The ideality of the universal must not, despite all Platonizing formulations, be understood as an in-itself-being unrelated to all subjectivity. Rather, like all objectualities of the understanding, it essentially refers back to correlative processes of productive spontaneity in which it comes to original givenness. The being of the universal in its various levels is essentially being-constituted in those processes.

### §83. The Empirically Typical Universality and Its Passive Preconstitution

#### a) The Gaining of Empirical Concepts from the Typology of Natural Experience-Apperception

**Paragraph 1.** The factual world of experience is experienced as typified. Things are experienced as tree, shrub, animal, snake, bird; more specifically as fir, linden, elderberry, dog, grass snake, swallow, sparrow, and so on. The typically apprehended item also has a horizon of possible experience with corresponding acquaintance-predelineations — a typology of as yet unexperienced but expected features. We see a dog and immediately anticipate its further behavior: its typical way of eating, playing, running, jumping.

**Paragraph 2.** Every typically apprehended thing can lead us to the general concept of the type in which we apprehend it. But we need not be directed toward the universal in this way; we can be directed toward the thing as an individual, while the passively preconstituted relation to its type remains unthematic. But at any time we can form a general concept on this basis.

#### b) Essential and Non-Essential Types. Scientific Experience Leads to the Exhibition of the Essential Types

**Paragraph 3.** Among the passively preconstituted typical universalities, there are those where no such openly endless typical horizon of further features attaches to the initially determining features. Immediate experience often sorts things by conspicuous differences that can conceal an actually obtaining inner belonging-together (as the example of the "whale" shows — classified by external analogy with fish, though it is a mammal). We speak in such cases of non-essential types. In comprehensive experience, individuals order themselves increasingly under essential types. This is the concern of empirical-scientific, natural-historical research. The scientific species-concepts seek, through systematic and methodical experience, to determine the essential types.

### §84. Levels of Universality

#### a) The Concrete Universality as the Universal of the Repetition of Completely Equal Individuals. Self-Standing and Abstract, Substantive and Adjective Universalities

**Paragraph 1.** The typical universalities under which the experienced is ordered are of different levels. Starting from the experience of individual objects, the lowest universal — the one we genetically most originally encounter first — is that which arises through the mere "repetition" of completely equal self-standing individuals. We call it the *concretum*. This universal of repetition of self-standing objects is the lowest universality, as a universality the most self-standing — not founded in other universalities.

**Paragraph 2.** The lowest concrete universalities found other universalities — those of their abstract moments, which of course also yield a universal of repetition, but a non-self-standing one: the lowest non-self-standing, abstract species. As universalities with an extension of originally non-self-standing instances, they are themselves originally adjective universalities. We contrast them with the originally self-standing universalities as substantive universalities.

#### b) The Higher-Level Universalities as Universalities on the Basis of Mere Similarity

**Paragraph 3.** When the equality of the extension-members of a universality is no longer complete equality, higher-level universalities arise. Complete equality was grasped as the limit of similarity. In the transition from similar to similar, a coincidence occurs that is not full coincidence; the overlapping similarity-members have a distance. Levels of universality are thus conditioned both by the magnitude of the similarity-distances and by the number of similar moments — the degree of approach to total similarity.

### §85. Material and Formal Universalities

**Paragraph 1.** A further important distinction is that between material (*sachhaltige*) and formal universalities. Depending on whether the compared objects are objects of plain experience (not yet syntactically formed) or syntactic formations, two fundamentally different kinds of universality arise. Material universalities arise from the comparison of content-filled objects and their material features. Formal universalities arise from the comparison of syntactic forms — the forms that objects take on through predicative activity. Concepts like equality, difference, unity, plurality, set, whole, part, object, property — all so-called purely logical concepts and all concepts expressible in the manifold of state-of-affairs forms and sentence forms — are pure form-concepts, formal universalities.

### Chapter II. The Gaining of Pure Universalities through the Method of Essence-Intuition

### §86. Contingency of Empirical Universalities and A Priori Necessity

**Paragraph 1.** Empirical universalities have an extension of actual and really possible instances. Their unity is "contingent" — a contingently given instance was the starting-point of concept-formation, and it led through equally contingent equalities and similarities. The counter-concept of this contingency is a priori necessity. Pure concepts must be formed whose formation does not depend on the contingency of the factually given starting-member. They must be able to prescribe rules in advance to all empirical instances. In the case of pure concepts, the infinity of the factual continuability is evidently given, precisely because they prescribe rules to experience in advance and thereby exclude a reversal or cancellation.

### §87. The Method of Essence-Intuition

#### a) Free Variation as the Basis of Essence-Intuition

**Paragraph 1.** For the gaining of pure concepts or essential concepts, empirical comparison cannot suffice. The universal initially standing out in the empirically given must above all be freed from its character of contingency. This achievement rests on the transformation of an experienced or phantasied objectuality into an arbitrary example that at the same time receives the character of the guiding "model" — the starting-member for the production of an openly endless manifold of variants, hence a variation. We let ourselves be guided by the fact as model for its reshaping in pure phantasy. Ever new similar images are to be gained as copies — phantasy-images that are all concrete similarities of the original. We freely and arbitrarily produce variants, each of which, as well as the whole process of variation, appears in the subjective experiential mode of the "arbitrary." It then shows itself that through this manifold of re-shapings, a unity runs through — that in such free variations of an original, an invariant is necessarily maintained as the necessary general form without which something of this kind as an example of its sort would be entirely unthinkable. This is a general essence (*allgemeines Wesen*). We can direct our gaze upon it as the necessarily invariant that prescribes the limits to all variation exercised in the mode of the "arbitrary."

#### b) The Arbitrariness-Shape of the Process of Variant-Formation

**Paragraph 2.** That the *eidos* is referred to a freely arbitrarily further producible manifold of variants brought to coincidence does not mean that an actual proceeding into infinity would be required, an actual production of all variants. What matters is that the variation as a process of variant-formation itself have an arbitrariness-shape — that the process be carried out in the consciousness of arbitrary further-formation of variants. Then, even if we break off, we have not meant the factual manifold as this factual series of somehow presenting themselves or arbitrarily drawn-upon objects; rather, to the variation-manifold essentially belongs the remarkable and extraordinarily important consciousness of "and so on at will." Through this alone is given what we call an "openly infinite" manifold.

#### c) The Holding-in-Grasp of the Whole Variation-Manifold as the Basis of Essence-Intuition

**Paragraph 3.** Upon this manifold — upon the foundation of the constituting open process of variation with the variants actually entering into intuition — there grounds itself as a higher level the proper intuiting of the universal as *eidos*. In the ongoing coincidence, a selfsame thing congruates that can now be purely discerned for itself. It is as such passively preconstituted, and the intuiting of the *eidos* rests in the active intuitive apprehension of what is so preconstituted. The presupposition for this is that the manifold as such, as plurality, is conscious and never entirely released from grasp. Only so do we gain the *eidos* as ideally identical — which only is as *hen epi pollōn* (one over many).

#### d) The Relation of Essence-Intuition to the Experience of Individuals. The Error of the Abstraction-Doctrine

**Paragraph 4.** The distinctive character of essence-intuition becomes clearer when contrasted with the intuitive experience of individual objects. Compared with the specific freedom in variation, in every experience of the individual there obtains a quite determinate binding. Everything we further experience must be brought into a nexus of consistency if it is to count as an object for us. In essence-intuition, we are not bound by the conditions of consistency in the same way. The old abstraction-doctrine — that the universal constitutes itself only on the ground of individually single intuitions through abstraction — is thus partly unclear, partly incorrect. What underlies essence-intuition is not a seen individual in the proper sense but rather a peculiar unity — an "individual" in the alternation of "non-essential" constitutive moments.

#### e) Congruence and Difference in the Overlapping Coincidence of Variation-Manifolds

**Paragraph 5.** With the congruence in the coincidence of the variation-manifolds, manifold difference is on the other side connected. In summarizing, the three main steps belonging to the process of ideation are: (1) productive running-through of the manifold of variations; (2) unitary connection in continuous coincidence; (3) discerning active identification of the congruating over against the differences. Every difference in the overlapping with other conflicting differences points to a new universal to be discerned — the universal of the elements that have come to the unity of conflict.

#### f) Variation and Alteration

**Paragraph 6.** Variation and alteration must be clearly distinguished. An alteration is always alteration of something real, something temporal, something enduring through a duration. Every real thing is alterable and only is in alteration or non-alteration. In variation, by contrast, we let the identity of the individual fall away and re-imagine it into other possible individuals. Yet the phases of alteration can also, in a changed gaze-direction, be treated as variants. Then it comes forth that no alteration is possible in which the alteration-phases do not generically belong together: a color can only change into another color and not, say, into a tone. Every possible alteration proceeds within a highest genus that it can never overstep.

### §88. The Sense of the Talk of "Intuiting" Universalities

**Paragraph 1.** "Intuiting" is used here in the quite broad sense that means nothing other than self-experiencing, having seen-for-oneself things, and on the basis of this self-seeing having the similarity before one's eyes, and thereupon carrying out that spiritual overlapping in which the common — the red, the figure, etc. — "itself" comes forth and comes to intuitive apprehension. This is of course not a sensory seeing. The universal red cannot be seen as one sees an individual, single red. But the extension of the language of seeing, not for nothing customary in general usage, is unavoidable.

### §89. The Necessity of an Express Exclusion of All Positings of Being for the Gaining of Pure Universality

**Paragraph 1.** One might think that our description of essence-intuition makes its task too difficult and unnecessarily operates with variation-manifolds and the peculiar functions of phantasy involved. But the "arbitrary" in such talk must not be mere talk or signify an incidental behavior on our part; it belongs to the fundamental character of the act of ideation itself. Even the universal gained through variation need not yet be pure in the proper sense. Although through the variation the relation to the contingent, actually existing starting-example is already excluded, a relation to actuality can still attach to the universal. Only when we become conscious of this binding and consciously put it out of play — freeing even the widest surrounding horizon of the variants from all binding, all experiential validity — do we create complete purity. We then stand in a pure phantasy-world, a world of absolutely pure possibilities.

### §90. Pure Universality and A Priori Necessity

**Paragraph 1.** Pure universalities have an extension of pure possibilities. Yet they also have a relation to empirical actuality, insofar as they "prescribe rules" to every actual thing. Every actual color must have the moment of brightness that belongs inseparably to the pure *eidos* color. The general truths in which we merely unfold what belongs to pure essential universalities precede all questions about facts and their factual truths. Therefore the essential truths are called a priori — preceding all factuality, all determinations from experience, in their validity. From every concrete actuality the path into the realm of ideal or pure possibility stands open, and therewith into that of a priori thinking. The method of fashioning pure possibilities and the intuitive formation of the associated pure essential universalities, "ideas" (essences, pure concepts), and essential laws is everywhere the same.

### §91. The Extension of Pure Universalities

#### a) The Totality of the Pure Conceptual Extension Offers No Individual Differentiation

**Paragraph 1.** Pure universalities have an extension of pure possibilities. The totality of this extension offers no individuation of the species. Individual differentiation exists only so far as a "world" reaches — actual individual differentiation in an actual world, possible individual differentiation in a possible world.

#### b) Possibility-Differentiation and Actuality-Differentiation

**Paragraph 2.** For every essence we must therefore distinguish: (1) the possibility-differentiation — the differentiation in the form of disconnected possibilities, referred back to disconnectedly giving phantasies or also experiences; (2) the differentiation in the framework of a possible actuality, whose form is a time. The totality of free possibilities in general is a realm of disconnectedness. Yet in it, every singled-out possibility simultaneously designates the idea of a totality of connected possibilities, and to this totality there necessarily corresponds a time. Every such totality defines a world. Two such worlds, however, are disconnected.

### §92. The Level-Structure of Pure Universalities and the Gaining of the Highest Concrete Genera (Regions) through Variation of Ideas

**Paragraph 1.** Pure universalities of quite different levels have already stood out in our investigations. The essences that contain within themselves necessity-laws for an entire object-sphere are distinguished from lowest species such as the *eidos* red. Just as with empirical universality, a level-structure is to be found, ascending from lower universalities to ever higher ones. From one and the same example as guiding image, one can arrive at very different pure essences through free variation. From red, we arrive at the *eidos* red; but by dropping the restriction to red and admitting green, yellow, etc. into the variation, we arrive at the *eidos* color as such. Thus we can proceed to the constitution of highest essential universalities as highest genera. Ideas, pure universalities, can themselves function as variants; from them, in a higher level, a universal is to be discerned — an idea from ideas or of ideas. This leads ultimately to the concept of regions (*Regionen*) — the highest concrete genera, which set an insurmountable limit to all variation. A fundamental concept of a region cannot be transformed into another through variation. Only formalization remains possible, through which they are both grasped under the formal category of "something in general."

### §93. The Difficulties of Gaining Highest Genera, Shown at the Gaining of the Region "Thing"

#### a) The Method of Producing the Example to Be Varied

**Paragraph 1.** The gaining of a highest concrete genus is not as simple as it might seem. Already the production of the example to be varied has its difficult method. If we start from a perceptual object, it is given to us in perception "originally," but in principle only incompletely; we must first obtain a full intuition of this thing through progressive experience. But we cannot set actual experience into play infinitely; what we gain is always "one-sidedly" and incompletely self-given, surrounded by a presumptive horizon. The Difficulty lies in the fact that the thing meant in the exemplary starting-intuition contains its "infinities" only implicitly, interwoven with a manifold of relativities — including those of normality and abnormality, of solipsistic proper-essence and causal connection.

#### b) The Problem of Gaining the Full Concretion. Abstract and Concrete Essential Contemplation

**Paragraph 2.** All underlying essential contemplation that remains stuck in an unexplicit relativity is not without results, but it is abstract and, as regards the sense of its result, marked by an incompleteness that has its great dangers. Only when all relativities are laid out and drawn into the essential contemplation does the idea of the regional essence of a thing as such arise — now in the context of an openly endless nature as such, further of a possible concrete world as such in relation to a subject-community as such whose open environment it is. Only then do we obtain an essential insight in full concretion.

### Chapter III. Judgments in the Mode of the *Überhaupt* (In-General)

### §94. Transition to the Consideration of the *Überhaupt*-Modifications of Judging as the Highest Level of Spontaneous Achievements

**Paragraph 1.** Concept-formation not only creates new objectualities and new forms of states of affairs; in one with the universal, there also arises the specifically so-called general judging — the *Überhaupt*-modification of judging. These show themselves as syntactic formations of an entirely new style, presupposing concept-formation and the constitution of general objectualities, extending to all conceivable forms of objects and states of affairs. It is a matter of the highest level of spontaneous achievements, which from the axiological standpoint are also the highest, the highest in cognitive value. In them lies everything scientific in the pregnant sense of all science.

### §95. The Origin of the *Überhaupt*-Modification from the Becoming-Indifferent of the Individual "This-Heres"

**Paragraph 1.** How are these new judgment-forms to be understood? They are not necessarily already given with the spontaneous constitution of general objectualities. General objects can enter as cores into judgments without the judgment itself undergoing the *Überhaupt*-modification. But whenever general terms appear in a judgment, a relation between individuality and universality is preconstituted in them. When this relation becomes thematic, *Überhaupt*-modifications arise.

**Paragraph 2.** The example: we see a rose in a garden and can consider it as an individual this-here. But another direction of interest is possible: the gaze can run through the chains of equality, and the individual differences can become "indifferent." There constitutes itself a form of meaning the individual in which it is regarded only according to what grounds the equal-validity: each only as "some A," as "a" rose, and not at all as this particular rose with these and these further individual determinations. What is indifferent exceeds the meaning expressed in the form "an A." We judge no longer "this rose is yellow" but "a rose (in this range) is yellow."

### §96. The Particular Judgment

#### a) The Particular Judgment as Inexistential Judgment. Particularity and the Number-Concept

**Paragraph 1.** On the basis of this transformation of interest, something new has formed in two respects. On the one hand, in the transition from equal to equal in this new attitude, the forms "an A," "an A and an A," "an A and another," etc. have arisen. We stand thereby at the origin of the primitive number-forms. On the other hand, new judgment-modifications have formed — the particular judgments, e.g., "a rose is yellow." They are thoroughly distinguished from singular judgments that refer to individually determined terms. The domain of particular judgments can also be an openly infinite one. In their sense, an inexistence is implicitly contained: they are inexistential judgments.

#### b) The Particular Judgment as Modification of the Determinate Judgment

**Paragraph 2.** The simplest form "some A is B" does not properly create a new objectuality of which B would be the predicate. "Some rose" is not a new object of which yellowness is asserted in the way it is asserted of some determinate thing. Rather, the particular formation designates a thought-operation originally to be exercised upon individually determinate states of affairs at their determinate terms.

#### c) Particular Phantasy-Judgments as A Priori Existential Judgments

**Paragraph 3.** If we transpose the judging and the judged states of affairs into pure phantasy, we gain new particularities — with reference to the fact that all transformation in the mode of the as-if yields its own forms, in which pure possibilities accrue to us from imagined actualities. All mathematical existential sentences have this modified sense: "there are" triangles, quadrilaterals, polygons — the true sense is not simply "there are" but "it is a priori possible that there are." They are in truth existential judgments about possibilities — about the possibility that there are such things.

### §97. The Universal Judgment

#### a) The Origin of the Universal *Überhaupt* from the Particular Modification

**Paragraph 1.** Starting from judging in a sphere of actuality: we find experientially and conceptually that this A is B, and in progressive cognition we always again find another A and always again find that it is B. In this progression, with each new case an ever stronger presumption arises. We now make the posit of some A, referred to this open sphere — but not in a merely particular attitude. The item posited as "some A" we grasp at the same time in the form "any one whatsoever," any one, arbitrarily which, from this open sphere. As soon as we form this thought of the universal "any one," a necessity of being-B attaches to it in its universality. Any one whatsoever is as such necessarily B.

**Paragraph 2.** The new thing here: we have not merely singled out "some one" in this particular form from the surveyed chain of anticipated A's, but the A just singled out is one for which any other of the chain could be substituted at will. It is, as it were, representative of an arbitrary one in general. This "an arbitrary one in general" is an entirely new and non-self-standing form — belonging to an entirely new sense-form of states of affairs and judgments that in this new sense refers back to plainly predicating possible judgments.

#### b) The Totality-Judgment

**Paragraph 3.** We have finally to mention a very essential turning of the original universal thought — the totality-thought and the totality-judgment (*Allheitsurteil*). If we form the collection "some A and some other A, etc." and determine it further through the thought that every A whatsoever shall belong to it, we gain the thought of the totality. "All A are B" says the plural judgment of totality, equivalent to "every A of the totality is B" — a logically superfluous complication of the simple thought "every A is B."

#### c) The Gaining of A Priori Necessities in the Universal Phantasy-Judgment

**Paragraph 4.** When we turn to universal phantasy-judgments, we find an immediate difference from universal judgments in the domain of actuality: in the latter, the generality is empirical-inductive generality, with the "empirical" or presumptive necessity belonging to it. In pure phantasy, proceeding from the a priori formed thought "a tone" and analyzing any intuitive tone-example, we find quality, intensity, and timbre. Freely varying, we recognize that every possible tone, any conceivable one whatsoever, encloses within itself a possible intensity, quality, and timbre. In the universal attitude, in which we think a tone in general, we also think as enclosed therein its having-intensity, its having-quality, its having-timbre. The universal state of affairs in the realm of a priori possibility obtains: as the concrete concept contains its part-concepts, so every possible state of affairs that something is a tone encloses the state of affairs that this selfsame individual has intensity, quality, and timbre. We can also carry out a formal abstraction and gain a formal law.

### §98. Summary

**Paragraph 1.** Husserl surveys what has been gained and fixes the terminology. The starting case was the original categorical judgment, in which an individual subject is itself and directly apprehended. Through all its modifications, individual objects pass as original terms, and their conceptual predicates are themselves originally material predicates. All such judgments, however complex, are called singular judgments. Their terms are called singular terms.

**Paragraph 2.** Three levels of judgment emerge: (1) Singular judgments — those having only singular terms, with the simplest categorical judgment "this is red" as the lowest. (2) Particular judgments — where any singular term can be particularized, making the whole sense of the judgment particular. (3) Universal judgments — any judgment having at least one universal term. Particularity and universality in the same judgment do not exclude each other.

**Paragraph 3.** A different distinction is that between individual and general judgments — the terms in the categorical judgments need not be only individual objects but can also be general objectualities. Nonetheless the judgment can be an unmodified, singular one. Accordingly, each can undergo the *Überhaupt*-modifications: there are individual universal judgments and general universal judgments.

**Paragraph 4.** Yet another distinction, crossing with both of the foregoing, is that between single judgments and plural judgments — judgments with one or several subjects or predicates. It crosses with both of the foregoing: for example, every individual universal judgment can be a single or a plural judgment, and conversely, a singular general judgment can be a plural one, and so on in arbitrary combinations.

## Appendix I (to §§40 and 42). The Apprehension of a Content as "Fact" and the Origin of Individuality. Time-Modes and Judgment-Modes

**Paragraph 1.** "The same" object that I am just phantasying could also be given in experience — this same merely possible object could also be an actual object. Conversely, of every actual object I can say it need not have been actual; it would then be "mere possibility." "The same object" does not mean: the object simpliciter (for in speaking of an object simpliciter we posit it as actual). Rather, it is a matter of an identically discernible content — a "full sense" — that lies in the experiencing consciousness (with the experience-character "actual") as well as in the corresponding phantasy-consciousness (with the character "phantasied," quasi-actual). This "full sense" is the individual essence of the respective object. It encompasses the on-both-sides identical time-duration and distribution of the time-fullness over this duration.

**Paragraph 2.** But in what sense is this "individual essence" a universal? It is in the coincidence of the object posited as "actual" and that posited as "quasi-actual" separated, yet in the case of their complete equality becoming one. If we set completely equal items opposite one another: an identical universal singularizes itself here and there, as individual actuality or as individual possibility.

**Paragraph 3.** The appendix then turns to the origin of individuality in time-consciousness. Every new original present that lights up is a new actual "positing" with a "content." In the flow of presentation — of the becoming of ever new present-points — the essentially identical content is conscious as continuously differentiating, as "new," as continuously other, although "content-wise" the same. As such it is originally conscious: here is the origin-point of individuality, factuality, the distinction in existence. The most original having, or apprehension, of a content as fact and of a distinguished content as distinguished fact accomplishes itself in the actuality of the original presentation — in the consciousness of the originary present of the content. It is conscious in the mode of Now, "present content," and in this mode individual, unique of this content.

**Paragraph 4.** The identity of the time-position and of the one time as a one-dimensional linear continuum is then elaborated through the two-dimensional continuum of the ever-streaming pasts with its one single source-point of the momentary present. Every time-point is the form of the identity of the same existence that continuously constitutes itself through the system of pasts streaming from the same source-point "Now." Through its position in time, every individual receives a determination that concerns its existence, its factuality as such.

**Paragraph 5.** The appendix concludes by raising the question whether the time-modes (present, past) can be related to the judgment, to the original doxa, as correlates of modes of judging. And correlatively: do those time-modes designate existence-modes? The originary consciousness is a source-consciousness from which the manifold modifications arise — acts that all "coincide" with the originary consciousness, that all "believe" the same, that are all being-consciousness of the same. These modifications are everywhere the same. The temporal modalities — present, past, future — are modes of the existent (*Daseienden*), of the individually being as temporally being.

## Appendix II (to §76). The Evidence of the Probability-Claim. Critique of the Humean Conception

**Paragraph 1.** The clarification of the relations of suggestion (*Anmutung*) and presumption (or of real possibility and probability) has great significance for the question of the legitimacy of grounding assertions about the future through past experiences — especially causal inferences. Hume first made this problem the subject of a comprehensive investigation, and he ends with skepticism. He finds no possibility of justifying even the slightest causal question, let alone any law of nature or the principle of the lawful unity of nature.

**Paragraph 2.** On one side stands the domain of relations of ideas, where relations are inseparably connected with the relata and can be grasped in essential generality through generalizing abstraction. On the other side stands the domain of matters of fact, where the causal relation is not a relation like that of higher or lower in qualities. Here, according to Hume, no room exists for a generalizing abstraction from the single case to the universality, and the denial of any causal relation implies not the slightest absurdity.

**Paragraph 3.** It is easy to see that this — like every — skepticism entangles itself in evident absurdity: if experience-judgments admit of no justification, then no psychological explanation is possible either, for psychology is itself an experiential science resting on the very principles whose untenability it is supposed to uncover. Hume himself did not overlook this circle; precisely for that reason he calls himself a skeptic.

**Paragraph 4.** Hume must fail here because he has not clarified for himself the essence of purely phenomenological analysis as distinguished from psychological analysis, and in connection with this, because he has not brought to clarity the essence of the rational justification possible in the domain of relations between ideas. The rationality there consists in nothing other than that we can elevate the relational laws into the adequate generality-consciousness, bring to clarity the sense of such general evidence, and then further recognize that the objective validity of the laws themselves consists in the ideal possibility of such an adequate general consciousness. One must seek the analogue in the domain of experience-judgments asserting general and necessity-connections.

**Paragraph 5.** Hume should have started from the evidence: the fact that under the circumstances U a W occurred in itself already lends something like weight to the claim "it occurs in general under circumstances U that W occurs," and this weight grows with the number of experienced cases. Husserl then demonstrates, using Hume's own dice example, that the distribution of probability across equally possible cases can be grasped with evidence. Four faces carry a figure, two are blank; the probability of the figure appearing is 4/6 versus 2/6 for a blank face — this is evidently justified.

**Paragraph 6.** In such considerations, no talk is made of the "mind" of the human being or of effects on the basis of empirical-psychological lawfulness. Rather, we simply look at the given, at the peculiar relation of motivation, at the experienceable character that the general assumption receives through the weight of prior experiences, and we carry out here — exactly as in the domain of relations between ideas — an ideating abstraction in which we intuit the relevant principle of probability in essential necessity. Every probability-claim is justified — a correct probability-claim — if it can be measured against the originary and proper empiry, if the originary motivational force of the intuitive state-of-things essentially belonging to it can be experienced. Since essential-lawful relations are at issue, a principle can be formulated: an empirical claim is justified if it is to be grounded through such a principle, that is, if the principle guarantees the ideal possibility of its verification.
