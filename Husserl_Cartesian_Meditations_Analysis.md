# Cartesian Meditations: An Introduction to Phenomenology

## A Paragraph-by-Paragraph Analysis of Edmund Husserl's *Cartesianische Meditationen*

---

## Introduction

### § 1. Descartes' Meditations as the Prototype of Philosophical Self-Reflection

Husserl opens with a direct address to his French audience, expressing his pleasure at speaking about transcendental phenomenology at this most venerable site of French science. He immediately situates his project in relation to Descartes, identifying France's greatest thinker as the one who gave phenomenology new impulses through his *Meditations*. He claims that the study of Descartes' work directly contributed to the transformation of the already emerging phenomenology into a new form of transcendental philosophy. There is a striking paradox at work here: phenomenology could almost be called a neo-Cartesianism, yet precisely through the radical unfolding of Cartesian motifs, it is compelled to reject nearly the entire known doctrinal content of Cartesian philosophy. Husserl thus signals from the outset that his relationship to Descartes is one of radicalization through transformation, not of doctrinal inheritance.

In the second paragraph, Husserl addresses his audience directly, expressing confidence that they will share his interest as he takes up those motifs of the *Meditationes de Prima Philosophia* that he believes possess an eternal significance, and as he characterizes the transformations and new formations from which the transcendental-phenomenological method and problematic spring. The phrase "Ewigkeitsbedeutung" (eternal significance) is crucial: Husserl believes that certain structural features of the Cartesian project — specifically, the radical return to the thinking subject and the demand for absolute foundations — are not historically contingent but belong to the permanent essence of philosophy itself.

The third paragraph recapitulates the well-known leading idea of Descartes' *Meditations* for the benefit of any beginning philosopher. The goal is a complete reform of philosophy into a science grounded in absolute foundations. For Descartes, this entails a corresponding reform of all sciences, since they are merely dependent members of the one universal science, which is philosophy. Only in their systematic unity can they become genuine sciences. But as they have historically developed, they lack this genuineness — the thoroughgoing and ultimate grounding in absolute insights, insights behind which one cannot go further. A radical rebuilding is therefore required, one that does justice to the idea of philosophy as a universal unity of sciences in the unity of such rational grounding.

This demand for rebuilding takes effect in Descartes as a subjectively oriented philosophy, and this subjective turn is carried out in two significant stages. First, anyone who seriously wishes to become a philosopher must, "once in his life," withdraw into himself and attempt within himself the overthrow of all the sciences that have hitherto held good for him, and attempt their reconstruction. Philosophy — wisdom (*sagesse*) — is an entirely personal affair of the philosophizing subject. It is to become his wisdom, his self-acquired knowledge striving universally, which he can justify from the very beginning and at every step from his own absolute insights. Having resolved to pursue this goal — the resolution that alone can bring one to philosophical becoming — one has thereby chosen the beginning of absolute cognitive poverty. The first task in this condition is to reflect on how one might find a method of progress that could lead to genuine knowledge.

The Cartesian *Meditations* thus do not wish to be a merely private affair of the philosopher Descartes, let alone a merely impressive literary form for a presentation of first philosophical foundations. They rather delineate the prototype of the necessary meditations of every beginning philosopher, from which alone a philosophy can originally grow. Husserl here makes a universal claim: the Cartesian meditative form is not a historical contingency but a structural requirement for any genuine philosophical beginning. A footnote cites Descartes' own "Lettre de l'auteur" to the translator of the *Principia* in support of this interpretation.

Turning to what strikes us today as the strange content of the *Meditations*, Husserl summarizes how Descartes effects a return to the philosophizing ego in a second and deeper sense — to the ego of the pure *cogitationes*. The meditator carries out this return through the well-known and very remarkable method of doubt. Refusing, in radical consistency with the goal of absolute knowledge, to let anything count as existing that is not preserved against every conceivable possibility of becoming doubtful, Descartes undertakes a methodical critique of what is held as certain in the natural life of experience and thought, with respect to its possibilities of doubt. He seeks, by excluding everything that leaves open possibilities of doubt, to win an eventual stock of the absolutely evident. In this method, the sensory certainty of experience, in which the world is given in natural life, does not withstand critique; accordingly, the being of the world must at this initial stage remain out of play. The meditator retains only himself, as the pure ego of his *cogitationes*, as absolutely indubitable — as irremovable even if this world were not. The thus reduced ego now carries out a kind of solipsistic philosophizing. It seeks apodictically certain paths by which, within its pure interiority, an objective exteriority can be disclosed. This takes place in the well-known manner: first, God's existence and *veracitas* are inferred, and then by means of these, objective nature, the dualism of finite substances — in short, the objective ground of metaphysics and the positive sciences. All inferences proceed, as they must, guided by principles that are immanent to the pure ego, innate in it.

### § 2. The Necessity of a Radical New Beginning in Philosophy

Husserl now asks whether it is worthwhile to trace the eternal significance of these Cartesian thoughts, and whether they are still capable of instilling living forces in our time. He immediately raises a concern: the positive sciences, which were supposed to receive an absolutely rational grounding through these meditations, have paid so little attention to them. Although in our time these sciences feel hampered by unclarities in their foundations after a brilliant development of three centuries, it does not occur to them, in their attempted reformulations of their foundations, to go back to the Cartesian *Meditations*.

On the other hand, it weighs heavily that the *Meditations* made epoch in philosophy in a quite unique sense, precisely through their return to the pure *ego cogito*. Indeed, Descartes inaugurates a wholly new kind of philosophy: changing its entire style, it takes a radical turn from naive objectivism to transcendental subjectivism, which in ever new and yet ever inadequate attempts seems to strive toward a necessary final form. Should this ongoing tendency not carry within it an eternal meaning — a great task, imposed upon us by history itself, in which we are all called to collaborate?

Husserl now turns to a sweeping diagnosis of the contemporary situation in philosophy. The fragmentation of present-day philosophy in its helpless busyness gives us cause to think. Since the middle of the previous century, when we try to view Western philosophy from the standpoint of the unity of a science, the decline is unmistakable compared with preceding times. In goal-setting, in problematic, and in method, this unity has been lost. When at the beginning of the modern era religious faith increasingly externalized itself into lifeless convention, intellectual humanity raised itself up on a new great faith — faith in an autonomous philosophy and science. The entire culture of humanity was to be led and illuminated by scientific insights and thereby reformed into a new autonomous culture.

But in the meantime, this faith too has fallen into inauthenticity and stunted growth, and not without reason. Instead of a unified and vital philosophy, we have a literature growing boundlessly but almost without coherence. Instead of a serious confrontation of conflicting theories — theories that in their very conflict attest to their inner belongingness, their shared basic convictions, and an unswerving faith in a true philosophy — we have merely the appearance of reporting and criticizing, a mere semblance of serious philosophizing with and for one another. There is nothing less manifest in this than a responsible mutual study in the spirit of serious collaboration and of an aim toward objectively valid results. But how could genuine study and genuine collaboration be possible where there are so many philosophers and almost as many philosophies? We still have philosophical congresses, Husserl observes wryly — the philosophers come together, but unfortunately not the philosophies. They lack the unity of a spiritual space in which they could be for one another, could act upon one another.

Husserl then asks whether we are not, in this wretched present, in a situation similar to that which Descartes found in his youth. Is it not time to renew his radicalism of the beginning philosopher — to subject the unmanageable philosophical literature, with its tangle of great traditions, more serious fresh starts, and fashionable literary activity (which counts on making an impression, but not on study) to a Cartesian overthrow, and to begin with new *Meditationes de Prima Philosophia*? Is not the hopelessness of our philosophical situation ultimately traceable to the fact that the driving forces radiating from those *Meditations* have lost their original vitality — and lost it because the spirit of radicalism of philosophical self-responsibility has been lost? Should not the supposedly exaggerated demand for a philosophy aimed at ultimate conceivable freedom from prejudice — a philosophy that shapes itself in genuine autonomy from ultimately self-produced evidences and is from there absolutely self-responsible — belong rather to the fundamental sense of genuine philosophy?

The longing for a vital philosophy has led in recent times to various renaissances. Should not the only fruitful renaissance be precisely that which reawakens the Cartesian *Meditations* — not to take them over, but to disclose first of all the deepest sense of their radicalism in the return to the *ego cogito*, and in further consequence, the eternal values that spring from there? At any rate, Husserl says, this marks the path that has led to transcendental phenomenology.

Husserl concludes the Introduction by inviting his audience to travel this path together. Cartesianly, as radically beginning philosophers, they will carry out meditations, naturally with the utmost critical caution and with readiness for every necessary transformation of the old Cartesian ones. Seductive errors into which Descartes and his successors fell must be clarified and avoided.

## First Meditation: The Way to the Transcendental Ego

### § 3. The Cartesian Overthrow and the Guiding Teleological Idea of a Grounding of Science

Husserl begins the First Meditation by enacting the Cartesian beginning: each of us starts anew, each for himself and within himself, with the resolve of a radically beginning philosopher, to set out of play all convictions that have hitherto held good, and among them all our sciences. The guiding idea of these meditations, as for Descartes, is that of a science to be grounded in radical genuineness, and ultimately a universal science. But since we possess no given science as an example of such genuineness — none stands in validity for us — how does it stand with the indubitability of this idea itself, the idea of an absolutely grounded science? Does it designate a legitimate teleological idea, a possible goal of a possible praxis? We may not presuppose even this, let alone hold in advance any norms of such possibilities as settled, or even a supposedly self-evident style-form that genuine science as such must possess. For that would ultimately mean presupposing an entire logic as a theory of science, whereas logic too must be included in the overthrow of all science.

Husserl notes that Descartes himself had in advance a scientific ideal, that of geometry or mathematical natural science. This ideal determines as a fateful prejudice the centuries and determines, critically unexamined, the *Meditations* themselves. It was for Descartes a self-evidence from the start that universal science must have the form of a deductive system, in which the entire edifice rests upon an axiom-founding foundation. A role analogous to that of the geometrical axioms in geometry is played for Descartes, with respect to universal science, by the axiom of the absolute self-certainty of the ego together with the axiomatic principles innate in this ego — except that this axiomatic foundation lies even deeper than that of geometry and is moreover destined to participate in geometry's own ultimate grounding. Husserl insists that none of this must determine us. As beginners, we have no normative ideal of science in force; only insofar as we create it anew can we possess one.

But we do not therefore abandon the general goal of absolute scientific grounding. It is to motivate the course of our meditations, as it did the Cartesian ones, and to take on concrete determinateness step by step within them. Only we must be careful about the manner in which we posit it as a goal — we must not even prejudge its possibility. But how is this manner of positing a goal to be clarified and thereby secured?

The general idea of science is naturally available to us from the factually given sciences. If, in our radical critical attitude, they have become merely presumed sciences, then their general teleological idea must likewise become merely a presumed one. We do not yet know whether it can be realized at all. Nevertheless, in this form of a presumption and in an indeterminate, fluid generality, we do have it, and with it the idea of a philosophy — as one whose realization is unknown whether and how possible. We take it as a provisional presumption to which we tentatively surrender ourselves, by which we tentatively let ourselves be guided in our meditations. We reflectively consider how it might be thought out as a possibility and how it might be brought to realization. We fall, admittedly, into initially bewildering complications — but how could they be avoided if our radicalism is not to remain an empty gesture but is to become a deed?

### § 4. Disclosure of the Teleological Sense of Science by "Living Into" It as a Noematic Phenomenon

Husserl states that the first thing will obviously be to clarify the leading idea that initially hovers before us in vague generality. This is not a matter of forming the concept of science through comparative abstraction on the basis of the actually existing sciences. The whole sense of our consideration implies that sciences as cultural facts and sciences in the true and genuine sense are not one and the same, or that the former carry within themselves, beyond their factuality, a pretension that is not attested as already fulfilled in mere factuality. It is precisely in this pretension that science as an idea lies — as the idea of genuine science.

How is this idea to be disclosed and grasped? Although no position-taking is permitted to us concerning the validity of the factual sciences — which are merely pretended sciences — nothing prevents us from living into their scientific striving and acting, and thus from making clear and distinct to ourselves what it ultimately aims at. If we thus enter with progressive depth into the intention of scientific striving, the constitutive moments of the general teleological idea of genuine science unfold for us, initially in a first differentiation.

Here belongs above all a first clarification of the activity of judging and of the judgment itself, with the distinction between immediate and mediate judgments: in mediate judgments there lies a sense-reference to other judgments, such that their judicative belief presupposes that of these others — in the manner of a belief on account of what is already believed. Further, there is the clarification of the striving for grounded judgments, of the activity of grounding, in which the correctness or truth of the judgment — or, in the case of failure, its incorrectness, its falsehood — is to be exhibited. This exhibition, in the case of mediate judgments, is itself mediate, resting on that of the immediate judgments contained in the judgment-sense and also concretely encompassing their grounding. A grounding once carried out, and the truth exhibited in it, can be returned to at will. By virtue of this freedom of re-actualization of the truth, which is conscious in the process as one and the same, truth is a lasting acquisition or possession, and as such it is called a cognition.

Proceeding further in this way — here naturally only in indications — we arrive presently, in the more precise interpretation of the sense of grounding and cognition, at the idea of evidence. In genuine grounding, judgments show themselves as correct, as in agreement — it is the agreement of the judgment with the matter of judgment (the thing or state of affairs) itself. More precisely, judging is a meaning and generally a mere presuming: it is such and such. The judgment (what is judged) is then a merely meant thing or state of affairs — a thing-meaning, a state-of-affairs meaning. But over against this there is, as the case may be, a distinguished kind of judicative meaning: it is called evidence. Instead of being a remote, merely propositional meaning about the thing, in evidence the thing is present as itself, the state of affairs as itself, and the judging subject is aware of the thing itself. A merely presumptive judging adjusts itself, through a conscious transference into the corresponding evidence, to the things, the states of affairs themselves. This transference carries within itself the character of fulfillment of the mere meaning, of a synthesis of concordant covering — it is evident awareness of the correctness of that previously remote meaning.

Doing this, fundamental components of the teleological idea governing all scientific activity at once emerge. For example, the scientist does not wish merely to judge but to ground his judgments. More precisely, he does not wish to let any judgment count as scientific cognition that he has not fully grounded and can then justify at any time and to the last detail by a freely possible return to the grounding that can be repeated. This may de facto remain at the stage of a mere pretension; but an ideal goal lies therein.

Yet one more thing must be added: we must distinguish judgment and evidence in the widest sense from pre-predicative judgment and pre-predicative evidence. Predicative evidence includes pre-predicative evidence. What is meant or evidently intuited comes to expression, and science wants generally to judge expressly, and to hold the judgment, the truth, fixed as something expressed. But expression as such has its own better or worse adaptation to what is meant and self-given, hence its own evidence or non-evidence that enters into predication; and thereby it also codetermines the idea of scientific truth as ultimately grounded and to-be-grounded predicative states of affairs.

### § 5. Evidence and the Idea of Genuine Science

Continuing to meditate in this way, we beginning philosophers recognize that the Cartesian idea of a science — and ultimately a universal science — out of absolute grounding and justification is nothing other than the idea that is the constantly guiding one in all sciences and in their striving for universality, however it may stand with its actual realization.

Evidence, in the very widest sense, is an experience of what is and of how it is — a mental seeing of the thing itself. Conflict with what evidence shows yields the negative of evidence (or negative evidence) and, as its content, evident falsehood. Evidence — to which indeed all experience in the usual narrower sense belongs — can be more or less perfect. Perfect evidence and its correlate, pure and genuine truth, is given as an idea residing in the striving for cognition, for fulfillment of the intending intention — an idea that can be drawn from living into such striving. Truth and falsehood, critique and critical adequation to evident givens, are an everyday theme, playing their constant role already in prescientific life. For this everyday life, with its changing and relative purposes, relative evidences and truths suffice. But science seeks truths that are valid once and for all and for everyone, and that remain valid; accordingly, it seeks verifications of a novel kind, carried through to the last. Even when science must concede, as it itself ultimately recognizes, that it does not de facto penetrate to a realization of a system of absolute truths and is compelled to modify its truths again and again, it nevertheless follows the idea of absolute or scientifically genuine truth and lives accordingly into an infinite horizon of approximations striving toward it.

There belongs to the idea of science and philosophy, in terms of intention, a knowledge-ordering from what is intrinsically earlier to what is intrinsically later knowledge; ultimately, not an arbitrarily chosen beginning and progress but one grounded in the nature of things themselves. In this way, through reflective living-into the general character of scientific striving, fundamental components of the teleological idea of genuine science — at first vaguely governing it — disclose themselves, without our having thereby prejudged either its possibility or a supposedly self-evident scientific ideal.

Husserl then warns against dismissing these investigations as belonging to general scientific theory or logic, which should be applied here as a matter of course. We must guard ourselves against precisely this self-evidence. As we have already said to Descartes: like all given sciences, logic too is set out of force by the general overthrow. We must first acquire for ourselves everything that has been achieved in the way of philosophical beginnings. Whether a genuine science of the type of traditional logic will later result for us, we cannot now know.

Through the preliminary work just carried out — more roughly indicated than explicitly executed — we have gained enough clarity to fix a first methodological principle for our entire further procedure. It is obvious that I, as a philosophically beginning person, in consequence of my striving toward the presumptive goal of genuine science, may not make or let stand any judgment that I have not drawn from evidence, from experiences in which the relevant things and states of affairs are present to me as themselves. Of course, I must also then at all times reflect on the respective evidence, weigh its range, and make evident to myself how far it reaches, how far its perfection — the genuine self-giving of the things — extends. Where it is still lacking, I may claim no finality, and may at best take the judgment into account as a possible intermediate stage on the way toward evidence.

Since the sciences aim at predications that express what is pre-predicatively intuited completely and in evident adaptation, provision must obviously be made also for this side of scientific evidence. Given the fluidity, ambiguity, and the all-too-great tolerance of ordinary language with respect to completeness of expression, what is needed — even when its means of expression are employed — is a re-grounding of meanings through original orientation to the scientifically obtained insights, and their fixation in these meanings. This too we reckon into our henceforth consistently normative methodological principle of evidence.

But what use would this principle and the entire meditation so far be if it offered us no means for making a real beginning — namely, for bringing the idea of genuine science into the course of realization? Since to this idea belongs the form of a systematic order of cognitions — genuine cognitions — the question of the beginning concerns the intrinsically first cognitions, which are to carry, and can carry, the entire structure of universal cognition. For us meditators, in our complete scientific cognitive poverty, evidences must accordingly be accessible that already carry the stamp of such a calling, insofar as they are recognizable as preceding all other conceivable evidences. But they must also carry with them, with regard to this evidence of precedence, a certain perfection, an absolute security, if from them a progress and construction of a science under the idea of a definitive cognition-system — given the infinity that presumably belongs to this idea — is to have a possible sense.

### § 6. Differentiations of Evidence. The Philosophical Demand for an Apodictic and Intrinsically First Evidence

At this decisive point of the beginning, we meditators must penetrate more deeply. The talk of absolute security or, equivalently, absolute indubitability requires clarification. It alerts us that the ideally demanded perfection of evidence, upon more precise interpretation, differentiates. At the present entry stage of philosophical meditation, we have the boundless endlessness of prescientific experiences, evidences more or less perfect. Imperfection here means, as a rule, incompleteness, one-sidedness, relative unclarity, indistinctness in the self-givenness of things or states of affairs, hence the encumbrance of experience with components of unfulfilled pre-meanings and co-meanings. Perfection then takes place as a synthetic progress of concordant experiences in which these co-meanings come to genuinely fulfilling experience. The corresponding idea of perfection would be that of adequate evidence, where it may remain open whether this lies in principle in the infinite.

Although this idea constantly guides the scientist's aim, there is nevertheless for the scientist a different kind of perfection of evidence that has a higher dignity — namely, that of apodicticity. This can occur even in evidences that are inadequate. It is an absolute indubitability in a quite definite and peculiar sense, the kind that the scientist demands of all principles, and whose superior value manifests itself in his effort to ground already evident foundations once again, at a higher level, by a return to principles, and thereby to bestow on them the highest dignity of apodicticity. The fundamental character of apodicticity is to be characterized as follows: every evidence is a grasping of something that is, or that is thus, in the mode of "it itself," in complete certainty of its being, which therefore excludes every doubt. But it does not thereby exclude the possibility that the evident might later become doubtful, that being might turn out to be semblance — examples of which are furnished by sensory experience. This open possibility of becoming doubtful, or of possible nonbeing despite the evidence, can also be recognized in advance, at any time, by a critical reflection on the evidence's performance. But an apodictic evidence has the signal distinction that it is not merely certainty of the being of the evident matters, but rather reveals itself through a critical reflection as the sheer inconceivability of their nonbeing; that it thus excludes in advance every imaginable doubt as objectless. The evidence of that critical reflection — and so also of the being of this inconceivability of the nonbeing of what lies before us in evident certainty — is again of this apodictic dignity, and likewise in every critical reflection of a higher level.

We now recall the Cartesian principle of absolute indubitability, which was to exclude every conceivable and even every de facto groundless doubt, as a principle for the construction of genuine science. If it has accrued to us through our meditation in clarified form, the question is now whether and how it can now help us to an actual beginning. In keeping with what was already said, the first determinate question of beginning philosophy takes this form: whether evidences can be exhibited for us that, as we must now say, apodictically carry with them the insight that they precede all conceivable evidences as intrinsically first, and for which it can at the same time be seen that they are themselves apodictic. Should they be inadequate, they would have to possess at least a recognizable apodictic content — a content of being that, by virtue of apodicticity, is secured once and for all, or absolutely. How, and whether, one can proceed further to an apodictically secured continuation of a philosophy must remain a *cura posterior*.

### § 7. The Evidence for the Existence of the World Is Not Apodictic; Its Inclusion in the Cartesian Overthrow

The question of intrinsically first evidences seems to be settled without difficulty. Does not the existence of a world offer itself without further ado as such an evidence? The everyday active life relates to the world; so too do all sciences — the factual sciences directly, the a priori sciences indirectly as instruments of method. The being of the world is above all self-evident — so much so that no one would think to utter it expressly in a proposition. We do indeed have the continuous experience in which this world stands constantly before our eyes as unquestionably existing. But however much this evidence is intrinsically earlier than all evidences of the world-directed life and of all world-sciences — whose constant supporting ground it is — we soon become doubtful as to how far it can lay claim to an apodictic character in this function. And when we pursue this doubt, it becomes apparent that it cannot claim the distinction of being the absolutely first evidence either.

Regarding the first point: the universal sensory experience, in whose evidence the world is constantly given to us, is obviously not to be taken straightway as an apodictic evidence that would absolutely exclude the possibility of becoming doubtful whether the world really is, or the possibility of its nonbeing. Not only can individual experiences undergo the devaluation of sensory semblance; the entire experienced coherence can turn out to be semblance under the title of a coherent dream. We need not take this reference to possible and actual reversals of evidence as already a sufficient critique of the evidence, or see in it a full proof of the conceivability of the world's nonbeing despite its constant experienced being. We retain only this much: the evidence of world-experience would first need a critique of its validity and range for purposes of a radical scientific grounding; we may therefore not take it as immediately apodictic without question. Accordingly, it is not enough to set out of force all given sciences and to treat them as inadmissible for us. Their universal ground, that of the experiential world, must also be deprived of its naive validity. The being of the world on the basis of natural experiential evidence must no longer be for us a self-evident fact, but itself only a validity-phenomenon.

Husserl then asks: if we hold to this, does any ground of being remain for any judgments at all, let alone for evidences, upon which to build — and indeed apodictically — a universal philosophy? Is not the world the title for the universe of what is, in general? Is it therefore unavoidable to take up the critique of world-experience, previously only sketched, now in extenso and as the first task? But what if the world were ultimately not the unconditionally first ground of judgment at all, and if with its existence an intrinsically earlier ground of being were already presupposed?

### § 8. The "Ego Cogito" as Transcendental Subjectivity

Here, following Descartes, we now make the great turning, which, if carried out in the right way, leads to transcendental subjectivity: the turn to the *ego cogito* as the apodictically certain and ultimate ground of judgment upon which every radical philosophy is to be founded.

As radically meditating philosophers, we now have neither a science valid for us, nor a world existing for us. Instead of simply being — that is, being valid for us in the natural way in the belief of experience — it is for us a mere claim to being. This also affects the intraworldly existence of all other egos, so that we properly ought no longer to speak in the communicative plural. Other human beings and animals are for me only experiential data by virtue of the sensory experience of their bodily bodies, whose validity, as itself in question, I may not employ. With the others, I naturally lose all the formations of sociality and culture. In short, not only corporeal nature but the entire concrete lifeworld is for me henceforth not existent but only a being-phenomenon.

But however it may stand with the reality-claim of this phenomenon, and however I may eventually decide critically for being or semblance, the phenomenon itself, as my phenomenon, is not nothing; it is precisely that which makes such a critical decision possible for me everywhere — and also possible whatever has for me the sense and validity of true being, of definitively decided or yet-to-be-decided being. And again: if I abstain from every experiential belief — so that the being of the experiential world remains out of force for me — this abstaining itself is what it is, and it is, together with the whole stream of experiencing life. And indeed it is for me constantly there, constantly conscious in terms of a present field perceptually in the most original originality, it as it itself; by way of memory, now these, now those past phases of the same become conscious again, and implicit in this: as the past phases themselves. At any time I can direct special attentive gazes reflectively upon this original life, grasp what is present as present, what is past as past, as how it itself is. So I now do as the philosophizing and abstention-exercising ego.

The world experienced in this reflective life remains, despite this, in a certain way for me further and exactly with the content at each time belonging to it, experienced as before. It continues to appear as it appeared before, only now I, as philosophically reflecting, no longer hold the natural belief of experience in force, in validity — though it is still there and grasped along with by the attentive glance. Likewise for all other kinds of meanings that belong to my lifestream beyond the experiencing ones: my non-intuitive representations, judgments, value-holdings, decisions, ends and means-settings, etc. — and in particular the position-takings necessarily operative in the natural, unreflective, unphilosophical attitude of life, insofar as these everywhere presuppose the world, hence include within themselves a being-belief concerning the world. Here too, the abstaining, the setting-out-of-force of the position-takings on the part of the philosophically reflecting ego does not mean their disappearance from its experiential field. The concrete experiences in question are indeed that upon which the attentive glance is directed, only now the attentive ego, as philosophical, practices abstention in relation to what is thus regarded. Everything that was in such experiences as meant in the validity-consciousness — the relevant judgment, theory, values, ends, etc. — is fully retained, only in the validity-modification of mere phenomena.

This universal setting-out-of-force ("inhibiting," "setting-out-of-play") of all position-takings toward the given objective world — initially of the being-position-takings (concerning being, semblance, possibly-being, presumably, probably-being, etc.) — or, as is also commonly said, this phenomenological *epochē* or this bracketing of the objective world — does not confront us with a nothing. What we rather acquire, and precisely thereby — or more distinctly: what I, the meditator, thereby acquire — is my pure life with all its pure experiences and all its pure generalities, the universe of phenomena in the sense of phenomenology. The *epochē* is, as can also be said, the radical and universal method whereby I apprehend myself purely as the ego together with my own pure conscious life, in which and through which the entire objective world is for me, and is as it precisely is for me. Everything worldly, everything spatiotemporal, is for me — that is, is valid for me — and indeed thereby: that I experience it, perceive it, remember it, think of it in any way, judge it, value it, desire it, etc. Descartes, as is well known, designates all this under the title *cogito*. The world is for me altogether nothing other than the one consciously being and valid for me in such *cogito*. Its entire and universal and special sense and its being-validity it draws exclusively from such *cogitationes*. In them my whole worldly life runs its course, which includes also my scientifically researching and grounding life. I cannot live into, experience, think into, value and act into any other world than the one that has sense and validity in me and from me myself.

If I place myself above this whole life and abstain from any accomplishment of any being-belief that takes the world straightforwardly as existing — if I direct my glance exclusively at this life itself, as consciousness of the world — I gain myself as the pure ego with the pure stream of my *cogitationes*. Thus the natural being of the world — the world of which I can and ever do speak — is indeed preceded, as intrinsically earlier being, by the being of the pure ego and its *cogitationes*. The natural ground of being is in its being-validity secondary; it constantly presupposes the transcendental. The phenomenological fundamental method of the transcendental *epochē*, insofar as it leads back to this ground, is therefore called the transcendental-phenomenological reduction.

### § 9. The Range of Apodictic Evidence of the "I Am"

The next question is whether this reduction makes possible an apodictic evidence for the being of transcendental subjectivity. Only if transcendental self-experience is apodictic can it serve as a foundation for apodictic judgments; only then is there a prospect for a philosophy, for a systematic edifice of apodictic cognitions from the intrinsically first experiential and judicative field. That the *ego sum*, or *sum cogitans*, is to be spoken apodictically, that we thereby acquire a first apodictic ground of being under our feet — this Descartes already saw; he emphasizes the indubitability of the proposition, and that even an "I doubt" would already presuppose the "I am." Here too, what is at issue is the ego that is aware of itself after it has set the experiential world out of force as possibly dubitable.

It is clear, after our more precise expositions, that the sense of the indubitability in which the ego comes to givenness through the transcendental reduction truly corresponds to our earlier concept of apodicticity. Admittedly, the problem of apodicticity, and thus of the first ground and foundation for a philosophy, is not thereby settled. Doubts immediately stir. Does not, for example, the transcendental subjectivity's respective past, accessible only through memory, belong to it inseparably? But can an apodictic evidence be claimed for memory? It would be wrong to wish thereby to deny the apodicticity of the "I am," which would only be possible by arguing past it externally, thus overlooking it. But instead, the problem of the range of our apodictic evidence must now become urgent.

We recall here an earlier remark that adequation and apodicticity of an evidence need not go hand in hand — perhaps this remark was aimed precisely at the case of transcendental self-experience. In this, the ego is originally accessible to itself. But only a core of what is genuinely adequately experienced is offered at each time by this experience: namely, the living self-presence, which the grammatical sense of the proposition *ego cogito* expresses, while beyond this only an indeterminate general, presumptive horizon extends, a horizon of what is not genuinely experienced but necessarily co-intended. To it belongs the mostly quite dark self-past, but also the transcendental capacities and the respective habitual properties belonging to the ego. Even external perception (which admittedly is not apodictic) is self-experience of the thing — it itself stands there — but in this standing-there-itself it has for the experiencer an open, endless, indeterminately general horizon of what is not properly self-perceived. Similarly, the apodictic certainty of transcendental experience pertains to my transcendental "I am" in the indeterminate generality of an open horizon that adheres to it. The being of the intrinsically first ground of cognition thus indeed stands absolutely firm, but not without further ado that which determines its being more closely and which, during the living evidence of the "I am," is not yet itself given but only presumed. This presumption, implicit in the apodictic evidence, is therefore subject to critique regarding the possibilities of its fulfillment, regarding its possibly apodictically delimitable range. How far can the transcendental ego deceive itself about itself, and how far do the absolutely indubitable components reach despite this possible self-deception?

With the positing of the transcendental ego, we stand at a dangerous point, even if we initially leave aside the difficult questions of apodicticity.

### § 10. Excursus: Descartes' Failure to Make the Transcendental Turn

It seems so easy, following Descartes, to grasp the pure ego and its *cogitationes*. And yet it is as though we were on a steep ridge, where to proceed calmly and safely decides philosophical life and death. Descartes had the earnest will to radical freedom from prejudice. But we know through more recent research — in particular the beautiful and profound work of Gilson and Koyré — how much scholasticism lurks in concealment as an unclarified prejudice in Descartes' *Meditations*. But not only that: first of all, the prejudice already mentioned above, stemming from the admiration of mathematical natural science and determining us too as an old legacy, must be kept at bay — as though the *ego cogito* were an apodictic axiom which, together with other axioms to be discovered and perhaps inductively grounded hypotheses, were to furnish the foundation for a deductively explanatory world-science, a nomological science, a science *ordine geometrico*, similar to mathematical natural science. In connection with this, it must also not count as self-evident that we have rescued, in our apodictic pure ego, a small corner of the world — the only unquestionable bit of the world for the philosophizing ego — and that it now comes down to inferring the rest of the world through rightly guided inferences following the principles innate in the ego.

Unfortunately, this is precisely what happens with Descartes, with the inconspicuous but fateful turn that makes the ego into a *substantia cogitans*, into a separated human *mens sive animus*, and into a starting-point for inferences following the causal principle — in short, the turn through which he became the father of the (as cannot yet be made visible here) countersensical transcendental realism. All this remains foreign to us if we remain faithful to the radicalism of self-reflection and thus to the principle of pure intuition or evidence — that is, if we here let nothing count as valid except what we actually have, directly and initially quite immediately, on the field opened to us by the *epochē* of the *ego cogito*; hence bring nothing to expression that we do not ourselves see. Therein Descartes erred, and so it comes about that he stands before the greatest of all discoveries, has in a certain way already made it, and yet does not grasp its authentic sense — the sense of transcendental subjectivity — and thus does not cross the entry gate leading into genuine transcendental philosophy.

### § 11. The Psychological and the Transcendental Ego. The Transcendence of the World

If I hold purely to what comes into view for me, the meditator, through free *epochē* regarding the being of the experiential world, it is the significant fact that I and my life remain untouched in my being-validity, however it may stand with the being or nonbeing of the world, however I may ever decide about it. This ego, which necessarily remains to me by virtue of such *epochē*, and its ego-life, is not a piece of the world, and if it says: "I am, *ego cogito*," that no longer means: I, this human being, am. No longer am I the human being who finds himself in natural self-experience and, in the abstractive restriction to the pure components of inner, purely psychological self-experience, finds his own pure *mens sive animus sive intellectus*. Naturally apperceived in this way, I and all other humans are themes of objective or positive sciences — biology, anthropology, and, included therein, psychology. But the phenomenological *epochē* demanded of the philosophizing person inhibits the being-validity of the objective world and thereby entirely excludes it from the field of judgment — and thus also the being-validity of all objectively apperceived facts, including those of inner experience. For me, the meditating ego — standing in and remaining in the *epochē*, positing myself exclusively as the ground of validity of all objective validities and grounds — there is therefore no psychological ego, no psychic phenomena in the sense of psychology, i.e., as component pieces of psychophysical human beings.

Through the phenomenological *epochē*, I reduce my natural human ego and my mental life — the realm of my psychological self-experience — to my transcendental-phenomenological ego, the realm of transcendental-phenomenological self-experience. The objective world that is for me, that ever was and will be for me, that ever can be with all its objects — draws, I said, its entire sense and its being-validity, the sense and validity it at each time has for me, from me myself, from me as the transcendental ego, first emerging with the transcendental-phenomenological *epochē*.

This concept of the transcendental and its correlate-concept, that of the transcendent, must be drawn exclusively from our philosophically meditating situation. It is to be noted: just as the reduced ego is not a piece of the world, so conversely the world and every worldly object is not a piece of my ego, is not to be found in my conscious life as a real part, as a complex of sense-data or acts really contained in it. To the very sense of everything worldly belongs this transcendence, although it acquires and can only acquire the entire sense that determines it, together with its being-validity, from my experiencing, my representing, thinking, valuing, acting — also the sense of an evidently valid being, from my own evidences, from my grounding acts. If this transcendence of unreal containedness belongs to the very sense of the world, then the ego itself, which carries the world as a valid sense and which is itself necessarily presupposed by the world, is called transcendental in the phenomenological sense; the philosophical problems arising from this correlation are accordingly called transcendental-philosophical.

## Second Meditation: Uncovering the Field of Transcendental Experience According to Its Universal Structures

### § 12. The Idea of a Transcendental Grounding of Knowledge

Our meditation now requires a further development, in which what has so far been brought out can first yield its proper benefit. What can I (the Cartesian meditator) do philosophically with the transcendental ego? To be sure, its being takes precedence for me epistemically over all objective being: in a certain sense it is the ground and foundation upon which all objective cognition takes place. But may this precedence mean that it is, in the ordinary sense, the epistemic ground for all objective cognition? Not that we wish to abandon the great Cartesian thought of seeking the deepest grounding of all sciences, and even of the being of an objective world, in transcendental subjectivity. But perhaps, with the Cartesian discovery of the transcendental ego, a new idea of knowledge-grounding also opens up — namely, as transcendental grounding.

Indeed, instead of wishing to use the *ego cogito* as an apodictically evident premise for supposedly necessary inferences to a transcendent subjectivity, we turn our attention to the fact that the phenomenological *epochē* discloses (for me, the meditating philosopher) a novel infinite sphere of being — a sphere of a novel, transcendental experience. Considering that to every kind of actual experience and its general modification-modes (perception, retention, recollection, etc.) there also belongs a corresponding pure phantasy — an experience-as-if with parallel modes — we expect also that there is an a priori science sustained in the realm of pure possibility (pure conceivability, phantasiability), a science that judges not about transcendental actualities but about a priori possibilities, and thereby at the same time prescribes rules a priori for actualities.

Yet in letting our thoughts run ahead in this manner to the conception of a phenomenological science that is to become philosophy, we immediately encounter difficulties with the methodological fundamental demand of apodictic evidence of the ego. For however absolute this evidence is for the ego's being for itself, it does not straightforwardly coincide with the evidence for the being of the manifold data of transcendental experience. Perhaps it can be shown, however, that the absolute evidence of the *ego sum* necessarily reaches into the manifold of self-experience of transcendental life, though only within certain delimitations that determine the range of such evidences (those of recollection, retention, etc.). More precisely, perhaps the following can be shown: not the mere identity of the "I am" is the absolutely indubitable stock of transcendental self-experience; rather, there extends through all the particular data of actual and possible self-experience — although they are not individually absolutely indubitable — a universal apodictic experiential structure of the ego (for example, the immanent time-form of the stream of experience). With this it is connected, and to this structure itself it also belongs, that the ego is apodictically pre-delineated for itself as a concrete entity with an individual content of experiences, capacities, dispositions — horizonally pre-delineated as an experiential object accessible through possible, infinitely perfectible and perhaps enrichable self-experience.

### § 13. The Necessity of Initially Excluding Problems of the Range of Transcendental Cognition

Actually to bring this out would be the great task of a critique of transcendental self-experience according to its individual forms, which interweave with one another, and according to the total performance achieved through the universal interweaving. This would obviously be a task of a higher level, which already presupposes that, initially following the in a certain way naively functioning evidence of concordantly progressing transcendental experience, we have looked around in its data and circumscribed them according to their generalities.

The expansion of the Cartesian *Meditations* just carried out will correspondingly motivate our further procedure in view of a philosophy (in the Cartesian sense described above). The scientific labors must proceed in two levels. In the first, the — as it soon becomes apparent — enormous realm of transcendental self-experience must be traversed, initially in mere surrender to the evidence inherent in it in its concordant course, and thus setting aside the questions of an ultimate critique mindful of apodictic principles of range. We thus proceed at this not yet in the full sense philosophical level similarly to the natural scientist in his surrender to the evidence of natural experience. The second level of phenomenological research would then concern the critique of transcendental experience and thereupon of transcendental cognition in general.

An extraordinarily singular science enters our purview — a science of concrete transcendental subjectivity as given in actual and possible transcendental experience, which forms the extreme contrast to the sciences in the previous sense, the objective sciences. Among these there is indeed a science of subjectivity, but of objective, animal subjectivity, belonging to the world. But now what is at issue is a so-to-speak absolutely subjective science, a science whose object in its being is independent of the decision about the nonbeing or being of the world. But more than this: its first and, it seems, its sole object is my, the philosophizing person's, transcendental ego. Certainly it lies in the sense of the transcendental reduction that it can at the outset posit as existing nothing other than the ego and what is contained in the ego itself. Certainly it therefore begins as a pure egology and as a science that seems to condemn us to a transcendental solipsism, albeit a transcendental one. It is not yet at all foreseeable how, in the attitude of reduction, other egos — not as mere worldly phenomena, but as other transcendental egos — should be positable as existing.

We must not let such misgivings frighten us as beginning philosophers. Perhaps the reduction to the transcendental ego merely carries with it the semblance of a permanently solipsistic science, while its consistent execution in accordance with its own sense leads over to a phenomenology of transcendental intersubjectivity, and by means of this develops into a transcendental philosophy as such. Indeed, it will turn out that a transcendental solipsism is only a philosophical substage and must be delimited as such for methodological purposes, in order to set into play properly the problematic of transcendental intersubjectivity as a founded, and therefore higher-level, problematic.

Definitively marked out in any case is an essential deviation from the Cartesian course, one that will henceforth be decisive for our entire further meditating. In contrast to Descartes, we immerse ourselves in the task of uncovering the infinite field of transcendental experience. The Cartesian evidence, that of the proposition "Ego cogito, ego sum," remains without fruit because Descartes not only neglected to clarify the pure methodological sense of the transcendental *epochē*, but also neglected to turn his attention to the fact that the ego can lay itself out infinitely and systematically through transcendental experience, and thus stands ready as a possible field of work, a wholly peculiar and separate one.

### § 14. The Stream of Cogitationes. Cogito and Cogitatum

We now shift the center of gravity of the transcendental evidence of the *ego cogito* (this word taken in the widest Cartesian sense) — while we set aside the questions of the range of the apodicticity of this evidence — from the identical ego to the manifold *cogitationes*, that is, to the streaming conscious life in which the identical ego (mine, the meditator's) lives. Upon this life, e.g., upon its sensory perceiving and representing, or upon its predicating, valuing, willing, the ego can at any time direct its reflective gaze, contemplate it, and interpret and describe it according to its contents.

One will perhaps say that following this research-direction is nothing other than carrying out psychological description on the basis of purely inner experience, the experience of one's own conscious life, where the purity of such description naturally demands that everything psychophysical be left out of consideration. Yet a purely descriptive consciousness-psychology, however much its genuine methodological sense has first been disclosed only with the new phenomenology, is not itself transcendental phenomenology in the sense in which we have determined it through the transcendental-phenomenological reduction. Pure consciousness-psychology is indeed an exact parallel to transcendental consciousness-phenomenology, but both must be strictly kept apart, while their confusion characterizes the transcendental psychologism that makes a genuine philosophy impossible. What is involved here is one of those seemingly trifling nuances that decisively determine philosophical paths and false paths.

It must not be overlooked that the *epochē* regarding all worldly being changes nothing about the fact that the manifold *cogitationes* relating to what is worldly carry this relation within themselves, that, for example, the perception of this table is still, as before, precisely a perception of it. Thus, every consciousness-experience in itself is consciousness of such and such, however it may stand with the legitimate reality-validity of this objectual entity and however I, as transcendentally oriented, may abstain from this or that natural validity. The transcendental title *ego cogito* must therefore be expanded by one member: every *cogito*, every consciousness-experience, we also say, intends something and carries in itself, in this manner of what is intended, its respective *cogitatum*, and each does so in its own way. The house-perception intends a house — more precisely, this individual house — and intends it in the manner of perception; a house-memory intends it in the manner of memory; a house-phantasy in the manner of phantasy; a predicative judging about the house intends it in the manner of judging, and so on. Consciousness-experiences are also called intentional, where the word intentionality then means nothing other than this general fundamental property of consciousness — to be consciousness of something, as *cogito* to carry its *cogitatum* within itself.

### § 15. Natural and Transcendental Reflection

For further clarification, we must distinguish the straightforwardly accomplished perceiving, remembering, predicating, valuing, purposing, etc., from the reflections through which — as grasping acts of a new level — those straightforward acts first disclose themselves to us. Straightforwardly perceiving, we grasp, say, the house and not the perceiving. Only in reflection do we direct ourselves to the perceiving itself and to its perceptual directedness-toward the house.

In the natural reflection of everyday life, but also of psychological science (hence of psychological experience of one's own psychic experiences), we stand on the ground of the world given as existing — as when in everyday life we say: "I see a house over there" or "I remember having heard this melody," etc. In the transcendental-phenomenological reflection, we lift ourselves above this ground through the universal *epochē* with respect to the being or nonbeing of the world. The thus modified, transcendental experience consists, we can say, in our looking at the respectively transcendentally reduced *cogito* and describing it, but without, as reflecting subjects, co-executing the natural being-positing that the originally straightforwardly executed perception or other *cogito* contains within itself. Thus in place of the original experience a substantially different one emerges; in this respect, reflection alters the original experience. But this holds for every, including every natural, reflection. It essentially alters the previously naive experience; the experience loses its original mode of the straightforward — precisely because reflection makes into an object what was previously an experience but not an object.

But the task of reflection is not to repeat the original experience but to contemplate and interpret what is to be found in it. The transition to this contemplation yields a new intentional experience which, in its own intentional character, has back-reference to the earlier experience — making this very experience, and not another, conscious and possibly evidently conscious. Precisely thereby an experiential knowledge, initially a descriptive one, becomes possible — the one to which we owe all conceivable acquaintance and cognition of our intentional life. This remains valid also for transcendental-phenomenological reflection. The non-co-executing of the being-position of the straightforward house-perception on the part of the reflecting ego changes nothing about the fact that its reflective experiencing is precisely experiencing of the house-perception, with all its previously belonging and continuing moments. And to these belong in our example the moments of the perception itself as streaming experience and the moments of the perceived house purely as such. On the one hand, the being-positing belonging to (normal) perceiving (the perceptual belief) in the mode of certainty is not absent, nor, on the other hand, the character of straightforward being-there on the side of the appearing house. The non-co-executing, the abstaining of the phenomenologically attuned ego, is its affair and not that of the perceptually contemplated perceiving.

We can also describe what is at hand here thus: if we call the ego that naturally experiences and otherwise lives into the world interested in the world, then the phenomenologically modified and consistently so maintained attitude consists in this: a splitting of the ego takes place, in which the phenomenological ego establishes itself as disinterested spectator above the naively interested ego. That this takes place is then itself accessible through a new reflection, which as transcendental again demands the carrying out of precisely this attitude of the disinterested spectator — with the sole interest remaining to it: to see and to describe adequately.

Thus all occurrences of the world-directed life, with all their simple and founded being-positions and the being-modes correlative to them — as being-in-certainty, being-possible, being-probable, further being-beautiful-and-good, being-useful, etc. — become accessible to description, purely from all co-meanings and pre-meanings of the observer. Only in this purity can they become themes of a universal critique of consciousness, as our aim at a philosophy necessarily demands. We recall the radicalism of the Cartesian idea of philosophy as the universal, ultimately apodictically grounded science. As such it demands an absolute universal critique, which must for its part first create a universe of absolute freedom from prejudice by abstaining from all position-takings that proffer any existent entity. This is accomplished by the universality of transcendental experience and description.

Following this methodological principle with respect to the double title *cogito–cogitatum (qua cogitatum)*, general descriptions open up, to be carried out in each case on individual *cogitationes* in the correlative directions. On one hand, descriptions of the intentional object as such, with respect to the determinations assigned to it in the relevant modes of consciousness — and assigned in the relevant modes that emerge when one directs one's glance toward them (such as the being-modes: certainly being, possibly or presumably being, etc.; or the subjective-temporal modes: being present, past, or future). This descriptive direction is called the noematic. Over against it stands the noetic. It concerns the modes of the *cogito* itself, the modes of consciousness, e.g., of perception, of recollection, of retention, with the modal differences dwelling in them, such as those of clarity and distinctness.

We now understand that through the universally exercised *epochē* regarding the being or nonbeing of the world, we have not simply lost the world for phenomenology; we retain it *qua cogitata*. And not only with respect to individual realities and their modes in particular consciousness-acts, but in general: it is always co-conscious in the unity of a consciousness that can itself become a grasping one, and the whole of the world is conscious in the form proper to it: that of spatiotemporal endlessness. In all change of consciousness, the universe, changeable in its details of experience and otherwise intended particulars, yet one and single, remains as the existing background of all natural life. Thus, in the consistent carrying out of the phenomenological reduction, there remains noetically the openly endless pure conscious life, and on the side of its noematic correlate, the intended world purely as such. The phenomenologically meditating ego can therefore become, not only in particular instances but in universality, the disinterested spectator of itself — and therein encompassed, of all objectivity that is for it, and as it is for it.

### § 16. Excursus: The Necessary Beginning of Transcendental as Well as "Purely Psychological" Reflection with the "Ego Cogito"

The ego offers itself, in reflection, in the generality of its life, as an openly infinite manifold of individual concrete experiences, whose changing structures are to be disclosed and descriptively grasped — this designates a first great field of tasks. Likewise on the other side, with respect to their modes of connection up to the unity of the concrete ego itself. This is naturally concrete only in the openly endless universality of its connected-unitary intentional life and the correlates implied in it as *cogitata*, which are for their part unified into whole universalities — among them, the appearing world as such. The concrete ego itself is the universal theme of description. Or more clearly: I, the meditating phenomenologist, set myself the universal task of the disclosure of myself as transcendental ego in my full concretion, thus with all the intentional correlates enclosed therein.

It is clear that neither for a transcendental-descriptive egology nor for a pure inner psychology (as a psychological fundamental discipline necessarily to be carried through), exclusively drawn from inner experience, is any other beginning possible than the *ego cogito*. It means blocking one's access to both when, misled by the still all-dominant tradition of sensualism, one begins with a doctrine of sensations. This implies: one interprets the conscious life in supposed self-evidence as a complex of data of outer and (at best) also inner sensibility, for whose combination into wholes one then lets Gestalt-qualities take care. To dispose of atomism, one adds the doctrine that in these data the Gestalten are necessarily founded, hence the wholes are intrinsically prior to the parts. But the radically beginning descriptive doctrine of consciousness does not have such data and wholes before it, except as prejudices. The beginning is the pure and so-to-speak still mute experience, which must now first be brought to the pure expression of its own sense. But the actually first expression is the Cartesian one of *ego cogito* — e.g., I perceive — this house; I remember — a certain street scene, etc.; and the first general thing about the description is the separation of *cogito* and *cogitatum qua cogitatum*.

### § 17. The Two-Sidedness of Consciousness-Research as a Correlative Problematic. Directions of Description. Synthesis as the Primal Form of Consciousness

If the beginning and the direction of task are clear to us from the outset, there result for us, in our transcendental attitude, important guiding thoughts for the further problematic. The two-sidedness of consciousness-research (leaving aside for now the question of the identical ego) is descriptively to be characterized as an inseparable belongingness-together; the mode of connection that unites consciousness with consciousness is the one exclusively proper to consciousness — synthesis.

If I take, for example, the perceiving of this cube as the theme of description, I see in pure reflection that this cube is continuously given as an objective unity in a multiform and changeable manifold of determinately belonging modes of appearance. These are not an incoherent succession of experiences in their passing away. They rather flow in the unity of a synthesis, according to which in them one and the same is conscious as what appears. The cube, the one and same, appears now in near-appearances, now in far-appearances, in the changing modes of Here and There with respect to an always co-conscious absolute Here (in the co-appearing own body). Every retained appearance of such a mode — say, the cube here in the near-sphere — shows itself in turn as a synthetic unity of a manifold of belonging modes of appearance. Namely, the near-thing as the same appears now from this side, now from that, and the visual perspectives change, as do the tactual, acoustic, and other modes of appearance.

If we attend in particular to any feature of the cube that shows itself in the cube-perception — e.g., the shape or coloring of the cube, or a surface of the cube by itself, or its square shape, its color by itself, etc. — the same thing repeats itself. We always find the relevant feature as a unity of a streaming manifold. Seen straightforwardly, we have perhaps the one unchanging shape or color; in reflective orientation, the belonging modes of appearance that link up in continuous sequence — those of orientation, perspective, etc. Each such mode of appearance is in itself — e.g., the shape- or color-Abschattung in itself — a presentation of its shape, its color, etc. Thus the respective *cogito* does not have its *cogitatum* conscious in an undifferentiated emptiness, but in a descriptive manifold-structure of a quite determinate, essentially belonging noetic-noematic construction.

### § 18. Identification as a Fundamental Form of Synthesis. Universal Synthesis of Transcendental Time

Let us now consider the fundamental form of synthesis, namely identification. It confronts us first as an all-pervasive, passively proceeding synthesis in the form of the continuous inner time-consciousness. Every experience has its experience-temporality. If it is a consciousness-experience in which, as *cogitatum*, a worldly object appears (as in the cube-perception), we must distinguish the objective temporality that appears — e.g., that of this cube — from the inner temporality of the appearing (e.g., of the cube-perceiving). The latter streams along in its temporal stretches and phases, which for their part are continuously changing appearances of one and the same cube. Their unity is a unity of synthesis — not a mere continuous connectedness of *cogitationes* (a kind of external being-glued-together), but connectedness in one consciousness, in which the unity of an intentional objectuality constitutes itself as the same amid manifold modes of appearance.

The same cube — in terms of consciousness, the same — can also be simultaneously or successively conscious in separate, very different kinds of consciousness, e.g., in separate perceptions, recollections, expectations, valuations, etc. Again, it is a synthesis that produces the consciousness of identity as a unified consciousness overarching these separate experiences, and thereby makes every knowledge of identity possible. But ultimately, even every consciousness in which non-identical things are unitarily conscious — every plurality-consciousness, relation-consciousness, etc. — is a synthesis in this sense.

Synthesis does not lie only in all individual consciousness-experiences and does not merely connect individual ones with individual ones occasionally; rather, the entire consciousness-life, as we have already said in advance, is synthetically unified. It is therefore a universal *cogito*, synthetically embracing within itself all particular consciousness-experiences, with its universal *cogitatum* — founded in various levels in the manifold particular *cogitata*. Yet this founding does not signify a construction in temporal succession of a genesis, since rather every conceivable individual experience is only a standing-out within an always already unitarily presupposed total consciousness. The universal *cogitatum* is the universal life itself in its openly infinite unity and totality. The fundamental form of this universal synthesis, which makes all other syntheses of consciousness possible, is the all-encompassing inner time-consciousness. Its correlate is immanent temporality itself, according to which all experiences of the ego, reflectively found, must present themselves as temporally ordered — as temporally beginning and ending, as simultaneous and successive — within the constant infinite horizon of immanent time.

### § 19. Actuality and Potentiality of Intentional Life

The manifoldness of intentionality belonging to every *cogito* is not thematically exhausted in the mere consideration of the *cogitata* as actual experiences. Rather, every actuality implies its potentialities, which are not empty possibilities but possibilities that are intentionally pre-delineated in content in the respective actual experience and moreover furnished with the character of being realizable by the ego. Thereby a further fundamental trait of intentionality is indicated. Every experience has a horizon — an intentional horizon of reference to potentialities of consciousness belonging to it — that changes in the course of the changing consciousness-context and in the course of the experience's own streaming phases. For example, to every outer perception belongs the reference from the properly perceived sides of the perceptual object to the co-intended sides not yet perceived, but only anticipated in expectation — those now perceptually to come — a steady protention with a new sense at each perceptual phase. Moreover, the perception has horizons of other possibilities of perception as such, which we could have if we actively directed the course of perception differently. In the corresponding memory, this returns in modified form. Furthermore, to every perception there belongs a past-horizon as a potentiality of recollections to be awakened, and to every recollection, as its horizon, the continuous mediate intentionality of possible recollections up to the respective actual perceptual Now.

The horizons are pre-delineated potentialities. We also say: one can interrogate every horizon as to what lies in it, interpret it, disclose the respective potentialities of conscious life. Precisely thereby we disclose the objective sense, only indicated in the actual *cogito* to a certain degree, implicitly intended. This sense, the *cogitatum qua cogitatum*, is never something finished and given; it clarifies itself only through this interpretation of the horizon and the continually newly awakened horizons. The pre-delineation itself is always imperfect, but in its indeterminacy it has a structure of determinacy. For example, the cube is still open on the unseen sides to much that is various, and yet it is already apprehended as a cube, and in particular as colored, rough, and the like — though each of these determinations still leaves open particularities. This leaving-open, prior to the actual closer-determinations that may never come, is a moment enclosed in the respective consciousness itself — precisely that which constitutes the horizon.

### § 20. The Distinctive Character of Intentional Analysis

It becomes apparent that consciousness-analysis as intentional is something totally different from analysis in the ordinary and natural sense. The conscious life is not a mere whole of consciousness-data, analyzable — in the broadest sense, divisible — into its independent or non-independent elements, with the unity-forms (Gestalt-qualities) to be reckoned among the non-independent. Intentional analysis does lead, and to that extent the word can still serve, in certain thematic directions of attention also to divisions; but its everywhere distinctive performance is the disclosure of the potentialities implied in the actualities of consciousness, whereby noematically there takes place interpretation, clarification, and possibly elucidation of what is meant in consciousness, of the objective sense. Intentional analysis is guided by the fundamental cognition that every *cogito*, as consciousness, is indeed in the broadest sense a meaning of its meant, but that this meant is at every moment more (with a More of what is meant) than what at the respective moment lies before us as explicitly meant. In our example, each perceptual phase was only a side of the object as perceptually meant. The phenomenologist does not operate in a merely naive devotion to the intentional object purely as such; he does not merely contemplate it straightforwardly, interpreting its meant features, parts, and properties. For then the intentionality that constitutes the intuitive or non-intuitive having-conscious and the interpretive contemplating would remain anonymous.

In other words: there would remain hidden the noetic manifolds of consciousness and their synthetic unity, by virtue of which — and as their essential unitary performance — we have an intentional object at all and in each case this determinate one continuously meant. The phenomenologist, researching all objectual entities and what is found therein exclusively as consciousness-correlate, does not merely contemplate them straightforwardly but penetrates disclosingly with his reflective gaze into the anonymous *cogitative* life, discloses the determinate synthetic courses of the manifold modes of consciousness and the still more deeply underlying modes of ego-comportment, which make the straightforward being-meant of the objectual, its intuitive or non-intuitive being, understandable. Or which make it understandable how consciousness, in itself and by virtue of its respective intentional structure, necessarily brings it about that in it such-and-such being and being-thus of an object can become conscious, can appear as such a sense.

The horizon-structure of all intentionality thus prescribes for phenomenological analysis and description a totally novel methodology — one that enters into action wherever consciousness and object, meaning and sense, real and ideal actuality, possibility, necessity, semblance, truth, but also experience, judgment, evidence, etc., emerge as titles for transcendental (in the parallel, purely psychological) problems and are to be taken up as genuine problems of subjective "origin."

### § 21. The Intentional Object as "Transcendental Clue"

The most general typology, in which as form all that is particular is enclosed, is designated by our general schema ego–cogito–cogitatum. On it refer the most general descriptions that we have attempted concerning intentionality, the synthesis belonging to it, etc. In the particularization of this typology and its description, the intentional object standing on the side of the *cogitatum* plays, for easily understandable reasons, the role of a transcendental clue for the disclosure of the typical manifold of *cogitationes* that, in possible synthesis, carry it within themselves as the same meant. The starting point is necessarily the straightforwardly given object, from which reflection leads back to the respective mode of consciousness and to the potential modes of consciousness enclosed in its horizon. Every such type is to be questioned concerning its noetic-noematic structure, is to be systematically interpreted and grounded with respect to its modes of intentional flow and typical horizons and their implications.

If one holds fast any object in its form or category and constantly holds in evidence the identity of the same amid the change of its modes of consciousness, it becomes evident that they, however fluid they may be and however ungrasped by ultimate elements, are by no means arbitrary. They remain bound to a structural typology that is unbreakably the same so long as the objectuality remains conscious precisely as this one and as so constituted, and so long as it is to be able to persist in the evidence of identity amid the change of consciousness-modes. To lay out this structural typology systematically is the task of the transcendental theory, which, when it holds to an objectual generality as clue, is called the theory of the transcendental constitution of the object as such — of the object of the relevant form or category, at the highest level: region.

### § 22. The Idea of the Universal Unity of All Objects and the Task of Their Constitutional Clarification

Object-types — in the phenomenological reduction grasped purely as *cogitata* and not in prejudices of an in-advance-valid scientific conceptuality — we found as clues for thematically connected transcendental investigations. The constituting consciousness-manifolds are not accidental but belong together from essential grounds in view of the possibility of such synthesis. They therefore stand under principles by virtue of which the phenomenological investigations do not lose themselves in disconnected descriptions but organize themselves from essential grounds. Every object, every object whatsoever (including every immanent one), designates a rule-structure of the transcendental ego. As something represented by it, however conscious, it at once designates a universal rule of possible further consciousness of the same.

This points ahead to a universal constitutive synthesis in which all syntheses function together in a determinately ordered manner and in which all actual and possible objectivities as such for the transcendental ego, and correlatively all their actual and possible modes of consciousness, are encompassed. We can also say: there announces itself an enormous task, which is that of the whole of transcendental phenomenology — the task of, in the unity of a systematic and all-encompassing order, carrying through all phenomenological investigations as correspondingly constitutive ones, at the moving clue of a step-by-step-elaborated system of all objects of possible consciousness, and therein of their formal and material categories — that is, built up strictly systematically upon one another, interconnected.

But we say better that what is involved here is an infinite regulative idea. The system of possible objects as such of possible consciousness, to be presupposed in evident anticipation, is itself an idea (but not an invention, not an "as if") and furnishes practically the principle for the hand to follow — through constant disclosure not only of the horizons internally proper to the objects of consciousness, but also of those pointing outward, to essential forms of connections — to link every relatively closed constitutive theory with every other. Already the tasks presenting themselves at the restricted clues of particular object-types prove to be highly complicated and lead everywhere, upon deeper penetration, to great disciplines.

## Third Meditation: Constitutional Problematic. Truth and Actuality

### § 23. A More Precise Concept of Transcendental Constitution Under the Titles "Reason" and "Unreason"

Phenomenological constitution was for us hitherto the constitution of an intentional object in general. It encompassed the title cogito–cogitatum in its full breadth. We now proceed to differentiate this breadth structurally and to prepare a more pregnant concept of constitution. It was hitherto immaterial whether what was at issue were truly existing or non-existing, possible or impossible objects. This distinction is not removed from question through the abstention from the decision for being and nonbeing of the world. It is rather, under the broadly grasped titles Reason and Unreason, as correlate-titles for Being and Nonbeing, a universal theme of phenomenology. Through the *epochē* we reduce to pure meaning (*cogito*) and what is meant purely as meant. To the latter — hence not to objects straightforwardly, but to objective sense — the predicates Being and Nonbeing and their modal variations refer. To the former, to the respective meaning, the predicates Truth (correctness) and Falsehood refer, though in the very widest sense.

Reason is not a contingent-factual capacity, not a title for possible contingent facts; rather, it is a title for a universal essential structural form of transcendental subjectivity as such. Reason refers to possibilities of verification, and these ultimately to making-evident and having-evident.

### § 24. Evidence as Self-Givenness and Its Variations

In the widest sense, evidence designates a general primal phenomenon of intentional life — in contrast with other kinds of having-conscious, which can a priori be empty, anticipatory, indirect, or improper: the quite distinctive mode of consciousness of the self-appearing, self-presenting, self-giving of a thing, a state of affairs, a generality, a value, etc., in the final mode of being-itself-there, immediately intuitable, given originaliter. For the ego this means: not confusedly, emptily intending something, but being at the thing itself, seeing, insight. Experience in the ordinary sense is a particular evidence; evidence in general, we can say, is experience in a widest yet essentially unified sense.

### § 25. Actuality and Quasi-Actuality

All these distinctions furthermore split into parallels by virtue of the distinction, running through the entire consciousness-sphere and correlatively through all being-modalities, between actuality and phantasy (actuality-as-if). On the latter side, a new general concept of possibility arises, which in the manner of mere conceivability (in an imagining-as-if-it-were) repeats in modified form all being-modes, beginning with simple being-certainty. Accordingly, modes of consciousness of positionality and those of quasi-positionality (of the As-if, of phantasizing) are distinguished, and to their particular modes correspond proper modes of the evidence of their intended objects.

### § 26. Actuality as Correlate of Evident Verification

With these brief remarks, formally general problems of intentional analysis are initially indicated. But not only that: with them there opens for us the significant recognition that these concepts in their formal-ontological generality indicate a universal structural lawfulness of conscious life as such, by virtue of which alone truth and actuality have and can have sense for us. In fact, that objects in the widest sense (real things, experiences, numbers, states of affairs, laws, theories, etc.) are for me — this initially means only that they are valid for me, that they are for me in other words consciously as *cogitata* at each time in the positional mode of certain belief. But we also know that we would at once have to give up this validity if a path of evident identity-synthesis led to conflict with what is evidently given, and that we can be certain of being-actual only through the right or true synthesis of evident verification. It is clear that truth, or true actuality of objects, can only be drawn from evidence, and that it alone is that whereby actually existing, veracious, legitimately valid object, of whatever form or kind, has sense for us. Every right stems from there, stems from our own transcendental subjectivity; every conceivable adequation springs as our verification, is our synthesis, has in us its ultimate transcendental ground.

### § 27. Habitual and Potential Evidence Functioning Constitutively for the Sense "Existing Object"

Every evidence establishes for me a lasting possession. I can always return to the actuality I myself have seen, in chains of new evidences as restitutions of the first evidence. Without such possibilities there would be no standing and lasting being for us, no real and ideal world. Each is for us from the evidence, or from the presumption, of being able to make evident and to repeat evidence once gained. Already from this it follows that the individual evidence does not yet create for us a lasting being. Everything that exists is in a broadest sense "in itself" and has over against it the contingent for-me of the individual acts, and likewise every truth is in this broadest sense "truth in itself." This broadest sense of the In-itself thus refers to evidence, but not to evidence as an experiential fact, but to certain potentialities grounded in the transcendental ego and its life.

### § 28. Presumptive Evidence of World-Experience. The World as a Correlate-Idea of Perfect Experiential Evidence

In another manner, evidences refer to infinities of evidences regarding the same object, and in a much more complicated way — namely always where they bring their object to self-givenness in an essential one-sidedness. This concerns nothing less than the entire range of evidences through which a real objective world, as a whole and according to any particular objects, is immediately intuitively there for us. The evidences belonging to them are outer experience, and it is to be recognized as an essential necessity that no other manner of self-giving is conceivable for such objects. But it is also to be recognized that to this kind of evidence there essentially belongs one-sidedness — more precisely, a manifold horizon of unfulfilled but fulfillment-requiring anticipations. The actuality-object of a world, and first of all a world itself, is an infinite idea, referred to infinities of concordantly unifiable experiences — a correlate-idea to the idea of a perfect experiential evidence, a complete synthesis of possible experiences.

### § 29. The Material- and Formal-Ontological Regions as Indices of Transcendental Systems of Evidences

One now understands the great tasks of transcendental self-interpretation of the ego, or of its conscious life, that grow with respect to the objectualities posited and to-be-posited in it. The title veracious being and truth (according to all modalities) designates, for every object intended and ever to-be-intended by me as transcendental ego, a structural distinction within the infinite manifolds of actual and possible *cogitationes* that can relate to it. Next to the formally general investigations — those that hold to the formal-logical (formal-ontological) concept of an object as such — we have the enormous problematic of the constitution that results for each of the highest, no longer formal-logical categories (regions) of objects: the regions that stand under the title "objective world." A constitutive theory is needed for physically constituted nature, for the human being, for the human community, for culture, etc. Each such title designates a great discipline with various directions of investigation corresponding to the naively ontological sub-concepts (real space, real time, real causality, real thing, real property, etc.).

## Fourth Meditation: Unfolding of the Constitutional Problems of the Transcendental Ego Itself

### § 30. The Transcendental Ego Inseparable from Its Experiences

Objects are for me, and are for me what they are, only as objects of actual and possible consciousness. If this is not to be empty talk or a theme of empty speculation, then what this being-for-me and being-thus concretely involves must be shown — what kind of actual and possible consciousness comes into question, what structured consciousness, what "possibility" here means, etc. This can be accomplished solely by constitutional investigation, first in the broader and then in the narrower sense described above, following the only possible method demanded by the essence of intentionality and its horizons. Already through the preparatory analyses, it becomes clear that the transcendental ego (in the psychological parallel, the soul) only is what it is in relation to intentional objectualities. To these also necessarily belong existing objects for the ego, and for it as world-related, not only objects in its adequately verifiable immanent time-sphere but also the world-objects demonstrated as existing in the concordance of the course of inadequate, merely presumptive external experience. It is therefore an essential property of the ego to constantly have systems of intentionality — including concordance-systems — partly running their course within it, partly available as fixed potentialities to be disclosed through pre-delineating horizons.

### § 31. The Ego as Identical Pole of Experiences

But now we must draw attention to a great gap in our presentation. The ego itself is for itself an existing entity in continuous evidence, hence continuously constituting itself in itself as existing. Only one side of this self-constitution have we so far touched upon, looking only at the streaming *cogito*. The ego grasps itself not merely as a streaming life but as I who experience this and that, who live through this and that *cogito* as the same. Until now, occupied with the intentional relation of consciousness and object, *cogito* and *cogitatum*, only that synthesis had emerged for us which polarizes the manifolds of actual and possible consciousness according to identical objects — hence in relation to objects as poles, as synthetic unities. Now a second polarization, a second kind of synthesis, confronts us, one that encompasses all the particular manifolds of *cogitationes* altogether and in its own way — namely as those of the identical ego, which as active in consciousness and affected lives in all consciousness-experiences and through them is related to all object-poles.

### § 32. The Ego as Substrate of Habitualities

But now it is to be noted that this centering ego is not an empty identity-pole (just as no object whatsoever is) but rather, by virtue of a lawfulness of transcendental genesis, wins with every act of a new objective sense radiating from it a new lasting property. If, for example, I decide for the first time in a judgment-act for a being and being-thus, the fleeting act passes, but henceforth I am and remain the ego so decided — I am of the relevant conviction. This means not merely that I remember or can continue to remember the act. I can do that even if I have meanwhile given up my conviction. After the crossing-out, it is no longer my conviction, but it has been so up to that point. So long as it holds good for me, I can return to it repeatedly and always find it again as mine, habitually proper to me — or find myself as the ego who is convinced, who is determined as a persisting ego through this lasting habitus. Likewise for every kind of decision, value-decision, and decision of the will. I make a resolution — the act-experience streams away, but the resolution persists. The ego preserves in such changes a lasting style with a thoroughgoing identity-unity: a personal character.

### § 33. The Full Concretion of the Ego as Monad and the Problem of Its Self-Constitution

From the ego as identical pole and as substrate of habitualities, we distinguish the ego taken in its full concretion (which we wish to call a monad, using Leibniz's word), by adding what the ego simply cannot be without concretely: namely, it can only be in the streaming manifoldness of its intentional life and in the objects meant therein and perhaps constituting themselves as existing for it. Since the monadically concrete ego encompasses the entire actual and potential conscious life, it is clear that the problem of the phenomenological interpretation of this monadic ego (the problem of its constitution for itself) must encompass within itself all constitutional problems whatsoever. In further consequence, the phenomenology of this self-constitution proves to coincide with phenomenology as a whole.

### § 34. Principled Elaboration of the Phenomenological Method. Transcendental Analysis as Eidetic

With the doctrine of the ego as pole of its acts and as substrate of habitualities, we have already, and at a significant point, touched upon the problematic of phenomenological genesis, and thereby the level of genetic phenomenology. Before we clarify its more precise sense, a renewed reflection on the phenomenological method is needed. A fundamental methodological insight must finally be brought into force that, once grasped, pervades the entire methodology of transcendental phenomenology.

Starting from the example of this table-perception, we vary the perceptual object "table" in completely free arbitrariness, but so that we hold fast perception as perception of something — of something, whatever it may be. We transform the fact of this perception, under abstention from its being-validity, into a pure possibility — among other entirely arbitrary pure possibilities — but pure possibilities of perception. We transpose the actual perception into the realm of non-actualities, of the as-if, which furnishes us the pure possibilities, pure from everything that binds to the fact and to every fact in general. The general type "perception" so gained floats in the air — in the air of absolutely pure conceivabilities. So divested of all facticity, it has become the eidos Perception, whose ideal extension comprises all ideally possible perceptions as pure conceivabilities. The perceptual analyses are then essential analyses; everything we have set forth about the syntheses belonging to the type perception holds with essential generality for all conceivable perceptions whatsoever. Beside the phenomenological reduction, eidetic intuition is the fundamental form of all particular transcendental methods.

### § 35. Excursus on Eidetic Inner Psychology

We overstep the self-enclosed circle of our meditations, which binds us to transcendental phenomenology, if we note that the entire content of the methodological fundamental consideration just carried through remains for us — with small modifications that however cancel the transcendental sense — when we strive, on the ground of the natural world-consideration, for a psychology as a positive science. In place of the eidetic transcendental phenomenology there appears an eidetic pure doctrine of the soul, referred to the eidos Soul, whose eidetic horizon admittedly remains unquestioned. Were it to be questioned, however, the path would open to overcoming this positivity — that is, to transference into the absolute phenomenology, that of the transcendental ego, which has no horizon that could lead it beyond its transcendental sphere of being.

### § 36. The Transcendental Ego as the Universe of Possible Forms of Experience. Essential-Lawful Regulation of the Compossibility of Experiences in Coexistence and Succession

Returning after the significant new framing of the idea of transcendental phenomenology through the eidetic method, we henceforth naturally keep within the framework of a purely eidetic phenomenology. The universal a priori belonging to a transcendental ego as such is an essential form that encloses within itself an infinity of forms, of a priori types of possible actualities and potentialities of life, with the objects to be constituted in it as actually existing. But not all individually possible types are compossible for a single possible ego; they are not so in arbitrary order, at arbitrary places in its own temporality. Essential laws of egological-temporal coexistence and succession govern. Whatever emerges in my ego — in intentional experiences, in constituted unities, in ego-habitualities — has its temporality and partakes in the form-system of universal temporality.

### § 37. Time as the Universal Form of All Egological Genesis

The essential laws of compossibility are in a broadest sense laws of causality — laws for an If-Then. But it is better here to avoid the prejudice-laden expression "causality" and to speak in the transcendental sphere of motivation. The most general form of all particular forms of concrete experiences is a form of all-linking and in every particular instance ruling motivation, which we can also address as a formal lawfulness of a universal genesis, according to which past, present, and future continually constitute themselves in one, in a certain noetic-noematic form-structure. But within this form, life runs its course as a motivated progression of particular constitutive performances with manifold particular motivations and motivation-systems, which according to general lawfulness produce a unity of the universal genesis of the ego. The ego constitutes itself for itself in the unity of a history.

### § 38. Active and Passive Genesis

Principles of constitutive genesis divide themselves according to two fundamental forms: principles of active and of passive genesis. In the former, the ego functions as a specifically ego-active, generatively constituting entity. Here belong all performances of practical reason in the broadest sense — logical reason too is practical. But every structure of activity necessarily presupposes, as its lowest level, a passivity that pre-gives, and in pursuing this we encounter constitution through passive genesis. What meets us in life as already ready-made, as a merely existing thing, is given in the originality of the itself in the synthesis of passive experience. While active syntheses carry out their performances, passive synthesis — furnishing all the matter for them — is constantly ongoing.

### § 39. Association as a Principle of Passive Genesis

The universal principle of passive genesis for the constitution of all objectualities ultimately pre-given to the active formations carries the title Association. It is a title of intentionality, descriptively demonstrable in its primal forms and standing under essential laws. Association is a transcendental-phenomenological fundamental concept. The old concept of association and of laws of association is only a naturalistic distortion of the corresponding genuine intentional concepts. Through phenomenology, this concept receives a completely new face: it is a highly comprehensive title for an intentional essential-lawfulness of the constitution of the pure ego, a realm of the innate a priori, without which an ego as such is therefore inconceivable. Only through the phenomenology of genesis does the ego become understandable as an infinite, in the unity of universal genesis linked together coherence of synthetically belonging performances — in levels that must throughout conform to the universal persisting form of temporality.

### § 40. Transition to the Question of Transcendental Idealism

With the reduction of phenomenological problematic to the unitary total title of the (static and genetic) constitution of the objectualities of possible consciousness, phenomenology appears legitimately to characterize itself also as transcendental theory of knowledge. The problem of the traditional theory of knowledge is that of transcendence. It wishes to clarify the principled possibility of cognition. The problem grows out of the natural attitude: I find myself as a human being in the world and at the same time as experiencing and scientifically cognizing it. Everything that is for me is so thanks to my cognitive consciousness. How can this entire play, running its course in the immanence of conscious life, gain objective significance? How can evidence claim to be more than a consciousness-character in me?

### § 41. The Genuine Phenomenological Self-Interpretation of the "Ego Cogito" as "Transcendental Idealism"

What does the transcendental self-reflection of phenomenology have to say? Nothing other than that this whole problem is countersensical — a countersense into which Descartes himself had to fall because he missed the genuine sense of his transcendental *epochē*. As soon as one proceeds to uncover, in systematic self-reflection as a pure ego, one's entire consciousness-field, one recognizes that everything existing for it is something constituting itself in it, and further that every kind of being has its particular constitution. Transcendence in every form is an immanent being-character constituting itself within the ego. Every conceivable sense, every conceivable being, whether called immanent or transcendent, falls within the domain of transcendental subjectivity, as the sense- and being-constituting subjectivity. To wish to grasp the universe of true being as something standing outside the universe of possible consciousness is nonsensical.

In this systematic concretion, carried through, phenomenology is eo ipso transcendental idealism, though in a fundamentally new sense: not psychological idealism, not one that wishes to derive a meaningful world from senseless sensual data; not Kantian idealism, which believes it can keep open at least as a limit-concept the possibility of a world of things in themselves — but an idealism that is nothing more than consistently carried-through self-interpretation of my ego as the subject of every possible cognition. This idealism is not a product of playful argumentation; it is the interpretation of sense carried out in actual work on every type of being conceivable for me. The proof of this idealism is therefore phenomenology itself.

## Fifth Meditation: Uncovering the Transcendental Sphere of Being as Monadological Intersubjectivity

### § 42. Exposition of the Problem of the Experience of Others in Contrast to the Objection of Solipsism

Husserl takes up the most pressing objection to transcendental phenomenology: if I, the meditating ego, reduce myself through the phenomenological *epochē* to my absolute transcendental ego, have I not become the *solus ipse*? Upon closer consideration: the transcendental reduction binds me to the stream of my pure consciousness-experiences and to the unities constituted through their actualities and potentialities. These unities seem inseparable from my ego. But how then with other egos, which are not mere representations in me but, in their very sense, Others? We must gain insight into the intentionality in which the *alter ego* announces and verifies itself, rather than resorting to dialectical arguments or supposedly metaphysical hypotheses whose purported possibility may turn out to be complete nonsense.

### § 43. The Noematic-Ontic Mode of Givenness of the Other as Transcendental Clue

Initially, I have in the experienced Other the transcendental clue. Others are experienced as actually existing world-objects — not as mere natural things, but as psychically governing in their respective natural bodies — and at the same time as subjects for this world, as experiencing this same world that I myself experience. In any case, within me, within my transcendentally reduced pure conscious life, I experience the world including the Others — not as my private synthetic formation, but as intersubjective, as a world existing for everyone, accessible to everyone in its objects. The clarification of how this is possible requires that I hold firmly to the fact that every sense that any being has for me is sense in or from my intentional life, from its constitutive syntheses.

### § 44. Reduction of Transcendental Experience to the Sphere of Ownness

If the transcendental constitution of foreign subjects is in question, then the sense in question cannot yet be that of objective, worldly-existing Others. We must initially exclude from the thematic field all constitutive performances of intentionality related to foreign subjectivity and delimit the total coherence of that intentionality in which the ego constitutes itself in its ownness. In the abstraction, a unitarily connected stratum of the phenomenon World remains — a stratum that is the essentially foundational one. Among the ownness-reduced bodies, I find in unique distinction my living body (*Leib*) — the sole one that is not merely a body (*Körper*) but precisely a living body, the sole object to which I experientially assign fields of sensation, and in which I immediately govern and act.

### § 45. The Transcendental Ego and the Ownness-Reduced Self-Apperception as Psychophysical Human Being

Everything transcendentally proper to me as this ultimate ego enters, by virtue of mundanizing self-apperception, into my soul as psychic. The mundanizing apperception I find before me, and can now from the soul as phenomenon and as part in the phenomenon Human Being go back to myself as the universal, absolute, transcendental ego. If I then as this ego reduce my phenomenon of the objective world to my ownness and add whatever else I find as my own, this total ownness of my ego is to be found again in the reduced world-phenomenon as the ownness of my soul, only that here it is a transcendentally secondary component of my world-apperception.

### § 46. Ownness as the Sphere of the Actualities and Potentialities of the Stream of Experience

Husserl now works out the positive characterization of the own. If, in transcendental reduction, I reflect upon myself, I am for myself given perceptually with an open, endless horizon of still undisclosed inner properties. My own discloses itself through explication, and has from its performance its original sense. A first result of explikating my own essential horizon is that I encounter my immanent temporality and therewith my being in the form of an open infinity of a stream of experience — and of all my properties enclosed therein, including my explications themselves. All the possibilities of the kind "I can or could set these or those experiential series in motion" — including looking ahead or looking back, penetrating disclosingly into the horizon of my temporal being — evidently belong essentially to myself.

### § 47. The Intentional Object Also Belongs to the Full Monadic Concretion of Ownness. Immanent Transcendence and Primordial World

What is essentially my own extends not only to the actualities and potentialities of the stream of experience but also to the constituted unities — wherever the constituted unity is concretely inseparable from the original constitution itself. This includes transcendent objects of outer sensibility, insofar as they are actually originally constituted through my own sensibility, my own apperceptions, as concretely inseparable from them. The entire reduced world — resulting from the exclusion of sense-components of the foreign — belongs to this sphere. In this way it becomes clear that the concretely taken ego has a universe of the self-own that is to be disclosed through an apodictic, at least apodictically form-pre-delineating, original interpretation of its apodictic *ego sum*.

### § 48. The Transcendence of the Objective World as Higher-Level Transcendence

That this own-essence can contrast for me with something other, or that I can become conscious of another — of something foreign — presupposes that not all my own modes of consciousness belong to the circle of those that are modes of my self-consciousness. There must, in contrast to self-experience, be still other experiences in systems of concordance. What is now to be made understandable is how, at the higher founded level, the sense-giving of objective transcendence comes about — and this as experience, not as the disclosure of a temporally progressing genesis but as a static analysis. The objective world is for me always already finished, the givenness of my continuing objective experience.

### § 49. Outline of the Course of Intentional Interpretation of the Experience of Others

The being-sense "objective world" constitutes itself on the substratum of my primordial world in several levels. First is the constitutive level of the Other or Others as such. Thereby a general raising of sense on my primordial world takes place, whereby it becomes the appearance of an objective world — the one and same world for everyone, myself included. Thus, the intrinsically first thing that is foreign is the other ego. And this makes constitutively possible a new infinite domain of the foreign: an objective nature and an objective world in general. In the very sense of this constitution, the Others do not remain isolated; rather, there constitutes itself a community of egos — ultimately a community of monads — and indeed one that constitutes the one and same world. To the constitution of the objective world there essentially belongs a harmony of the monads.

### § 50. The Mediate Intentionality of the Experience of Others as "Appresentation" (Analogical Apperception)

The actual difficulties pertain to the transcendental clarification of the experience of Others. A certain mediacy of intentionality must be at work here — one proceeding from the primordial world, which presents a being-also-there that yet never is itself-there. What is involved is appresentation. Such appresentation already occurs in outer experience (front side appresenting back side), but the appresentation leading into another original sphere must a priori exclude the possibility of fulfilling presentation. The underlying ground of genuine perception is provided by the continuously ongoing perception of the primordially reduced world. The motivation for the appresentation runs through the similarity-association of a body in my primordial sphere with my own body, which furnishes the motivational foundation for the analogizing apperception of the former as another body — and this is indeed not an analogical inference but an apperception.

### § 51. "Pairing" as an Associatively Constitutive Component of the Experience of Others

Pairing — the configured appearance as a pair, and in further consequence as a group or plurality — is a universal phenomenon of the transcendental sphere. It is a primal form of passive synthesis which we designate as association, in contrast to the passive synthesis of identification. In a pairing association, two data given intuitively as standing out within the unity of a consciousness found a unity of similarity and thus are constituted as a pair. There occurs an intentional reaching-over, a living mutual awakening, a mutual overlapping covering according to objective sense. As its performance, there takes place a transfer of sense upon the paired.

### § 52. Appresentation as a Kind of Experience with Its Own Style of Verification

But how is such an apperception possible and not immediately cancelled? The appresentation is interwoven with an original presentation (of the Other's body as a piece of my ownness-given nature). In this interweaving, foreign body and foreign governing ego are given in the manner of a unitary transcending experience. Regarding the experience of Others, its fulfilling-verifying continuation can proceed only through synthetically concordantly progressing new appresentations and through the way they owe their being-validity to the motivational connection with the constantly belonging presentations. The experienced foreign body attests itself continuously as body only in its changing, concordant comportment such that this has its physical side that indicatively appresents the psychic.

### § 53. The Potentialities of the Primordial Sphere and Their Constitutive Function in the Apperception of the Other

My corporeal body has the givenness-mode Here; every other body has the mode There. But I can change my position so that I could transform every There into a Here. Therein lies that I would see the same things from there in correspondingly different modes of appearance. This contributes essentially to clarifying the associative performance of the experience of Others: I apperceive the Other not simply as a duplicate of myself, but with such modes of appearance as I myself would have if I were there. The Other is appresentatively apperceived as the ego of a primordial world, in whose monad his body is constituted in the mode of the absolute Here, as the functioning center of his governing.

### § 54. Explication of the Sense of the Foreign-Experiencing Appresentation

The association constituting the mode "Other" is not direct. The body in my primordial environment does not pair directly with the mode of appearance my body actually has (in the mode Here), but rather reproductively awakens a similar one belonging to the constitutive system of my body as a body in space — it recalls my bodily appearance if I were there. Through the associative covering of the data, a higher-level association occurs. The analogically apprehended object is analogically adapted to the analogous appearance. What is appresented from that body there is not my own psychic life; I am bodily Here, the center of a primordially oriented world. My entire primordial ownness has the content of the Here and not of that determinate There. The foreign body in the There enters a pairing association with my body in the Here and becomes the kernel of an appresentation: the experience of a co-present ego, which must necessarily be appresented as now co-present in the mode There.

### § 55. Association of Monads and the First Form of Objectivity: Intersubjective Nature

More important is the clarification of the community that progressively forms itself through the experience of Others. The first thing constituted in the form of community, and the foundation of all other intersubjective communalities, is the commonality of nature — together with that of the foreign body and the foreign psychophysical ego in pairing with one's own. Through the appresentation itself and its necessary unity with the presentation functioning alongside it, the identity-sense of my primordial nature and of the appresentatively other nature is already necessarily established. It is therefore legitimate to speak of perception of Others and of the objective world — although this perception takes place exclusively within my ownness-sphere. My ego constitutes in itself an other ego, and indeed as existing.

### § 56. Constitution of the Higher Levels of the Intermonadic Community

Hereby the first and lowest level of community is clarified. From me, constitutively the primal monad, I gain the other monads as psychophysical subjects. In the sense of a human community, there lies a reciprocal being-for-one-another that brings with it an objectivating equalization: I and everyone as a human being among other human beings. A corresponding open community of monads — transcendental intersubjectivity — is constituted purely in me from sources of my intentionality.

### § 57. Clarification of the Parallelism of Inner-Psychic and Egological-Transcendental Interpretation

Not difficult to clarify from here is the necessary parallelism of inner-psychic and egological-transcendental interpretations. The pure soul is a self-objectivation of the monad occurring within the monad. Eidetically and empirically, a pure psychology — one exclusively interpreting the intentional own-essence of a soul — corresponds to a transcendental phenomenology and conversely.

### § 58. Problem-Articulation in the Intentional Analytic of Higher Intersubjective Communities. Ego and Environment

The constitution of humanity is not yet completed with the foregoing. Social acts, through which all human personal communication is established, must also be accounted for. With the actual social communalization, there constitute themselves within the objective world the various types of social communities — among them those that have the character of personalities of a higher order. Furthermore, there comes into consideration the constitution of the specifically human cultural environment for every human being and every human community, and its though restricted kind of objectivity. Every human being first understands, at its core and with an undisclosed horizon, his own concrete environment, his culture, as a human being of the historically shaping community. A deeper understanding requires stepwise access to further layers of the past and the present.

### § 59. Ontological Explication and Its Place in the Whole of Constitutional Transcendental Phenomenology

Through the connected analyses and pre-delineations, we have gained fundamental insights. Starting from the experiential world given as existing and exercising transcendental reduction, we have separated the self-constitution of the ego in its primordial own-essentiality from the constitution of all foreign entities from sources of own-essentiality. Every a priori gained straightforwardly, though it is a system of the a priori, first becomes philosophically intelligible when the constitutive problematic is opened up as that of the specifically philosophical level — when the natural epistemic ground is exchanged for the transcendental. Therein lies that everything natural, straightforwardly pre-given, is rebuilt in new originality and not merely subsequently interpreted as already final.

### § 60. Metaphysical Results of Our Interpretation of the Experience of Others

These results are metaphysical, if it is true that ultimate cognitions of being are to be called metaphysical. But nothing less than metaphysics in the usual sense is here in question. My ego, apodictically given to me, can a priori only be a world-experiencing ego by being in community with others of its kind. There can therefore be only a single community of monads — that of all coexisting monads — and accordingly only a single objective world, only one objective time, only one objective space, only one nature. Leibniz is right that infinitely many monads and groups of monads are conceivable, but not all these possibilities are compossible, and not multiple worlds can exist simultaneously.

### § 61. The Traditional Problems of "Psychological Origin" and Their Phenomenological Clarification

Within the human and animal world, the familiar problematic of psychophysical genesis confronts us, including the problem of psychic genesis. The constitutive problem of the sense-thing and of spatiality is the problematic that descriptively pursues the synthetic connections of thing-appearances. For the problems of the "psychological origin of the space-representation, the time-representation, the thing-representation," no physics, physiology, or experimentally moving psychology has anything to say. These are exclusively problems of intentional constitution. Phenomenology means also for psychology a principled new formation. The complex of research concerning the primordial world may also be designated as "transcendental aesthetics" in a very expanded sense, taking over the Kantian title.

### § 62. Survey Characterization of the Intentional Interpretation of the Experience of Others

The objection of solipsism dissolves in its groundlessness after the interpretations carried out. At no point has the transcendental attitude been abandoned. Our theory of the experience of Others wished to be nothing more than the interpretation of its sense from its constitutive performance. What I concordantly verify as Other is, in transcendental attitude, eo ipso the existing Other, verified within the experiencing intentionality of my ego. In me I experience and cognize the Other; in me it constitutes itself — appresentatively mirrored and not as original. The seeming solipsism is dissolved, although the proposition retains its fundamental validity that everything that is for me draws its being-sense exclusively from me myself. This idealism proved to be a monadology that draws its content purely from the phenomenological interpretation of the transcendental experience disclosed in the transcendental reduction — from the most originary evidence, in which all conceivable evidences must be grounded. Phenomenological interpretation is therefore nothing like metaphysical construction; it stands in the sharpest contrast to all such by its procedure within the framework of pure intuition.

## Conclusion

### § 63. The Task of a Critique of Transcendental Experience and Cognition

In the investigations of this meditation and already of the two preceding ones, we have moved on the ground of transcendental experience. We have trusted it thanks to its originally lived-through evidence. We have thereby lost sight of the demand for apodictic cognition, but have by no means dropped it. Only we preferred to draw the outlines of the enormous problematic of the first — in its own way still naively laden — phenomenology, rather than entering into the further and ultimate problematic: that of phenomenology's self-critique with a view to determining range, limits, and modes of apodicticity.

### § 64. Closing Word

Our meditations have essentially fulfilled their purpose: to demonstrate the concrete possibility of the Cartesian idea of a philosophy as a universal science out of absolute grounding. Daily practical life is naive; it is an experiencing-into, thinking-into, valuing-into, acting-into the pre-given world. All intentional performances are carried out anonymously. Not otherwise in the positive sciences — they are naivetes of a higher level, without the intentional performances from which everything ultimately springs having been interpreted. There is only one radical self-reflection: the phenomenological. A consistently pursued phenomenology constructs a priori the forms of conceivable worlds in correlation with the constitutive a priori of the constituting intentional performances. This universal concrete ontology would be the intrinsically first science-universe out of absolute grounding.

So the idea of a universal philosophy is realized — not as a universal system of deductive theory, as if everything existing stood in the unity of a calculation, but as a system of phenomenological, thematically correlative disciplines, on the lowest ground not of the axiom *ego cogito* but of a universal self-reflection. The necessary way to cognition ultimately grounded in the highest sense is that of universal self-cognition, first monadic and then intermonadic. A radical and universal continuation of Cartesian Meditations — of universal self-cognition — is philosophy itself and encompasses all self-responsible genuine science.

The Delphic word γνῶθι σεαυτόν has gained a new meaning. Positive science is science in world-lostness. One must first lose the world through *epochē* in order to regain it in universal self-reflection. *Noli foras ire*, says Augustine, *in te redi; in interiore homine habitat veritas*.
