# Edmund Husserl, *Logical Investigations*, Volume I: Prolegomena to Pure Logic (1900)

## Paragraph-by-Paragraph Analysis

---

## Preface (*Vorwort*)

### Paragraph 1

Husserl opens the *Prolegomena* by situating its genesis within his own intellectual biography. The logical investigations whose publication he begins with these prolegomena arose, he tells us, out of unavoidable problems (*unabweisbare Probleme*) that repeatedly obstructed and ultimately interrupted his longstanding efforts toward a philosophical clarification of pure mathematics. Beyond the questions concerning the origin of the fundamental concepts and basic insights of mathematics, these efforts also concerned the difficult questions of mathematical theory and method. What should have appeared easily comprehensible and transparent on the basis of traditional or variously reformed logic — namely, the rational essence of deductive science with its formal unity and symbolic methodology — presented itself to Husserl as dark and problematic when he studied the actually given deductive sciences. The deeper he penetrated analytically, the more he became conscious that the logic of his time could not reach the actual science it was supposedly called to clarify.

This paragraph is decisive for understanding the *Prolegomena* as a whole: it establishes that Husserl's critique of psychologism did not arise from any abstract philosophical programme but from a concrete encounter with the gap between the science of logic and the sciences logic was meant to illuminate. The confession that the logic of his day could not reach actual science is the motivating aporia of the entire volume.

### Paragraph 2

Husserl specifies the particular mathematical domain that forced the most general reflections: the logical investigation of formal arithmetic and the theory of manifolds (*Mannigfaltigkeitslehre*), a discipline and method that reaches beyond all particularities of special number-forms and extension-forms. This work compelled him to considerations of a very general kind that rose above the narrower mathematical sphere and strove toward a general theory of formal deductive systems.

Here Husserl signals that his path to logic was not through traditional syllogistic but through the foundations of modern mathematics — specifically, through the highly abstract regions of formal arithmetic and the theory of manifolds (what we would today associate with set theory and abstract algebra). The reference to *Mannigfaltigkeitslehre* connects Husserl to the Riemannian and Cantorian traditions. His philosophical problem is fundamentally about what makes formal-deductive systems possible as such.

### Paragraph 3

Of the series of problems that pressed themselves upon him, Husserl singles out one for closer description. The evident possibility of generalising or modifying formal arithmetic — such that without essential change in its theoretical character and its computational methodology it can be led beyond the quantitative domain — had to awaken the insight that the quantitative does not at all belong to the most general essence of the mathematical or the "formal" and the calculatory method grounded in it. When he then encountered in "mathematising logic" a genuinely quantity-free mathematics — an unassailable discipline of mathematical form and method, treating partly the old syllogisms and partly new inferential forms foreign to the tradition — the important problems took shape for him: problems concerning the general essence of the mathematical as such, the natural connections or possible boundaries between the systems of quantitative and non-quantitative mathematics, and in particular the relation between the formal element of arithmetic and the formal element of logic. From here he had to press forward to the more fundamental questions about the essence of the form of cognition (*Erkenntnisform*) as distinct from the matter of cognition (*Erkenntnismaterie*), and about the sense of the distinction between formal (pure) and material determinations, truths, and laws.

This paragraph is philosophically rich. Husserl is tracing how his engagement with the mathematical logic of his time (Boole, Schröder) revealed that the mathematical as such is not essentially bound to quantity. This opened the question of the relationship between formal logic and formal mathematics — the question that would later culminate in his conception of a "pure theory of manifolds" (*reine Mannigfaltigkeitslehre*) in Chapter 11. The distinction between *Erkenntnisform* and *Erkenntnismaterie* — the form and matter of knowledge — already anticipates the central architectonic distinction of the *Prolegomena*.

### Paragraph 4

But in a quite different direction as well, Husserl found himself entangled in problems of general logic and epistemology. He had started from the prevailing conviction that it is psychology from which logic in general, and especially the logic of the deductive sciences, must hope for its philosophical clarification. Accordingly, psychological investigations occupy a very broad space in the first (and only published) volume of his *Philosophy of Arithmetic*. This psychological foundation (*psychologische Fundirung*) never fully satisfied him in certain connections.

Husserl is here making a crucial autobiographical confession: the *Philosophy of Arithmetic* (1891) was his own exercise in psychologism. The *Prolegomena* is thus in part an act of self-critique — a public reckoning with his own earlier methodological commitments. This lends the anti-psychologistic arguments a particular force and sincerity.

### Paragraph 5

Where it was a question of the origin of mathematical representations or the practically determined elaboration of methods, the accomplishment of psychological analysis seemed clear and instructive. But as soon as a transition was made from the psychological connections of thinking to the logical unity of thought-content (the unity of theory), no proper continuity or clarity could be established. This troubled him all the more because of the principled doubt about how the objectivity of mathematics and of all science could be reconciled with a psychological grounding of the logical. Since his whole method — to clarify given science logically through psychological analyses — was thus shaken, he saw himself driven to ever more general critical reflections on the essence of logic and especially on the relationship between the subjectivity of knowing and the objectivity of the content of knowledge. Left in the lurch everywhere by logic where he hoped for answers to the specific questions he had to put to it, he was finally compelled to set aside his philosophical-mathematical investigations entirely until he could achieve secure clarity in the fundamental questions of epistemology and in the critical understanding of logic as a science.

This is the pivotal paragraph of the Preface. Husserl identifies the exact point where psychologism breaks down: the transition from the psychological connections of the act of thinking (*Denken*) to the logical unity of thought-content (*Denkinhalt*). The psychological analysis of how we think does not and cannot yield the objective, ideal unity that characterises logical and mathematical truths. The tension between the subjectivity of the knowing act and the objectivity of what is known is the fundamental problem that generates the entire *Prolegomena*. This paragraph also establishes the autobiographical-philosophical narrative: Husserl had to abandon his own work in philosophy of mathematics because the foundational questions of logic and epistemology demanded resolution first.

### Paragraph 6

In publishing these attempts at a new founding of pure logic and epistemology, grown over many years of labour, Husserl does so in the conviction that the independence with which he parts from the prevailing logical direction will not be misunderstood, given the serious substantive motives that have guided him. The course of his development has brought it about that he has moved far from the men and works to whom he owes his scientific formation in fundamental logical convictions, and has drawn considerably closer to a number of researchers whose writings he was previously unable to appreciate at their proper value and whom he therefore consulted too little during his work.

Husserl is diplomatically managing his relationship to his teachers (primarily Brentano and Stumpf) and acknowledging the anti-psychologistic tradition (Bolzano, Lotze, Leibniz) that he came to appreciate only through the painful process of abandoning his own psychologism.

### Paragraph 7

He had to forgo any subsequent insertion of comprehensive literary and critical references to related research. As for the frank criticism he has exercised against psychologistic logic and epistemology, he wishes to recall the Goethean saying: "One is against nothing stricter than against recently abandoned errors." Signed: Halle a. S., 21 May 1900, Prof. Dr. E. G. Husserl.

The Goethe quotation is telling. Husserl acknowledges that his critique of psychologism has the severity of a convert's polemic. The implicit message is that precisely because he once held these views himself, he is positioned to criticise them with special force and specificity. The dating places the text at the very turn of the century — a symbolic moment for a work that would redefine the trajectory of European philosophy.

## Introduction (*Einleitung*)

### § 1. The Dispute over the Definition of Logic and the Essential Content of Its Doctrines

#### Paragraph 1

Husserl opens the Introduction by quoting J. S. Mill's observation that there is as great a difference of opinion about the definition of logic as about the treatment of the science itself, and that most writers have used the same words only to express different thoughts. Since Mill made this observation, many decades have passed and significant thinkers have devoted their best energies to logic; yet these sentences still serve as an apt signature of the state of logical science. We are still far from any universal agreement about the definition of logic and the content of its essential doctrines. Under the influence of Mill especially, the psychological direction among the three main orientations in logic — the psychological, the formal, and the metaphysical — has gained a decided preponderance in terms of the number and significance of its representatives. But the two other directions persist, and the disputed questions of principle reflected in the various definitions of logic remain unresolved.

Husserl sets the scene by emphasising that logic is a discipline in crisis — not for lack of effort, but because the fundamental questions about what logic *is* have not been settled. The tripartite division into psychological, formal, and metaphysical orientations maps onto the major schools: the psychologistic (Mill, Sigwart, Erdmann), the formal (the mathematical logicians and the Leibnizian tradition), and the metaphysical (broadly Hegelian or Aristotelian). Husserl will argue that none has correctly identified the proper domain of logic.

#### Paragraph 2

Even within the dominant psychological school, unity of conviction exists only regarding the demarcation of the discipline, its essential goals and methods. But in the doctrines actually taught, and especially in the opposing interpretations of the traditional formulas and doctrines, the saying about *bellum omnium contra omnes* applies. It would be futile to try to delimit a sum of substantive propositions or theories in which we could see the iron stock of the logical science of our epoch and its legacy to the future.

This sharply worded paragraph drives home the point that even the victorious psychologistic school cannot produce consensus on content. The appeal to Hobbes's war of all against all is deliberately provocative: the supposed science of rational thinking cannot rationally settle its own self-understanding.

### § 2. The Necessity of a Renewed Discussion of the Questions of Principle

#### Paragraph 1

Given this state of the science, which does not permit individual conviction to be distinguished from universally binding truth, the return to questions of principle remains a task that must be taken up ever anew. This seems especially to hold for those questions that play the determining role in the dispute among the orientations and thereby in the dispute over the correct demarcation of logic. Interest in these very questions has visibly cooled in recent decades. After Mill's brilliant attacks against Hamilton's logic and Trendelenburg's no less celebrated though less fruitful logical investigations, they seemed largely settled. When the psychological direction then gained its preponderance, all effort concentrated merely on the comprehensive development of the discipline in accordance with the principles accepted as valid. Yet the very fact that so many attempts by significant thinkers to bring logic onto the sure path of a science have failed to achieve a decisive success leaves open the suspicion that the goals pursued are not as clearly worked out as would be necessary for successful investigation.

Husserl here makes a Kantian gesture: just as Kant asked why metaphysics had failed to achieve the status of a science, Husserl asks why logic has similarly failed. The implied diagnosis is that psychologism gained dominance not through philosophical argument but through intellectual fashion, and that its dominance has obscured rather than resolved the foundational questions.

#### Paragraph 2

The conception of the goals of a science finds its expression in the definition of that science. It cannot be Husserl's view that a successful working-out of a discipline must be preceded by an adequate conceptual determination of its domain. The definitions of a science mirror the stages of its development; as the science advances, the knowledge of the conceptual nature of its objects, the delimitation and position of its domain, advances as well. However, the degree of adequacy of the definitions — the conceptions of the domain expressed in them — also exerts a counter-influence on the course of the science itself. This counter-influence can be of lesser or very considerable weight depending on the direction in which the definitions deviate from the truth.

Husserl acknowledges that definitions follow rather than precede scientific practice — a pragmatically realistic position. But he insists that bad definitions can actively impede a science, particularly when they lead to a fundamental misidentification of the domain of inquiry. This sets up the central charge against psychologism: that it has misidentified the domain of logic.

#### Paragraph 3

The domain of a science is an objectively closed unity; it does not lie in our arbitrary choice where and how we delimit fields of truth. The realm of truth is objectively articulated into domains, and research must orient itself according to these objective unities and order itself into sciences. There is a science of numbers, of spatial figures, of animal beings, but not special sciences of prime numbers, trapezoids, and lions — or of all of these taken together.

This paragraph contains an important implicit ontological thesis: the domains of the sciences are not conventional but have an objective basis. The "realm of truth" (*Reich der Wahrheit*) has its own internal articulation, and scientific disciplines must answer to this articulation. This anti-conventionalist position about the unity of the sciences will ground Husserl's later argument that logic has its own proper domain of ideal objects.

#### Paragraph 4

Where a group of cognitions and problems that force themselves upon us as belonging together leads to the constitution of a science, the inadequacy of the demarcation can only consist in the domain-concept being initially grasped too narrowly, so that the chains of grounding connections extend beyond the considered domain and concentrate into a systematically closed unity only in a wider one. Such narrowness of horizon need not adversely affect the fruitful progress of the science.

Husserl concedes that a too-narrow definition of a domain is not necessarily catastrophic — the science can still function within a restricted purview and expand later. This is the less dangerous form of error.

#### Paragraph 5

Far more dangerous is another imperfection in the demarcation of a domain: the confusion of domains (*Gebietsvermengung*), the mixing of heterogeneous elements into a supposed domain-unity, especially when it is grounded in a complete misunderstanding of the objects whose investigation is the essential goal of the intended science. Such an unnoticed *μετάβασις εἰς ἄλλο γένος* can entail the most harmful consequences: the fixing of mistaken goals; the following of methods that are principally wrong because incommensurable with the true objects of the discipline; the confounding of logical strata, such that the truly foundational propositions and theories are displaced, often in the strangest disguises, among entirely alien sequences of thought.

This paragraph identifies the specific danger Husserl will charge psychologism with: not merely narrowing the domain of logic but *confusing* it through a *μετάβασις εἰς ἄλλο γένος* — a "passing over into another kind," as Aristotle called it. By treating the laws of logic as laws about psychological processes, psychologism conflates heterogeneous domains: the ideal domain of logical truths and the empirical domain of mental events. This Aristotelian term is deployed with precision and philosophical self-awareness.

#### Paragraph 6

Husserl invokes Kant's dictum: "It is not augmentation but disfigurement of the sciences when their boundaries are allowed to run into one another." The following investigation hopes to make clear that the previous and especially the psychologically grounded logic of the present day has almost without exception succumbed to the dangers just discussed, and that through the misunderstanding of theoretical foundations and the resulting domain-confusion, the progress of logical knowledge has been essentially impeded.

The Kantian citation serves as an authority-stamp on Husserl's diagnosis. The claim is sweeping: virtually all existing logic has suffered from domain-confusion. This is not a minor corrective but a radical reassessment.

### § 3. The Contested Questions. The Path to Be Taken.

#### Paragraph 1

The traditional disputed questions connected with the demarcation of logic are four: (1) Whether logic is a theoretical or a practical discipline (a "technology" or *Kunstlehre*). (2) Whether it is a science independent of other sciences, especially psychology or metaphysics. (3) Whether it is a formal discipline — whether it has to do only with the "mere form of cognition" or must also take account of its "matter." (4) Whether it has the character of an a priori and demonstrative or an empirical and inductive discipline.

Husserl lays out the four classic disputed questions that define the battle-lines in the philosophy of logic. These questions are not independent — they form an interconnected complex. One's answer to any one of them constrains or influences one's answers to the others.

#### Paragraph 2

All these questions are so intimately connected that one's position on any one of them at least to some extent co-determines one's position on the others. There are really only two parties. One holds that logic is a theoretical, formal, demonstrative discipline independent of psychology. The other takes it to be a technology dependent on psychology, which automatically excludes its having the character of a formal and demonstrative discipline in the sense that arithmetic serves as a model for the opposing side.

Here Husserl simplifies the landscape to its essential polarity: the anti-psychologistic party (logic as theoretical, formal, a priori) versus the psychologistic party (logic as practical technology, dependent on psychology, empirical). This bipartition will structure the entire *Prolegomena*.

#### Paragraph 3

Since Husserl's aim is not so much to participate in these traditional controversies as to clarify the principled differences at play in them, and ultimately to clarify the essential goals of a pure logic, he proposes the following path: Take as a starting point the presently almost universally accepted determination of logic as a *Kunstlehre* and fix its sense and justification. From there, naturally the question arises about the theoretical foundations of this discipline and in particular about its relationship to psychology. This question essentially coincides — if not in its entirety then at least in a main part — with the cardinal question of epistemology concerning the objectivity of knowledge. The result of the investigation will be the singling out of a new and purely theoretical science, which constitutes the most important foundation for any *Kunstlehre* of scientific knowledge and possesses the character of an a priori and purely demonstrative science. This is the science intended by Kant and the other representatives of a "formal" or "pure" logic, but not correctly grasped and determined in its content and scope. As the final success of these deliberations there results a clearly outlined idea of the essential content of the contested discipline.

This paragraph is the roadmap for the entire *Prolegomena*. Husserl will: (1) concede that logic can be understood as a *Kunstlehre*; (2) investigate its theoretical foundations; (3) show that psychology cannot provide these foundations; (4) isolate a purely theoretical, a priori science — "pure logic" — that underlies the normative-practical dimension; and (5) give a clear determination of this pure logic's domain and content. The reference to Kant as having *intended* but not *correctly grasped* this science is important: Husserl positions himself as completing a Kantian project, not as inventing something ex nihilo.

## First Chapter: Logic as a Normative and Especially as a Practical Discipline

### § 4. The Theoretical Incompleteness of the Individual Sciences

#### Paragraph 1

It is an everyday experience that the excellence with which an artist masters his material, and the decided and often sure judgement with which he evaluates works of his art, only quite exceptionally rests on a theoretical knowledge of the laws that prescribe direction and order to the course of practical activities and that simultaneously determine the evaluative standards according to which the perfection or imperfection of the finished work is to be assessed. As a rule, the practising artist is not the one who can give a proper account of the principles of his art. He does not create according to principles and does not evaluate according to principles. In creating, he follows the inner activity of his harmoniously cultivated powers; in judging, the finely developed artistic tact and feeling. But this holds not only of the fine arts but of arts in general in the broadest sense. It thus also applies to the activities of scientific creation and the theoretical evaluation of its results — the scientific grounding of facts, laws, and theories.

Husserl opens the first chapter with an analogy between artistic and scientific practice. Just as artists produce excellent work without explicitly knowing the principles governing their practice, scientists carry out sophisticated theoretical work without necessarily possessing clarity about the ultimate logical foundations of their procedures. The artist-scientist parallel is not merely rhetorical: it establishes that the gap between *doing* science and *understanding the foundations of* science is a real and pervasive phenomenon.

#### Paragraph 2

Even the mathematician, physicist, and astronomer does not need insight into the ultimate grounds of his activity to carry out even the most significant scientific achievements. Although the results possess for him and others the force of rational conviction, he cannot claim to have everywhere identified the ultimate premises of his inferences and to have investigated the principles on which the soundness of his methods rests. But with this is connected the imperfect condition of all sciences. Husserl means here not mere incompleteness — the failure to have explored all truths of their domain — but the lack of inner clarity and rationality that we must demand independently of the extension of the science. Even mathematics, the most advanced of all sciences, may not claim an exceptional position. Its old and still unresolved disputes about the foundations of geometry, and about the justifying grounds of the method of the imaginary, show how little it is the ideal of all science that it is still often taken to be.

The specific examples are telling: the foundations of geometry (recalling the non-Euclidean revolution and the debates about Euclid's parallel postulate) and the "method of the imaginary" (the use of imaginary and complex numbers in real analysis, whose logical justification was then still unclear). These are precisely the problems Husserl encountered in his own mathematical-philosophical work. The deeper point is that even the most rigorous science lacks *philosophical* self-transparency about its own foundations.

#### Paragraph 3

Although the sciences have grown great despite these defects and have helped us to a previously undreamed mastery over nature, they cannot satisfy us theoretically. They are not crystal-clear theories in which the function of all concepts and propositions is fully comprehensible, all presuppositions exactly analysed, and thus the whole elevated above every theoretical doubt.

Husserl sets a very high standard: science should ideally be completely transparent regarding all its presuppositions. The practical success of science does not discharge this philosophical debt. This distinction between practical success and theoretical self-understanding is fundamental to Husserl's entire philosophical project.

### § 5. The Theoretical Supplementation of the Individual Sciences through Metaphysics and Theory of Science

#### Paragraph 1

To achieve this theoretical goal, there is needed, as is fairly generally recognised, first a class of investigations belonging to the domain of metaphysics. Their task is to fix and examine the unexamined and mostly even unnoticed yet highly significant presuppositions of a metaphysical sort that underlie at least all sciences directed at real actuality. Such presuppositions are, for example: that there exists an external world spread out in space and time, where space has the mathematical character of a three-dimensional Euclidean manifold and time that of a one-dimensional orthoid manifold; that all becoming is subject to the law of causality, and so forth. These presuppositions, which belong entirely within the framework of Aristotle's first philosophy, are currently designated — inaptly enough — as epistemological.

Husserl distinguishes the metaphysical presuppositions of the natural sciences (existence of the external world, spatio-temporal structure, causality) from the properly logical-epistemological ones. His remark that these are "inaptly" called epistemological reflects his concern about the confusion of distinct philosophical disciplines — a concern that motivates the entire *Prolegomena*.

#### Paragraph 2

But this metaphysical grounding does not suffice to achieve the desired theoretical completion of the individual sciences; it concerns only the sciences that deal with real actuality, and not all sciences do this — certainly not the purely mathematical sciences, whose objects (numbers, manifolds, etc.) are thought as mere bearers of purely ideal determinations, independent of real being or non-being. It is different with a second class of investigations: these concern all sciences equally, because they are directed, in short, at what makes sciences in general into sciences. Hereby the domain of a new and complex discipline is marked out, whose distinctive feature is to be a science of science, and which is therefore most aptly named a *Wissenschaftslehre* — a theory of science.

The distinction between sciences of real actuality and sciences of ideal objects is fundamental. Mathematics does not require metaphysical grounding (it does not depend on the existence of an external world) — but it still requires *logical* grounding. The *Wissenschaftslehre* or theory of science is the discipline that addresses the conditions for the possibility of science *as such*, regardless of whether the science in question is empirical or ideal. The term *Wissenschaftslehre* deliberately echoes Bolzano, whose 1837 work of that title is one of Husserl's most important sources.

### § 6. The Possibility and Justification of a Logic as Theory of Science

#### Paragraph 1

The possibility and justification of such a discipline — as a normative and practical discipline belonging to the idea of science — can be grounded through the following consideration. Science, as the name says, is directed at knowledge (*Wissen*). Not that science itself is a sum or web of acts of knowing. Objectively, science has its existence only in its literature; only in the form of written works does it have its own, though richly relational, existence with respect to human beings and their intellectual activities. In this form it propagates itself through millennia and outlasts individuals, generations, and nations. It represents a sum of external arrangements which, having arisen from the acts of knowing of many individuals, can in turn pass over into such acts of countless others. It suffices for Husserl that science provides certain proximate preconditions for the production of acts of knowing — real possibilities of knowledge whose actualisation can be regarded by a "normal" or "appropriately gifted" person under known "normal" circumstances as an achievable goal of willing. In this sense, then, science aims at knowledge.

This paragraph contains an important objectivist thesis about scientific knowledge: science is not identical with the mental acts of any individual knower. It has an objective existence in its literature, independently of any particular mind. This already implicitly challenges psychologism, since it locates the being of science not in psychological events but in ideal-objective structures that are publicly accessible. The distinction between the act of knowing and the objective content known will become the fulcrum of the entire anti-psychologistic argument.

#### Paragraph 2

In knowledge (*Wissen*) we possess truth. In actual knowledge — to which we are ultimately referred back — we possess it as the object of a correct judgement. But this alone is not sufficient; for not every correct judgement, every positing or rejection of a state of affairs that agrees with truth, is knowledge of the being or non-being of that state of affairs. For that there is required — if knowledge in the narrowest and strictest sense is meant — evidence (*Evidenz*), the luminous certainty that what we have acknowledged is, or what we have rejected is not; a certainty we must distinguish in a well-known way from blind conviction, from vague and however firmly decided opinion, if we are not to founder on the rocks of extreme scepticism.

Husserl introduces the concept of *Evidenz* — a term that will be central to his entire philosophy. Evidence is not a psychological feeling of certainty; it is the "luminous certainty" (*lichtvolle Gewißheit*) that accompanies the direct intellectual grasp of truth. The distinction between evidence and mere conviction (however strong) is the epistemological foundation for the distinction between genuine knowledge and mere opinion. The warning against scepticism indicates that collapsing evidence into subjective feeling leads to the inability to distinguish knowledge from belief at all.

#### Paragraph 3

At this strict concept of knowledge, ordinary usage does not stop. We speak of an act of knowing also where a clear memory is connected with the judgement that we earlier made an evidence-accompanied judgement of identically the same content, and especially where the memory also concerns a demonstrative course of thought from which this evidence arose and which we are confident we could reproduce together with its evidence. ("I know that the Pythagorean theorem holds — I can prove it"; instead of the latter one might also say: "but I have forgotten the proof.")

This paragraph relaxes the concept of knowledge to include cases where we do not currently possess evidence but remember having possessed it, or are confident we could reproduce it. This realistic concession prevents the concept of knowledge from becoming so strict as to be useless for describing actual scientific practice.

#### Paragraph 4

We thus grasp the concept of knowledge in a broader but still not entirely lax sense, distinguishing it from groundless opinion by reference to certain "marks" or "criteria" of the truth of the assumed state of affairs or the correctness of the judgement made. The most perfect criterion of correctness is evidence; it counts for us as an immediate awareness of truth itself. In the incomparably greater number of cases we lack this absolute cognition of truth; instead we have evidence for the more or less high probability of the state of affairs, at which sufficiently high degrees the firmly decided judgement tends to attach itself. The evidence of the probability of a state of affairs A does not ground the evidence of its truth, but it does ground those comparative and evident evaluations by which we can distinguish, according to positive or negative probability-values, reasonable from unreasonable, better-grounded from worse-grounded assumptions, opinions, and conjectures.

Husserl distinguishes between evidence of truth (direct insight) and evidence of probability (indirect rational support). Most of our knowledge rests on the latter. But even probability-based knowledge is not groundless opinion: we can *evidently* assess comparative probabilities and thus distinguish reasonable from unreasonable beliefs. This account preserves a role for evidence even in empirical science, where absolute certainty about particular facts is rarely available.

#### Paragraph 5

Ultimately, then, all genuine and especially all scientific knowledge rests on evidence, and as far as evidence reaches, so far reaches the concept of knowledge. There remains a duality in the concept of knowledge (or, equivalently, cognition). Knowledge in the strictest sense is evidence that a certain state of affairs obtains or does not obtain. Evidence that a state of affairs is probable to such-and-such a degree is, in respect of *that* (its being probable), knowledge in the strictest sense; but in respect of the state of affairs itself (not its probability), it is knowledge in the broader, modified sense. In this latter sense one speaks, corresponding to degrees of probability, of a now greater, now lesser measure of knowledge, and the knowledge in the pregnant sense — the evidence that S is P — counts as the absolutely fixed ideal limit that the probabilities for the P-being of S asymptotically approach in their ascending series.

Husserl offers a unified account: strict knowledge (direct evidence of truth) and probabilistic knowledge (evidence of probability) are not unrelated species but lie on a continuum, with strict evidence as the ideal limit of increasing probability. This elegant account avoids both the dogmatist's claim that all knowledge must be certain and the sceptic's claim that probable knowledge is not genuine knowledge.

#### Paragraph 6

But more belongs to the concept of science and its task than mere knowledge. If we experience inner perceptions, singly or in groups, and acknowledge them as existing, we have knowledge but not yet science. Nor is the case different with disconnected groups of acts of knowledge in general. Science wishes to give us not mere manifoldness of knowledge but manifoldness in a particular form. Also, material kinship does not yet make up the unity peculiar to science. A group of isolated chemical cognitions would certainly not justify speaking of a chemical science. What is evidently required further is systematic connection in the theoretical sense, and that includes grounding of knowledge and appropriate linkage and ordering in the sequence of groundings.

This paragraph sharply distinguishes knowledge from science: science requires not just an accumulation of true judgements but their systematic interconnection through grounding relations (*Begründungszusammenhang*). A mere aggregate of truths about chemistry does not constitute a science of chemistry. What makes it a science is the theoretical ordering — the way individual truths are connected to one another through relations of logical grounding and explanation. This is a foundational claim about the nature of science that will shape the rest of the *Prolegomena*.

#### Paragraph 7

The essence of science thus involves the unity of grounding-connections, in which the individual cognitions, the groundings themselves, and the higher complexes of groundings that we call theories receive a systematic unity. The purpose of science is not knowledge simpliciter but knowledge of such a scope and form as corresponds in the highest possible perfection to our highest theoretical goals.

Husserl here articulates the ideal of science as *systematic unity* through grounding. Theories are complexes of groundings; science is the systematic ordering of theories. The aspiration toward such unity is not a mere aesthetic preference but belongs to the very concept of science.

#### Paragraph 8

That systematic form appears to us as the purest embodiment of the idea of knowledge, and that we practically strive for it, does not express a merely aesthetic trait of our nature. Science does not wish to be and must not be the field of an architectonic game. The systematic character that belongs to science is not invented by us but lies in the things themselves, where we simply find it and discover it. Science wants to be the means for our knowledge to conquer the realm of truth in the greatest possible scope; but the realm of truth is not an unordered chaos — unity of lawfulness reigns in it. Thus the investigation and presentation of truths must be systematic: it must mirror systematic connections and use them as a stairway of progress to penetrate from given or already attained knowledge into ever higher regions of the realm of truth.

Husserl makes a strong realist claim: the systematic unity of science reflects an objective order in reality (or in the domain of truth). We do not impose systematicity on truths from outside; we discover it. The "realm of truth" (*Reich der Wahrheit*) has its own lawful structure, and scientific method is the progressive disclosure of this structure. This realism about logical order is directly opposed to the psychologistic view that logical structure is merely a reflection of how we happen to think.

#### Paragraph 9

Science cannot dispense with these helpful stairways. Evidence — on which all knowledge ultimately rests — is not a natural accompaniment that arises with the mere representation of states of affairs without any methodical-artistic arrangements. Otherwise people would never have been led to construct sciences. Methodical procedures lose their sense where the intention already carries the success. Why explore grounding relations and construct proofs if one is granted truth in immediate awareness? In fact, however, evidence — which stamps a represented state of affairs as truth — or absurdity — which stamps it as falsehood (and similarly for probability and improbability) — arises immediately only for a relatively very restricted group of primitive states of affairs. For countless true propositions, we grasp them as truths only when they are methodically "grounded" — that is, when we start from certain cognitions and follow a certain path of thought to the intended proposition. There may be manifold paths of grounding for the same proposition, starting from these or those cognitions; but the characteristic and essential circumstance is that there are infinite manifolds of truths that could never be transformed into knowledge without such methodical procedures.

Husserl makes a decisive epistemological point: most truths are not self-evident. Evidence arises only for a limited class of "primitive" states of affairs; for the vast majority of truths, evidence can be obtained only through chains of reasoning — through method. This is what makes science necessary and what makes a *Wissenschaftslehre* (theory of science) necessary: we need to understand the methods by which non-self-evident truths are brought to evidence through grounding in evident truths.

#### Paragraph 10

That we need groundings — that we need to go beyond the immediately evident and thus trivial in order to advance in knowledge — is what makes sciences possible and necessary, and with sciences also a theory of science, a logic. If all sciences proceed methodically in the pursuit of truth and employ more or less artificial aids to bring to cognition truths or probabilities that would otherwise remain hidden, then the comparative study of these methodical aids — in which the insights and experiences of countless generations of researchers are stored up — ought to furnish the means for establishing general norms for such procedures and likewise rules for their inventive construction for the various classes of cases.

This paragraph completes the justification for a *Wissenschaftslehre*. Since all sciences employ methods, and since methods can be compared and generalised, there is both the possibility and the need for a discipline that studies scientific method as such. This discipline — logic as *Wissenschaftslehre* — will extract the general norms embedded in successful scientific practice.

### § 7. Continuation. The Three Most Significant Peculiarities of Groundings

#### Paragraph 1

To go somewhat deeper into the matter, let us consider the most significant peculiarities of those remarkable thought-processes we call groundings. First, in relation to their content, they have the character of fixed structures. We cannot, in order to arrive at a certain cognition (e.g. the Pythagorean theorem), choose entirely arbitrary cognitions from those immediately given as starting points, nor may we in the further course insert or delete arbitrary thought-elements: if the evidence of the proposition to be grounded is to actually light up, the grounding must truly be a grounding.

The first "peculiarity" of groundings is that they are structurally constrained. Not just any collection of propositions can serve as premises for a given conclusion. Groundings have a determinate logical structure that cannot be arbitrarily altered. This is an obvious point but one that Husserl emphasises precisely because psychologism tends to explain logical structure as a product of psychological habit — which would make it contingent rather than necessary.

#### Paragraph 2

Second, we notice something further. Before any comparative regard for examples of groundings, it might appear conceivable that every grounding is unique in content and form. A caprice of nature might have formed our mental constitution so stubbornly that the familiar talk of manifold grounding-forms would be utterly senseless, and all that could be found in common when comparing any groundings would always be only this one thing: that some proposition S, which is in itself without evidence, acquires the character of evidence when it occurs in connection with certain cognitions P₁, P₂... assigned to it once and for all without any rational law.

Husserl introduces a counterfactual scenario: what if every grounding were sui generis, obeying no general law? In that imagined situation, there would be no such thing as a logical form — each inference would be a brute psychological fact with no generalisable structure.

#### Paragraph 3

But that is not the case. Blind arbitrariness has not thrown together some heap of truths P₁, P₂... and then arranged the human mind so that it must inevitably connect the cognition of S with the cognition of those premises. In no single case is it so. Not arbitrariness and chance but reason and order — that is to say, governing law — rules in grounding-connections. An example makes this clear. When we apply the proposition "an equilateral triangle is equiangular" to a particular triangle ABC, we carry out a grounding that explicitly reads: every equilateral triangle is equiangular; the triangle ABC is equilateral; therefore it is equiangular. Placing alongside this the arithmetical grounding: every decimal number with an even final digit is an even number; 364 is a decimal number with an even final digit; therefore it is an even number — we notice at once that these groundings have something in common, an identical inner constitution, which we express intelligibly in the "inferential form": every A is B; X is A; therefore X is B. Not just these two groundings have this same form, but countless others. Moreover, the inferential form represents a class-concept under which falls an infinite manifold of propositional connections of the sharply defined constitution. At the same time the a priori law holds that every purported grounding that proceeds in accordance with it is also actually a correct one, provided it proceeds from correct premises.

This is the second peculiarity: groundings have *general forms*. The same logical form can be instantiated in infinitely many different material contexts. Moreover, the correctness of inferences that conform to valid forms is guaranteed a priori — not by empirical generalisation but by the very nature of the form. This is a direct anticipation of Husserl's anti-psychologistic argument: if logical correctness is guaranteed by form, then it cannot be explained by psychological laws, which are empirical and contingent.

#### Paragraph 4

And this holds universally. Wherever we ascend from given cognitions by grounding to new ones, the grounding-process possesses a certain form that is common to it with countless other groundings and that stands in a certain relation to a general law that permits all these individual groundings to be justified in a single stroke. No grounding stands isolated. None links cognitions to cognitions without there being stamped upon it — whether in the external mode of connection or in both this and the inner structure of the individual propositions — a definite type that, grasped in general concepts, immediately leads over to a general law pertaining to an infinity of possible groundings.

Husserl generalises the point: every grounding instantiates a form, and every form is associated with a law. This is the principle that will later be articulated as the thesis that logical laws are laws about *forms* — ideal regularities that govern all possible inferences of a given type. No inference is a mere isolated event; it always exemplifies a universal structure.

#### Paragraph 5

Finally, a third point may be highlighted as remarkable. Before comparing the groundings of different sciences, one might think it possible that grounding-forms are bound to cognitive domains. Could it not be that no grounding-form exists that is common to two sciences, e.g. mathematics and chemistry? Yet this is evidently not the case, as already the above example shows. No science in which laws are not applied to singular cases, and thus in which inferences of the form that served us as an example do not frequently occur. The same holds for many other inferential types. Indeed, all other inferential types can be so generalised, so "purely" grasped, that they become free of every essential relation to any concretely delimited cognitive domain.

The third peculiarity is that logical forms are *domain-independent*. The same inferential forms apply across all sciences — chemistry, mathematics, physics, history. This domain-independence is what makes a *general* logic possible, as opposed to a plurality of special logics for individual sciences.

### § 8. The Relation of These Peculiarities to the Possibility of Science and Theory of Science

#### Paragraph 1

These peculiarities of groundings — whose remarkableness does not strike us because we are too little inclined to make the everyday into a problem — stand in evident relation to the possibility of a science and further to a theory of science. That groundings exist does not by itself suffice. Were they formless and lawless — were it not a fundamental truth that all groundings possess a certain "form" that is not peculiar to the here-and-now inference but typical for a whole class, and that the correctness of inferences of that class is guaranteed by their form — and were all this the opposite, then there would be no science.

Husserl draws the consequence: without the lawfulness of logical forms, there could be no science at all. The law-governed character of grounding-forms is a condition of the possibility of science.

#### Paragraph 2

Talk of method, of a systematically regulated progression from cognition to cognition, would then have no sense; every step would be a matter of chance. Premises would happen to come together in consciousness, and evidence would happen to arise. It would be impossible to learn anything from one achieved grounding that could be applied to new groundings of new material. No grounding would have anything exemplary for any other; none would embody a type; and thus no judgement-group, considered as a premise-system, would have anything typical about it that could, in a new case with entirely different "matter," press itself upon us through the laws of ideational association and facilitate the gaining of a new cognition. To search for a proof of a given proposition would have no sense. The cleverest person would have no advantage over the dullest.

This extends the thought-experiment: in a world of lawless grounding, there could be no transfer of logical skills from one domain to another, no heuristics for proof-discovery, and no advantage to logical training. This vividly illustrates what is at stake in the lawfulness of logical form.

#### Paragraph 3

Rich imagination, comprehensive memory, the capacity for strained attention and the like are fine things, but they gain intellectual significance only in a thinking being whose grounding and inventing stands under lawful forms. For it holds generally that in any psychical complex not merely the elements but also the connecting forms exercise associative or reproductive efficacy. The form of our theoretical thoughts and thought-connections can thus prove fruitful. Just as the form of certain premises lets the corresponding conclusion spring forth with special ease, so the form of a proposition to be proved can bring to mind grounding-forms that have earlier yielded similarly formed conclusions. Even if it is not clear and proper memory, it is an analogue — a kind of latent memory, "unconscious excitation" (in Erdmann's sense). The practised thinker finds proofs more easily because the types of proofs have been more deeply engraved through manifold experience.

Husserl here gives a psychologically realistic account of how logical forms function in actual scientific practice — through something like trained pattern-recognition. But — and this is the crucial point — the *psychological* mechanism by which logical forms become available to the thinker is not the same as the *logical* laws that govern those forms. The psychological process of learning to recognise inferential patterns presupposes the objective existence of those patterns. This is the wedge between the psychological and the logical that Husserl will drive home in the rest of the *Prolegomena*.

#### Paragraph 4

To a certain extent, scientific thinking of any kind exercises one for scientific thinking in general; beyond this, mathematical thinking specially predisposes for mathematical thinking, physical for physical, etc. The former rests on the stock of typical forms common to all sciences; the latter on other forms that have their special relation to the particularity of the individual sciences. The peculiarities of scientific tact, of anticipatory intuition and divination, are connected with this.

This paragraph grounds the familiar experience that logical training is partly general and partly domain-specific in the existence of both domain-independent and domain-specific logical forms.

#### Paragraph 5

On the one hand, regulated form makes the existence of sciences possible. On the other hand, the widely prevailing independence of form from the domain of knowledge makes possible the existence of a theory of science (*Wissenschaftslehre*). Were this independence not to hold, there would be only co-ordinate logics corresponding individually to individual sciences, but not a general logic. In truth we find both necessary: theory-of-science investigations that concern all sciences equally, and supplementary special investigations that concern the theory and method of individual sciences.

This paragraph draws together the architectonic conclusion of the preceding analysis: the universality of logical forms is what makes a *general* logic possible, while the existence of domain-specific forms justifies *special* methodologies. Logic as *Wissenschaftslehre* encompasses both.

### § 9. The Methodical Procedures in the Sciences: Partly Groundings, Partly Auxiliary Operations for Groundings

#### Paragraph 1

A supplementation is needed regarding Husserl's restriction to groundings, which do not exhaust the concept of methodical procedure. But groundings have a central significance that will justify the provisional restriction.

This transitional paragraph acknowledges that scientific method is broader than grounding alone, but signals that grounding is the core.

#### Paragraph 2

All scientific methods that do not themselves have the character of actual groundings are either thought-economical abbreviations and surrogates of groundings — which, having once received their sense and value through groundings, in their practical use contain the achievement but not the insightful thought-content of groundings — or they are more or less complicated auxiliary operations that serve the preparation, facilitation, securing, or enablement of future groundings and may not claim a significance independent of and on a par with those scientific fundamental processes.

Husserl classifies all non-grounding methods as either (a) abbreviations/surrogates of groundings (e.g. algorithmic methods that mechanically produce results whose justification lies in a prior grounding) or (b) auxiliary operations that serve groundings (e.g. definitions, notational systems). In either case, groundings remain the fundament.

#### Paragraph 3

An example of the second group: an important prerequisite for securing groundings generally is that thoughts be expressed by means of well-distinguished and unambiguous signs. Language offers a widely applicable sign-system, but it is a highly imperfect instrument for rigorous research. The harmful influence of equivocations on the soundness of inferences is well known. The cautious researcher must therefore employ language with methodical precautions: terms that lack unambiguous and sharp meaning must be defined. In the nominal definition we thus see a methodical auxiliary procedure for securing the groundings — those primarily and properly theoretical procedures.

The example of definition as an auxiliary method illustrates the subordination of all non-grounding methods to the service of grounding. Definitions do not directly advance knowledge; they secure the conditions under which groundings can be reliably carried out.

#### Paragraph 4

Similarly with nomenclature. Short and characteristic designations for important and frequently recurring concepts are indispensable where these concepts could only be expressed very cumbersomely with the original stock of defined expressions; for unwieldy, multiply nested expressions make grounding-operations difficult or even impracticable.

Another example of an auxiliary method in the service of grounding: good notation facilitates the inferential operations that constitute the core of scientific method.

#### Paragraph 5

Similarly, the method of classification may be considered from this perspective, and so on.

A brief remark extending the point to classification.

#### Paragraph 6

Examples of the first group — abbreviations or surrogates of groundings — are the extraordinarily fruitful algorithmic methods, whose peculiar function is to spare us through artificial arrangements of mechanical operations with sensory signs a maximal portion of the actual deductive mental work. These methods gain sense and justification only from the nature of grounding thought. Here also belong the literally mechanical methods (e.g. apparatuses for mechanical integration, calculating machines), and also the methodical procedures for establishing objectively valid experiential judgements (e.g. methods for determining star-positions, electrical resistance, inert mass, etc.). Each such method represents a sum of arrangements whose selection and ordering is determined by a grounding-connection that generally demonstrates that a procedure of this kind must necessarily yield an objectively valid individual judgement, even when carried out blindly.

Husserl makes a striking point about algorithmic and mechanical methods: they are "blind" procedures — they can be executed without understanding — but they are *justified* by a prior grounding that shows why they must yield correct results. The algorithm is a surrogate for insight, not a replacement for it. This is an important philosophical observation about the nature of formal methods in mathematics and science.

#### Paragraph 7

Enough examples. It is clear: every actual advance of cognition is accomplished through grounding; all the methodical arrangements and contrivances that logic treats beyond groundings have their relation to grounding. They owe their typical character — which belongs essentially to the idea of method — to this relation.

The concluding summary: grounding is the core of all scientific method, and all other methods derive their methodical character from their relation to grounding.

### § 10. The Ideas of Theory and Science as Problems of the Theory of Science

#### Paragraph 1

A further supplementation is needed. The *Wissenschaftslehre* has to do not only with the forms and lawfulness of individual groundings (and their associated auxiliary operations). Individual groundings are found also outside of science; therefore individual groundings — or even jumbled heaps of groundings — do not yet make a science. What is required is a certain unity of the grounding-connection, a certain unity in the gradation of groundings; and this unity-form itself has its high teleological significance for the attainment of the supreme cognitive goal toward which all science strives.

Husserl now lifts the discussion from individual groundings to the architectonic level: what makes a *science* as a whole, rather than a collection of individual proofs? The answer is the systematic unity of grounding-connections — the way individual demonstrations are organised into a structured whole.

#### Paragraph 2

The task of the *Wissenschaftslehre* will thus also be to treat of sciences as such-and-such systematic unities — in other words, of what characterises them in form as sciences, what determines their mutual delimitation, their inner articulation into domains and into relatively closed theories, what their essentially different kinds or forms are, and the like. Not only valid and invalid groundings but also valid and invalid theories and sciences are to be distinguished. The task presupposes the investigation of groundings to a considerable extent but is not independent of it.

Husserl here points to a higher-order task for the *Wissenschaftslehre*: not just the analysis of individual inferences but the analysis of theories and sciences as systematic wholes. This anticipates his later concept of a "pure theory of manifolds" (*reine Mannigfaltigkeitslehre*) as the study of possible theory-forms.

### § 11. Logic or Theory of Science as Normative Discipline and as Technology

#### Paragraph 1

Logic — in the sense of a *Wissenschaftslehre* — turns out to be a normative discipline. Sciences are mental creations directed toward a certain goal and therefore also to be assessed according to that goal. Whether a science is truly a science, a method truly a method, depends on whether it conforms to the goal it strives for. Logic wants to investigate what belongs to the genuine, valid sciences as such — what constitutes the idea of science — so that we can measure the empirically given sciences against their idea. In this, logic reveals itself as a normative science, distinguishing itself from the comparative, historical study of sciences as concrete cultural products of their respective epochs.

Logic as *Wissenschaftslehre* is normative because it establishes the standards — derived from the *idea* of science — against which actual sciences are to be measured. The distinction from the *historical* study of science is important: logic does not merely describe how scientists have in fact proceeded but prescribes the standards they ought to meet.

#### Paragraph 2

For this is the essence of a normative science: that it grounds general propositions in which, in relation to a norming ground-measure (e.g. an idea or a supreme purpose), certain characteristics are specified whose possession guarantees conformity to the measure, or conversely, constitutes an indispensable condition for such conformity. What logic as *Wissenschaftslehre* gives us are special criteria, not universal ones — just as therapeutics does not give universal symptoms. In establishing that certain methods M₁, M₂... arise with respect to the supreme goal of the sciences and the factual constitution of the human mind, it expresses propositions of the form: every method of the form M₁ (or M₂...) is a correct method.

Husserl specifies the form of normative propositions in logic: they state that methodical procedures of such-and-such a type are *correct* methods, where correctness is defined by conformity to the idea of science. The analogy with therapeutics (which gives special diagnostic criteria, not a universal test for health) is apt: logic provides specific norms for specific types of scientific procedure.

### § 12. Definitions of Logic Belonging Here

#### Paragraph 1

From this point of view, definitions of logic may be understood that characterise it as a *Kunstlehre* — a technology or practical art. A *Kunstlehre* is understood as a discipline that establishes norms for the achievement of a practical end — here, the end of scientific knowledge. Logic as *Kunstlehre* of thinking, of scientific research, of scientific judgement, of grounding and demonstrating, etc. — all such characterisations point in this direction.

Husserl concedes the legitimacy of defining logic as a *Kunstlehre* — a practical technology of correct thinking. This concession is strategic: he will later argue that even if logic has this practical dimension, it must also contain or rest upon a purely theoretical dimension that is not itself practical.

#### Paragraph 2

Such definitions are compatible and can supplement one another. The various formulations reflect different emphases or aspects of the same practical-normative enterprise. Husserl notes that these definitions are perfectly acceptable as far as they go — the question is whether they exhaust the concept of logic.

Husserl's stance is irenic at this stage: the definitions of logic as *Kunstlehre* are not wrong, merely incomplete.

## Second Chapter: Theoretical Disciplines as Foundations of Normative Ones

### § 13. The Dispute over the Practical Character of Logic

#### Paragraph 1

The preceding discussion has shown that the conception of logic as a *Kunstlehre* or normative discipline directed at scientific knowledge has a certain justification. But the question now arises whether this exhausts the concept of logic, or whether there is not a purely theoretical core that the *Kunstlehre* interpretation fails to capture. This is the old question about the "practical" versus "theoretical" character of logic.

Husserl now turns to the historical debate about whether logic is essentially practical (a technology of correct thinking) or essentially theoretical (a science of the laws of truth). He will argue for the latter.

#### Paragraph 2

Husserl surveys the positions. Many defenders of the view that logic is essentially a *Kunstlehre* insist that it has no proper theoretical content of its own. Others concede that logic contains theoretical elements but deny that these constitute an independent theoretical science. The defenders of a "pure" logic, on the other hand — the Kantians, the Herbartians — argue that there is an independent theoretical discipline underlying the normative-practical superstructure.

The survey maps the intellectual landscape: psychologistic logicians deny or minimise the theoretical core; the formalist and anti-psychologistic tradition (Kant, Herbart, Bolzano) insists on it.

#### Paragraph 3

Husserl notes that the purely theoretical discipline in question should be compared to the relation between pure mathematics and practical calculation or mensuration. Pure mathematics is a theoretical science that provides the foundation for the practical art of calculating; similarly, a "pure logic" would be a theoretical science providing the foundation for the practical art of correct reasoning. And just as the practical art may draw on more than one theoretical discipline (measurement draws on geometry, physics, etc.), so too the *Kunstlehre* of scientific method may draw on psychology, the theory of knowledge, and pure logic — with pure logic being perhaps the most important but not the only foundation.

This paragraph introduces the crucial analogy between pure mathematics/practical calculation and pure logic/practical logic. Just as no one would confuse pure arithmetic with the practical art of reckoning, one should not confuse pure logic (a theoretical science of ideal truths) with the practical art of correct thinking. This analogy will be developed extensively.

#### Paragraph 4

Husserl concedes that the objection might be raised that this is merely a restoration of the Aristotelian-scholastic logic whose worthlessness history has supposedly demonstrated. But perhaps the traditional logic was only a highly incomplete and distorted realisation of the idea of pure logic — a first beginning, commendable in its way. And perhaps the contempt for traditional logic is an unjustified aftereffect of the Renaissance mood. Leibniz, a truly creative theoretical spirit, rejected the anti-scholastic hostility and warmly defended Aristotelian logic, even as he recognised its need for expansion and improvement.

Husserl's defence of the Aristotelian tradition is deliberate: he is arguing that the formal-logical tradition, however imperfect in its historical realisation, was on the right track in pursuing a pure theoretical logic. The abuse it suffered at the hands of the Renaissance and the psychologistic movement was a historical injustice, not a philosophical refutation.

### § 14. The Concept of Normative Science. The Ground-Measure or Principle that Gives It Unity

#### Paragraph 1

Husserl now proposes to establish a thesis of decisive importance: that every normative and likewise every practical discipline rests on one or more theoretical disciplines, insofar as its rules must possess a theoretical content separable from the thought of norming (of the ought), whose scientific investigation belongs to those theoretical disciplines.

This is one of the central theses of the *Prolegomena*: normative disciplines presuppose theoretical ones. The norms of any *Kunstlehre* have a theoretical content that can be extracted and studied in its own right. This is the lever that will prise apart pure logic from psychologistic logic.

#### Paragraph 2

To clarify this, Husserl first considers the concept of a normative science in its relation to a theoretical one. The laws of the former state what *ought to be*, although it perhaps is not and under given circumstances cannot be; the laws of the latter state simply what *is*. The question is what is meant by *Seinsollen* (oughtness) as opposed to simple *Sein* (being).

Husserl sets up the key conceptual distinction: normative propositions say what *ought* to be; theoretical propositions say what *is*. The question is how to understand "ought" (*Sollen*).

#### Paragraph 3

The original sense of "ought," which has reference to some wishing or willing, to a demand or command ("You ought to obey me"), is too narrow. We also speak of an "ought" independent of anyone's wishing or willing. "A warrior ought to be brave" does not mean that we or anyone else wishes or wills or demands this. Rather, it means: only a brave warrior is a "good" warrior, and since the predicates good and bad divide the extension of the concept warrior between them, a warrior who is not brave is a "bad" warrior. Because this value-judgement holds, anyone who demands bravery of a warrior is right to do so.

Husserl analyses normative judgements as equivalent to value-judgements. "A warrior ought to be brave" is equivalent to "A warrior who is not brave is a bad warrior," which in turn is equivalent to "Only a brave warrior is a good warrior." The "ought" is grounded in a value-assessment, not in a subjective wish or command. This analysis separates the logical structure of norms from their psychological motivations.

#### Paragraph 4–8 (abbreviated for continuity)

Husserl proceeds to develop a formal analysis of normative propositions in various forms: "A must be B," "A must not be B," "A need not be B," "A may be B" — showing how each is equivalent to a corresponding value-judgement about good and bad. He shows that every normative proposition presupposes a certain kind of valuation (*Werthhaltung*) through which the concept of "good" (in some determinate sense: useful, beautiful, moral, etc.) for a certain class of objects arises. These normative propositions come in various logical forms: those stating necessary conditions, sufficient conditions, or both necessary and sufficient conditions for the attribution of the positive or negative value-predicate.

The analysis is formal-logical in character. Husserl is showing that the structure of normative propositions can be precisely analysed in terms of logical relations among value-predicates, and that every normative discipline presupposes a fundamental valuation that provides the "ground-measure" or "principle" giving it unity.

### § 15. Normative Discipline and Technology

#### Paragraph 1

A normative discipline becomes a *Kunstlehre* (technology) when it not only evaluates what is given according to a fundamental norm but also provides directions for the *production* of what conforms to the norm. The *Kunstlehre* goes beyond normative assessment to include practical guidance — rules for action.

This distinction between normative science and technology is important for Husserl's architectonic: logic as normative discipline evaluates correctness; logic as *Kunstlehre* prescribes methods for achieving correctness in practice.

### § 16. Theoretical Disciplines as Foundations of Normative Ones

#### Paragraph 1

Husserl now establishes the key thesis: every normative proposition of the form "An A that has the property B is good" or "An A that lacks B is bad" presupposes a theoretical proposition about the relationship between the properties in question and the ground-measure of value. The normative content can be separated from the theoretical content. The investigation of this theoretical content belongs to certain theoretical disciplines.

#### Paragraph 2

What gives a normative discipline its unity is the fundamental norm or value-standard by reference to which all its particular norms are formulated. The theoretical content underlying these norms may be drawn from one or several theoretical disciplines. The question for the logic of logic is: which theoretical disciplines provide the essential foundations for the normative logic?

This is the setup for the decisive question of the *Prolegomena*: is psychology the theoretical discipline that fundamentally underlies normative logic, or is there some other — specifically, a purely logical and ideal — discipline that plays this role?

## Third Chapter: Psychologism, Its Arguments, and Its Position on the Usual Counter-Arguments

### § 17. The Question Whether the Essential Theoretical Foundations of Normative Logic Lie in Psychology

#### Paragraph 1

Husserl states the contested question directly: do the essential theoretical foundations of normative logic lie in psychology? Psychologism answers yes; the anti-psychologistic tradition answers no. The psychologistic view seems natural: since logic deals with thinking, and psychology is the science of thinking, psychology must provide the theoretical basis for logic.

The question is posed with a simplicity that belies its depth. The apparent naturalness of psychologism — reasoning about reasoning is psychology — is precisely what makes it so difficult to dislodge.

### § 18. The Line of Argument of the Psychologists

#### Paragraph 1

The psychologistic argument runs as follows: Whatever logic may be — technology, normative discipline, or both — it deals with mental activities: with presentations, judgements, inferences, proofs, theories, and the like. Since these are all psychological phenomena, the science of these phenomena — psychology — must provide the theoretical foundation for the logical norms concerning them.

Husserl presents the psychologistic argument in its strongest form before undertaking to dismantle it.

### § 19. The Usual Arguments of the Opposing Party and Their Psychologistic Resolution

#### Paragraph 1

The anti-psychologistic counter-arguments typically include: (1) Logic is a prescriptive discipline (it tells us how we *should* think); psychology is descriptive (it tells us how we *do* think). (2) Logic is a priori; psychology is empirical. (3) The laws of logic are exact; psychological laws are at best vague generalisations.

#### Paragraph 2

The psychologistic rejoinders include: (1) The distinction between prescriptive and descriptive does not show that the prescriptive discipline has no psychological foundation. Therapeutics prescribes but is founded on physiology and other descriptive sciences. (2) The distinction between a priori and empirical, or exact and vague, does not show that psychology could not ground exact logical laws, since psychology might eventually discover exact laws.

#### Paragraph 3

The psychologistic rejoinders also include the "circle" argument: the anti-psychologists claim that psychology presupposes logic and therefore cannot ground it (since logic would then be grounding itself through psychology). But the psychologists reply that the same could be said of any discipline: every science presupposes logic in the sense that it employs logical procedures. Confusing *reasoning in accordance with* logical rules and *reasoning from* logical rules (as premises) is the error behind the circularity charge.

Husserl presents the psychologistic counter-arguments with notable fairness. The distinction between reasoning *according to* logical rules (which every science does) and reasoning *from* logical rules (which only logic itself does) is a genuine insight, and Husserl acknowledges its force.

### § 20. A Gap in the Psychologistic Line of Argument

#### Paragraph 1

Undeniably, the anti-psychologists appear to be at a disadvantage with their arguments, and many consider the dispute decisively settled. Yet what should provoke philosophical wonder is the fact that a dispute existed at all and still persists, and that the same arguments are repeatedly raised and their refutations not accepted as binding. If everything were really as clear as the psychologistic side asserts, this situation would be hard to understand, especially since unbiased, serious, and acute thinkers stand also on the opposing side. Perhaps the truth again lies in the right middle; perhaps each party has recognised a good piece of truth but has shown itself unable to demarcate it with conceptual sharpness.

Husserl's suggestion that the truth lies "in the right middle" is diplomatically phrased but philosophically precise: he will argue that the psychologistic party is right that psychology contributes *something* to the foundation of logic, but wrong in thinking it provides the *essential* foundation. The essential foundation belongs to a pure, non-psychological theoretical discipline.

#### Paragraph 2

The psychologistic argument proves only that psychology is co-involved (*mitbetheiligt*) in the foundation of logic, not that it is alone or even predominantly involved, not that it provides the *essential* foundation in the defined sense. The possibility remains open that another science contributes to the foundation in an incomparably more significant way. And here a place may be found for that "pure logic" which according to the opposing party leads its existence independently of all psychology — a naturally delimited, self-enclosed science.

This is the decisive turn: the psychologistic argument, at most, establishes that psychology is *one* contributor to the foundation of logic. It does not and cannot establish that psychology is the *essential* or *primary* contributor. The door is thus opened for pure logic.

---

## Fourth Chapter: Empiricist Consequences of Psychologism

### § 21. Characterisation of Two Empiricist Consequences of the Psychologistic Standpoint and Their Refutation

#### Paragraph 1

Husserl now undertakes a different strategy: instead of directly refuting psychologism's arguments, he examines its *consequences*. If the essential theoretical foundations of logic lie in psychology, and psychology is an empirical, factual science, then certain consequences follow — consequences that Husserl will show to be absurd.

The shift from examining arguments to examining consequences is a powerful polemical move. Even if the psychologistic arguments seem plausible, the position can be refuted by showing that it leads to unacceptable results.

#### Paragraph 2 (First consequence)

First: if the theoretical foundations of logic are empirical and vague (as psychological generalisations necessarily are), then the logical norms grounded in them must also be empirical and vague. But the specifically logical laws — the principle of contradiction, the laws of syllogistic, the principles of probability inference, etc. — are of absolute exactness. Any interpretation that would invest them with empirical indeterminacies or make their validity dependent on vague "circumstances" would fundamentally alter their true sense. They are genuine laws, not "merely empirical" — that is, approximate regularities.

This is one of Husserl's most effective arguments against psychologism. Psychological laws are inherently inexact generalisations (as the late-19th-century state of psychology made clear). Logical laws are absolutely exact. If logical laws were derived from psychological laws, they would have to share psychology's inexactness — but they manifestly do not. Therefore they cannot be derived from psychology.

#### Paragraph 3 (Second consequence)

Second: even if someone denied the universal inexactness of psychological laws and claimed to found logical norms on supposedly exact laws of nature about thinking, the situation would not be much improved. No law of nature is a priori — i.e. recognisable with insight. The only way to ground a natural law is through induction from individual facts of experience. But induction grounds not the validity of the law but only a more or less high probability of its validity. Therefore, all logical laws would have only the rank of mere probabilities. Against this, nothing seems more evident than that the "purely logical" laws are, without exception, valid a priori. Not through induction but through apodictic evidence do they find their grounding and justification. What is justified with insight is not mere probabilities of their validity but their validity or truth itself.

The second argument strikes at the heart of the matter: logical laws are a priori — their validity is grasped with direct intellectual evidence, not inferred from empirical observations. If they were natural laws, they could be known only with probability, never with certainty. But we know the principle of contradiction, for example, with apodictic certainty. Therefore logical laws are not natural laws and cannot be grounded in psychology, which is a natural science.

### § 22. The Laws of Thought as Supposed Natural Laws That in Isolated Efficacy Cause Rational Thinking

#### Paragraph 1

Husserl now examines the specific psychologistic view that the laws of logic are natural laws governing the causal processes of thinking — laws that, when operative in isolation (i.e. without interference from other psychological factors), necessarily produce correct reasoning.

#### Paragraph 2

If this were the case, then deviations from logical norms would be "unnatural" distortions caused by interfering factors — just as an unsupported body falls unless impeded. But this analogy fails: when an object does not fall because it is supported, the law of gravitation is not violated — it remains in force while being counteracted. The psychologistic would have to say the same about logical laws: they are always operative but sometimes overridden. But this makes them causal laws about mental processes, not normative laws about truth and validity.

#### Paragraph 3

The real objection is that this view confuses ideal laws about truth with causal laws about thinking. The principle of contradiction does not say: given certain psychological preconditions, a person will not simultaneously believe P and not-P. It says: P and not-P cannot simultaneously be true. The former is a psychological generalisation (and a vague one at that); the latter is an ideal law about propositions and truth-values, valid with absolute exactness and unconditional necessity.

This is one of the clearest formulations of Husserl's core argument. The equivocation between "laws of thought" understood psychologically (how thinking proceeds) and "laws of thought" understood logically (how thinking *ought to* proceed, i.e. what is valid) is the fundamental error of psychologism.

### § 23. A Third Consequence of Psychologism and Its Refutation

#### Paragraph 1

A third consequence: if logical laws were empirical laws about mental facts, they would have to have empirical content — they would have to assert something about mental facts. But the law of contradiction says nothing whatsoever about facts of consciousness, about judgements as real psychological events. It says: of two contradictory propositions, one is true and the other false. "Propositions" here are not judgements (mental acts) but the ideal contents of judgements. The law of contradiction is a law about propositions and truth, not about mental processes and their regularities.

#### Paragraph 2

One could put the point this way: if the law of contradiction were a psychological law, it would have to be formulated approximately thus: "It is psychologically impossible for anyone to simultaneously hold a belief and its negation." But this is (a) an empirical claim that could be false (perhaps some pathological states involve holding contradictory beliefs), and (b) even if true, it would be a completely different proposition from the logical law, which says nothing about anyone's mental states but only about the incompatibility of contradictory propositions.

### § 24. Continuation

#### Paragraph 1

Husserl extends the analysis. The psychologistic interpretation yields something quite different from the logical principle. The psychologistic version is a vague generalisation about mental life; the logical principle is an exact, unconditionally valid ideal law. The two cannot be identified without a fundamental confusion of ideal and real, of normative validity and causal regularity.

The conclusion of Chapter 4 is clear: psychologism, when its consequences are drawn out, leads to the dissolution of the very distinction between logical truth and empirical generalisation — and thereby destroys the foundations of all science.

## Fifth Chapter: The Psychological Interpretations of the Logical Basic Principles

### § 25. The Principle of Contradiction in the Psychological Interpretation of Mill and Spencer

#### Paragraph 1

Husserl now turns to specific attempts to interpret the fundamental logical principles psychologically, beginning with Mill and Spencer on the principle of contradiction. Mill interpreted the principle of contradiction as asserting that believing and not-believing the same proposition are two mental states that exclude each other — that the affirmation and denial of the same proposition cannot coexist as psychological events.

#### Paragraph 2

But this interpretation is defective. Even granting that the mental states of affirming and denying are psychologically incompatible, this is a claim about our psychological constitution — about what we *can* do mentally — not about the logical incompatibility of contradictory propositions. The logical principle is about truth: contradictory propositions cannot *both be true*. This has nothing to do with whether any mind can or cannot simultaneously entertain them.

### § 26. Mill's Psychological Interpretation of the Principle Yields Not a Law but a Wholly Vague, Scientifically Untested Empirical Proposition

#### Paragraph 1

Husserl presses the point: Mill's version, if taken as an empirical-psychological generalisation, does not even have the status of a confirmed scientific law. It is a vague assertion whose scope and limitations have never been systematically investigated. No one has carried out controlled experiments on whether human beings can or cannot simultaneously affirm and deny the same proposition under all circumstances.

#### Paragraph 2

Moreover, even if such experiments confirmed the generalisation, it would remain a mere empirical regularity — valid "for us" and perhaps not for beings with a different mental constitution. The logical law of contradiction, by contrast, is valid absolutely, for all possible minds, not just for the human species.

### § 27. Appendix: On Some Fundamental Defects of Empiricism

#### Paragraph 1

Husserl pauses for a general methodological reflection on empiricism. Empiricism errs in treating all knowledge as derived from experience and all laws as empirical generalisations. But there are laws (logical and mathematical laws) that are *a priori* — known with apodictic evidence, not with the merely probabilistic support of induction. Empiricism's inability to acknowledge this category of knowledge is its fundamental weakness.

### § 28. Analogous Objections against the Other Psychological Interpretations of the Logical Principle. Equivocations as Sources of Delusion

#### Paragraph 1

Husserl generalises his critique: every attempt to interpret logical principles as laws about mental acts rests on equivocations — systematically trading on the ambiguity between ideal and real, between propositions and judgements, between logical validity and psychological occurrence. He analyses several such equivocations in detail, showing how the psychologistic interpretation in each case substitutes a vague psychological claim for a precise logical one.

### § 29. The Supposed Double-Sidedness of the Principle of Contradiction

#### Paragraph 1

Some writers (Sigwart, for example) claim that the principle of contradiction has two sides: it is simultaneously a natural law of thinking (describing how the mind in fact operates) and a normative law for the logical regulation of thinking. Husserl objects that this "two-sidedness" is illusory: the natural-law interpretation and the normative interpretation yield different propositions altogether, and conflating them obscures the essential nature of the logical principle.

### § 30 (continuation of Sigwart's doctrine)

Husserl examines Sigwart's position in further detail, showing how Sigwart equivocates between descriptive and normative readings of logical laws and how his position, when made precise, collapses into either pure psychologism or a tacit admission of a non-psychological logical law.

## Sixth Chapter: Syllogistic in Psychologistic Illumination. Inferential Formulas and Chemical Formulas

### § 30. Attempts at Psychological Interpretation of the Syllogistic Propositions

#### Paragraph 1

Husserl examines attempts to interpret the laws of syllogistic as psychological laws about how the mind connects judgements. On the psychologistic view, "From all A being B and all B being C, it follows that all A are C" would be a law about how certain mental acts (judgements) generate further mental acts. Husserl argues that this interpretation misses the logical point entirely: the syllogistic law is about the *truth-relationships* among propositions, not about the *causal connections* among mental acts.

### § 31. Inferential Formulas and Chemical Formulas

#### Paragraph 1

Husserl introduces a vivid analogy to drive home the absurdity of psychologistic interpretations of logical laws. If the laws of syllogistic were laws about how judgements combine in consciousness to produce new judgements, then they would be analogous to the laws of chemistry that describe how substances combine to produce new substances. Just as H₂O is a formula for the chemical combination of hydrogen and oxygen, the syllogistic figure Barbara would be a formula for the mental combination of two judgements to produce a third. But this analogy is deeply misleading. Chemical formulas describe causal processes in nature; syllogistic formulas express ideal relationships among propositions. The "combination" of premises to yield a conclusion is not a causal process but a logical relationship — a relationship that holds whether or not any mind ever grasps it.

This analogy is one of Husserl's most memorable polemical devices. By making explicit the absurdity of treating logical laws as psychological chemistry, he shows how far psychologism departs from the actual character of logical truth.

## Seventh Chapter: Psychologism as Sceptical Relativism

### § 32. The Ideal Conditions for the Possibility of a Theory in General. The Strict Concept of Scepticism

#### Paragraph 1

Husserl now introduces a decisive formal argument against psychologism. Every theory, simply by being a theory, must satisfy certain ideal conditions — conditions that belong to the very meaning of the concepts "theory," "truth," "proposition," and the like. Any position that, by its own content, denies or undermines these conditions is *sceptical* in a precise sense: it destroys the very possibility of its own theoretical validity.

This is the structure of a self-refutation argument. Husserl will show that psychologism, when its consequences are drawn out, violates the conditions for the possibility of any theory — including itself.

#### Paragraph 2

Husserl defines scepticism in the strict sense: a theory is sceptical if its content contradicts the conditions of the possibility of any theory at all. This includes theories whose content denies the existence of truth, of logical laws, or of the distinction between correct and incorrect reasoning.

### § 33. Scepticism in a Metaphysical Sense

#### Paragraph 1

Husserl distinguishes the strict, logically precise concept of scepticism he has just defined from the looser, more common use of the term. In common philosophical parlance, "sceptical" is applied to any theory that, on grounds of principle, seeks to demonstrate a considerable restriction on human knowledge — especially if it banishes entire spheres of real being or especially valued sciences (metaphysics, natural science, rational ethics) from the domain of possible cognition.

#### Paragraph 2

Among these "inauthentic" forms of scepticism, one in particular is regularly confused with the genuine, epistemological scepticism Husserl has defined: the metaphysical scepticism that restricts cognition to psychical existence and denies the existence or knowability of "things in themselves." Such theories are metaphysical in character; they have in themselves nothing to do with genuine scepticism, and their thesis is free of all logical and noetic absurdity. Their legitimacy is simply a question of arguments and proofs. Confusions and genuinely sceptical turns arise only through the paralogistic influence of tempting equivocations or through independently fostered sceptical basic convictions.

Husserl's point is sharp and important: Kantian or phenomenalist restrictions on knowledge (denying access to things in themselves) are not sceptical in his strict sense. They do not violate the conditions of the possibility of theory as such. They are substantive metaphysical claims that must be evaluated on their merits. Only theories that undermine the very concept of truth, evidence, or valid inference are sceptical in the logically precise and damning sense Husserl intends. This distinction protects his argument from the charge that it proves too much — that it refutes all forms of epistemological modesty. It does not. It refutes only those positions that, by their own content, make theoretical cognition impossible.

### § 34. The Concept of Relativism and Its Varieties

#### Paragraph 1

Husserl now defines *relativism*: the doctrine that truth is relative — relative to an individual (*individual relativism*) or to a species (*specific relativism* or *anthropologism*). Individual relativism holds that what is true for one person may not be true for another. Specific relativism holds that what is true for humanity may not be true for some other species of rational beings.

### § 35. Critique of Individual Relativism

#### Paragraph 1

Individual relativism — the position of the Protagorean sophist — is self-refuting. If truth is relative to the individual, then the proposition "truth is relative to the individual" is itself only true for the one who asserts it. The relativist cannot coherently claim that relativism is *true* without presupposing the absolute (non-relative) concept of truth he claims to reject.

### § 36. Critique of Specific Relativism and in Particular of Anthropologism

#### Paragraph 1

Specific relativism (*Anthropologismus*) — the view that logical laws are laws of the human mind and that a differently constituted species might have different logical laws — is no better off. If the principle of contradiction is merely a law of human thinking, then it is conceivable that for some other species, contradictory propositions could both be true. But "contradictory propositions are both true" is not merely a claim we happen to reject; it is a proposition that violates the meaning of "true" and "false" altogether. Truth is not something that *could* vary from species to species; a being for whom contradictory propositions were both "true" would simply not mean by "true" what we mean.

#### Paragraph 2

More formally: if the anthropologistic thesis were correct, then truth would be a function of the species' constitution. Different species would have different "truths," different "logics." But then there would be no truth at all in the proper sense — only species-relative beliefs. And the anthropologistic thesis itself would be only a species-relative belief, with no claim to objective validity.

Husserl's argument here anticipates many later arguments against cultural and cognitive relativism. The key move is always the same: relativism about truth, when consistently applied, undermines its own claim to be true.

### § 37. General Remark. The Concept of Relativism in an Extended Sense

#### Paragraph 1

Husserl broadens the concept of relativism beyond the two forms already discussed (individual and specific). Any theory that, in whatever way, makes the truth of logical principles dependent on matters of fact — whether facts about individuals, species, cultures, historical epochs, or any other contingent conditions — is relativistic in an extended sense. Every form of relativism shares the same fundamental defect: it makes truth depend on something that could be otherwise and thereby abolishes the absolute character of truth. A truth that holds only "under certain conditions" or "for certain beings" is, strictly speaking, not a truth at all.

This broadened concept of relativism is designed to catch versions of psychologism that might escape the narrow definitions of individual or specific relativism — for example, views that relativise truth to "the present stage of human development" or "the social conditions of knowledge-production." All such views share the structural defect of making truth contingent.

### § 38. Psychologism in All Its Forms Is a Relativism

#### Paragraph 1

Husserl now delivers the coup de grâce: psychologism in every one of its varieties and individual configurations is nothing other than relativism — even if not always recognised or explicitly admitted as such. It makes no difference whether the psychologism appeals to "transcendental psychology" and believes, as a formal idealism, that it can rescue the objectivity of knowledge, or whether it rests on empirical psychology and openly embraces subjectivism. In either case, logical laws are made to depend on facts about the knowing subject — whether the subject is understood transcendentally or empirically — and this dependence is what constitutes relativism.

#### Paragraph 2

The identification of psychologism with relativism is not a mere polemical gesture; it is a rigorously argued conceptual thesis. The structure of the argument is: (1) psychologism grounds logical laws in psychological facts; (2) psychological facts are contingent — they could have been otherwise; (3) therefore, on the psychologistic view, logical laws could have been otherwise; (4) but this is relativism about logical truth; (5) and relativism about logical truth is self-refuting. Every version of psychologism, however subtle, must somewhere make this move from contingent psychological fact to logical law, and every version thereby inherits the self-refutation of relativism.

### § 39. Anthropologism in Sigwart's Logic

#### Paragraph 1

Husserl undertakes a detailed critical examination of Sigwart's logic as a paradigm case of anthropologism. Sigwart explicitly denies that the norms of logic can be understood independently of the nature of human thinking. For Sigwart, logical laws are norms that express the conditions under which human thinking succeeds in achieving its natural purpose — the attainment of truth. The laws of logic are thus, for Sigwart, expressions of the teleological structure of human cognition.

#### Paragraph 2

Husserl objects that Sigwart's position, however sophisticated, makes truth species-relative. If logical laws express conditions for the success of *human* thinking, then a differently constituted species might have different logical laws — different conditions for the success of *their* thinking. Sigwart would resist this consequence, but Husserl argues it is unavoidable given his premises. The moment logical laws are made to depend on the nature of a particular kind of thinking being, the door to relativism is opened.

#### Paragraph 3

Husserl traces several specific equivocations in Sigwart's text, showing how Sigwart repeatedly slides between a psychological reading (how humans in fact think) and a logical reading (how thinking must proceed to be valid) without noticing the transition. This unacknowledged equivocation is the mechanism by which Sigwart maintains the appearance of a coherent position while in fact oscillating between incompatible claims.

### § 40. Anthropologism in B. Erdmann's Logic

#### Paragraph 1

Husserl turns to Erdmann's logic, which represents an even more explicit form of anthropologism. Erdmann directly addresses the relativistic consequences that might follow from his position and attempts to defuse them — but, Husserl argues, unsuccessfully.

#### Paragraph 2

Erdmann acknowledges that if logical laws depend on the human constitution, then beings with a different constitution might have different logical laws. But he argues that this is a merely theoretical possibility with no practical significance: we have no reason to believe such beings exist, and even if they did, their "logic" would be incommensurable with ours and therefore irrelevant to us. Husserl replies that this response misses the point entirely. The question is not whether alien logicians actually exist but whether the very *concept* of truth allows for species-relativity. If truth is absolute — if what is true is true regardless of who thinks it — then the possibility of species-relative truth is not merely unlikely but *incoherent*.

#### Paragraph 3

Husserl analyses Erdmann's position in extensive detail, showing that Erdmann's attempt to restrict logical validity to the human species while maintaining that logical laws are "objectively valid" is self-contradictory. Objective validity that holds only for one species is not objective validity at all. Erdmann is caught between the psychologistic commitments that make him relativise logic to human constitution and the philosophical intuition that logical laws are more than species-specific habits of thought. He cannot consistently maintain both.

## Eighth Chapter: The Psychologistic Prejudices

### § 41. First Prejudice

#### Paragraph 1

Husserl identifies the first psychologistic prejudice: the claim that prescriptions regulating the mental activity of knowing are obviously psychological in character. Since logic prescribes how we ought to judge, infer, and prove, and since judging, inferring, and proving are mental activities, the theoretical investigation of these activities belongs to psychology.

#### Paragraph 2

Husserl's response: the proposition "the prescriptions of logic regulate mental activities" does not entail "the *theoretical foundations* of these prescriptions belong to psychology." A prescription for agriculture (e.g. regarding crop rotation) regulates farming activities, but its theoretical foundation may lie in chemistry, botany, meteorology, etc. — not in a science of farming as such. Similarly, the prescriptions of logic regulate mental activities, but the *theoretical truths* that ground these prescriptions may belong to a quite different science — a pure science of ideal relations among propositions, truths, and inferential forms.

### § 42. Explanatory Elaboration

#### Paragraph 1

Husserl elaborates: the crucial question is whether the theoretical content of logical laws is psychological. The law "from 'all A are B' and 'all B are C' follows 'all A are C'" has a theoretical content: a certain relation of implication holds among propositions of certain forms. This content is about propositions and their relationships, not about mental acts. That we *grasp* these propositions in mental acts does not make the propositions themselves psychological entities — just as the fact that we perceive physical objects through perceptual acts does not make physical objects psychological.

### § 43. Review of the Idealist Counter-Arguments

#### Paragraph 1

Husserl reviews the counter-arguments offered by the idealist (anti-psychologistic) tradition and acknowledges their defects. In particular, the Kantians and Herbartians often failed to clearly articulate what they meant by "pure logic" and failed to distinguish it sharply from normative logic. Their definitions were often confused, and their actual logical work frequently mixed pure and applied elements indiscriminately. Nevertheless, their fundamental insight — that there is a domain of ideal logical truths irreducible to psychology — was correct.

### § 44. Second Prejudice

#### Paragraph 1

The second prejudice: logic deals with presentations, judgements, inferences, proofs, etc. But these are psychological phenomena; therefore the theoretical science of these phenomena — psychology — must provide the foundation for logic.

#### Paragraph 2

Husserl's response: there is an equivocation in the word "judgement" and its cognates. "Judgement" can mean the mental act of judging (a real psychological event) or the propositional content judged (an ideal meaning). Logic deals with judgements in the second sense — with propositions, with the ideal contents of mental acts — not with the acts themselves. The same equivocation affects "inference," "proof," "concept," etc. Once this equivocation is exposed, the psychologistic argument loses all force.

### § 45. Refutation: Even Pure Mathematics Would Become a Branch of Psychology

#### Paragraph 1

Husserl offers a reductio: if the psychologistic argument were valid, it would prove too much. Numbers are also objects of mental acts (counting, calculating); therefore, by the same logic, arithmetic would be a branch of psychology. But no one seriously maintains that the laws of arithmetic are laws about mental counting-processes. Therefore the argument-form is invalid, and the analogous conclusion about logic is equally unjustified.

### § 46. The Research Domain of Pure Logic, Like That of Pure Mathematics, Is an Ideal One

#### Paragraph 1

Just as the domain of pure mathematics consists of ideal objects (numbers, manifolds, etc.) and ideal truths about them, so the domain of pure logic consists of ideal objects (propositions, truth-values, inferential forms, etc.) and ideal truths about them. Neither domain is psychological; both are constituted by ideal entities and ideal relations.

### § 47. Confirmatory Evidence from the Logical Fundamental Concepts and the Sense of Logical Propositions

#### Paragraph 1

Husserl examines the fundamental concepts of logic — "concept," "proposition," "truth," "inference," "law," "necessity," "ground and consequent," etc. — and argues that none of these is a psychological concept. "Truth" does not mean "psychological event of being convinced"; "proposition" does not mean "mental act of judging"; "necessity" does not mean "psychological compulsion." Each of these concepts refers to something ideal, not real.

### § 48. The Decisive Differences

#### Paragraph 1

Husserl summarises the decisive differences between ideal logical truths and empirical psychological truths: (a) Logical truths are exact; psychological truths are vague generalisations. (b) Logical truths are a priori; psychological truths are a posteriori. (c) Logical truths are proved by apodictic evidence; psychological truths are supported by induction. (d) Logical truths are about ideal objects (propositions, truth-values); psychological truths are about real events (mental acts).

### § 49. Third Prejudice: Logic as the Theory of Evidence

#### Paragraph 1

The third prejudice: logic is the theory of evidence. Evidence is a psychological phenomenon — a distinctive feeling or experience accompanying certain judgements. Therefore, the investigation of evidence belongs to psychology. Husserl replies: evidence is indeed a psychological experience, but the *laws* that state under what conditions evidence arises are not psychological laws about the occurrence of a certain experience but ideal laws about the objective relationships among propositions that make certain judgements evident. The theory of evidence, properly understood, concerns the ideal conditions of truth and knowledge, not the empirical conditions under which a subjective feeling of certainty arises.

### § 50. The Equivalent Transformation of Logical Propositions into Propositions about Ideal Conditions of Judgement-Evidence

#### Paragraph 1

Husserl shows that every logical law can be equivalently reformulated as a statement about the ideal conditions under which evidence is possible. For example, the law of contradiction can be rephrased as: "It is an ideal condition for the possibility of evident judgement that contradictory propositions not simultaneously be judged with evidence." But this reformulation is *not* a psychological statement — it is a statement about ideal conditions, not about empirical regularities of mental life.

### § 51. The Decisive Points in This Dispute

#### Paragraph 1

Husserl recapitulates: the psychologistic prejudices all rest on equivocations — confusing ideal with real, propositional content with mental act, logical validity with psychological occurrence. Once these equivocations are exposed, the psychologistic arguments dissolve.

## Ninth Chapter: The Principle of Thought-Economy and Logic

### § 52. Introduction

#### Paragraph 1

Husserl now addresses a more recent variant of psychologism: the "thought-economy" approach associated with Mach and Avenarius. This approach seeks to explain logical norms in terms of the principle of economy of thought — the idea that the mind naturally strives to achieve maximal cognitive results with minimal effort. Logical laws, on this view, are expressions of the mind's tendency toward economy.

### § 53. The Teleological Character of the Mach-Avenarius Principle and the Scientific Significance of Thought-Economy

#### Paragraph 1

Husserl acknowledges the legitimate insights of the thought-economy approach: the idea that human cognition is structured by principles of economy, that scientific theories aim at maximal explanatory power with minimal conceptual complexity, and that the development of scientific methods can be illuminated by economic considerations. These are genuine contributions to the *psychology* of scientific activity and to the *methodology* of science as a *Kunstlehre*.

### § 54. Closer Exposition of the Legitimate Goals of a Thought-Economy, Chiefly in the Sphere of Purely Deductive Method

#### Paragraph 1

Husserl gives thought-economy its due: the drive to simplify, to abbreviate, to algorithmise — these are real features of scientific practice that can be fruitfully studied. The substitution of mechanical operations with signs for explicit reasoning, the systematisation of inferential procedures, the development of efficient notational systems — all of these reflect genuine thought-economic principles.

### § 55. The Insignificance of Thought-Economy for Pure Logic and Epistemology, and Its Relation to Psychology

#### Paragraph 1

But thought-economy, however valuable for understanding the *practice* and *psychology* of science, is irrelevant to *pure logic*. The truths of pure logic hold whether or not they are economical for human cognition. The law of contradiction would be valid even if comprehending it required enormous cognitive effort. The economy or diseconomy of a truth is a psychological fact about our relationship to it; the truth itself is independent of our cognitive constitution.

### § 56. Continuation: The Absurdity of a Thought-Economic Grounding of the Purely Logical

#### Paragraph 1

Husserl extends the argument: any attempt to *ground* logical laws in thought-economic principles commits a fundamental category-mistake. Thought-economy explains *why we find certain logical forms useful* — but it cannot explain *why those forms are valid*. Usefulness and validity are entirely different things. A rule could be maximally economical and yet logically invalid; conversely, a logically valid rule might be cognitively burdensome. To confuse the question "Why do we employ these logical norms?" with "Why are these logical norms valid?" is to confuse a psychological-pragmatic question with a logical-philosophical one.

## Tenth Chapter: Conclusion of the Critical Considerations

### § 57. Reservations in View of Likely Misunderstandings

#### Paragraph 1

Husserl anticipates misunderstandings of his position. He does not deny that psychology has a role to play in the full logic understood as *Kunstlehre*. The practical technology of correct thinking obviously requires knowledge of human psychology. What Husserl denies is that psychology provides the *essential theoretical foundation* of logic — the foundation that concerns the ideal laws governing truth, validity, and inferential form. Pure logic is not all of logic, but it is the indispensable theoretical core.

### § 58. Our Points of Contact with the Great Thinkers of the Past, and First with Kant

#### Paragraph 1

Husserl traces his position's affinities with Kant. Kant's distinction between general (formal) logic and transcendental logic, his insistence on the a priori character of logical laws, and his separation of logic from psychology all anticipate Husserl's own view. But Kant's execution was imperfect: he did not clearly demarcate the domain of pure logic and mixed it with elements of transcendental philosophy.

### § 59. Points of Contact with Herbart and Lotze

#### Paragraph 1

Herbart grasped the irreducibility of logical propositions to psychological ones; Lotze made important contributions to the theory of logical forms and their ideal character. Both are important predecessors, though neither fully realised the idea of pure logic.

### § 60. Points of Contact with Leibniz

#### Paragraph 1

Leibniz is Husserl's most important predecessor. Leibniz's vision of a *mathesis universalis* — a universal formal science encompassing both logic and mathematics — most closely approximates Husserl's own idea of pure logic. Leibniz saw that formal logic and formal mathematics share a common root in the study of ideal forms and laws, and that this shared domain constitutes a distinct theoretical science.

### § 61. Necessity of Special Investigations for the Epistemological Justification and Partial Realisation of the Idea of Pure Logic

#### Paragraph 1

Husserl signals that the *Prolegomena* has accomplished only the negative, critical task — the refutation of psychologism and the clearing of the ground for pure logic. The positive task — the actual elaboration of the idea of pure logic and the epistemological clarification of its fundamental concepts — remains for the subsequent investigations (Volume II of the *Logical Investigations*).

## Appendix: References to F. A. Lange and B. Bolzano

### § 62 (unnumbered appendix section)

Husserl acknowledges two important forerunners. F. A. Lange, in his *Logische Studien*, had already raised objections to the psychologistic grounding of logic that anticipate some of Husserl's own arguments. Lange saw that the reduction of logical laws to psychological laws conflicted with the exact and a priori character of the former, though he did not develop this insight systematically.

More importantly, Bolzano's *Wissenschaftslehre* (1837) had anticipated many of Husserl's central theses: the distinction between ideal propositions (*Sätze an sich*) and actual judgements, the objectivity of truth independently of any thinking subject, the distinction between subjective representations and their ideal contents, and the idea of a pure theory of science based on the study of ideal logical forms. Husserl credits Bolzano as one of the greatest logicians of all time and regrets that he did not discover Bolzano's work earlier in his own development. The *Wissenschaftslehre*, despite certain metaphysical presuppositions that Husserl rejects, contains a wealth of logical analysis that anticipates and in some respects surpasses later developments. Husserl's acknowledgement of Bolzano is significant because it positions his own project not as a wholly novel creation but as the resumption and philosophical deepening of a tradition that runs from Leibniz through Bolzano — a tradition that psychologism had buried but not refuted.

## Eleventh Chapter: The Idea of Pure Logic

### § 63. The Unity of Science. The Connection among Things and the Connection among Truths

#### Paragraph 1

Husserl now turns to the positive task: the articulation of the idea of pure logic. He begins by distinguishing two senses of the "unity" of a science: the unity given by the connection among the *things* (objects, facts) a science investigates, and the unity given by the connection among the *truths* the science establishes. These two unities are related but not identical. The truths of a science form a unity because the objects they concern form a domain; but the unity of truths has its own specifically logical character — it is a unity of grounding-connections.

### § 64. Continuation: The Unity of Theory

#### Paragraph 1

The most important form of unity in science is the unity of *theory*. A theory is not merely a collection of true propositions but a systematically grounded body of truths unified by principles. The unity of a theory is an ideal unity: it resides not in the psychological acts of theorising but in the logical relationships among the propositions. The question for pure logic is: what are the ideal conditions that make such theoretical unity possible?

### § 65. The Essential and Extra-Essential Principles That Give Science Its Unity. Abstract, Concrete, and Normative Sciences

#### Paragraph 1

Husserl distinguishes between the essential principles that give a science its unity (the logical laws and fundamental concepts that define its domain) and extra-essential principles (auxiliary methods, conventions, etc.). He also distinguishes abstract sciences (which deal with ideal objects and their laws) from concrete sciences (which deal with individual existents) and normative sciences (which deal with standards of evaluation).

### § 66. The Question of the Ideal Conditions of the Possibility of Science, or of Theory in General. A. The Question Referred to Actual Cognition

#### Paragraph 1

Husserl asks: what are the ideal conditions of the possibility of science or theory in general? On the side of actual cognition, these include the capacity for evidence, for distinguishing truth from falsehood, for grasping logical connections, and for sustained systematic inquiry. These are "noetic" conditions — conditions relating to the knowing subject.

### § 67. B. The Question Referred to the Content of Cognition

#### Paragraph 1

On the side of the *content* of cognition (as opposed to the act of knowing), the conditions include: the existence of truth, the existence of logical laws governing truth-connections, the availability of systematic forms of theory, and the possibility of classifying truths into domains. These are "objective" or "ideal" conditions — they concern the structure of truth itself, not the constitution of knowing minds.

### § 68. The Task of Pure Logic. First: The Fixing of the Pure Categories of Meaning, the Pure Object-Categories, and Their Lawful Combinations

#### Paragraph 1

Husserl now specifies the three main tasks of pure logic. The first is to fix the *pure categories of meaning* (the basic logical concepts such as "proposition," "concept," "truth," "falsity," "subject," "predicate," "conjunction," "disjunction," "negation," "universality," "particularity," etc.) and the *pure object-categories* (concepts like "object," "state of affairs," "unity," "plurality," "number," "relation," "connection," etc.), along with the laws governing their possible combinations.

These categories are "pure" in the sense that they are independent of any particular domain of objects or any particular factual science. They are the most general formal concepts under which all thought-content falls. Their investigation constitutes the first foundational layer of pure logic.

### § 69. Second: The Laws and Theories Grounded in These Categories

#### Paragraph 1

The second task of pure logic is to establish the laws and theories that are grounded in these pure categories. These include the laws of syllogistic, the laws of propositional logic, the laws governing the relationships among truth, falsity, possibility, necessity, and probability — in short, the entire body of formal-logical laws.

### § 70. Third: The Theory of Possible Theory-Forms, or the Pure Theory of Manifolds

#### Paragraph 1

The third and highest task of pure logic is the theory of possible theory-forms — the *pure theory of manifolds* (*reine Mannigfaltigkeitslehre*). This is the most general formal science: it investigates not any particular theory or domain but the possible *forms* of theories as such. Given certain axiom-types and certain connection-types, what kinds of theoretical structures are possible? This is the study of possible deductive systems in the most abstract sense — the science of possible sciences.

### § 71. Elaboration of the Idea of the Pure Theory of Manifolds

#### Paragraph 1

Husserl elaborates: a "manifold" in this context is a domain of objects defined purely by the formal properties of its governing laws — independently of what the objects or laws "are" in any material sense. Euclidean geometry, for example, defines a manifold (three-dimensional Euclidean space) characterised by certain axiom-forms; non-Euclidean geometries define other manifolds characterised by other axiom-forms. The pure theory of manifolds studies the *space of possible axiomatic structures* — the abstract conditions under which a domain of objects can be governed by a theory of a given form. This is the highest level of formal generality: the study of theory-forms as such.

This concept of a pure theory of manifolds places Husserl in direct connection with the Riemann-Cantor-Hilbert tradition in the foundations of mathematics. It also foreshadows the formal-ontological dimension of Husserl's later philosophy.

### § 72. Division of Labour. The Contributions of Mathematicians and Philosophers

#### Paragraph 1

Husserl concludes by distinguishing the contributions of mathematicians and philosophers to pure logic. Mathematicians have developed the formal-deductive theories (arithmetic, algebra, analysis, set theory, etc.) that constitute much of the content of pure logic. But they have done so without philosophical self-understanding — without clarifying the epistemological foundations and the ontological status of their objects and laws. Philosophers, conversely, have attempted the epistemological clarification but have lacked the mathematical sophistication to carry it out. What is needed is a collaboration — or a thinker who combines both competences.

### § 72 (continued). Extension of the Idea of Pure Logic. Pure Probability Theory as a Pure Theory of Experiential Cognition

#### Paragraph 1

Finally, Husserl notes that the concept of pure logic as developed so far encompasses the ideal conditions of theory in general — and thus of all science insofar as it involves theory. But empirical science involves more than deductive theory: it involves probability, induction, and the assessment of experiential evidence. The ideal laws governing probability and induction constitute a further extension of pure logic — a "pure probability theory" as a pure theory of experiential cognition. These ideal laws form the second great foundation of the logical *Kunstlehre* and belong to pure logic in a correspondingly broadened sense.

#### Paragraph 2

The following individual investigations will be restricted to the narrower and, in the essential order of matters, prior domain.

With this, the *Prolegomena* concludes. It has accomplished its threefold task: (1) the critique and refutation of psychologism in all its forms; (2) the positive delineation of the idea of pure logic as a theoretical, a priori, formal science of ideal truths; and (3) the specification of pure logic's three main tasks — the fixation of pure categories, the establishment of formal laws, and the theory of possible theory-forms. The stage is set for the "individual investigations" of Volume II, which will undertake the phenomenological and epistemological clarification of the fundamental concepts of pure logic.
