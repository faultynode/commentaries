# Paragraph-by-Paragraph Commentary on Fichte's *The Science of Knowing* (1804 Lectures on the *Wissenschaftslehre*)

## First Lecture

Fichte opens the lectures with a direct address to his audience — "Honorable Guests" — establishing from the first word that these are public lectures, not private seminars. The form of address signals the mixed, educated, non-specialist audience he is speaking to: courtiers, professionals, and citizens, not merely fellow academics.

He begins by acknowledging the sheer difficulty of beginning. The problem is self-referential: even remarking on the difficulty of beginning is itself difficult. This is not mere rhetorical throat-clearing. Fichte is signaling at the outset that the kind of thinking he will ask of his audience is not linear or straightforward; it turns back on itself at every point. The only solution is to "cut the knot boldly" — that is, to proceed with a direct act rather than trying to find a smooth logical on-ramp. He then offers a polite disclaimer: what he is about to say about the intellectual deficiencies of the age applies to the world at large, not to the people assembled before him.

Fichte then delivers his diagnosis of the present age. The chief characteristic of the time, he says, is that life has become "merely historical and symbolic" — that is, people relate to life at a remove, as a collection of reports, facts, and signs, rather than living it immediately. Thinking, as one dimension of life, has suffered the same fate. People have heard *about* thinking — they know historically that various thinkers have thought in various ways, and that each has failed in some respect — but they do not undertake the act of thinking for themselves. The age is one of second-hand knowledge. The person who tries to rouse his contemporaries from this condition faces a peculiar trap: everyone will agree that the criticism applies to others, never to themselves. Moreover, each person has enough historical familiarity with different philosophical positions that they can always dodge an accusation by claiming allegiance to whichever school is not being criticized at the moment. If you rebuke them for superficiality and indifference to truth, they will insist they know full well that only one thing can be true. If you rebuke them for dogmatic rigidity, they will praise their own skepticism. The audience's very eclecticism makes them slippery and immune to genuine self-examination.

Given this impasse, Fichte adopts a direct, almost authoritarian posture. He simply *presupposes*, "briefly and sweetly at a single stroke," three fundamental claims: (1) There is a single truth, and everything apart from it is unconditionally false. (2) This truth can actually be found and can present itself as immediately and unconditionally evident. (3) Not even the smallest particle of this truth can be communicated historically or appropriated from another person's mind. These three presuppositions define the entire program of the lectures. The first establishes the absoluteness of the philosophical aim. The second asserts the real possibility of philosophical insight — against skeptics who would deny it. The third is the most radical: it declares that truth is essentially performative. A lecturer can provide "the terms for insight" — the conditions under which insight becomes possible — but the listener must enact those conditions in himself. Insight then arises of its own accord, spontaneously, as a self-producing event within the individual who has fulfilled its preconditions. The object of the *Wissenschaftslehre* is wholly unlike any object already familiar from ordinary experience. It is "completely new, unheard of, and totally unknown" to anyone who has not already worked through the science of knowing. No one can reach it except by letting it produce itself in him — and it does so only if the person first produces the conditions for its emergence. Whoever fails to do this will find the entire lecture series to be words about nothing at all: "pure, bare nothingness, an empty vessel, word breath, the mere movement of air." This is a stark warning: the lectures will not yield their content to passive auditors. Fichte calls this "the first prolegomenon."

Fichte then asks his audience to accept an extraordinary condition. He, the lecturer, wishes to be "considered silenced and erased." The audience members must "come forward and stand in my place." Whatever is thought in this assembly must be thought and recognized as true only insofar as *they themselves* have thought it and seen it to be true. This is the performative principle in action: Fichte renounces all authority as a transmitter of doctrine. He does not want his listeners to believe him; he wants them to reproduce the philosophical insight independently, in their own inner activity. If they merely accept what he says on his authority, they have gained nothing.

Fichte announces that he will devote the first four lectures to introductory remarks, and he insists strenuously that these introductory remarks are not to be taken lightly in the way that introductions usually are. They have genuine philosophical content, and everything that follows depends on them. Their purpose is to redirect the listener's "spiritual eye" away from the objects it has been casually surveying, toward the specific point at issue in the science of knowing — and indeed to bring that point into existence for the first time. The introductory lectures are meant to initiate the audience into the *art* of philosophy and to make them fluent in a set of rules and maxims of thinking that will be employed throughout the lecture series.

Fichte adds a cautionary note about comprehension. The introduction will be relatively easy to follow, but its ease should not be taken as a measure of what the science of knowing itself will demand. Understanding the introduction gives one an accurate *concept* of the science of knowing — authorized by its originator — but not "the tiniest spark of the science itself." This distinction between the mere concept and the real substance is universally applicable but has special force here. Possessing the concept has its uses — it prevents one from misunderstanding or underestimating what one does not yet possess — but it does not make one a philosopher. One who has only the concept "is and remains a sophist," merely a less superficial one than those who lack even the concept.

After these "prefatory remarks about the prefatory remarks," Fichte turns to the substantive question. He has promised a discourse on the science of knowing, and his audience expects one. So: what is the science of knowing? He begins with what everyone would grant — it is one of the possible philosophical systems, "one of the philosophies." This classifies it by genus.

To determine its specific difference, he asks: what is philosophy in general, and what is commonly required of it? Philosophy should present the truth. But what is truth? Fichte approaches this negatively: we do not count as truth anything that could equally well be otherwise — the multiplicity and variability of opinion, for instance. Truth is therefore "absolute oneness and invariability." Dropping the supplemental term "opinion," Fichte formulates the essence of philosophy as tracing all multiplicity back to absolute oneness. He insists this be taken with full seriousness: *all* multiplicity, *absolutely without exception*. Wherever even the possibility of a distinction remains, the philosophical task is unfinished. This standard of absolute oneness is also a criterion for refutation: whoever can point out the smallest remaining distinction within what a system has posited as its highest principle has refuted that system.

Fichte clarifies what this absolute oneness involves. It is what is true and in itself unchangeable, with its opposite "purely contained within itself" — meaning that whatever stands against it is not genuinely external to it but already enclosed within it. "Tracing back" multiplicity to oneness is not a one-directional derivation but a reciprocal insight: the philosopher conceives multiplicity through oneness and oneness through multiplicity. Oneness, as a principle (call it A), illuminates multiplicity; and conversely, multiplicity in its deepest ground can be grasped only as proceeding from A. This reciprocal illumination is what constitutes philosophical insight.

The science of knowing shares this task with all philosophy. All philosophers have aimed, consciously or not, at this reduction of multiplicity to unity. If some philosopher could be shown historically not to have had this aim, one could prove philosophically that insofar as he wished to count as a philosopher, he must have had it. For merely apprehending multiplicity in its factual occurrence is not philosophy but history. Whoever wants only that, and posits it as the absolute, intends that nothing should exist except history. If such a person then claims there is something additional called "philosophy," he contradicts himself.

Since all philosophies share the aim of tracing multiplicity back to oneness, the difference between them can only lie in what they propose as that oneness — what they identify as the absolute. (Fichte notes in passing that philosophy's task could equivalently be expressed as "the presentation of the absolute.") But this differentiation is only superficial and historical.

Fichte now makes a stronger claim. To the extent that general agreement is possible among living individuals regarding any manifold, the principle of oneness must in truth be one. Divergent principles would produce divergent and mutually incoherent worlds, making any agreement impossible. If only one principle is right and true, then only one philosophy is true, and all others are false. Where several philosophies present different absolutes, either all are false, or all but one.

A further consequence: a philosophy that has not found the one true absolute does not possess the absolute at all but only something relative — a product of an unnoticed disjunction that necessarily has an opposing term. Such a philosophy traces multiplicity back not to genuine oneness but only to a subordinate, relative oneness. It is therefore refuted not only by the true philosophy but by itself, once one reflects carefully on what philosophy's task actually requires. Thus the differentiation of philosophies by their principles of oneness is merely provisional and historical.

Returning to the classification: if the science of knowing claims to be completely distinct from all previous systems, it must have a different principle of oneness. What did previous philosophies have as their principle? Fichte clarifies that he is not aiming to narrate the history of philosophy or enter into historical controversies; he is simply developing his own concept, and what he says could serve his purpose even if it were assumed arbitrarily rather than historically demonstrated. His claim: in all philosophies prior to Kant, the absolute was located in *being* — in the dead thing as thing. The thing was supposed to be the in-itself. In a parenthetical aside, he adds that even after Kant, all philosophers — including self-described Kantians and supposed commentators on and improvers of the science of knowing — have remained with this same absolute being. Kant has not been understood in his true principle, which he himself never clearly articulated. What matters is not what one *calls* being but how one grasps it inwardly. Even if one calls the absolute "I," if one fundamentally objectifies it and separates it from oneself, it remains the same old thing-in-itself.

But — and this is the decisive turn — everyone willing to reflect can see that all being posits a thinking or consciousness of itself. Mere being is always only one half of a whole (being together with the thought of being), and is therefore one term of a more fundamental disjunction. Absolute oneness can therefore reside neither in being alone nor in consciousness alone, neither in the thing nor in the representation of the thing. It resides in the principle of the absolute oneness and indivisibility of both, which is simultaneously the principle of their disjunction. Fichte names this principle "pure knowing" — knowing in itself, completely objectless knowing, because if it required an object for its being it would not be knowing in itself. Pure knowing is distinct from consciousness, which always posits a being and is therefore only a half. This, Fichte asserts, is Kant's discovery, and what makes Kant the founder of transcendental philosophy. The science of knowing, like Kantian philosophy, is transcendental philosophy: it does not posit the absolute in the thing (as pre-Kantian philosophy did) or in subjective knowing (which is impossible, because reflecting on the subjective term already involves the objective term), but in the oneness of both.

How then does the science of knowing differentiate itself from Kantianism? Before answering, Fichte pauses to observe that whoever has caught a genuine inner glimpse of this higher oneness — the oneness beyond both being and consciousness — has already, in this first hour, gained something almost entirely lacking in the philosophical era: an insight into the true "home" for the principle of genuine philosophy. The common misunderstanding of the science of knowing is that, as idealism, it locates the absolute in thinking or consciousness — which is just one half of the disjunction and can no more be the absolute than its opposite. Yet this misreading is accepted universally, by friends and enemies alike.

Fichte closes the First Lecture with a pointed remark about his "improvers" (a thinly veiled reference to Schelling and others). Having misunderstood the science of knowing as locating the absolute in consciousness, these improvers have "switched" the absolute to the other term — being — while appending to it the word "I," which Fichte regards as the central achievement of Kant's entire life and of his own. The irony is bitter: the very word "I" that was meant to designate the principle beyond the being/consciousness split has been reified into a new name for the thing-in-itself.

## Second Lecture

Fichte opens the Second Lecture by announcing a review of the first, but immediately reveals an ulterior motive: he wants to say something about how to retain and reproduce lectures of this kind, and whether transcribing them is useful. He notes that people differ widely in their capacity for memory and divided attention, and presents himself as particularly deficient in both — lacking ordinary memory and able to focus on only one thing at a time. His recommendations, he says, are therefore not authoritative; each listener must judge for themselves.

His ideal listener is one who can reproduce the lecture at home — not mechanically or by rote, but through pondering and reflection. This reproduction must be free: the listener should be able to move backward from the conclusions to the premises, forward from the premises to the conclusions, and in both directions at once from the center of the argument. Moreover, this reproduction should be independent of the lecturer's specific words and phrasings. Since the entire lecture series constitutes a single self-contained presentation, the ideal listener should be able to grasp the individual lectures as integral parts of a whole and reconstruct any one of them starting from any other — from the first, the last, or any intermediate session.

The second methodological principle concerns the relationship between novelty and genuine understanding. What is most noteworthy for each listener in each session is whatever new element that person genuinely learns and comprehends. What we genuinely comprehend becomes part of ourselves; a truly new insight produces a personal transformation. Once one has genuinely become something, one cannot cease to be it. For exactly this reason, Fichte claims, the science of knowing — more than any other philosophy — can reactivate the dormant instinct for thinking: because it introduces genuinely new concepts and insights that produce such transformations.

Fichte then reviews the core insight of the previous lecture. The new element for those unfamiliar with the science of knowing (and what may have appeared in a new light for those already acquainted with it) was this: if one reflects properly, all being presupposes a thought or consciousness that has it as its object. Being is therefore one term of a disjunction, with thinking as the other half. Oneness is not found in either term but in the connection of both — and this connection is the same as pure knowing in and for itself, which is knowledge of nothing in particular. Equivalently: oneness is "truth and certainty," but not certainty about any particular thing, since that would already presuppose the disjunction of being and knowing. Fichte insists that if someone has grasped this single point clearly and vividly, all the rest of the first lecture could be developed from it by logical reflection: How did we arrive at proving that being has a correlative term? Was it polemical? Was being taken to be absolute oneness? Yes, until Kant. And how did we come to investigate absolute oneness at all? Because we were doing philosophy, and the essence of philosophy was said to be the assertion of absolute oneness and the reduction of all multiplicity to it.

But this reconstruction must not lack depth. "Reducing multiplicity to oneness" is a brief, comfortable formula, easy to memorize and trot out. But does one really know what one is saying? Can one construct a lucid and transparent account of it? Fichte pushes further: the "oneness" that is neither being nor consciousness but the union of both has been presented as the absolute of transcendental philosophy. But it cannot yet be completely clear and transparent to the listener, because all of philosophy is contained in the transparency of this oneness. From this point forward, the lectures will do nothing other than work to heighten the clarity of this one concept. If it were already fully clear, the lectures would be unnecessary. Nevertheless, everyone must possess more than the bare empty formula — one must have a living image of this oneness, firmly fixed and never leaving one. Fichte's lectures address this fixed image; they will extend and clarify it together with the audience. Whoever does not have even this image cannot be addressed at all; for such a person, the entire discourse becomes talk about nothing.

Fichte states the matter plainly: without the listener's own free, personal re-creation of the exposition in its living depth, the lectures are useless. The subject cannot remain in the form in which Fichte expresses it. A "middle term" must intervene between Fichte's act of exposition and the listener's active mastery: the listener's own independent rediscovery. Without this middle term, everything ends with the act of exposition and the listener never attains mastery.

Whether this re-creation is carried out with pen in hand (as Fichte himself would do, lacking memory but possessing an imagination that can be held in check only by writing) or in free thought (for those with better memory and a tamer imagination) is unimportant. What matters is that each person does it in their individually appropriate way. Taking notes during the lecture itself cannot substitute for this proper re-creation; notes can only serve as a resource for the task, which must be undertaken independently. Fichte's deliberate speech, his pauses after important paragraphs, and his repetitions of significant phrases should make it possible to catch the main points. For his own part, if he had to attend such lectures, he would not try to note down more than main points, since he cannot listen energetically and write simultaneously. For him, the living lecture and the spiritual act of keen thinking carried out in his presence would matter more than isolated, dead words. But others may well be able to do both at once.

With this settled, Fichte returns to the substantive question: What is the science of knowing? All transcendental philosophy (including Kant's, from which the science of knowing does not yet differ in this respect) posits the absolute neither in being nor in consciousness but in their union. Truth and certainty in and for itself equals A. From this it follows that for this kind of philosophy the difference between being and thinking, taken as valid in itself, totally disappears. Everything that can arise in such a philosophy is contained in the insight consummated in the previous lecture: that no being is possible without thinking, and vice versa. Nothing can occur in the manifest sphere of being without simultaneously occurring in the manifest sphere of thinking, if one considers the matter rightly. However, according to the insight that the absolute is not a half but indivisible oneness — an insight that reaches beyond all appearance — the absolute is in itself neither being nor thinking. Fichte presents this schematically: A divides into B (being) and T (thinking).

Fichte then adds a further subdivision. If, in addition to its fundamental division into B and T, A also divides itself into x, y, z, then two consequences follow: (1) Everything — including absolute A and x, y, z — is only a modification of A. (2) It all must occur in B as well as in T. That is, every subdivision within the absolute must manifest on both sides of the being/thinking disjunction.

Now suppose a philosophical system has no doubt that the dichotomy of B and T is mere appearance, so that it qualifies as genuinely transcendental. But suppose further that this system remains caught in an absolute division of A into x, y, z. Despite its transcendentalism, such a system has not penetrated through to pure oneness. Having eluded one disjunction (being vs. thinking), it has fallen into another (x vs. y vs. z). Despite the admiration it deserves for discovering the primordial illusion, the discovery of this new disjunction refutes it as the true, fully accomplished philosophy.

Fichte identifies this system as Kant's. If Kant is studied not as the Kantians have studied him — clinging to the letter of the text — but on the basis of what he actually says, raising oneself to what he does not say but must presuppose in order to say what he does, then no doubt remains about his transcendentalism. Kant conceived A as the indivisible union of being and thinking. But he did not conceive it in its pure self-sufficiency; he conceived it only as a common basic determination or accident of three primordial modes (x, y, z). The result is that there are actually three absolutes in Kant's system, and the true unitary absolute fades to their common property.

Fichte maps this onto Kant's three Critiques. In the *Critique of Pure Reason*, the absolute (x) is sensible experience; Kant even speaks disparagingly of Ideas and the higher spiritual world. From earlier writings and hints, one might conclude that Kant himself did not think this was the whole story, but Fichte claims he could show that these hints are just one more inconsistency — if one follows out the premises of the first *Critique*, the supersensible world must totally disappear, leaving only a noumenon that is completely realized in the empirical world. Kant's "high inner morality" then corrected the philosopher, and the *Critique of Practical Reason* appeared, in which the I comes to light as something in itself through the categorical imperative, yielding a second absolute: the moral world (z). But not all phenomena of self-observation have been accounted for — there remain the beautiful, the sublime, and the purposive, which are neither theoretical cognitions nor moral concepts. Moreover, the introduction of the moral world as the one world in itself leaves the empirical world without foundation. So the *Critique of Judgment* appears, and in its Introduction — the most important part of this "very important book" — Kant confesses that the sensible and supersensible worlds must come together in a common but wholly unknown root. This is a third absolute (y), supposedly the connection of the other two. But Fichte argues that since Kant declares this root inscrutable, it cannot actually function as a genuine unifying ground. One can neither comprehend it as such, nor conceive the other two terms as originating from it. To grasp it, one must grasp it as an independent absolute, leaving one trapped forever in three absolutes. Kant's final addition to his system did not repair the problem; he only generously disclosed it.

Fichte then characterizes the science of knowing within this historical movement. Its essence consists, first, in discovering the root that Kant declared indiscernible — the root in which the sensible and supersensible worlds come together — and then in providing the actual conceptual derivation of both worlds from a single principle. The maxims of Kant and his followers — "we must stop short and cannot go farther" — are rejected as maxims of weakness or inertia that are then universalized. The science of knowing's own maxim is to admit absolutely nothing inconceivable and to leave nothing unconceived. It is willing not to exist at all if something is pointed out that it has not grasped, for it will be everything or nothing. Yet even the science of knowing must finally admit something inconceivable — but at least it will conceive this as exactly what it is: absolutely inconceivable and nothing more. In this way it will also conceive the precise point at which absolute conceiving is able to begin.

This, Fichte says, suffices as a historical characterization of the science of knowing in relation to its only genuine neighbor, Kantianism. It cannot be directly compared to any earlier philosophies or recent derivatives, since it shares nothing with them. It shares only the genus of transcendentalism with Kant, and must demarcate itself from him on that basis.

Finally, Fichte recasts the characterization in pure concepts, independent of historical setting. A is admitted; it divides both into B and T (being and thinking) and into x, y, z. Both divisions are equally absolute; neither is possible without the other. The insight that constitutes the science of knowing's distinction from Kantianism lies not in seeing the division into B and T (accomplished in the first lecture), nor in seeing the division into x, y, z (not yet finished but provisionally presupposed), but in seeing the immediate inseparability of these two modes of division. The two divisions cannot be grasped immediately, as it had seemed; they can be grasped only mediately, through the higher insight into their oneness. Fichte calls the attention of returning listeners to this "most important clue," noting that it provides a characterization of the entire enterprise much earlier than it appeared in his first lecture series, where it did not emerge until the middle of the work.

## Third Lecture

Fichte begins by clarifying a potential misunderstanding from the end of the previous lecture. He had spoken as if the absolute A first divides into B and T (being and thinking), and then separately into x, y, z. But this sequential presentation is merely an artifact of discursive speech. In reality, both divisions occur at a single stroke — they are totally identical, comprehended in one self-contained act. Fichte explicitly acknowledges that he is "constructing what cannot be constructed," with full awareness that discursive language cannot capture the simultaneity of these divisions.

He then continues characterizing the science of knowing in comparison with Kantian transcendentalism. Kant understood A as the link between B and T, but did not grasp it in its absolute autonomy. Instead, he made it the common property of three absolutes. The science of knowing must therefore hold that knowing — once characterized as A — is a purely self-sustaining substance, that we can realize it as such for ourselves, and that the genuine realization of the science consists in exactly this realization. (Fichte marks this as a "genuine realization" with which they are not yet concerned; they are still at the level of stating the simple concept, which is not the thing itself.) This is the thesis of the Third Lecture.

To demonstrate that knowing can actually appear as something self-standing, Fichte invites the audience to look at their own inner experience. In experience, one finds the object and its representation, with all their modifications. But knowing itself — as knowing — remains the same self-identical knowing throughout all variations of the object. If one says "yes" to this (and Fichte is confident everyone will), then knowledge manifests itself (as A) independently of the variation of its objects, in total abstraction from objectivity. It presents itself as substantive — as staying the same through all change in its object — and thus as oneness, qualitatively changeless in itself.

This self-identity of knowing presents itself with irrefutable manifestness — with a certainty that requires no reasons and cannot be abandoned even if reasons are demanded. The listener has not run through and exhausted all possible changes of object to verify that knowing remains the same in each case. The self-identity of knowing manifests itself independently of all such empirical experiments, completely a priori, as self-sustaining and self-identical independently from all subjectivity and objectivity.

Fichte then inserts a crucial clarification to prevent a relapse into the misunderstanding criticized in the First Lecture — the view that the science of knowing locates the absolute in subjective knowing (as opposed to its object). In the experiment just conducted, they started with consciousness presenting an object: the pair T.B. (thinking-being) in continuous variation. In each new moment, B (being) made T (thinking) different, because T was nothing other than T-for-this-B and disappeared with it. But when they raised themselves to the second part — asking whether knowing is not one and the same throughout, and finding it to be so — they rose above all differences of T as well as of B. Therefore the correct formulation is: knowing (which for this very reason is not subjective) is absolutely unalterable and self-identical independently from all variability of both object and subject (without which the object does not exist). The changeable is neither object nor subject but simply the "mere pure changeable, and nothing else." This changeable, in its continuing changeability (which is itself unchangeable), divides itself into subject and object. The purely unchangeable — in which the division of subject and object falls away, along with change itself — stands opposed to the changeable.

Fichte marks this as a splendid example of an a priori insight that arises from exhaustive, continuous searching and cannot be derived from experience. He entreats anyone to whom this insight has been evident to hold onto this very example and, if the "old empiricist demon" shows up, to attack it and send it away. He wants to be spared the eternal struggle about whether there is manifestness or something a priori (for both are the same). One comes to the conclusion that there is only by producing it in oneself. This has happened here today, and Fichte asks simply that it not be forgotten.

The result so far: knowing (in the sense of A) has appeared as self-sufficient, independent of all changeability, and as self-same, self-contained oneness — just as the historical characterization of the science of knowing had presupposed. They therefore seem to have realized the principle of the science of knowing in themselves.

But this is only seeming. They see *that* it is so, but they do not see into *what* it authentically is as this qualitative oneness. Precisely because they grasp only a "that" (a factical insight), they remain trapped in a disjunction — between changeability and unchangingness — and thus in two absolutes, to which they might append a third (the undiscoverable root of both), ending up in the same shape as Kant's philosophy. The ground of this duality is that the "that" must seem self-creating (as their insight seemed to be), but this appearance is possible only under the condition that a point of origin appears, produced by us. Both changeability and unchangingness are grasped equally and immediately, and we are inwardly torn between two or three immediate terms. The solution is obvious: one of the two must be grasped mediately, and this mediately grasped term cannot be unchangingness (which as the absolute can only be realized absolutely) but must be changeability. The unchangeable must be penetrated not merely in its being (already accomplished) but in its essence, its one absolute quality, in such a way that changeability would be seen as necessarily proceeding from it.

Fichte states the point concisely for ease of memory: the insight that knowing is a self-sustaining qualitative oneness (an insight that is purely provisional and belongs to a theory *about* the science of knowing) leads to the question: *What is knowing in this qualitative oneness?* The true nature of the science of knowing resides in answering this question. To answer it, one must inwardly construct the essence of knowing — or, what amounts to the same thing, this essence must construct itself. In this constructive act, it is without doubt and is what it is as existing; and as existing, it is what it is. Therefore the science of knowing and the knowing that presents itself in its essential oneness are entirely one and the same. They merge reciprocally into one another and permeate each other; in themselves they are not different, and the difference we still make is merely verbal. Primordially essential knowing is constructive, hence intrinsically genetic. This would be the original knowing or certainty in itself. "Manifestness in itself is therefore genetic."

This yields the deepest characteristic difference between the science of knowing and all other philosophies, including Kant's. All philosophy should terminate in knowing in and for itself. Knowing, or manifestness in and for itself, is actively genetic. The highest *appearance* of knowing, which no longer expresses its inner essence but only its external existence, is factical — and since it is still the appearance of knowing, it is factical manifestness. All factical manifestness, even if it is the absolute, remains something objective, alien, self-constructing but not constructed by knowing — and therefore inwardly unexplained. Exhausted speculation, skeptical of its own power, calls this "inexplicable." Kantian speculation ends at its highest point with factical manifestness: the insight that at the basis of both the sensible and supersensible worlds there must be a genetic principle that creates and determines both. This insight is correct in itself, but it could occur to Kant only as a result of reason's absolute but unconsciously operative law — the law that reason come to rest only with absolute oneness. This basic law of oneness remains merely factical for Kant; he lets it work on him mechanically but does not bring this action and its law into conscious awareness. If he did so, "pure light would dawn on him and he would come to the science of knowing." Kant's factical manifestness is not even the highest kind, because he lets its object emerge from two related terms and grasps it not inwardly as oneness but as duality — his highest principle is a "synthesis post factum." This means discovering in self-observation two terms of a disjunction and, compelled by reason, seeing that they must be one, while being unable to say how, given this oneness, they can likewise become two.

Fichte distinguishes this from what is needed: a "synthesis a priori which is equally an analysis" — a synthesis that simultaneously provides the basis for both oneness and duality. The highest factical manifestness was presented today: the insight into knowing's absolute self-sufficiency, without determination by anything external or changeable. This surpasses Kant's absolute, which is determined by the transition between the sensible and supersensible. Since this factical element is itself to become actively genetic and developing, change in general will be grounded in it as genesis pure and simple — though no particular change will be so grounded.

Fichte then extends his criticism beyond philosophy to mathematics, which also claims manifestness and whose representatives sometimes elevate it above philosophy. Even granting that mathematics has more going for it than its current state suggests, its principles admit nothing more than factical manifestness. How does the arithmetician elicit a solid number one? What fixes and holds the geometer's space while he draws lines through it? These and other ingredients are given through nothing other than factical intuition. This is no objection to mathematics as mathematics, but mathematics should recognize that it is neither the first nor self-sufficient — its principles of possibility lie in another, higher science.

Since in the actual sciences generally no principles are available other than factically manifest ones, and since the science of knowing intends to introduce entirely genetic manifestness and then deduce the factical from it, the science of knowing is wholly different in spirit and life from all previous scientific employment of reason. It is not known to anyone who has not studied it directly, and nothing can substitute for such study. No external perspective or premise from previous life or science can serve to evaluate, attack, or refute it, because any such premise is merely factically manifest, and the science of knowing accepts nothing of this kind unconditionally. Anyone who argues against it from such a premise argues from a point that has not been accepted (*ex non concessis*) and makes himself ridiculous. The science of knowing can only be judged internally — attacked and refuted only by pointing out an internal contradiction, inconsistency, or insufficiency. Until now the opposite order has been tried: first judging and refuting, then (God willing) understanding. The blows have completely missed the science of knowing, which has remained hidden like an invisible spirit, and have struck only the chimeras these critics created with their own hands.
## Fourth Lecture

Fichte opens by expressing satisfaction that even within the prolegomenon, they have achieved a clear and deep insight into the scientific form of the science they are pursuing.

He recaps the results so far: knowing has been grasped as unchangeable, self-same, and self-sustaining, beyond all change and beyond the subjectivity-objectivity that is inseparable from change. But this insight is not yet the science of knowing itself — only its premise. The science of knowing must still actually *construct* the inner, qualitatively unchangeable being of knowing; and in doing so, it will simultaneously create change (the second term) as well. The true meaning of this simultaneous double construction will become clear only when they actually carry it out, which belongs within the science of knowing itself, not in preliminary remarks.

Fichte addresses a question that has been raised about the schema (A above, with x, y, z and B-T below, connected through a "point"). He had said the science of knowing stands "in the point." Someone asked whether it doesn't rather go in A. His answer: strictly, it belongs in neither but in the oneness of both. A by itself is objective and therefore inwardly dead — it should become actively genetic. The point, on the other hand, is merely genetic. Mere genesis is nothing at all; but this is not mere genesis — it is the determinate genesis required by the absolute qualitative A. It is a "point of oneness." This point of oneness can be realized immediately, but it can be neither expressed nor reconstructed in its immediacy, since all expression or reconstruction is conceiving and is intrinsically mediated. It is expressed by beginning from A and showing it cannot persist alone, then linking it to the point; or by beginning from the point and showing it cannot persist alone, then linking it to A — all the while knowing that neither can exist by itself and that all this talk cannot express the implicit truth. The implicitly unreconstructible something can only be pictured in an empty, objective image as the organic oneness of both. Since reconstruction is conceiving, and since this conceiving explicitly abandons its own intrinsic validity, this is precisely a case of "conceiving the inconceivable as inconceivable."

This connects to the organic split between B-T and x, y, z discussed in the Third Lecture. When Fichte speaks, he must put one before the other, but in reality it is exactly the same stroke. This deeper connection must itself be a result and lower expression of the higher connection just described. Fichte then draws a major consequence: secondary knowing, or consciousness, with its whole lawful play — fixed change, manifold (within and without), sensible and supersensible, time and space — comes to be, in principle, through this division, taken merely as division. Everything attributed to the subject as originating from it derives from this. From a particular perspective (the science of knowing's synthetic perspective), the disjunction must be just as absolute as oneness; otherwise we would be stuck in oneness and never get outside it to changeability. Fichte notes in passing that this distinguishes his system from Spinoza's, which wants absolute oneness but cannot build a bridge to the manifold, and conversely, having the manifold, cannot get to oneness.

As scientists of knowing, Fichte says, we never escape the principle of division inwardly and empirically (in terms of what we do and promote), but we do escape it intellectually, with regard to what is valid in itself — and in this very regard the principle of division surrenders and negates itself.

Fichte asks: since we are actually reasoning as we have been, where does our reasoning stand if we remain in consciousness? It stands in our construction by means of the principle of division — not in the position of what is to be valid insofar as it is constructed, but in the position of what is intrinsically valid. It stands wholly autonomously between the two principles of oneness and separation, simultaneously annulling both and positing both. The standpoint of the science of knowing is therefore by no means a synthesis post factum, but a synthesis a priori, taking neither division nor oneness as given but creating both at one stroke.

From an even higher perspective: the absolute oneness of the science of knowing is neither A nor the point, but the inner organic oneness of both. There is no other description of this point of oneness than the one given. Therefore this description is the original and absolutely authentic one. Its constituents: the organic oneness of both is a construction or a concept — indeed the single absolute concept, abstracted from nothing existing, since even its own separate existence (and hence the existence of everything conceptual) is denied. The construction as such is denied by the manifestness of what exists autonomously. The inconceivable, as the inconceivable and nothing more, is posited by this manifestness — posited through the negation of the absolute concept, which must be posited precisely so that it can be annulled.

From this, Fichte derives four numbered results:

First, the necessary unification and indivisibility of the concept and the inconceivable is clearly seen into. If the absolutely inconceivable is to be manifest as solely self-sustaining, the concept must be annulled — but to be annulled it must first be posited, because the inconceivable becomes manifest only through the negation of the concept. A supplementary identification follows: inconceivable equals unchangeable; concept equals change. Therefore, if the unchangeable is to appear, there must be change.

Second, inconceivability is only the negation of the concept, an expression of its annulment. It originates from the concept and from knowing themselves — it is a quality transferred by means of absolute manifestness. Abstracting from this quality, nothing remains for oneness except absoluteness or pure self-sufficiency in itself.

Third (and most important): what is pure self-sufficient knowing in itself? The science of knowing must answer this by constructing knowing's presupposed inner quality. The construction now underway — negating the concept by means of manifestness, the self-creation of inconceivability — is this living construction. Inconceivability originates in the concept and in pure immediate manifestness; likewise the whole quality of the absolute, and the fact that quality can be applied to it at all, originates in the concept. The absolute is not intrinsically inconceivable (this makes no sense); it is inconceivable only when the concept itself tries for it, and inconceivability is its only property. Having recognized inconceivability as an alien quality introduced by knowing, only pure self-sufficiency or substantiality remains in the absolute. This self-sufficiency enters only within immediate manifestness, within intuition, and thus is only the representative and correlate of "pure light." Light is the genetic principle by which, first, all manifestness opens into genetic manifestness (since pure light manifests itself implicitly as genesis); and second, the relationship of concept to being is further determined: if there is to be an expression and realization of the absolute light, the concept must be posited so that it can be negated by the immediate light, since the expression of pure light consists just in this negation. The result of this expression is being in itself, period — inconceivable precisely because pure light is simultaneously the destruction of the concept. Thus, "pure light has prevailed as the one focus and the sole principle of both being and the concept."

Fourth, this inconceivable — as the bearer of all reality in knowing — is absolute only as inconceivable, and cannot be thought in any other way. No additional hidden qualities can be attributed to it. No quality can be added to light beyond the characteristic that it annuls the concept and leaves absolute being. If such additions were made, we would (as Kant did) run up against something unexplained and perhaps inexplicable. Whatever yet-to-be-determined characteristics reality may carry, they do not require any new absolute grounding principle beyond the one principle of pure light. The multiplicity and change of various traits is to be deduced purely from the interaction of light with itself, in its multifarious relations to concepts and to inconceivable being.

Fichte then invites the audience to reflections that will raise what has been said to a higher level and provide a unified perspective. The focus of everything is pure light. For light to truly come requires that the concept be posited and annulled and that an intrinsically inconceivable being be posited. If it is granted that the light should exist, then everything else is possible as well. This has been seen into; it is true; it remains true forever; and it expresses the basic principle of all knowing.

But now Fichte shifts from the content of this insight to its form — to the actual situation of the listeners who had the insight. As he recalls, the process was one of freely constructing concepts and premises, holding them up to each other, and being gripped by the conviction that they belong together absolutely and form an indivisible oneness. The listeners created at least the conditions for the self-manifesting insight and thus appeared to themselves as unconditionally active.

But Fichte cautions against haste. Did the listeners create what they created because they wished to, on the basis of earlier knowledge — which they would have created on the basis of even earlier knowledge, and so on to infinity, never arriving at a first creation? Somewhere, if the concept is created, it must absolutely and thoroughly create itself, without anything antecedent and without any necessity of a "we." The "we" always requires prior knowing and cannot achieve immediate knowing. Therefore we cannot create the conditions — they must emerge spontaneously. Reason must create itself, independent from volition, freedom, or self. But this proposition, disclosed through analysis, contradicts the first (given reflexively and immediately). Which is true?

Before answering, Fichte returns to the insight itself. They realized that if light is to be, the concept must be posited and negated. The light itself is therefore not immediately present in this insight; the insight does not dissolve into light and coincide with it. Instead it is only an insight *in relation to* the light, one that objectifies it and grasps it by its inner quality. Whatever the principle and true bearer of this insight might be — whether ourselves (as it seems) or pure self-creating reason (as it also seems) — the light is present in this bearer not immediately but only mediately, in a representative and likeness of itself. This applies not just to the science of knowing but to any possible consciousness that must posit a concept in order to annul it. The science of knowing therefore rests on a completely different point than many may have assumed after the Third Lecture, because knowing was undoubtedly understood too simply.

To answer the question: both are clear (that we created the insight and that reason must create itself), therefore both are equally true. Manifestness rests neither in one nor the other but entirely between both. This yields the first important result: the principle of division — not a division between two intrinsically distinct terms (like A and the point), but a division of something that remains inwardly self-same through all division. The very same primal concepts that are to be constructed appear one time as immanent (in the unconditionally evident final being, the I) and the other time as emanent (in reason, absolute and in itself, which is nonetheless completely objectified). It is a division pure and in itself, without any alteration in the object.

Further, manifestness oscillates between these two perspectives. If it is to be really constructed, it must be constructed as oscillating — from a to b and from b to a, completely creating both — thus as oscillating between the twofold oscillation, giving rise to a "three or fivefold synthesis." The common element in all these determinations is the representative of light, seen in its familiar inner quality. Everything is the same one common consciousness of light. This consciousness, held in common, cannot be really constructed but only thought by means of the science of knowing; it can be regarded or represented in three or fivefold modifications only from the standpoint that this science alone oversees.
## Fifth Lecture

Fichte opens with a note that a Wednesday lecture may be canceled due to a general day of prayer, and that he would have continued with preliminary matters had he not seen the need to spare the audience the heaviest material. He has already shared everything conducive to understanding the lectures except for two things: first, the knack for grasping them, which cannot really be imparted; and second, some observations that tend to be poorly received and which he had hoped to omit this time.

The knack for grasping the lectures is the knack of full, complete attention. This should be acquired and exercised before entering the study of the science of knowing. In his written prospectus, Fichte established the sole serious precondition for understanding: participants should have experience with fundamental scientific inquiry — not for the specific information gained, since none of that is presupposed, but because such study awakens and exercises complete attention, and incidentally confers familiarity with scientific terminology. Full, complete attention means throwing oneself into the present object with all one's spiritual power, being completely absorbed in it so that no other thought can occur. It is categorically distinct from partial attention — hearing with half an ear, thinking with half one's power, interrupted by fugitive thoughts and fantasies that eventually overwhelm the mind until one falls into a dreamy fog with eyes wide open. Full attention has no degrees; it is logically contradictory to scattered attention (which admits of many degrees). It fills the spirit completely, while incomplete attention does not.

Everything in understanding the lectures depends on possessing this kind of attention. Every obstacle to understanding follows solely from its absence. If one possesses it, the phenomenon of believing one cannot intuit the particular theorems because one is too quickly confused will fall away. Fichte reassures: in the nature of the science, the same thing is constantly repeated in the most various terms for the most diverse purposes, so that an insight missed on one occasion can be recovered on another. But strictly, each theorem should be grasped when first presented, and those for whom this does not happen have only themselves to blame. The decisive proof that his demand is possible under conditions of complete attention: any distinction between faster or slower mental capacity has no place in the science of knowing. The presentation aims at minds as such, if only they can pay attention. The procedure is: first, the audience is required to construct a specific concept internally (anyone paying attention to the description can do it); next, hold together what has been constructed; and then, without any further assistance, an insight will spring up by itself, like a lightning flash. The speed of one's mind is irrelevant to this final event, because the mind as such has no role in it. We do not create truth — truth creates itself by its own power wherever the conditions of its creation are present, in the same way and at the same rate. If the manifestness did not arise for someone who really carried out the construction, this means only that the construction faded because some distraction intervened — that is, total attention was not placed on the present operation.

With this lack removed, another common phenomenon would also be destroyed at its root: the recurrence of illusions that have already been revealed as illusions. If one has really seen that in intuiting the one, eternally self-same knowing, all differentiation into subjective and objective completely disappears (arising only in the changeable), then one should never again be deceived by the illusion that one is oneself the subject that objectively posits this one knowing. That disjunction is always and everywhere the same illusion and never the truth. If one has truly seen this, one has dissolved into the insight and cannot cease being what one has become — unless one never fully entered the insight in the first place, in which case the old illusion returns at the first opportunity. Crucially, the sequence is: the insight does not leave because the illusion steps in; rather, the illusion enters because the insight has already left.

Fichte then turns to what blocks apprehension of the science of knowing by preventing proper attention from arising. He takes these up collectively in their oneness. They arise from a lack in one's love of science — either a weak, powerless, distracted love, or a secret hatred of knowledge due to some other love already present in the mind.

The other love that leads to secret hatred of knowledge is a perverted self-love: love for the empirically arisen self rather than for the self immersed in the good, the true, and the beautiful. This love takes two forms. The first is self-valuation, which becomes pride. This pride is unwilling to admit that anything could occur in the domain of knowing that it had not itself discovered. The science of knowing's claim to absolute novelty seems to be a contemptuous insult. Such a person focuses not on understanding but on catching the science in some failing, and this divided purpose ensures that the right idea is missed. The person finds just what they were looking for: weaknesses in a confused concept of the enterprise.

The second mode is love for the empirical self's self-enjoyment — the free play of mental capacities with the objects of knowing. Fichte characterizes it pointedly: it calls making something up "thinking" and the invention of a truth for oneself "thinking for oneself." A science that brings all thinking under the most stringent rules and annuls all freedom of spirit in the one, eternal, self-sustaining truth can hardly please such a disposition. Fichte warns against this secret inner polemic not for his own sake but for the listener's, because correct attention — let alone understanding — is impossible while engaged in it.

There is also a ruling passion for the merely empirical, for an inability to feel and enjoy one's spirit except as trained memory. These "personified memories" are not capable of secret hatred, but they become ill-tempered. They want "results" — things that can be observed and reproduced. When they think they have grasped something, the next lecture arrives with further qualifications, different arrangements, changed symbols and expressions, so that not much remains. The purest inner oneness and coherence produces in such people the most extreme confusion and contradiction, simply because they recognize only external, schematic coherence.

Fichte then addresses the simple cold, weak love of science. Whoever seeks, desires, or wishes in science for something besides science itself does not love it as it should be loved and will never enjoy its complete favor. Even the most beautiful purpose (moral improvement) is too lowly here. Love of the absolute (or God) is the rational spirit's true element; science is the absolute's sweet expression, and like the absolute, it can be loved only for its own sake. In a soul given over to this love, there is no room for anything common or ignoble, and purification is intrinsic to it. This love can only be recognized by one who has it. To those who do not yet have it, the only advice is to remove all false loves and subordinate purposes, and the right love will manifest spontaneously.

Fichte now transitions to the substantive topic. He suspects the previous lecture may have seemed too rigorous for a fourth lecture, and this served in part to discover what mode of presentation the new audience requires. He now repeats the previous lecture's content in a more suitable form.

He makes a general methodological remark valid for all previous and subsequent lectures. Their procedure is almost always this: (a) they perform something, guided by a rule of reason operating immediately in them; what they really are at their highest peak remains factical; (b) they then search out and reveal the law that guided them mechanically in the initial action; they see mediately into what they had previously seen into immediately, on the basis of its principle and the ground of its being; they penetrate it in the genesis of its determinateness. In this way they ascend from factical terms to genetic ones. These genetic elements can themselves become factical in another perspective, compelling a further genetic ascent, until they arrive at the absolute source. This is marked as the ongoing procedure: x is the developmental link to y, and y in turn to z. Whoever has not comprehended z from the start — or has lost that comprehension — for that person neither x nor y exists and the entire lecture has been an oration about nothing.

Applying this: the factical term in the last lecture was neither in A nor in the point, but unconditionally in both. This has been grasped; it has made itself evident. However it is analyzed, it contains A, the point, and (in the background) a union of both. With the first two terms denied as the true point of oneness, the third is posited. It is so, factically.

On another level: how has this insight arisen? Not by further reflection on the content (which was completely abandoned) but by focusing on the procedure, asking about genesis. The initial, materially constituted immediacy becomes mediately visible: once such an origination is posited, the factical insight is posited — but solely by means of establishing the origin.

How did they do it? By making a division in something that ought to be a oneness. (The specific terms separated — A and the point — can be set aside; what matters is the act as act.) This division shows itself to be invalid in immediate insight. They did not produce this insight because they wished to; it produced itself absolutely, in absolutely self-generating and self-presenting manifestness, or "pure light." The distinction, insofar as it should be valid by itself, is annulled by manifestness. The same manifestness posits a self-same, intrinsically valid oneness incapable of inner disjunction. The principle of division equals the principle of construction, and thus of the concept. This principle, in its absoluteness (dividing the intrinsically one, wholly by itself, in contradiction to truth), is negated in its absoluteness — seen unconditionally as negated, and therefore negated in and through the absolute light. In this annulling of the absolute concept in relation to intrinsic being, being is inconceivable. Without this relation, it is not even inconceivable but only absolute self-sufficiency. Even the predicate "is" derives from manifestness. Hence the sole remaining ground and midpoint is the pure light.

Fichte affirms that this has intrinsic clarity and incomparable manifestness. It could not be presented with greater order, distinctness, clarity, and precision. Whoever does not see it must be lacking in undivided attention.

The part still to be added: another genetic analysis of the achieved insight. In the reflexion by which the initially unfolded process becomes factical, since they have produced nothing (the insight as insight has produced itself), they cannot ask how they did this, but they can rise to greater clarity. If they only see that it is the light, they are not immediately consumed in this light; they have the light present through its agent or representative — through an insight *into* the light, into its originality or absolute quality. They cannot ask without contradiction how the light itself is produced (since it is recognized as the principle of absolute creation, the question would deny the insight). But they can ask how the insight *into* light has been produced — that is a different question. They need only attend to how the production of this insight took place. Both are true: they put themselves in the condition; and yet they could not have done so on their own grounds alone. Not the light and not even the insight into light, but the insight into the insight into light stands between both.

Regarding the distinction between the immanence and emanence of the production of an insight into light, one must not forget that this extends to the insight and to the light itself. The objective light (qua objective) neither is nor can become the one true light. Pure light enters insight under this aspect. But here something has been won: the highest object is no longer substance for itself but light. Substance is only the form of light as self-sufficient. On the other hand, insight (subjectivity) — actually the inner expression and life of light — disengages itself from the negation of the concept and of division. Doing deposes being, and being deposes doing — this is the fundamental reversal. Doing replaces actual being; being beyond all being deposes doing. Being negates itself in the other as a result of its own doing. Thus they arrive again at the previous point, finding the previous principle again in this self-negation. The division into being and doing is intrinsic to constructing non-separation or oneness, so this negation is true in a certain respect: primordially the division into being and doing is nothing at all.
## Sixth Lecture

Fichte announces that in this and the next talk he will continue developing what has already been presented, aiming to benefit both new and returning listeners. Since the science of knowing, like all philosophy, traces all multiplicity back to absolute oneness (and correlatively deduces multiplicity from oneness), it stands neither in oneness nor in multiplicity but persistently between both. It never descends into absolute multiplicity (which exists as mere empirical givenness) but maintains the perspective from above, from the standpoint of the origin. Therefore, in the science of knowing, they will be very busy with multiplicity and disjunctions.

These disjunctions are new and previously unrecognized. In the usual modes of representation and speech, these differences collapse unnoticed into oneness, and when drawn, they seem extremely minute. (Fichte wryly notes that his critics call this "hairsplitting" — and insists it must be so, because a science that traces everything multiple back to oneness and allows any constructible distinction to remain hidden has failed in its purpose.) One of the main problems for the science of knowing is making its precise distinctions visible and fixing them in the mind so they are never again confused. Fichte proposes to lay out the general schema and basic rule of division in advance, though in an empty and purely formal way, and to deduce it from its principles.

The first general point: since they must stand neither in oneness nor in multiplicity but between both, no oneness that appears as a simple oneness can be the true oneness. The true oneness can only be the principle simultaneously of both the apparent oneness and the apparent multiplicity. And it cannot be this externally — merely projecting oneness and multiplicity as objective appearances. It must be so inwardly and organically: it cannot be a principle of oneness without at the same moment being a principle of disjunction, and vice versa, and it must be comprehended as such. Oneness consists in this absolute, inwardly living, active, powerful, and irrepressible essence. Oneness cannot consist in what we see or conceive in the science of knowing (that would be something objective); rather it consists in what we *are*, pursue, and live. This is introduced once and for all to eliminate errors about this central point. The audience is warned: do not content yourself with a oneness taken relatively and one-sidedly, as if it were the absolute. And if Fichte himself, or any other philosopher, remains content with such a oneness, the audience should state strongly that the philosopher has stopped halfway.

The second point follows: since the true oneness is the principle simultaneously of the apparent oneness and of disjunction, and not one without the other, it makes no difference whether what is provisionally presented as the highest principle at each juncture is regarded as a principle of oneness or of disjunction. Both perspectives are one-sided — the necessary point of view, but not true in themselves. Implicitly the principle is neither; it is both as an organic oneness and is itself their organic oneness. Only principles enter the circle of the science. Whatever is not in any possible respect a principle but is only a principled result (*Principiat*) and phenomenal falls to the empirical level, which they understand on the basis of its principle but never scientifically construct. Every principle that enters the science is simultaneously a principle of oneness and multiplicity and is truly understood only insofar as conceived in those terms. Their scientific life and activity, insofar as it penetrates and merges with the principle, never enters oneness opposed to multiplicity nor into multiplicity; it maintains itself undisturbed between both. Each principle yields an absolutely self-differentiating oneness, schematized as a with x, y, z beneath it. The question is whether this oneness is the highest. If not — if there are several such a's — then they need a new a, until the highest oneness is uncovered, which would be the absolute disjunction, just as described for the absolute oneness. This gives the first general model for the procedure of the science of knowing.

The third point goes deeper. Regarding the explanation pursued so far (not about the principle of disjunction as such, since strictly there is no such thing, but their view of the one implicit principle *as* a principle of disjunction — a one-sided view that they must start with since the science of knowing finds us trapped in this one-sidedness), they find themselves in the familiar inexpressibility: oneness is to separate itself at one stroke into being and thinking and into x, y, z, both equally immediately. In verbal and diagrammatic expression, they are compelled to make one division immediate and the other mediate, although inner insight contradicts this. Expressing this in logical form: in this actual disjunction there are two distinguishing grounds (*fundamenta divisionis*), neither of which can occur without the other. The way this was expressed is probably an empirically discovered turn of phrase, found on the occasion of explaining Kant's philosophy and adding a disjunction between sensible and supersensible being and thinking. The claim that both distinguishing grounds are absolutely inseparable was grounded on this: if what is evident in empirical self-observation is to be explained, then the inseparability must be assumed. This "must" grounds itself directly on a law of reason operating mechanically and without awareness. Thus there was at bottom only an empirical basis with a supersensible one postulated on top — a synthesis post factum. This cannot be accepted by the science of knowing; it must not merely report this inseparability but must grasp it conceptually in its principle and from its principle as necessary. It must see into it genetically and mediately. "Grasping conceptually" means seeing the distinguishing grounds as themselves disjunctive terms of a higher oneness, in which they are one and inseparable as they are when enacted, remaining one and the same stroke. But they are separable and conceptually distinguishable, so that (for example) the ground for distinguishing being and thinking can appear as a modification of the ground for distinguishing sensible and supersensible, and vice versa. When enacted, this disjunction in the oneness of the mere concept concretes into a factical oneness not further distinguishable, and every eye that remains factical is closed to the higher world of the conceptual beyond.

Fichte then makes several important additional remarks. First, he claims to have specified the boundary point between all factical insight and truly philosophical/genetic insight, and to have opened the sources of the entirely new world in concepts that appears only in the science of knowing. This new world is found in the negation of the primordial disjunctive act as immediate and in the insight into this act's principle — materially that it is thus, and formally that it is at all.

Second, the essence of the science of knowing's final scientific form has been explained from a single point more precisely than before. The main point is seeing into the oneness of the distinguishing ground — being and thinking as one, and sensible and supersensible as one. Whoever has understood this (as an empty form) and holds it firmly can scarcely err in its subsequent employment.

Third, as a memory aid: the path of the lectures presents something in factical manifestness and then ascends to genetic insight on the basis of its principles. This is what they have just done. Since the Second Lecture, the inseparability of both distinguishing grounds was developed historically from Kant; now they raise themselves — not to a genetic insight into the principle of this inseparability itself (since the inseparability itself is not yet actually known) — but to a genetic insight into the *form* of this principle, if such inseparability and principle are to exist.

Fourth, they have already jumped past this principle, which was discussed in its form simply for intelligibility's sake, in order to derive it deductively. Good preliminaries for this derivation are already in hand — the point of oneness and difference between A and the point, about which they had said it might be only a profounder view of the higher principle.

Fichte repeats the previously constructed oneness a third time. What was evident was an absolute dividing principle — not A and the disjunctive point (which are principled results that disappear when one looks to the principle) but rather the living absolute separation within us. He stresses that no one should assume the act of thinking the distinction between A and the point is grounded in an original distinction in these things independent of our thinking. In A and the point, one thinks only oneness, which should be unconditionally one with no distinction within itself. The thinker thereby makes clear that the distinction is not based in the object but in one's own thinking as thinking, which becomes the distinguishing principle. But the validity of this product of thought surrenders and dies in relation to the thing itself — and with it, A and the point are uprooted as intrinsically valid. Away with all words and signs! Nothing remains except living thinking and insight, which cannot be shown on a blackboard or represented in any way but can only be surrendered to nature.

This yields the intuition that it rests neither in A nor in the point but in the absolute oneness of both, intuited unconditionally without sources or premises. Absolute insight presents itself here: pure intuition, pure light, from nothing out of nothing, going nowhere — bringing oneness with it but in no way based on it. Everything depends on each person correctly identifying with this insight, in this pure light. The light exists only insofar as it vitally intuits in the individual. It exists only in living self-presentation as absolute insight, and whoever is not grasped by it never arrives at the living light, whatever substitute they may have.

Finally, Fichte distinguishes this surrender into the living light from the consideration of the light in its inner quality — which will inwardly objectify and kill the light. Only the light remains as eternal and absolute; through its own inner essence, it sets down what is self-subsistent. But there is no life or expression of the light except through negating the concept (and hence through positing it). No expression of the light could arise unless they first unconditionally posit and see a life as a necessary determination of the light's being. In adding life to light, however, they have divided the two and thereby killed the light's inner liveliness by the concept. They contradict themselves: denying that life can be distinguished from light, the very thing they have just accomplished. This contradiction may be essential and necessary, since it may implicitly be the negation of the concept. In this consideration, light shows itself through its mere positedness as the ground for both self-subsisting being and the concept — the concept in a twofold sense: negated in its intrinsic validity, and posited as absolute but not realized (actual as appearance and vitality but in no way conditioning the light's inner essence). By the concept's being posited, A and the point are also posited — as appearance, and not as primordial appearance but as conditioning appearance, the inner life of the primordial appearance. Appearing should occur again as the unity of the distinguishing grounds; its life comes from the liveliness of the concept, which in turn stems from the light's liveliness — thus an appearance of appearance of appearance. Everything is brought together again when enacted. This is the schema of an established, rule-governed descent.
## Seventh Lecture

Fichte states the purpose: to give a brief account of the rules according to which the disjunctions to be made in the science of knowing proceed. Three points are outlined: (1) the disjunction is into principles, each equally a principle of unity and of disjunction; (2) a deduction from this to a general schema of the total empirical domain according to the form of its genetic principle — this is an entirely new explanation, prompted by Fichte having observed that some in the audience perceived something deeper but could not find it themselves; (3) a remark that the investigation has already gone beyond this principle to a higher one and has begun to deduce the principle itself. The insight achieved is restated: it rests neither in A nor in the point — the oneness beyond is nothing in itself, although posited as in itself; it exists only through the light and in the light, as the light's projection. Light itself — contemplation of light.

Fichte announces a further step that opens a whole new side of the investigation. They have previously begun to derive the principle of oneness and disjunction of materially different principles of division, without recognizing that this was what they were doing.

Setting aside prior work temporarily, Fichte asks the audience to recall and consider: when they observe the light, the light is objectified, alienated, and killed as something primordial. What is attributed materially to the light in this observation has been explained and connected to the schema. Now they will explain the observing itself in its inner form — no longer asking what it contains and leads to, but how it itself inwardly occurs, while rising to its principle and viewing it genetically.

It is immediately clear that the light is in us (in what we ourselves are and do in observing) not immediately but through a representative or proxy that objectifies and kills it. Where then does the highest oneness and true principle rest? No longer in the light itself, since we dissolve in the light as living beings. Neither in the representative and image of the light, because a representative without what is represented, or an image without what it images, is nothing. An image as such has no intrinsic self-sufficiency but points toward an external, primordial source. Here they have not only factical manifestness (as with A and the point) but conceptual manifestness: oneness only with disjunction, and vice versa. Something imaged (like the light) is not thinkable without an image, nor an image (as image) without something imaged.

Fichte flags this as important. In grasping this, one carries out an act of thinking that has essence, spirit, and meaning — fully and completely self-identical and unchangeable. This cannot be shared directly but can be constructed either from the concept of something imaged (which posits an image) or from the image (which posits something imaged). The question: apart from the arrangement of terms (which is irrelevant), have two different things been thought in the two concepts, or exactly the same thing? The listener must elevate himself past the irrelevant arrangement to the essential matter of content, spirit, and meaning, and then the intended insight will manifest. If so, then an absolute oneness of content is manifest here that remains unaltered as oneness but splits only in the vital fulfillment of thinking into an inessential disjunction, grounded neither in the content nor spoiling it. Either an objective disjunction into something imaged and its image, or (preferably) a subjective-objective disjunction into a conception of something imaged based on the directly posited image, and a conception of the image based on the directly posited something imaged. The latter is preferable because in that case one has the disjunction firsthand.

In this genetically oriented view of the light, the principle is the concealed oneness that cannot be described further but is lived only immediately in this act of seeing. As the primordial concept's content, it presents itself as absolute oneness and as absolute disjunction in its living fulfillment. The something imaged in the concept's content should be the light. Therefore their principle (that is, they themselves) rests no longer in the light or in the light's representative, but in the oneness in and between the two — a oneness realized in their act of thinking.

Fichte calls the concept situated here the "primordial concept" (*Urbegriff*). What had previously been taken as the source of the absolutely self-sufficient — what appeared as the original and was original for them — actually first arises, in its objectivity, from this concept as one of its disjunctive terms. The concept is therefore more original than the light itself; so far as they have gone, it is what is truly original. This provides a deeper genetic explanation of the representatives of the original light mentioned earlier.

The concept is thereby determined further and grasped more deeply. Until now it was a dividing principle that, as self-sufficient, expired in the light and preserved only a bare factical existence as an appearance qualifying the original light's appearance. It had no contents except what pure light added through immediate intuition in a higher synthetic unity. Now the concept has its own implicit content, self-subsistent, totally unchangeable, and undeniable. The principle of division (which arises again and is negated as intrinsically valid) no longer is essential to it but only conditions its life, its appearance. The concept's content is self-subsistent — exactly the same substantial being previously projected out of intuition, now manifest in the concept as prior to all intuition and as the principle of objective and objectifying intuition itself. Previously the concept and life/appearance of light qualified each other reciprocally and externally. Now the single concept grounds its appearance through its own essential being. In this concept, image and what it images are posited absolutely, organically constructed only through one another. The concept's appearance announces and is the exponent of its inner being — as an organic unity of the "through-one-another" (*Durcheinander*), which must be presupposed. Its permanent, unchanging being and its inner organization of the through-one-another are completely one. Absolute oneness is therefore grounded and explained through itself.

Fichte emphasizes the importance of fundamentally seeing into the inner organic oneness of the primordial concept, since this is what will be needed going forward. Does the image, as image, completely and unconditionally posit something imaged? If yes, does the something imaged likewise posit an image? Both can be seen as posited immediately by the other, but only if one of the two is posited as prior. Fichte asks the audience to abstract from their own insight — something constantly done in ordinary life (when it shouldn't be) and essential for entering the science of knowing. The question concerns truth in itself, recognized as being and remaining true even if no one saw it: is it not true in itself that image entails something imaged and vice versa? What remains as pure truth in its briefest expression? Perhaps that a posits b and b posits a? But should this truth be divided into two parts linked by the empty expletive "and" — a word scarcely understood, the least understandable word in language, unexplicated by any previous philosophy? (It is the synthesis post factum.) Beyond this, the determination of terms derives solely from their place in the sequence: image is consequent because something imaged is antecedent, and vice versa. Entering more deeply into the meaning, the terms dissolve into the expressions "antecedent" and "consequent" — all this dissolves into appearance.

What common element remains behind as the condition for the whole exchange? Obviously only the "through-one-another" (*Durcheinander*) that initially holds together every inference, and which leaves the consequence relation exactly as free in general as it has appeared to be.

Fichte introduces the focal point as the concept of a pure enduring through-one-another in living appearance, schematized diagrammatically. The concept proceeds from act alive, but not finished and complete — whoever wants completion must do it themselves. On the other side, the concept projects the one eternally self-same light as intuition. What stands beneath the concept in the schema are parts of its externalization and further modifications — not of the light but of its appearance (*Ansicht*) in the living concept. Only through life to the concept and only through the concept to life and the appearance of the light in itself, but the first modification is never pure, always in one or another variant. This is the creation of the science of knowing in its possible modifications.
## Eighth Lecture

Fichte opens by declaring that they have arrived at a focal point in the lectures — one that facilitates a clear insight and overview more effectively than any other, and that will permit greater brevity in what follows. He therefore counsels against economizing on time now.

He restates the achieved insight: actually and in fact (not merely provisionally), an absolute, self-grounded insight negates an equally absolutely created division (one not grounded in things) as invalid, and posits in the background a self-subsistence that cannot itself be described more precisely. At this point, the main thing is that everyone assembled has really and actually seen into this as presented, and that they will never forget this self-insight or allow it to fade, but will take root in it and merge with it.

What has been said is not just a report by any philosopher but exists unconditionally, remaining always true before anyone sees into it and even if no one ever does. They have penetrated to the core and viewed the truth with their own eyes. Nor is this proposed as a hypothetical proposition shown to be true by its usefulness in explaining phenomena (as in Kantian and every other philosophy). It is immediately true independently of all phenomena and their explicability. Whatever genuinely follows from it, if sufficiently determined, is as unconditionally true as the original insight. Whatever contradicts it or any of its results is unconditionally false and should be abandoned. This categorical decisiveness between truth and error is the condition for all science. It is far removed from the skeptical paralysis that in the present age parades as "wisdom" — doubting what is unconditionally manifest and wanting to clarify it by the most derivative means.

Regarding the explanation of phenomena from a manifest principle: if the principle is sound and the inferences correct, the explanation will go well. But since the principle grants true insight into the essence of phenomena as such, it may well happen that many things do not even deserve to be called genuine phenomena — they dissolve into deceptions and phantasms, though all ages held them to be phenomena or even self-subsisting realities. Science, far from acquiring its law from the factical apprehension of appearances, legislates for them. Only what can be derived from the principle counts as a phenomenon; what cannot be derived is an error simply because of its non-derivation, even if it may also be immediate.

This is the second observation: as a result of the repeated insight, a new world of light has opened, transcending actual knowledge, and a world of error in which nearly every mortal finds himself has perished.

Fichte then presents the result in its first numbered point. By annihilating the formal concept (the condition for its own real appearance and vivacity), the light, as the one true self-sufficiency, posits a self-sufficient being that is not further determinable and that — because the concept that tries to grasp it is insufficient — is inconceivable. The light is simply one, the concept that disappears in the light is one (the division of what is one in itself), and being is one. It can never be an issue of anything other than these three. The one existence arises in the intuition of what is independent and in the concept's negation. If one wants to call the absolutely independent One, the self-consuming being, "God," then all genuine existence is the intuition of God.

But Fichte immediately adds a critical qualification — and announces that a whole world of errors will be extinguished thereby. This being, despite the fact that the light posits it as absolutely independent (because the light loses itself in its life), is actually not so, precisely because it bears within itself the predicate "is," "persistence," and therefore death. What is truly absolutely independent is the light itself. Divinity must therefore be posited in the living light and not in dead being. And certainly not in us, as the science of knowing has often been misunderstood to say — however one tries to understand that, it is senseless.

Fichte addresses the fundamental difficulty of any philosophy that wants to avoid dualism and is serious about oneness: either we must perish, or God must. We will not, and God ought not. The first brave thinker who saw the light about this understood that if negation is to be carried out, we must undergo it ourselves. That thinker was Spinoza. In Spinoza's system, every separate existence vanishes as independently valid. But Spinoza then kills even his absolute or God — his substance equals being without life — because he forgets his very own act of insight, the life in which the science of knowing as transcendental philosophy makes its entrance.

In a parenthetical aside on atheism: only those can accuse the science of knowing of atheism who want a dead God — inwardly dead at the root, dressed up afterward with apparent life, temporal existence, will, and sometimes blind caprice, through which neither God's life nor ours becomes comprehensible. Nothing is gained by this except adding one more number to the crowd of finite beings — one just as constrained and finite as ourselves and in no way different in kind.

One term is being, the other (the negated concept) is without doubt subjective thought, or consciousness. Thus one of the two basic disjunctions — that into B and T (being and thinking) — has now been grasped in its oneness and as proceeding completely and simply from its oneness (L = light). Parenthetically, this simultaneously provides the schema for the negation of the I in the pure light. If one posits (as everyone could easily agree) that the principle of the negated concept is the I — since the I appears as freely constructing the concept in response to an invitation — then the I's destruction in the face of what is valid in itself is simultaneously my destruction, since as the concept's principle I no longer exist. Being grasped and torn apart by manifestness that I do not make, but that creates itself, is the phenomenal image of my being negated and extinguished in pure light.

For the second numbered point: this is a result of the light itself and its inward living expression. Things must remain here as a consequence of the insight. But Fichte claims that, with correct reflection, they are already beyond it. They have considered the light and objectified it. The light therefore has a twofold expression and existence: partly its inner expression and existence (conditioned by the negation of the concept, conditioning and positing absolute being), and partly an external and objective expression and existence, in and for their insight.

Regarding the latter: they did not possess it immediately but raised themselves to it from the beginning of the investigation — first by abstracting from the variety of objective knowings to the absolute, self-presenting insight that knowing must remain self-same in all variation, and then by deeper genetic examination of this insight. This has been their procedure; the new and unknown spiritual world in which they pursue their path has arisen by this procedure alone. It now appears that they could properly have neglected this procedure (as they undoubtedly did every day before coming to the science of knowing). Taking up this appearance — without inquiring into its validity — it contains the following: the light's external existence in insight, as the one absolute, eternally self-same in its fundamental division into being and thinking, is conditioned by a series of abstractions and reflections conducted freely — in short, by the artificially created science of knowing. This external existence arises only in this way and for it, and otherwise not at all.

But regarding the inner existence and expression of the light: if the light exists unconditionally (whether we have insight or not, and insight depends on appearing freedom), it is in and for itself the very same one, eternally self-identical and thoroughly necessary, if only the light exists. Therefore there are two different modes of light's life and existence: one mediately and externally in the concept, the other simply immediate through itself, even if no one realizes it. Strictly speaking, in actual fact no one ever does realize it; this inner life of the light is completely inconceivable.

Light is originally divided into being and thinking. That the light lives unconditionally means it splits itself completely originally, and inexplicably, into being and concept (which persists even though negated as concept). Insight can follow this split reconstructively, but must leave the inner split standing as impenetrable. This yields, in addition to the previously discovered formal inconceivability, a material content of light that remains ever inconceivable.

Fichte marks this as a major point expressed more clearly than he has previously achieved. That the light lives absolutely through itself means: it splits itself absolutely into B and T. But "absolutely through itself" also means "independent of any insight and absolutely negating the possibility of insight." Yet they have seen and had insight into the fact that light splits itself into B and T — consequently this split no longer resides in the light but in the insight into the light. What remains? The inward life of the light itself in pure identity — from itself, out of itself, through itself, without any split; a life that exists only in immediate living and has itself and nothing else. "It lives" — and thus it will live and appear, and otherwise no path leads to it. This cannot be described further; it is precisely what cannot be realized, what remains behind after completely fulfilled insight, and therefore what should exist through itself. All predicates assigned to it — "what remains behind after insight," "what ought to exist through itself," and even "absolute substance" — are only negative criteria, in themselves null and void. The system does not begin with negation and death but pursues death to its last resort in order to arrive at life. This lies in the light that is one with reality, and reality opens up in it. The whole of reality as such, according to its form, is nothing more than the graveyard of the concept that tries to find itself in the light.

Their enterprise has achieved a new standpoint and penetrated more deeply. The light, previously understood only in its form as self-creating manifestness (and hence assigned only formal being), has transformed into one living being without disjunctive terms. What was assumed to be the original light has changed into mere insight and representation of the light. Not only has the concept recognized as concept been negated, but even light and being as well. Previously, the concept's being was to have been negated — but how could one arrive at this being, even though empty? It was to be negated by something that was itself nothing. Now they have an absolute reality in the light itself, from which the appearing being and its nonexistence in the face of the absolute might be comprehensible.

Fichte notes explicitly what direct experience teaches: this reality in the original light is unconditionally and completely one and self-same, and no insight is allowed into how it might arrive inwardly at division and multiplicity. The division into being and thinking — along with whatever depends on it — resides in the concept that perishes in the presence of reality and has nothing to do with reality and the light. According to the testimony of appearance in life (provisionally granted phenomenological truth), another disjunction ought to arise that stands higher than or on the same level as being and thinking, ranging across both — taken for a disjunction in reality. Since this contradicts the previous insight, it is certainly false. This new ground of disjunction must lie in a determination of the concept not yet recognized or sufficiently explored. The concept, as concept, must itself be conceivable, so no new inconceivability can appear here. If this determination is grasped conceptually, everything it contains can be derived conceptually from it. Whatever range of differences may come forward in appearing reality, it is clear a priori that they are B-T + C + L (being-thinking plus concept plus light) — one and the same, remaining eternally self-identical, different only in the concept. Reality is not only generally completely deduced and comprehensible, but also divided and analyzed a priori into all its possible parts — excluding light itself, which is not a part but the one true essence.

Insight can have insight into itself, the concept can conceive itself — as far as one reaches, the other reaches. The concept finds its limits, conceives itself as limited, and its completed self-conceiving is the conceiving of this limit. Beyond the limit lies the one, pure living light. Insight points beyond itself to life, or experience — not to the "miserable assembly of empty and null appearances" but to that experience which alone contains something new: a divine life.
## Ninth Lecture

Fichte announces that in the next three lectures he will enter a deeper investigation than any so far, one that will secure a stable focus and permanent guide for the science even before they possess this guide. To avoid confusion, they must hold firmly to what has been provisionally laid down.

Formally, they are already located beyond the prolegomenon and actually inside the science of knowing, because they have already created in themselves insights that have transposed them into an entirely new world — raised above all factical manifestness, in whose realm the prolegomenon always remains. The transition from prolegomenon to science happened unnoticed: they had to elucidate the science of knowing's procedure by examples, and since the audience's readiness made it possible, they used the actual thing as the original example. Now they drop this as mere example and take things up in earnest; they are inside the science.

Fichte recalls the position reached: I (Image) positing something imaged in it equals B (being), and vice versa, united in the oneness of the light (L). On one side, the connection I-L-B, the essential element of all light; on the other, the modifications without which it does not exist. This indicates the general way but nothing specific is yet known. It remains only the prolegomenon to the investigation.

This provides a hint concerning an important point. Knowing should divide itself at one stroke according to two distinct principles of division: B-T/oneness and x,y,z/oneness. They now see that the light, in itself eternally one and self-identical, does not divide itself in itself but rather divides itself in its insight and as being seen into multiplicity (whatever x, y, z may be). The same light, in itself and in its eternal identity independent from insight into it, divides itself into being and thinking. If the light does not even exist except in being the object of insight, this is again divided; and the light does not exist in itself without dividing into being and thinking. Therefore this disjunction is absolutely one and indivisible according to both distinguishing grounds. This proposition, with whatever further qualifications it may receive, is true in itself and will never be permitted to fall. (Having fixed termini in the investigation allows one to follow its most divergent turns without confusion.)

Regarding the concept — which lies neither in the light (as what is imaged) nor in the insight (as the image itself) but between these two — they realize that formally in itself this concept is a mere "through-one-another" (*Durcheinander*), without external consequences: without antecedent and without consequent, both of which arise only out of the living exhibition of the concept. Since the concept, as absolute relation of imaged to image and vice versa, is only this relationship, it makes no difference that the thing imaged should be self-sufficient light and the image should be this particular image. Something imaged and the image, simply as such, are sufficient. The imaged thing and image are also of little concern to the concept's inner essence (presupposed as absolutely self-sufficient); this inner essence is evidently a mere through-one-another. That this through-one-another manifests in the image and the thing imaged has shown itself empirically. But who authorizes us to say either that this through-one-another *must* manifest or exist, or (if it must) that it must construct itself directly in the image and the thing imaged rather than in an endless variety of other ways? Through this consideration, they lose the subordinate terms and their distinguishing grounds for a system of genetic knowing. And who authorizes the assumption that the thing imaged could only be the light, so that necessarily the image of the light must bring in the other distinguishing ground? Thereby they also lose the second half of any system that does not rest content with factical manifestness and rejects everything not seen genetically as necessary.

This result comes out the only way it could, once one thinks seriously. If one posits the concept (the absolute through-one-another) as an independent self-subsisting being, everything external to it disappears and no escape is possible — just as happened previously with the light when it was likewise posited. Any independent being annuls any other being external to it. Whenever a being of this sort is posited, it will similarly have this result, which resides in its form.

This observation provides exactly the right task for further procedure. Fichte wishes they could come to know this procedure in its unity right now, so they would not go astray among the various forms and changes it may assume. The genetic relation whose interruption has come to light must be completed. This cannot be done by inserting new terms (where would they get them? — they are scarcely capable of adding in thought where nothing exists). The currently absent genetic relation must be found in the terms already available, which have not yet been considered completely genetically but so far still partly factically. If the only important thing were arriving at the goal, it would not matter which available term they began with. If they worked through only one to its implicit creative life, the flood of light that simultaneously overcomes and connects everything would necessarily dawn. But they also have the task of following the shortest way, so they naturally hold on to what has shown itself most immediately — light and concept — in regard to which they now find themselves in doubt as to which is the true absolute. If they work through both so that each shows itself as the principle of the other, then: (a) in each they have grasped mediately the distinguishing ground immediately present in the other, and (b) beginning from both, they have obtained a yet higher common principle of distinction and oneness for both on essential grounds.

Both light and concept then lose their absolute character and retain only relative validity. Their knowledge of the emerging science of knowing transcends them as something absolutely presupposed; according to its external form this is a synthesis post factum. But since this transcendence is itself genetic in its inner essence — and it is not merely said (as Kant said, and they themselves preliminarily) that "there must surely be some higher oneness," but rather this oneness is actually constructed — it is a genetic synthesis. The science of knowing, genetic in its principles and permeating the higher oneness, is permeated by it and is therefore identical with it, stepping down into multiplicity and being simultaneously analytic and synthetic — truly, livingly genetic. Their task is discovering the oneness of L (light) and C (concept) in this prescribed way; this discovery is the common point of the next stage. The procedure's modifications are grounded in the necessity of properly permeating C by genetically permeating L, and vice versa — constant shifts in standpoint, being tossed from one to the other.

Fichte does not conceal that this procedure is not without difficulty and demands particularly high attention. But whoever has actually seen into what has been presented and holds fast to the schema and the common point for the investigation will not be led astray. This is the only truly difficult part of the science. The other part — deducing the mediate and secondary disjunctions — is a brief and easy affair for those who have properly achieved the first, however monstrous it may appear to those who know nothing of the first. This second part has the task of deducing all possible modifications of apparent reality. The individual trapped in factical manifestness wonders at this because it is the only difficulty accessible and apparent to him. But until it has its own openly declared principle, this deduction is nothing more than a clever discovery that has recourse to the reader's genius but can never justify itself before rigorous reason.

Fichte takes this down to specific cases: the deduction of time and space (with which Kantian philosophy exhausts itself and in which certain Kantians remain imprisoned for life), of the material world in its levels of organization, of the world of understanding in universal concepts, of the realm of reason in moral or religious ideas, or even the world of minds — none of this presents difficulty and is certainly not the masterpiece of philosophy. All these things are actually nonexistent; each is the very easily grasped appearance of the truly existing One. Some have believed in the existence of bodies (truthfully, in nothing presented as nothing) and at most in the existence of souls (in ghosts), and have conducted deep researches into the relation of body and soul or the soul's immortality. Fichte does not support skepticism about the latter or wish to wound faith. The science of knowing legislates nothing about the soul's immortality, since in its terms there is neither soul, nor dying, nor mortality, nor therefore immortality — only life, eternal in itself. Whatever exists, is in life, and is as eternal as life. The science of knowing holds with Jesus that "whoever believes in me shall never die" but is given to have life in himself. The science of knowing does not cut off errors individually (as soon as error is removed on one side it springs up on the other) but insists on cutting off the single root for all branches. It asks for patience and that one not sympathize with individual symptoms of disease: if only the inner man is first healed, the individual appearances will take care of themselves.

What was built up today was the declaration of the proper standpoint and the consideration of the unity of following proceedings — its coherence as a first part with a later second part. Everything previous can be viewed as a condition of clear insight into today's material. Nothing was gained for material insight into the investigation's object; there is an important point found last time (*aliud agendo*), dropped today as not relevant, which will be investigated again tomorrow for the purpose clearly announced. But regarding form, a general perspective and orientation has been achieved that will guard against future confusions. The schema serves as provisionally valid and will endure only revisions grounded in growing insight, not capricious ones.

For those who attended the previous lecture series: what is now called "concept" was named in the first series "inner essence of knowing"; what is called "light" was there "knowing's formal being" — the former the intelligible, the latter intuition. The inner essence of knowing can only be manifested in the concept (indeed in the original concept); this concept, as implicitly insight, must posit insight, or light. The task expressed as "finding the oneness of C and L" is the same as "the essence of knowing not without its being, and vice versa, nor intellectual knowing without intuition and vice versa, which are to be understood so that the disjunction within them must become one in the oneness of the insight." This insight has returned under various guises and relations, always according to synthetic rules. The difference now — and an advantage of the present path — is that from the start, even before plunging into the labyrinth of appearance, they can recognize future observations in their spiritual oneness.

Fichte concludes with a remark on the form of presentation: one possesses as genuine property only what one possesses independently of the form in which it was received. Only what is received living in the moment strikes living minds; not forms deadened by being passed from hand to hand or by long intervals. Even if lecturing to the same audience again, he would have taken an almost equally divergent path and given the same advice about its utility.
## Tenth Lecture

Fichte states that the next task is clearly determined: to see into L (light) as the genetic principle of C (concept) and vice versa, and thus to find the oneness and disjunction of the two. He adds parenthetically: who among the audience, prior to studying the science of knowing, has known L or C in the purity and simplicity in which they have been presented here? (Not in general and with confusion — any philosophy distinguishes immediate presentation of an object from an abstract concept — but true L and C.) Their task concerns itself with doing this, and with its resolution the science of knowing is completed in its essentials. The science answers a question it must itself pose and dissolves a doubt it has first raised. It should not seem strange that there is no bridge to it from the usual point of view and that everything must be learned from within it.

Something happened for the resolution of this task in the previous session, which they now briefly review. As factical manifestness makes clear, light plainly arises in a dual relation: partly as inwardly living (through which inner life it must divide itself into concept and being), and partly in an external insight that is freely created and objectifies the light along with its inner life.

Taking up the first: what makes the inward life inward? Obviously that it is not external. It becomes external in being seen by the insight. Therefore, the inward life exists because it is outside any insight, inaccessible to it, and negates it. Light's absolute inner life is posited as existing only in living itself and not otherwise, encountered only immediately in living and nowhere else. The genuinely, truly real (*Reale*) in knowing rests here. But they have just spoken of this inner life and therefore in some way conceived it — yet how? As absolutely inaccessible to insight. They have conceived and determined it only negatively. It is not conceivable in any other way. The concept of reality, of the inner material content of knowing, is only the negation of insight and arises only from it. In truth this is no negation but the highest affirmation (*Position*) — which is indeed once again a concept, but in truth we in no way conceive it; rather we have it and are it.

This point is to be completed and determined right now and will have its application uninterruptedly. Two ways for the light to live absolutely: internally and externally. Externally in insight; internally, therefore, absolutely not in the insight and not for it, but turning it away. By this means, the system is protected against the greatest offense with which one can charge a philosophy: vacuity. Reality, as genuine true reality, has been deduced. No one will confuse this reality with being (objectivity) — the latter is subsistence-for-self and dependence-on-self, closed in on itself and therefore dead. The former exists only in living, and living exists only in it; it can do nothing else but live. Because the system has taken life itself as its root, it is secured against death, which in the end grasps every other system somewhere in its root. Since light and life are absolutely one, this reality can be only one and eternally self-same. Thereby the system has won enduring oneness and secured itself from the charge of duality in its root.

Insight, Fichte asserts, is completely negated in living light. But then they see, and see into, the disjunction into C and B. Therefore, this disjunction — previously ascribed to the inner light itself — should not be ascribed to it but instead to the insight that takes its place, or to the original concept of light. The concept reaches higher; the true light withdraws itself. The absolute negation of the concept may well remain a nothing for the science of knowing (which has its essence in concepts), becoming an affirmation only in living.

Fichte then offers two further comments belonging to philosophy's art and method. First, he retraces how they arrived at an error in which they had previously hovered, how they now recognize it as erroneous, and what process led them to the higher term. The insight that had been named "pure, absolute light" arose immediately without premises or conditions (in terms of content), but in its form it remained factical and dependent on prior completion in concrete cases. They might have inferred from the following that it could not have its application here: although dividing the concept into A and the point was given up as inadequate, there remained a new disjunction in what was taken as absolute — simultaneously negating and positing, the former through its formal being, the latter through its essence. But no disjunction can be absolute and merely factical; as surely as it is a disjunction it must become genetic, since disjunction is genetic in its root. Result: since the initial supposition grounded itself partially on factical insight, it must be given up.

How then did they arrive at this insight of giving something up and at the higher term? By means of (i) the distinction between two ways the light exists — inwardly and outwardly — admittedly given only with factical manifestness; (ii) genetic insight into this distinction and the question how something absolutely inward might arise as inward; and (iii) elevating into a genetic perspective what had been thought previously only in faded, factical terms. The entire disjunction between inner and outer arises only in a factical point of view. Here too the observation holds: facticity erased on one side pops up on the other, and they will not be entirely relieved of it until the present task is completed. (For returning listeners: the distinction between the light's inner and outer life is the same as the distinction between immanent and emanent forms of existence from the previous series.)

Second remark: C and L are both only concepts. C is purely disjunction in general, unable to give further account of itself — from the current standpoint, it has simply two terms not further distinguishable. L is not disjunction in general but the specific disjunction into being and concept. The latter, as the principle of disjunction in general, has enduring inner content, as does B (the principle of oneness). Therefore L's terms are not just two terms in general but have internal difference. From the current standpoint, L is by no means negated, nor can it be from that perspective. If it must be negated (as it evidently must a priori, since otherwise the zero state in which no further disjunction truly remains could never be reached), completely different means will have to be employed.

Fichte characterizes the point reached in relation to the task. C and L are to be reduced to oneness, and this must be done so that C is so rigorously mastered that they see into it as the genetic principle of light, and vice versa. With which term to begin is left either to caprice or philosophical skill. In the previously discussed point, L was taken as a starting point; the concept then proceeded genetically from L, since L transformed itself into the concept. More exactly: their own observing — not then visible, which they lived and merged into — divided itself beyond the then regnant L, and in this division negated L into 0/C, creating both out of itself. This shift in viewpoint is not merely a change of word and sign but a real change: what stood previously (whether called L or C) was to be the absolute (a real predicate) and should divide itself into C and B (also a real predicate). Both predicates combine to form a synthetic sentence determining the absolute. In its essence, this sentence is contradicted by the really opposite sentence: the principle of the disjunction into being and thinking is not absolute but subordinate; in the absolute the two are not distinguished. Another correction follows: what were supposed to be two different distinguishing grounds, held apart by the distinctness of light in itself and its representative concept, now collapse into one and the same concept. This concept could very easily provide the one eternally self-same disjunctive factor, which does not appear in the original appearance but appears as doubled in the secondary appearance — in appearance as appearance.

The spirit of the task remains the same through all shifts in perspective: L through C and vice versa. Their true L is now = 0, and it is clear this cannot be approached more closely — it negates all insight. The first path is already completed on the initial attempt. Nothing remains besides taking up C and testing whether through it they can further determine — not 0, which remains purely unchangeable and indeterminable — but rather what they now are and live as the truly highest term. A new classificatory division, the determination on the basis of C, becomes the second principal part of the present work.

Fichte offers preliminary hints. The concept's inward and completely immutable essence has been acknowledged as a "through" (*Durch*). Though this insight is not factical in its content but a purely intellectual object, it has a factical support: the construction of image and thing imaged and the indifference of the inference between them. They would be permitted to use this basic quality of the concept if, in applying it, they succeeded in negating its factical origin. If one embraces a "through" energetically, the same principle is readily seen as a disjunction. But the same question recurs: how should a dead "through," defined as they have defined it, come to life? It has no basis in itself for actualization.

Fichte proposes: how would it be if the internal life of the absolute light (= 0) were the "through"'s life? Then the "through" would be deducible from the light by a syllogism: (i) if there is to be an expression — an outward existence of the immanent life as such — then this is possible only with an absolutely existent "through"; (ii) but there must be such an expression; (iii) hence, the absolute "through" (the original concept, or reason) exists absolutely.

Further: how would it be if this living "through" (living by an alien life, but still living), as the oneness of the "through," divided itself into thinking and being — that is, in itself and in the origin of its life? This division, as a division of the enduring "through" as such, would be comprehensive and inseparable from it and its life. How would it be if it did not remain trapped in its essence as "through" but might itself objectify and deduce the latter — proceeding according to the law of the "through" — and in this objectification split itself again in the second way? And since a "through" can exist only by means of a "through" (its being as "through" is only mediate), and the first mode of being would scarcely be possible without the second, the first division could not be without some of the second, and vice versa. Since this "through" is our own inner essence, and a "through" dissolves completely into another "through," everything based in these terms must be completely conceivable and deducible.

In all these "how would it be if" clauses, Fichte has regarded "0" as life. But it is not merely life — it is something indivisibly joined with life, grasped by the purely negative concept of reality. If reality is indivisible from life, and life lives in a "through," then reality lives as absolute reality, but since it is a "through," it lives it in a "through" and as a "through." Consider what follows if the one absolute reality, which can only be lived immediately, occurs in the form of an absolute "through": it cannot be grasped anywhere unless an antecedent arises through which it is to be, and since it is grasped only as a "through," it must have a consequence. This follows unavoidably by every act of apprehending reality. The infinite divisibility in absolute continuity — what the science of knowing calls "quantifiability" as the inseparable form of reality's appearance — arises as the basic phenomenon of all knowing.

In this brief paragraph, Fichte claims to have pulled together the entire content of the science of knowing. Whoever has grasped it and sees it as necessary — the premises and conditions for this manifestness have already been completely laid out — can learn nothing more here and can only clarify analytically what has already been seen into. Whoever has not yet seen into it has at least been well prepared for what follows.
## Eleventh Lecture

Fichte notes that in the previous lecture he succeeded in presenting the essence and entire contents of the science of knowing in a few brief strokes. Against his initial purpose, he will now use today's hour for further observations about this sketch, since certainty about the form in advance will make the actual working out of the contents easier.

The concept (C) has been identified with the "through" (*Durch*), in which disjunction resides. Fichte had said previously that the "through" has nearly all the natural tendencies of life but in itself is only death. He now reflects further: it is immediately clear what it means to say a "through" actually arises, has taken place, or exists. Anyone who considers the possibility of this existence will see that, considered formally, something more belongs to it besides the pure "through." In the "through" we find only the bare formal duality of terms; for completion, it needs a transition from one to the other — a living oneness for the duality. Life as life cannot lie in the "through," although the form life assumes here (as transition from one to the other) does lie in it. Life comes entirely from itself and cannot be derived from death. Result: the existence of a "through" presupposes an original life grounded not in the "through" but entirely in itself.

This is seen into at once. But what is contained in this insight? The insight formed in positing the "through"'s existence brings life with it — in image and concept. In this insight, life is grasped in the form of a "through" (only mediately). The explanation of the "through" is itself a "through." The first "through" posits its terms at one stroke, and the explanatory "through" posits the first as positing them at one stroke (horizontal arrangement). But regarding inner meaning, the first "through" does not posit its terms at one stroke: life should be the condition and the "through"'s existence what is conditioned — life should be the antecedent and the "through"'s existence the consequent (perpendicular arrangement). Both exist only in connection with each other.

The concept remains the focus of everything. It constructs a living "through" hypothetically: should this latter be, then the existence of living follows. A hypothetical "should" is not grounded on any existing thing but purely in the concept, collapsing if the concept collapses. The concept announces itself in this "should" as pure, existing in itself, as creator and sustainer from itself, of itself, and through itself. The "should" is the immediate expression of the concept's independence. If its inner form and essence are independent, so are its contents. The existence of a "through" announces itself as completely absolute and a priori, in no way grounded on another preceding real existence. The concept is the antecedent and absolute *prius* to the hypothetical positing of the "through"'s existence; the latter is the concept's expression, through which the concept preserves itself as an absolutely inward "through."

In its vivacity, the concept changes into an insight that, unconditioned, produces itself — insight into a life in and of itself that must necessarily be presupposed. Ascending: the absolute concept is the principle of intuition into life in itself. It is possible to think the existence of a "through" in a shallow, faded way without any insight into the presupposed life arising. For insight to arise, this existence must be conceived with full energy and vivacity. In the pale imitation, the "through" is not really thought as it must be (as a genetic principle); if it were, what ought to be manifest would be manifest. The true focus, the genuine ideal *prius*, is no longer just the concept but the inward life whose outcome (*posterius*) is the concept. The "should" is not the highest exponent of reason's independence; the appearance of inner energy is this highest exponent. The hypothetical "should" is an exponent of this exponent. The concept is not the principle of intuition; rather the inner immediate life of reason — merely existing and never appearing — which appears as energy (the expression of a "through" immanent in itself) — is the principle of concept and intuition at once and in the same stroke, the absolute principle of everything. This, Fichte says, would be the idealistic argument.

Climbing higher to get to know the real spirit and root of this mode of argument: there may be the intuition of an absolute life, but how does it come to be? Construct this being, or grasp it in its becoming — this has happened, and the inner life of reason as a living "through" has been deduced as the genetic principle for this being. The basic character of the ideal perspective is that it originates from the presupposition of a being that is only hypothetical and therefore based wholly on itself. It naturally finds this same being to be absolute again in its genetic deduction, since it does not begin in order to negate itself but to produce itself genetically. The maxim of the "form of outward existence" is the principle and characteristic spirit of the idealistic perspective. By its means, reason (already known as a living "through") becomes the absolute — absolute in the genetic process because already existing as the constant presupposition. Absolute reason, as absolute, is therefore a "through" = the form of outward existence. Prior and absolute being shows itself as inwardly static, motionless, and dead at just this point of going "through"; the inward life of reason shows itself in this being's hypothetical quality.

But this idealistic perspective does not arise purely in the genetic process, since it assumes a being as given — therefore it is not the science of knowing's true standpoint. Furthermore, in the idealistic perspective, reason lives as absolute reason. But it lives only *as* absolute (in the image of this "as"); it does not live absolutely. Its life or absoluteness is itself mediated by a higher "through," making it derivative. Fichte stresses the importance of this sharp, penetrating critique of idealism, since beginners are easily tempted to remain one-sidedly trapped in this perspective, where their speculative power first develops.

Now turning things around to grasp from the other side. If the "through" is to come to existence, an absolute life grounded in itself is presupposed. This life is the true absolute and all being originates in it. Intuition itself is negated — not as empirically given (if they try to remain in an energy state and consider nothing further, they always find they still grasp it in intuition). This is idealism's stubbornness: not letting one go further. Idealism, as something absolute, does not allow itself to be explained away by reasoning but yields only to the arrival of what is primordially absolute. By recognizing the absolute immanent life, they negate intuition as something genetically explicable. If immanent life is self-enclosed and all reality encompassed in it, one cannot see how to achieve an objectifying and expressive intuition of it — one can even see that such an intuition could never arise. This last insight cannot itself be newly comprehended but simply carried out directly; it is the absolute, self-originating insight. However stubbornly one holds to immediate consciousness of this intuition, it does not help: what has been asserted and demonstrated is that this consciousness is not merely inconceivable but can be conceived as impossible. The truth of what it asserts is denied, but not its bare appearance.

The place for denying ourselves at the root is here — in the intuition of the absolute, which might well be our root and has played that role. Whoever perishes here will not expect restoration at some relative, finite, limited place. This annulment is achieved not through absence of thought but through the highest thinking — the thought of absolute immanent life and devotion to the maxims of reason, of genesis, of the absolute "through," which denies its applicability here and thus denies itself through itself. Everything has dissolved into the one = 0.

Reasoning conducted in these terms is realistic. There is no progression or multiplicity in it except pure oneness. The two highest disjunctive terms stand absolutely opposed: life's inner and outer life, the forms of immanent and emanent existence, separated by an impassable gulf and truly realized contradiction. If one wishes to think them united, they are united exactly by this gulf and contradiction.

Fichte now discovers the inner spirit and character of the realistic perspective. This perspective departs from the maxim: do not reflect on the factical self-givenness of thinking and insight (or on how it occurs in mind); reckon only the content of insight as valid. Do not attend to the external form of thought's existence in ourselves, but only the inner form of that thought. An absolute truth is posited that manifests as the content of thinking, and it alone can be true. Made genetic, it was just so.

Realism's presupposition of inner absolute truth is described more precisely: this implicit truth appears as an image (*Bild*), living, completely determined, and immutable, holding and bearing itself in immutability. This truth reveals itself in absolute life, and can only reveal itself in the latter, because life is just as truth is — self-grounded, held and sustained by itself. Truth is in and through itself only life's image, and only an image of life gives truth. By means of truth, only the image is added. They stand between life itself and the image of life; in terms of content (which alone matters in realism), these are fully identical, different only in form (which realism leaves aside).

It is noteworthy that the image — which holds and sustains itself — should exist only in truth as truth, when the image seems to be exactly the same as thought and truth the same as being. In realism, therefore, they are led to a perspective so similar to idealism that it could be the same.

Fichte concludes by observing that both idealism and realism grounded themselves on assumptions, and both assumptions — in their facticity — grounded themselves on an inner maxim of the thinking subject. Both rest on an empirical root. This is less remarkable for idealism (which asserts facticity) than for realism (which, in its effects and contents, denies and contradicts what it fundamentally is). Both are equally possible and, given their premises, equally consistent. Each contradicts the other: absolute idealism denies the possibility of realism; realism denies the possibility of being's conceivability and derivability. This conflict in maxims can be alleviated only by setting out a law of maxims, and they must search for such a law.

Fichte sketches in advance how a settlement might work. All expressions of the science of knowing so far show a predilection for the realistic perspective. This preference is justified because idealism renders even the being of its opposite impossible (decidedly one-sided), while realism at least leaves the being of its opposite undisputed — only making it inconceivable, and thereby revealing its inadequacy as the principle for a science in which everything must be conceived genetically. Perhaps a simple misunderstanding underlies realism's proof that an expressive intuition of absolute life can never arise. Perhaps what needs to be asserted is only that such an intuition, as valid for itself and self-supporting, can never arise — leaving room for an interpolation: this intuition might well arise, and must arise under certain conditions, simply as a phenomenon not grounded in itself. Insight into this interpolation could provide the science of knowing's standpoint and the true unification of idealism and realism — so that the very intuition previously called "our selves at root" would be the first appearance and ground of all other appearances. Because this would not be error but genuine truth, it and all its necessary modifications would be valid as appearances. Seeming and error enter where appearance is taken for being itself. These arise necessarily from truth's absence, and can themselves be derived as necessary from the assumption that this absence is necessary.
## Twelfth Lecture

Fichte opens by noting that from the recent discussion period, it was obvious that the audience has followed even the deepest investigations well, and that a comprehensive vision of the inner spirit and outer method of the science has grown in them. He will now carry the investigation forward in full strength and depth.

He offers a brief review in four parts. First: the production of an insight that may have many genetic aspects in its content but is factical at its root. If there really is to be a "through," then as a condition of its possibility, an inner life independent of the "through" and resting on itself must be presupposed. Second: they made this insight into an object to analyze its form. They proposed that their concept of an actual "through" was energetic and living, that the concept's inward life was the principle of an energetic insight into a life beyond — intuited in the insight as self-sufficient. It was the principle of intuition and of life in the intuition. The characterization of this life "in and for itself" is not intrinsically valid; it can be explained from the mere form of intuition as projecting self-sufficiency in the form of external existence. This is the idealistic perspective.

Third: the opposite view also proved possible. The presupposed life in itself should be entirely and unconditionally in-itself, and it is intuited as such. All being and life originates with it; apart from it, nothing can be. The subjective condition of this perspective is that one not stubbornly hold to idealism's principle (the energy of reflection) but yield patiently to the opposite insight. This is the realistic perspective. Fichte warns against confusing "idealism" and "realism" here with artificial philosophical systems — these are natural idealism and realism arising without conscious effort in common knowing, at least in its derived expressions and appearances. The science of knowing intends to derive them as wholly natural disjunctions and partialities of common knowing.

Fourth: both perspectives were closely specified in their inner nature and character. They elevated themselves above both (since they can move from one to the other), ascending from the facticity of both to their genesis out of their relative and mutual principles. The insight they lived and were was the genesis of idealism and realism, just as those were the genesis for previously created terms. According to the basic law of the science, they constantly rise to a higher genesis until finally losing themselves completely in it.

The characterization: idealism locates itself in the standpoint of reflection, makes it absolute, and its development is nothing more than the genesis of what it already was without genesis other than its own absolute origin. It is factical at root — not relative to something external (like Kant's highest principle) but relative to itself. It posits itself unconditionally, and everything else follows. Realism proceeds no differently: abstracting from its thinking, it presupposes the bare content of thought as solely valid and unconditionally true, consistently denying any other truth not contained in that content. But residing in the content is itself an absolutely given fact, making itself absolute without giving further account — just like idealism. Both are factical at root; each bears in itself the mark of its insufficiency as a highest principle for the science of knowing.

At the highest point of contradiction between the two terms absolutely demanding unification, they find: 0 and C, or form and content, or the forms of outer and inner existence, or essence and existence. The absolute disjunction appears to have been obtained; its unification promises to bring absolute oneness and resolve the task fundamentally.

Fichte introduces today's considerations as still preliminary — they must advance further just to get to the point. The problem cannot be resolved by combining or rearranging what they know so far. Everything up to now is only preparation. Something entirely new must be brought up: something that has remained empirical and concrete must be investigated and mastered genetically. The rule is to investigate the facticity. One of the factical principles of the two perspectives must be developed — which one?

If one grasps idealism's principle first, it is incontrovertible. Realism attacks immediately, calling it stubbornness and a false maxim. Idealism in turn makes realism's beginnings impossible. Realism at least maintains a negative relationship to idealism, whereas in idealism even the possibility of such relatedness is extirpated. They must therefore attend to realism, temporarily abstracting from idealism. Since realism cannot be absolutely valid (they want to correct it) and cannot be combated from idealism's perspective, they must fight it on its own grounds — catching it in self-contradiction. Through this contradiction (which brings a disjunction), realism's empirical principle would become genetic, and in this genesis perhaps become the principle of a higher realism and idealism united. Realism's crucial point was life's in-itself (*Ansich*) and within-itself (*Insich*). They hold to this alone and could meanwhile let go of life.

How does realism bring about the in-itself? Fichte invites the audience to think the in-itself energetically. He asserts: the in-itself has meaning only to the extent that it is *not* what has been constructed, and completely denies everything constructed, all construction, and all constructability. When one says "thus it is in itself, unconditionally in itself," one is saying it exists independently from all asserting, thinking, and intuiting, and from whatever else outside the in-itself may have a name. The in-itself is to be described purely as what negates thinking.

This is the first surprising observation: here for the first time realism is understood genetically (previously it was made evident only factically from its consequences). They now see that realism (or they themselves in its perspective) acts like the in-itself, which negates everything outside itself. Realism to some extent *is* the in-itself and collapses into it; for this reason, in the appearance of the insight, it annuls everything outside itself. Something about realism that was previously only factical has been comprehended genetically.

Reflecting more closely on their own insight: Fichte had called on them to think the in-itself exactly and energetically. Without this exact thinking, they would not have seen into it. Perhaps for their whole life they had thought the in-itself faintly, without this insight being produced. (If this insight had opened vividly for anyone, the discovery of the science of knowing would not have taken so long.) Their insight into the negation of thinking in itself presupposes positive thought: "In thought, thinking annuls itself in the face of the in-itself."

Further consequences: the negation of thinking over against the in-itself is not thought in free reflection but is immediately evident — this is intuition, and since the absolute in itself is found here, this is the absolute intuition. What the absolute intuition projects is therefore negation, absolute pure nothing — in opposition to the absolute in itself. Thus idealism, which posits an absolute intuition of life, is refuted at its root by a still deeper founding of realism. It may come up again as appearance, but taken as absolute, it is merely illusion. The fundamental negation of ourselves over against the absolute remains.

The negation is intuited; the in-itself is thought. How is it thought? They constructed it, assembling it from parts — just as at the beginning they constructed oneness as neither empirically manifest identity nor multiplicity but the union of both. But in truth they did not actually construct it; it constructed itself by means of itself. Intuition — the absolute springing forth of light and insight — was bound directly together with this construction. They did not produce this; it produced itself and drew them forward. The absolute's absolute self-construction and the original light are completely and entirely one and inseparable. Light arises from self-construction just as self-construction comes from absolute light. Nothing at all remains of a pregiven "us" — this is the higher realistic perspective.

But they still hold to the requirement that they should be able to think the in-itself energetically. The living self-construction of the in-itself within the light must have yielded to them, and this energy must be the first condition of everything — which results in an idealism lying even higher.

Two further considerations. First, they are aware of this thinking, this energy, and their claim that it exists is grounded solely on this awareness. But awareness presupposes the light. Since the light, at least in its objective form, does not exist in itself apart from the absolute but has its source in the in-itself, they cannot appeal to it — it bears witness against them. If one retains this higher presupposition of the light in all deliverances of self-consciousness (the source of all idealistic assertions), then idealism's constant spirit in its highest form and its fundamental error is that it remains fixed on facticity — on the objectifying light from which one can never begin factically but only intellectually.

Second, one must reflect in opposition to the idealistic objection: you are not thinking the in-itself, constructing it originally. Nor is it known through something that is not itself the in-itself; rather it is merely known, setting itself down in your knowing and as your knowing. You have been doing this your whole life in various forms whenever you expressed the judgment "so and so exists." Philosophy made war with you not because of this procedure but because of its thoughtlessness. Your energy adds something only now that you are aware of this action and its significance. Before listening to the idealist at all, they must inquire more exactly how far the testimony of intuition is valid.

Fichte concludes: whether the in-itself appears in its oneness as the concept of some philosophical system (killed or never having lived) or in a particular determination as the "is" of a particular thing, the faded in-itself is always intuition and therefore dead. For the science of knowing it exists *in the concept* and is therefore living. For the science of knowing there is nothing in intuition, since everything is in the concept. This is the most decisive distinguishing ground between the science of knowing and every other standpoint for knowing. The science of knowing grasps the in-itself conceptually; every other mode of thought does not conceive it but only intuits it and thereby kills it. The science of knowing grasps each mode of thought from its own perspective as negations of the in-itself — not absolute negations but privative ones. What is found to be not absolutely valid on the ascent, the science of knowing will take up again on its descent as possible negations of the absolute insight.
## Thirteenth Lecture

Fichte announces that he will continue climbing freely. "Freely" means he cannot provide the foundation for the distinctions that will emerge before using them; he must first acquaint the audience with them in use, although a firm rule of ascent stands at the basis. Instead of the initially given crucial points L (life) and C (concept), they now have two perspectives: realism (= genesis of life) and idealism (= genesis of the concept). These two perspectives are their present guide until they find their principle of oneness and can dispense with them directly. Here especially they need the capacity to hold firmly to what is presented and separate it from everything that may be rationally bound to it. Otherwise one will leap ahead, anticipate the inquiry, and fail to grasp the genetic process linking terms to their higher principles; the two will flow into one another factically. It is not actually "one" who leaps ahead — not the self — but speculative reason running along automatically. Fichte warns: once speculation is aroused, it is as active as empirical association of ideas, surrounded by a freer, lighter atmosphere. One must be as watchful against leaps of speculation as previously against empiricism's stubbornness. Those for whom the present investigation appears very easy should make it harder for themselves, since the appearance of ease may indicate that the subject is being grasped by speculative fantasy rather than by pure, ever-serene reason.

To work. The in-itself reveals itself immediately as entirely independent from knowledge or thought of itself, wholly denying thought in its own essential effects. They did not construct the in-itself in its true, clear concept; rather it constructed itself just as it was in the constructive process, as denying thinking. Immediate insight, the absolute light, was immediately united with this concept. The absolute in-itself revealed itself as the source of the light; hence the light is in no way primordial. This bears the stamp of a higher realism.

Another idealism tried to raise itself against this realism: since they saw into the in-itself as negating vision, they must have reflected energetically on it. Although they cannot deny that it constructed itself (and the light as well), all of this was qualified by their own vigorous reflection, which is therefore the highest term. Since it bases itself on absolute reflection, this is obviously idealism — and since it rests not on reflection about something conditioned (carrying out a "through") but on reflection on the unconditioned in-itself, it is a higher idealism.

This idealism was quickly struck down. Addressing it in personified form: "You think the in-itself — that is your principle. On what basis do you know this? You can only answer: 'I just see it, am immediately aware of it.'" In realism, they had insight into the in-itself's self-construction — a living in-sight that was swept along by the living thing itself. But in idealism, the seeing to which it appeals is different: they would not report witnessing thinking as thinking (as producing the in-itself) in the act of producing. Rather, intuition accommodates itself immediately only to thinking that is essentially opaque and factical. It remains completely ambiguous whether thinking originates from intuition, intuition from thinking, or both from a deeper hidden oneness.

Fichte makes the point more precise. Could one ever think the in-itself without thereby being aware of thinking it? No. But this awareness is in no way part of thinking itself; it is a higher vantage point from which thinking is observed. If the in-itself constructs itself in thinking, and the light is released in this process, then thinking and the light arise simultaneously and indistinguishably in the same act. The idealist's claim to have reflected on this — and thereby to be the source — fails because the very awareness that makes this claim is itself part of the light, which the in-itself has already released.

Fichte then develops a crucial argument. The in-itself, as absolutely in-itself, is absolutely one. Thinking of the in-itself is also absolutely one, since the in-itself was absolutely one and thinking of it was the complete negation of thinking in the face of the in-itself, which is what constituted the thinking. Thinking and the in-itself were the same; consciousness of it was only one consciousness; therefore it was one. The self, or I, that arises here is consequently the absolute I — pure, eternally self-identical, and unchanging — but not the absolute (as will soon become more precisely evident). A specific disjunctive principle will need to be identified if (a) the one in-itself should appear in a multifaceted perspective while remaining perpetually the same single in-itself or categorical "is," and (b) there arises a manifold view of thinking, reflection, and the reflecting subject (all of which are also to remain the same one in the background). If they remain trapped in the absolute I and never rise beyond it, they may never discover this disjunctive principle genetically, and it will have to be disclosed factically.

In a historical parenthetical: even at best, the science of knowing has been taken for the idealistic system just described — presupposing the absolute I to be the absolute and deriving everything from it. No author known to Fichte, friend or foe, has risen to a higher conception. If a higher understanding is to arise for anyone besides the originator, it could only be among the present listeners, but it would not be written; or it will be hit upon by them.

The intrinsic validity of this idealism is refuted; nevertheless, it may preserve existence as an appearance and indeed as the foundation of all appearance. It was refuted on the grounds that it is factical and that a higher development points to its origin. A fact of consciousness is factical; in this idealistic system, consciousness itself is a fact, and since consciousness is for it the absolute, the absolute would be a fact. From the first moment of its arising, the science of knowing declared that the primary error of all previous systems was that they began with something factical and posited the absolute in it. The science of knowing, by contrast, lays as its ground an enactment — called by the Greek term "genesis." Therefore it has always gone beyond the idealism described. It has shown this regarding its basic point, the I: it has never admitted that the I — as found and perceived — is its principle. "As found" it is never the pure I but the individual personal being of each person. The science of knowing recognizes the I as pure only as produced; as a science, it never places the I at the pinnacle of its deductions, because the productive process always stands higher than the product. The production of the I, and with it the whole of consciousness, is now the task.

The rejected idealism (which is the same as absolute immediate consciousness) leads to a forceful conclusion: the science of knowing denies the validity of immediate consciousness's testimony absolutely as such and proves this denial. Solely in this way does it bring reason to peace and oneness. Only pure reason, grasped merely by intellect, remains as solely valid. To the anticipatory worry — "how can I grasp something in intellect without being conscious?" — Fichte answers: of course you cannot. But the ground of truth does not rest in consciousness; it rests only in truth itself. Consciousness must always be separated from truth as making no difference to it. Consciousness remains only an outer appearance of truth, from which one can never escape and whose grounds are to be given. But believing the grounds of truth lie in consciousness is illusion.

Thus the science of knowing keeps its promise as a doctrine of truth and reason, expunging all facticity from itself. The primordial fact and source of everything factical is consciousness, and it can verify nothing — whenever truth is at issue, one should abstract from it. As a phenomenology (a doctrine of appearance and illusion, possible only on the ground of the first part), the science of knowing deduces both consciousness and appearance as existing, but simply as factical.

It is also completely clear why nothing external can be brought against the science of knowing; one must always begin with it to gain entrance. Any beginning point for fighting it is either grasped in intellect (in which case one is already within the science of knowing and in no case in conflict with it) or not grasped intellectually (in which case one must appeal to immediate consciousness for verification — but that appeal is turned aside with the instruction that precisely *because* you are immediately aware of it and appeal to that fact, it must be false).

Fichte concludes with a remark on skepticism: the possessor of the science of knowing, who surveys all disjunctions in consciousness (which, if one assumes the validity of consciousness in itself, become contradictions), could present a skepticism that totally negated everything assumed so far — one that would make those who have been playing with skeptical doubts "blanch and cry out: 'Now the joke goes too far!'"
