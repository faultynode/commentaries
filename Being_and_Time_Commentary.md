# Being and Time — Paragraph-by-Paragraph Commentary

## Epigraph and Prefatory Paragraphs

Heidegger opens *Being and Time* with an epigraph from Plato's *Sophist* (244a). In this passage, the Eleatic Stranger confesses to Theaetetus that, while others may have long been familiar with what they mean when they use the expression "being" (*on*), he and his interlocutors once thought they understood it but have now fallen into perplexity (*aporia*). The choice of this passage is programmatic. Heidegger signals from the outset that the entire treatise is motivated by a recognition that the meaning of "Being" — the most basic concept in all philosophy — has become obscured rather than clarified over the course of the philosophical tradition. By citing Plato's *Sophist* rather than, say, the *Republic* or *Parmenides*, Heidegger points to a moment in Plato's own thought where the question of Being was treated as genuinely aporetic, not as something settled by the theory of Forms.

The first paragraph following the epigraph poses two rhetorical questions in parallel. First: do we today have an answer to the question of what we mean by "being"? No. Therefore, the question of the meaning of Being must be raised anew. Second: are we today even in the *perplexity* of not understanding the expression "Being"? Again, no — we are not even troubled by the question. This double negation is crucial. It is not merely that we lack an answer; we lack even the sense that a question needs to be asked. The situation is worse than Plato's *aporia*, because the ancients at least recognized their confusion. Our condition is one of forgetfulness of the question itself. Heidegger then states the aim of the treatise: the concrete working-out of the question of the meaning of Being. He adds that the provisional goal is the interpretation of time as the possible horizon for any understanding of Being whatsoever. This is the thesis of the entire book compressed into a single sentence: temporality is the horizon within which Being is understood.

The second short paragraph states that the aim, the investigations it entails, and the path toward it all require an introductory clarification. This brief transitional remark sets up the Introduction that follows, which occupies §§1–8 and lays out the necessity, structure, and method of the question of Being before the substantive analysis begins.

## Preliminary Remark (*Vorbemerkung*)

The *Vorbemerkung* is a brief note Heidegger added to later editions. He explains that *Being and Time* first appeared in Spring 1927 in Husserl's *Jahrbuch für Philosophie und phänomenologische Forschung*, Volume VIII, and simultaneously as a separate print. The present reprint is unchanged in its text but has been revised with respect to citations and punctuation. The pagination largely matches earlier editions.

Heidegger then notes that the designation "First Half" (*Erste Hälfte*) that appeared in previous editions has been removed. The second half, he says, can no longer be appended after a quarter century without the first being presented anew. Nevertheless, the path of the first half remains necessary if the question of Being is to move our Dasein. He refers readers to his *Introduction to Metaphysics* (a 1935 lecture course published by the same press) for further elucidation. This remark is of great historical significance: it is Heidegger's acknowledgment that the projected Second Part of *Being and Time* — which was to contain the "destruction" of the history of ontology through critical engagements with Kant, Descartes, and Aristotle — was never completed and, by the time of this note, could not simply be added on. The incomplete status of the work became one of the central facts of its reception.

## Introduction: The Exposition of the Question of the Meaning of Being

### First Chapter: Necessity, Structure, and Priority of the Question of Being

### § 1. The Necessity of an Explicit Repetition of the Question of Being

The opening paragraph of §1 states that the question of Being has today fallen into oblivion, even though our age prides itself on reaffirming "metaphysics." People consider themselves spared the effort of rekindling the "battle of giants concerning being" (*gigantomachia peri tēs ousias* — another reference to Plato's *Sophist*, 246a). Yet this question is not arbitrary. It kept the research of Plato and Aristotle in suspense, only to fall silent thereafter as a thematic question of actual investigation. What they achieved has been preserved through various shifts and "overpaintings" down to Hegel's *Logic*. And what was once wrested from the phenomena through the highest exertion of thought, even if fragmentarily and in first attempts, has long since been trivialized. The point is that the question of Being was genuinely alive in Greek thought — it was the driving question — but has since been covered over, distorted, and rendered banal by the tradition.

The second paragraph deepens the diagnosis. On the basis of Greek approaches to the interpretation of Being, a dogma has formed that declares the question of the meaning of Being superfluous and even sanctions the neglect of the question. The dogma says: "Being" is the most universal and emptiest concept. As such, it resists every attempt at definition. This most universal and therefore indefinable concept needs no definition. Everyone uses it constantly and already understands what they mean by it. Thus what drove ancient philosophizing into unrest and sustained it in that unrest has become a sun-clear self-evidence, such that anyone who still asks after it is charged with a methodological error. Heidegger is describing the crystallization of a philosophical common sense that treats Being as too obvious to investigate — a trivialization that amounts to a foreclosure of genuine inquiry.

The third paragraph is transitional. Heidegger says these prejudices cannot be fully discussed at the outset of the investigation. They have their roots in ancient ontology itself. But ancient ontology can only be adequately interpreted — with respect to the ground from which its fundamental concepts grew, the appropriateness of its demonstration of the categories, and their completeness — by means of the prior clarification and answering of the question of Being. Heidegger therefore proposes to carry the discussion of prejudices only far enough to make the necessity of a repetition of the question of the meaning of Being evident. There are three such prejudices.

The fourth paragraph presents the first prejudice: "Being" is the most universal concept. Heidegger cites Aristotle (*Metaphysics* B 4, 1001a21) and Thomas Aquinas (*Summa Theologiae* I-II, q. 94, a. 2): an understanding of Being is already included in everything one apprehends of beings. But the "universality" of Being is not the universality of a genus. "Being" does not delimit the highest region of beings insofar as these are conceptually articulated according to genus and species — Aristotle himself says "being is not a genus" (*Metaphysics* B 3, 998b22). The universality of Being "transcends" (*übersteigt*) all generic universality. In the terminology of medieval ontology, "Being" is a *transcendens*. Aristotle already recognized the unity of this transcendental universal, over against the multiplicity of the highest generic concepts, as the unity of analogy. With this discovery, Aristotle placed the problem of Being on a fundamentally new footing relative to Plato's approach, though he did not thereby illuminate the obscurity of these categorial connections. Medieval ontology — both Thomist and Scotist — discussed the problem extensively without achieving fundamental clarity. And when Hegel determines "Being" as the "indeterminate immediate" and lays this determination at the foundation of all further categorial explications in his *Logic*, he remains within the same line of sight as ancient ontology, except that he relinquishes the problem of the unity of Being vis-à-vis the multiplicity of categories that Aristotle had already posed. Therefore: to say "Being" is the most universal concept cannot mean it is the clearest and in need of no further discussion. The concept of Being is rather the *darkest*.

The fifth paragraph presents the second prejudice: the concept "Being" is indefinable. This was inferred from its supreme universality — and rightly so, if *definitio fit per genus proximum et differentiam specificam*. Being cannot indeed be grasped as a being; nothing can be added to Being in the way entities are attributed to a genus. Being cannot be derived from higher concepts or presented through lower ones. But does it follow that Being can no longer pose a problem? Not at all. All that can be concluded is that Being is not something like a being. The mode of determination appropriate to beings — "definition" in the sense of traditional logic, which itself has its foundations in ancient ontology — is not applicable to Being. The indefinability of Being does not dispense with the question of its meaning but rather positively demands it.

The sixth paragraph presents the third prejudice: "Being" is the self-evident concept. In all cognition, assertion, and comportment toward beings, and in every relating-to-oneself, "Being" is used, and the expression is "without further ado" understandable. Everyone understands "the sky is blue," "I am happy," and the like. But this average intelligibility only demonstrates the *unintelligibility*. It reveals that in every comportment and being toward beings as beings, an enigma lies a priori. That we already live in an understanding of Being and that the meaning of Being is at the same time shrouded in darkness proves the fundamental necessity of repeating the question of the meaning of Being.

The seventh paragraph adds that the appeal to self-evidence in the domain of philosophical basic concepts — and especially with regard to the concept "Being" — is a dubious procedure, if indeed the "self-evident," and only it, is to become and remain the explicit theme of analytic, which Kant called "the business of philosophers" (the "secret judgments of common reason"). Heidegger is invoking Kant's distinction between the everyday use of concepts and their philosophical clarification. What seems most obvious is precisely what demands the most rigorous investigation.

The eighth and final paragraph of §1 draws the conclusion. The consideration of the prejudices has also made clear that not only the answer to the question of Being is lacking, but even the question itself is obscure and without direction. To repeat the question of Being therefore means first of all to adequately work out the formulation of the question. This sets up §2, which will analyze the formal structure of the question.

### § 2. The Formal Structure of the Question of Being

The first paragraph states that the question of the meaning of Being is to be posed. If it is a or even *the* fundamental question, then such questioning requires appropriate transparency. Therefore it must be briefly discussed what belongs to a question as such, in order to make the question of Being visible as a distinctive one.

The second paragraph provides a formal analysis of the structure of any question whatsoever. Every questioning is a seeking. Every seeking takes its prior direction from what is sought. Questioning is a cognitive seeking of beings in their that-being and so-being. This cognitive seeking can become "investigation" — the uncovering determination of what the question is about. Questioning, as a questioning-about, has its *Gefragtes* (what is asked about). All questioning-about is in some way a questioning-of something — the *Befragtes* (what is interrogated). In the investigative — i.e., specifically theoretical — question, what is asked about is to be determined and brought to a concept. In what is asked about there lies the *Erfragtes* (what is to be found out) — that at which the questioning arrives at its goal. Questioning itself, as a comportment of a being (the questioner), has its own character of Being. A question can be carried out as "just asking around" or as an explicit formulation of the question. What is distinctive about explicit formulation is that the questioning first becomes transparent to itself with respect to all the constitutive characters of the question just named. Heidegger is here establishing a formal apparatus — *Gefragtes*, *Befragtes*, *Erfragtes* — which he will immediately apply to the question of Being specifically.

The third paragraph turns this formal apparatus upon the question of Being. As a seeking, the questioning requires prior guidance from what is sought. The meaning of Being must therefore already be available to us in some way. As indicated: we always already move in an understanding of Being. From it arises the explicit question of the meaning of Being and the tendency toward its concept. We do not know what "Being" means. But already when we ask "what is Being?" we maintain ourselves in an understanding of the "is" without being able to fix conceptually what this "is" signifies. We do not even know the horizon from which we might grasp and fix the meaning. This average and vague understanding of Being is a *fact* (*Faktum*). The crucial point is that the question of Being is not asked from a position of total ignorance. We always already have a pre-conceptual understanding of Being — it is operative in every "is" we utter. The question aims at making explicit what is already implicitly understood.

The fourth paragraph addresses the status of this vague pre-understanding. However much this understanding of Being may fluctuate and blur and approach the boundary of mere acquaintance with a word, this indeterminacy of the always-already-available understanding of Being is itself a *positive phenomenon* that requires clarification. But an investigation into the meaning of Being will not wish to provide this clarification at the outset. The interpretation of the average understanding of Being can gain its necessary guiding thread only from the fully developed concept of Being. From the light of the concept and the modes of explicit understanding that belong to it, it will be possible to determine what the obscured or not-yet-illuminated understanding of Being means, and what kinds of obscuration or impediment to explicit clarification are possible and necessary. Heidegger is describing a hermeneutic circle in miniature: we need the concept to clarify the pre-understanding, but the concept must grow from the pre-understanding.

The fifth paragraph adds that the average, vague understanding of Being can furthermore be permeated by inherited theories and opinions about Being, in such a way that these theories remain hidden as the sources of the prevailing understanding. What is sought in the question of Being is not something completely unknown, even though initially quite ungraspable. Traditional ontological doctrines invisibly shape how we understand Being prior to any explicit questioning — this is why a "destruction" of the ontological tradition will be needed (as §6 will explain).

The sixth paragraph identifies the *Gefragtes* (what is asked about): Being — that which determines beings as beings, that with respect to which beings, however they may be discussed, are always already understood. The Being of beings "is" not itself a being. The first philosophical step in understanding the problem of Being consists in not "telling a story" (citing Plato, *Sophist* 242c) — that is, not determining beings as beings by tracing them back to another being as their origin, as though Being had the character of a possible being. Being as what is asked about therefore requires its own mode of exhibition, essentially different from the discovery of beings. Accordingly, what is to be found out — the meaning of Being — will require its own conceptuality, which differs essentially from the concepts in which beings achieve their meaning-determinations. This paragraph formulates the *ontological difference* — the fundamental distinction between Being and beings — which is one of the most important conceptual innovations of *Being and Time*.

The seventh paragraph identifies the *Befragtes* (what is interrogated). Insofar as Being constitutes what is asked about, and Being means Being of beings, it follows that the interrogated entity in the question of Being is beings themselves. Beings are, as it were, questioned with regard to their Being. But if they are to yield the characters of their Being without distortion, then they must first have become accessible as they are in themselves. The question of Being demands, with regard to what is to be interrogated, the attainment and prior securing of the right mode of access to beings. But we call many things "being," and in various senses. Beings include everything we talk about, everything we intend, everything we comport ourselves toward, as well as what and how we ourselves are. Being lies in that-being and so-being, in reality, presence-at-hand, subsistence, validity, Dasein, and in the "there is." From which being shall the meaning of Being be read off? From which being shall the disclosure of Being take its departure? Is the starting point arbitrary, or does a particular being have a priority in the working-out of the question of Being? Which is this exemplary being, and in what sense does it have a priority? These questions set up the crucial move toward Dasein as the privileged point of entry for ontological inquiry.

The eighth paragraph draws the consequences. If the question of Being is to be explicitly posed and carried out in full transparency, then the working-out requires: explication of the mode of looking at Being, of understanding and conceptually grasping the meaning, preparation of the possibility of the right choice of exemplary being, and working out the genuine mode of access to this being. Looking-at, understanding-and-grasping, choosing, and accessing are constitutive comportments of questioning and are thus themselves modes of Being of a particular being — the being that we, the questioners, in each case ourselves are. Working out the question of Being means: making transparent a being — the questioning being — in its Being. The questioning of this question, as a mode of Being of a being, is itself essentially determined by that about which it asks — by Being. This being which we ourselves in each case are, and which among other things has the possibility of Being called questioning, we grasp terminologically as *Dasein*. Here Heidegger introduces the central term of the entire treatise. *Dasein* — literally "being-there" or "there-being" — is the technical name for the being that we ourselves are, the being for which Being is an issue. The explicit and transparent formulation of the question of the meaning of Being demands a prior, adequate explication of a being (Dasein) with respect to its Being.

The ninth paragraph raises the objection of circularity. Does such an undertaking not fall into an obvious circle? Having to determine a being in its Being first, and only then wanting to pose the question of Being — what is that other than going in a circle? Is not what the answer is supposed to provide already "presupposed" for the working-out of the question? Heidegger responds that formal objections like the argument about "circularity in proof" are always sterile when applied to concrete paths of investigation. They contribute nothing to the understanding of the matter and only hinder advance into the field of inquiry.

The tenth paragraph resolves the circularity objection. Factually, there is no circle in the question as formulated. Beings can be determined in their Being without the explicit concept of the meaning of Being already needing to be available. If this were not the case, there could never have been any ontological knowledge at all — and its factual existence can hardly be denied. "Being" is indeed "presupposed" in all previous ontology, but not as an available concept — not as what it is as something sought. The "presupposing" of Being has the character of a prior looking-toward Being, such that from this looking, the given being is provisionally articulated in its Being. This guiding looking-toward Being grows out of the average understanding of Being in which we always already move, and which ultimately belongs to the essential constitution of Dasein itself. Such "presupposing" has nothing to do with positing a principle from which a sequence of propositions is deductively derived. A "circle in proof" cannot lie in the question of the meaning of Being, because in answering the question it is not a matter of a deductive grounding but rather of an exhibiting ground-laying (*aufweisende Grund-Freilegung*).

The eleventh and final paragraph of §2 refines the point. There is no "circle in proof" in the question of the meaning of Being, but there is rather a remarkable "back-or-forward-relatedness" of what is asked about (Being) to the questioning as a mode of Being of a being. The essential involvement of the questioning in what it questions belongs to the innermost meaning of the question of Being. But this only means that the being of the character of Dasein has a relation — perhaps even a distinctive one — to the question of Being itself. But has a determinate being thereby already been shown in its priority of Being, and the exemplary being that is to function as the primarily interrogated being in the question of Being been given in advance? With what has been discussed so far, neither the priority of Dasein nor its possible or even necessary function as the primarily interrogated being has been decided. But something like a priority of Dasein has *announced itself*. Heidegger is careful here: Dasein's privilege is not yet proven, only suggested. The proof requires the next two sections.

### § 3. The Ontological Priority of the Question of Being

The first paragraph states that the characterization of the question of Being along the guiding thread of the formal structure of the question has made it clear that this question is a distinctive one, requiring a series of fundamental considerations. But the distinction of the question of Being will come fully to light only when it is adequately delimited with respect to its function, its aim, and its motives.

The second paragraph reviews. So far the necessity of a repetition of the question was motivated by the dignity of its origin, but above all by the lack of a determinate answer, indeed by the lack of a sufficient formulation of the question. But one may wish to know what this question is supposed to *serve*. Does it remain merely the business of a free-floating speculation about the most general generalities — or is it the most principled and most concrete question at once?

The third paragraph begins the argument for ontological priority by examining the sciences. Being is always the Being of a being. The totality of beings can, according to its various domains, become the field for uncovering and delimiting determinate subject-areas. These areas — history, nature, space, life, Dasein, language, and the like — can be thematized as objects in corresponding scientific investigations. Scientific research carries out the elevation and initial fixing of subject-areas naively and roughly. The working-out of an area in its fundamental structures has in a certain way already been accomplished by pre-scientific experience and interpretation of the domain of Being in which the subject-area is delimited. The resulting "basic concepts" initially serve as guiding threads for the first concrete disclosure of the area. Though the weight of research always lies in this positivity, its genuine progress occurs not so much in the accumulation of results stored in "handbooks" as in the questioning of the fundamental constitutions of the domain — a questioning that is mostly reactively driven by increasing knowledge of the subject matter.

The fourth paragraph states that the genuine "movement" of the sciences occurs in the more or less radical and self-transparent revision of their basic concepts. The level of a science is determined by the extent to which it is capable of a crisis of its basic concepts. In such immanent crises of the sciences, the relationship between positive inquiry and the interrogated subject-matter itself begins to waver. Everywhere today, tendencies have awakened to place research on new foundations.

The fifth paragraph gives examples from particular sciences. Mathematics, the apparently most rigorous and firmly structured science, has fallen into a "foundational crisis." The dispute between formalism and intuitionism concerns the securing of primary access to what is to be the object of this science. The theory of relativity in physics grows from the tendency to exhibit the inherent coherence of nature as it "in itself" subsists. As a theory of the conditions of access to nature itself, it seeks to preserve the invariance of the laws of motion through determination of all relativities, thereby confronting the question of the structure of the subject-area given to it — the problem of matter. In biology, the tendency awakens to question back behind the determinations of organism and life provided by mechanism and vitalism, to determine anew the mode of Being of living beings as such. In the historical humanities, the drive toward historical reality itself has intensified through and beyond tradition and its presentation. Literary history is to become problem-history. Theology seeks a more primordial interpretation of the Being of humanity toward God — one that is prescribed by and remains within the meaning of faith itself. It slowly begins to understand again Luther's insight that its dogmatic systematics rests on a "foundation" that did not grow from a primarily faithful questioning, and whose conceptuality not only does not suffice for the theological problematic but conceals and distorts it. Heidegger is arguing that all the sciences are at bottom confronted with the question of the Being of their respective domains.

The sixth paragraph draws a general conclusion about basic concepts. Basic concepts are the determinations through which the subject-area underlying all thematic objects of a science comes to prior understanding that guides all positive investigation. These concepts receive their genuine legitimation and "grounding" only through a correspondingly prior investigation of the subject-area itself. But since every such area is won from the domain of beings themselves, such prior, basic-concept-generating research amounts to nothing other than the interpretation of beings with respect to the fundamental constitution of their Being. Such research must run ahead of the positive sciences — and it can. The work of Plato and Aristotle is proof. Such grounding of the sciences differs fundamentally from the "limping-along" logic that investigates an accidental state of a science with respect to its "method." It is *productive logic* in the sense that it leaps ahead, as it were, into a determinate domain of Being, first discloses that domain in its constitution of Being, and makes the resulting structures available to the positive sciences as transparent directives for inquiry. For example, what is philosophically primary is not a theory of concept-formation in history, nor a theory of historical knowledge, nor a theory of history as an object of historical science, but the interpretation of what is authentically historical in its historicality. So too the positive yield of Kant's *Critique of Pure Reason* lies in its approach to working out what belongs to a nature in general — not in a "theory" of knowledge. His transcendental logic is an a priori material logic of the domain of Being called nature.

The seventh paragraph adds a further level. But such questioning — ontology in the broadest sense — itself still needs a guiding thread. Ontological questioning is indeed more primordial than the ontic questioning of the positive sciences. But it remains itself naive and opaque if its investigations into the Being of beings leave the meaning of Being as such undiscussed. And precisely the ontological task of a non-deductively-constructive genealogy of the various possible ways of Being requires a prior understanding of "what we really mean by this expression 'Being'."

The eighth paragraph states the ontological priority in full. The question of Being therefore aims at an a priori condition of the possibility not only of the sciences that investigate beings and in doing so always already move in an understanding of Being, but at the condition of the possibility of the ontologies themselves that precede and ground the ontic sciences. All ontology, however rich and firmly riveted a categorial system it may have at its disposal, remains at bottom blind and a perversion of its ownmost intention if it has not first sufficiently clarified the meaning of Being and grasped this clarification as its fundamental task.

The ninth and final paragraph concludes that rightly understood ontological research itself gives the question of Being its ontological priority — beyond the mere resumption of a venerable tradition and the advancement of a hitherto opaque problem. But this substantive-scientific priority is not the only one.

### § 4. The Ontic Priority of the Question of Being

The first paragraph begins by noting that science can be defined as the whole of a grounding-connection of true propositions. This definition is neither complete nor does it capture science in its meaning. Sciences, as comportments of human beings, have the mode of Being of this being (the human). This being we terminologically grasp as Dasein. Scientific research is neither the only nor the nearest possible mode of Being of this being. Dasein itself is moreover distinguished before all other beings. This distinction must be made provisionally visible.

The second paragraph states the central thesis: Dasein is a being that does not merely occur among other beings. It is rather ontically distinguished by the fact that in its Being, its Being is an issue for it (*es diesem Seienden in seinem Sein um dieses Sein selbst geht*). To this constitution of Being of Dasein belongs the fact that in its Being it has a relation of Being toward its Being. And this in turn means: Dasein understands itself in some way and degree of explicitness in its Being. It is proper to this being that with and through its Being, this Being is disclosed to it. Understanding of Being is itself a determination of Being of Dasein. The ontic distinction of Dasein lies in the fact that it is ontological. Being-ontological does not yet mean: developing ontology. If we reserve the title "ontology" for the explicit theoretical questioning into the meaning of beings, then the meant being-ontological of Dasein is to be designated as *pre-ontological*. But this does not simply mean being ontic, but rather being in the manner of an understanding of Being. This is one of the most concentrated and important passages in the entire work. Dasein's distinctive ontic feature is that it has an understanding of Being — it is, so to speak, constitutively ontological. This pre-ontological understanding is the ground upon which any explicit ontological inquiry must build.

The third paragraph introduces the term *existence* (*Existenz*). Being itself, toward which Dasein can comport itself in this or that way and always somehow does comport itself, we call existence. And because the essential determination of this being cannot be accomplished by specifying a content-full *what* — its essence lies rather in the fact that it has in each case its Being to be as its own — the title Dasein has been chosen as a pure expression of Being for the designation of this being. Heidegger here separates Dasein from the traditional metaphysics of essence: Dasein has no fixed "whatness" (*essentia*). Its essence is rather its existence — its having to be.

The fourth paragraph distinguishes existentiell from existential understanding. Dasein always understands itself from its existence, from a possibility of itself to be itself or not itself. Dasein has either itself chosen these possibilities, or it has gotten into them, or it has always already grown up in them. Existence is decided in the manner of seizing or neglecting only by the respective Dasein itself. The question of existence is only to be settled through existing itself. The understanding of itself that leads in this process we call the *existentiell* understanding. The question of existence is an ontic "affair" of Dasein. It does not require the theoretical transparency of the ontological structure of existence. The question that aims at the laying-out of what constitutes existence we call the *existential* question. The coherence of these structures we call *existentiality*. Its analytic has the character not of an existentiell but of an existential understanding. The task of an existential analytic of Dasein is pre-indicated in the ontic constitution of Dasein in terms of its possibility and necessity. This distinction between existentiell (the concrete, lived-level) and existential (the ontological-structural level) is fundamental to the entire methodology of *Being and Time*.

The fifth paragraph observes that since existence determines Dasein, the ontological analytic of this being always already requires a prior looking toward existentiality. But we understand existentiality as the constitution of Being of the being that exists. In the idea of such a constitution of Being, however, the idea of Being as such already lies. And so the possibility of carrying through the analytic of Dasein also depends on the prior working-out of the question of the meaning of Being in general. Again the hermeneutic circle appears: the analytic of Dasein requires a concept of Being, which is what the analytic is meant to produce.

The sixth paragraph broadens the scope. Sciences are modes of Being of Dasein in which it comports itself also toward beings that it does not itself need to be. But to Dasein belongs essentially: Being-in-a-world. The understanding of Being that belongs to Dasein therefore equally primordially concerns the understanding of something like "world" and the understanding of the Being of beings that become accessible within the world. The ontologies that take beings of non-Dasein-like character of Being as their theme are accordingly founded and motivated in the ontic structure of Dasein itself, which includes the determinateness of a pre-ontological understanding of Being. This is Heidegger's argument that all regional ontologies are ultimately rooted in Dasein's pre-ontological understanding.

The seventh paragraph draws the central conclusion: therefore fundamental ontology, from which alone all other ontologies can spring, must be sought in the existential analytic of Dasein.

The eighth paragraph summarizes Dasein's threefold priority. First, an *ontic* priority: this being is determined in its Being by existence. Second, an *ontological* priority: on the ground of its existential determinateness, Dasein is in itself "ontological." Third, equally primordially belonging to Dasein as constitutive of its understanding of existence: an understanding of the Being of all non-Dasein-like beings. Dasein has therefore the third priority as the *ontic-ontological condition of the possibility of all ontologies*. Dasein has thus shown itself as the being that must be interrogated ontologically before all other beings.

The ninth paragraph adds a crucial qualification. The existential analytic is itself ultimately existentially — that is, *ontically* — rooted. Only when philosophical-research-oriented questioning is itself existentielly seized as a possibility of Being of the existing Dasein in each case does the possibility exist of disclosing the existentiality of existence and thereby the possibility of tackling a sufficiently grounded ontological problematic at all. The ontic priority of the question of Being has thereby also become clear. Philosophy is not a disembodied theoretical exercise; it must be existentielly — concretely, in one's own existence — taken up.

The tenth paragraph notes that the ontic-ontological priority of Dasein was seen early on, without Dasein itself being grasped in its genuine ontological structure or even becoming a problem aiming in that direction. Aristotle says that the soul is, in a certain way, beings (*hē psychē ta onta pōs estin*; *De Anima* III.8, 431b21). The soul that constitutes human Being discovers, in its modes of being (*aisthēsis* and *noēsis*), all beings with respect to their that-being and so-being — that is, always also in their Being. Thomas Aquinas took up this proposition in a characteristic discussion. Within the task of deriving the "transcendentals" — the characters of Being that lie beyond every possible content-full generic determination of a being and necessarily belong to every something whatsoever — the *verum* is to be shown as such a transcendental. This is accomplished by appeal to a being that, in keeping with its mode of Being, is suited to "come together" with every being of any kind whatsoever. This distinguished being, the *ens quod natum est convenire cum omni ente*, is the soul (*anima*). The priority of "Dasein" that emerges here, though ontologically unclarified, has nothing in common with a bad subjectivization of the totality of beings. Heidegger is distancing himself from idealism even as he claims Dasein's special status.

The eleventh paragraph recapitulates the state of the argument. The demonstration of the ontic-ontological distinction of the question of Being is grounded in the provisional indication of the ontic-ontological priority of Dasein. But the analysis of the structure of the question of Being as such (§2) also uncovered a distinctive function of this being within the question itself. Dasein revealed itself as the being that must be previously worked out ontologically if questioning is to become transparent. Now it has also been shown that the ontological analytic of Dasein constitutes fundamental ontology, and that Dasein functions as the being that must be fundamentally interrogated in advance with respect to its Being.

The twelfth and final paragraph concludes: when the interpretation of the meaning of Being becomes the task, Dasein is not only the primarily interrogated being; it is moreover the being that in each case already relates in its Being to what is asked about in this question. The question of Being is then nothing other than the *radicalization* of a tendency of Being that belongs essentially to Dasein itself — the pre-ontological understanding of Being. This is a powerful conclusion: the question of Being is not an arbitrary theoretical project but the making-explicit of something that already belongs to Dasein's own mode of Being.

### Second Chapter: The Double Task in the Working-Out of the Question of Being. The Method of the Investigation and its Outline

### § 5. The Ontological Analytic of Dasein as the Laying-Bare of the Horizon for an Interpretation of the Meaning of Being in General

The first paragraph restates the situation. In characterizing the tasks that lie in the "posing" of the question of Being, it was shown that not only a fixing of the being that is to function as the primarily interrogated being is needed, but also an explicit appropriation and securing of the right mode of access to this being. Which being takes on the privileged role within the question of Being has been discussed. But how is this being — Dasein — to become accessible and, as it were, aimed at in understanding interpretation?

The second paragraph makes a crucial cautionary point. The demonstrated ontic-ontological priority of Dasein could mislead one into the opinion that this being must also be the ontically-ontologically primary *given* — not only in the sense of "immediate" graspability of the being itself, but also regarding an equally "immediate" givenness of its mode of Being. Dasein is indeed ontically not only near but the nearest — we are in each case ourselves it. Nevertheless, or precisely for this reason, it is ontologically the *farthest*. Although it belongs to its ownmost Being to have an understanding of that Being and to maintain itself in a certain interpretedness of its Being, this by no means implies that this nearest pre-ontological self-interpretation can serve as an adequate guiding thread, as if this understanding of Being had to spring from a thematically ontological reflection on one's ownmost constitution of Being. Dasein rather has, in accordance with a mode of Being belonging to it, the tendency to understand its own Being *from the being toward which it essentially, constantly, and proximally comports itself* — from the "world." In Dasein itself and thus in its own understanding of Being lies what Heidegger will later demonstrate as the *ontological reflection of the understanding of the world upon the interpretation of Dasein*. This point is methodologically fundamental: Dasein's self-understanding is initially and for the most part distorted by its absorption in the world. We do not first know ourselves transparently and then encounter the world; rather, we understand ourselves *from* the world.

The third paragraph states the consequence: Dasein's ontic-ontological priority is therefore the ground for the fact that its specific constitution of Being — understood in the sense of the "categorial" structure belonging to it — remains *concealed* from it. Dasein is ontically "nearest" to itself, ontologically farthest, yet pre-ontologically not alien. The paradox is now fully visible: what we are closest to in experience is what we understand least at the structural-ontological level.

The fourth paragraph notes provisionally that an interpretation of this being faces peculiar difficulties that are grounded in the mode of Being of the thematic object and of the thematizing comportment itself — not in some deficient equipment of our cognitive faculty or in the seemingly easily remedied lack of an adequate conceptuality. The difficulties of understanding Dasein are intrinsic, not accidental.

The fifth paragraph observes that because Dasein possesses not only an understanding of Being but this understanding develops or decays with Dasein's own mode of Being, Dasein can have access to a rich interpretedness. Philosophical psychology, anthropology, ethics, "politics," poetry, biography, and historiography have all pursued the comportments, capacities, powers, possibilities, and fates of Dasein in various ways and to varying degrees. But the question remains whether these interpretations were carried through with the same existential primordiality as they perhaps were existentielly primordial. The two do not necessarily coincide, though they do not exclude one another. Existentiell interpretation can demand existential analytic, insofar as philosophical cognition is grasped in its possibility and necessity. Only when the fundamental structures of Dasein are sufficiently worked out in explicit orientation toward the problem of Being itself will the previous gains of Dasein-interpretation receive their existential justification. Heidegger is claiming that all previous human self-understanding — in literature, psychology, ethics, etc. — lacks the ontological grounding that only an existential analytic can provide.

The sixth paragraph insists that an analytic of Dasein must therefore remain the first concern in the question of Being. But then the problem of securing the guiding mode of access to Dasein becomes especially urgent. Negatively stated: no arbitrary idea of Being and actuality, however "self-evident," may be brought constructively-dogmatically to bear on this being; no "categories" prescribed by such an idea may be forced upon Dasein without ontological examination. The mode of access and interpretation must rather be chosen so that this being can show itself *in itself from itself*. And it is to show the being as it is *proximally and for the most part* — in its average everydayness. In this everydayness, not arbitrary and accidental but *essential* structures are to be exhibited — structures that maintain themselves as determinative of Being in every mode of Being of factical Dasein. With a view to the fundamental constitution of everydayness, the preparatory exhibition of the Being of this being then grows. This is the methodological decision for which *Being and Time* is known: the starting point of the analysis is not extraordinary states of existence but rather the average, everyday way Dasein goes about its life.

The seventh paragraph clarifies the limits of the analytic as so conceived. It remains entirely oriented toward the guiding task of working out the question of Being. It cannot aim to give a complete ontology of Dasein — which would indeed have to be built out if something like a "philosophical" anthropology were to stand on a philosophically adequate basis. With a view to a possible anthropology or its ontological foundation, the following interpretation gives only some, though not insignificant, "pieces." The analysis of Dasein is moreover not only incomplete but also initially *provisional*. It first brings out the Being of this being without interpretation of its *meaning*. It is rather meant to prepare the laying-bare of the horizon for the most primordial interpretation of Being. Once this horizon is won, the preparatory analytic of Dasein demands its *repetition* on a higher and properly ontological basis. The structure of *Being and Time* is thus circular in a positive sense: Division One provides a provisional analysis that Division Two repeats at a deeper, temporally grounded level.

The eighth paragraph announces the central thesis: the meaning of the Being of the being we call Dasein will be exhibited as *temporality* (*Zeitlichkeit*). This demonstration must prove itself in the repeated interpretation of the provisionally exhibited structures of Dasein as modes of temporality. But with this interpretation of Dasein as temporality, the answer to the guiding question — the meaning of Being in general — is not yet given. What is provided is the ground for gaining that answer.

The ninth paragraph develops the connection between Dasein's Being and time as the horizon of all understanding of Being. It was indicated that to Dasein belongs, as ontic constitution, a pre-ontological understanding of Being. What must now be shown is that *time* is that from which Dasein implicitly understands and interprets something like Being. Time must be brought to light and genuinely grasped as the horizon of all understanding of Being and every interpretation of Being. To make this evident requires a primordial explication of time as the horizon of the understanding of Being, drawn from temporality as the Being of Dasein that understands Being. This task also includes the demand to delimit the concept of time so gained against the *vulgar* understanding of time — the understanding that has become explicit in a time-interpretation that has held sway from Aristotle through Bergson. It must be shown that and how this concept of time and the vulgar understanding of time spring from temporality. This will restore to the vulgar concept of time its own proper right — against Bergson's thesis that the time it means is space.

The tenth paragraph observes that "time" has long served as an ontological (or rather ontic) criterion for the naive distinction of the various regions of beings. "Temporal" beings (natural events and historical happenings) are distinguished from "non-temporal" beings (spatial and numerical relations). "Timeless" meaning of propositions is distinguished from the "temporal" course of propositional assertions. A "chasm" between the "temporal" and the "supra-temporal" eternal is found, and attempts at bridging it are made. "Temporal" here means in each case "being in time" — a determination that is itself still obscure. The fact stands: time, in the sense of "being in time," functions as a criterion for the separation of regions of Being. How time comes to this distinguished ontological function, and with what right something like time functions as such a criterion, and above all whether in this naively ontological use of time its properly possible ontological relevance comes to expression — none of this has hitherto been asked or investigated. Time has, within the horizon of the vulgar understanding of time, "of itself" fallen into this "self-evident" ontological function and has maintained itself in it to this day.

The eleventh paragraph states the counterposition. Over against this, it must be shown, on the basis of the worked-out question of the meaning of Being, that and how the central problematic of all ontology is rooted in the rightly seen and rightly explicated phenomenon of time.

The twelfth paragraph draws out the consequence. If Being is to be grasped from time, and the various modes and derivatives of Being become intelligible from a looking-toward time, then Being itself — not merely beings as beings "in time" — has been made visible in its "temporal" character. But "temporal" can then no longer mean merely "being in time." Even the "non-temporal" and "supra-temporal" are, with respect to their Being, "temporal" — not merely in the privative sense against something "temporal" as "being in time," but in a positive, though yet to be clarified, sense. Because the expression "temporal" is already claimed by pre-philosophical and philosophical usage in the noted sense, and because it will be used for another meaning in the following investigations, Heidegger calls the primordial meaning-determinateness of Being and its characters and modes from time their *Temporalität* (translated as "Temporality" to distinguish it from *Zeitlichkeit*, "temporality" in the narrower sense that belongs to Dasein). The fundamental ontological task of interpreting Being as such therefore includes the working-out of the Temporality of Being. In the exposition of the problematic of Temporality, the concrete answer to the question of the meaning of Being is first given. The distinction between *Zeitlichkeit* (Dasein's temporality) and *Temporalität* (the Temporality of Being itself) is crucial; only the former is thematized in the published portion of *Being and Time*.

The thirteenth paragraph adds that because Being is graspable only from the perspective of time, the answer to the question of Being cannot lie in an isolated and blind proposition. The answer is not grasped in the mere repeating of what it propositionally states, especially if it is passed along as a free-floating result for mere notice-taking of a "standpoint" perhaps divergent from previous treatment. Whether the answer is "new" is of no consequence. Its positive quality must lie in being old enough to learn to grasp the possibilities prepared by the "ancients." The answer gives, in accordance with its ownmost meaning, a directive for concrete ontological research to begin inquiry within the freed horizon — and it gives only that.

The fourteenth and final paragraph states that if the answer to the question of Being becomes a guiding directive for research, then it is adequately given only when from it the specific mode of Being of previous ontology, the fates of its questioning, finding, and failing come to be seen as necessarily belonging to Dasein.

### § 6. The Task of a Destruction of the History of Ontology

The first paragraph states that all research — especially that moving within the purview of the central question of Being — is an ontic possibility of Dasein. Dasein's Being finds its meaning in temporality. But temporality is at the same time the condition of the possibility of *historicality* (*Geschichtlichkeit*) as a temporal mode of Being of Dasein itself, regardless of whether and how Dasein is a being "in time." The determination "historicality" lies prior to what is called history (world-historical happening).

The second paragraph explains historicality. Historicality means the constitution of Being of the "happening" of Dasein as such, on the ground of which alone something like "world-history" is possible and something can historically belong to world-history. Dasein is, in each case in its factical Being, how and "what" it already was. Whether explicitly or not, it *is* its past. And not merely in the way that its past pushes itself along "behind" it, possessing what is past as a still-present property that occasionally has after-effects. Dasein "*is*" its past in the manner of its Being, which, roughly put, "happens" in each case out of its future. Dasein, in its respective way of Being, has grown up in and into an inherited interpretation of Dasein, and initially and within a certain scope constantly understands itself from this interpretation. This understanding discloses the possibilities of its Being and regulates them. Its own past — and this always means the past of its "generation" — does not follow after Dasein but rather always already goes ahead of it. This is a compressed statement of Heidegger's conception of historicality: the past is not simply behind us but constitutively shapes our possibilities in advance.

The third paragraph discusses the varying degrees to which Dasein recognizes its own historicality. This elementary historicality can remain hidden from Dasein itself. But it can also be discovered and cultivated. Dasein can discover tradition, preserve it, and explicitly pursue it. The discovery of tradition and the disclosure of what it "hands down" and how it does so can be taken up as an independent task. Dasein thereby brings itself into the mode of Being of historical questioning and research. But historiology (*Historie*) — more precisely, historicality (*Historizität*) — is possible only as a mode of Being of questioning Dasein because Dasein is at bottom determined by historicality. If historicality remains hidden from Dasein, the possibility of historical questioning and discovery is also denied to it. The absence of historiology is not proof against the historicality of Dasein but, as a deficient mode of this constitution of Being, proof for it. An age can be un-historical only because it is "historical."

The fourth paragraph turns to the consequence for the question of Being. If Dasein has seized the possibility within it not only of making its existence transparent but of inquiring after the meaning of existentiality itself — that is, the meaning of Being in general — and if in such inquiry the view has been opened to the essential historicality of Dasein, then the insight is inescapable: the questioning about Being, whose ontic-ontological necessity has been indicated, is itself characterized by historicality. The working-out of the question of Being must thus take its directive from the ownmost meaning of the questioning itself as historical: it must become *historical* in order, through the positive appropriation of the past, to bring itself into full possession of its ownmost possibilities of questioning. The question of the meaning of Being is, in accordance with its own mode of actualization, brought by itself to understand itself as historical.

The fifth paragraph anticipates a finding of the subsequent analysis. The preparatory interpretation of the fundamental structures of Dasein — with respect to its nearest and average mode of Being, in which it is thus also initially historical — will make the following evident: Dasein has not only the inclination to *fall prey* to its world and to interpret itself reflectively from it; Dasein also at the same time falls prey to its more or less explicitly seized tradition. Tradition relieves Dasein of its own guidance, questioning, and choosing. This holds not least for the understanding that is rooted in Dasein's ownmost Being — the ontological understanding.

The sixth paragraph describes how tradition conceals. The tradition that comes to dominance initially and for the most part makes what it "hands down" so little accessible that it rather *covers it up*. It delivers what has come down to self-evidence and blocks access to the original "sources" from which the traditional categories and concepts were in part genuinely drawn. Tradition even makes such an origin altogether forgotten. It develops the absence of need even to understand such a return as necessary. Tradition uproots the historicality of Dasein to the extent that Dasein moves only in the interest in the manifold variety of possible types, directions, and standpoints of philosophizing in the most remote and foreign cultures and seeks through this interest to veil its own groundlessness. The consequence is that Dasein, for all its historical interest and philologically "objective" interpretation, no longer understands the most elementary conditions that alone make possible a positive return to the past in the sense of a productive appropriation of it.

The seventh paragraph recalls that it was shown at the outset (§1) that the question of the meaning of Being has not only gone unsettled, not only been inadequately posed, but has, for all interest in "metaphysics," *fallen into oblivion*. Greek ontology and its history, which through manifold filiations and distortions still determines the conceptuality of philosophy today, is proof that Dasein understands itself and Being in general from the "world," and that the ontology thereby arising falls prey to tradition, which lets it sink to self-evidence and to material merely to be worked over anew (as for Hegel). This uprooted Greek ontology becomes in the Middle Ages a fixed body of doctrine. Its systematics is anything but a mere piecing-together of inherited fragments into a building. Within the limits of a dogmatic adoption of the Greek basic conceptions of Being, there lies in this systematics much unlifted work pointing further. In its scholastic form, Greek ontology passes essentially through Suárez's *Disputationes Metaphysicae* into the "metaphysics" and transcendental philosophy of modernity and still determines the foundations and aims of Hegel's *Logic*. Insofar as in the course of this history certain distinguished domains of Being come into view and henceforth primarily guide the problematic (Descartes' *ego cogito*, subject, I, reason, spirit, person), these remain — in keeping with the pervasive neglect of the question of Being — unquestioned with respect to their Being and the structure of their Being. Rather, the categorial stock of traditional ontology, with corresponding formalizations and merely negative restrictions, is transferred to this being; or else, with the aim of an ontological interpretation of the substantiality of the subject, dialectic is called to aid.

The eighth paragraph announces the project of destruction. If the question of Being itself is to attain transparency regarding its own history, then a loosening of the hardened tradition and a dissolving of the concealments produced by it are required. This task we understand as the *destruction* (*Destruktion*), carried out along the guiding thread of the question of Being, of the inherited stock of ancient ontology, down to the original experiences in which the first and subsequently guiding determinations of Being were won. This proof of the provenance of the ontological basic concepts — as an investigating exhibition of their "birth certificate" — has nothing to do with a bad relativizing of ontological standpoints. The destruction equally has no negative sense of a shaking off of the ontological tradition. It is rather to stake out the tradition in its *positive possibilities* — and that always means within its limits, which are factually given with the respective questioning and the delimitation of the possible field of investigation prescribed by it. The destruction does not relate itself negatively to the past; its critique strikes at "today" and the prevailing treatment of the history of ontology, whether doxographic, intellectual-historical, or problem-historical. But the destruction does not wish to bury the past in nullity; it has a *positive* intention; its negative function remains unexpressed and indirect. This is one of the most important methodological declarations in the entire work. *Destruktion* is not destruction in the ordinary English sense but rather a critical *de-structuring* that frees the original experiences from the sedimented layers of traditional interpretation.

The ninth paragraph notes that within the framework of the present treatise, whose aim is a fundamental working-out of the question of Being, the destruction can be carried through only at fundamentally decisive stations of the history of ontology.

The tenth paragraph identifies the first station in the history of ontology to be subjected to destruction. In keeping with the positive tendency of the destruction, the question must first be asked whether and to what extent in the course of the history of ontology the interpretation of Being has been thematically brought together with the phenomenon of time, and whether the problematic of Temporality required for this has been fundamentally worked out. The first and only one who moved a stretch of the investigative way toward the dimension of Temporality — or rather, was driven in that direction by the pressure of the phenomena themselves — is *Kant*. If the problematic of Temporality is once fixed, then it becomes possible to shed light on the obscurity of the doctrine of schematism. On this path it can also be shown why this domain had to remain closed to Kant in its proper dimensions and central ontological function. Kant himself knew he was venturing into an obscure domain. Heidegger quotes Kant's famous remark about the schematism as "a hidden art in the depths of the human soul." What Kant recoiled from must be thematically brought to light if the expression "Being" is to have a demonstrable meaning.

The eleventh paragraph indicates that the following treatise attempts to interpret the schematism chapter and from there the Kantian doctrine of time. At the same time, it will be shown why the insight into the problematic of Temporality had to remain denied to Kant. Two things prevented this insight: first, the neglect of the question of Being as such, and in connection with it the lack of a thematic ontology of Dasein — in Kantian terms, a prior ontological analytic of the subjectivity of the subject. Instead, Kant dogmatically takes over Descartes' position despite all essential developments. Second, Kant's analysis of time remains oriented toward the inherited vulgar understanding of time despite the taking-back of this phenomenon into the subject — which ultimately prevents him from working out the phenomenon of a "transcendental determination of time" in its own structure and function. Because of this double after-effect of tradition, the decisive connection between time and the "I think" remains shrouded in complete darkness; it does not even become a problem.

The twelfth paragraph identifies the second station: Descartes. Through adopting Descartes' ontological position, Kant also perpetuates an essential omission: that of an ontology of Dasein. This omission is, in the sense of Descartes' ownmost tendency, a decisive one. With the "cogito sum," Descartes claims to provide philosophy with a new and secure foundation. But what he leaves undetermined in this "radical" beginning is the mode of Being of the *res cogitans* — more precisely, the meaning of Being of the "sum." The working-out of the unexpressed ontological foundations of the "cogito sum" constitutes the second station on the path of the destructive regress into the history of ontology. The interpretation provides proof that Descartes not only had to neglect the question of Being as such but also shows why he came to believe that the absolute "certainty" of the cogito relieved him of the question of the meaning of Being of this being.

The thirteenth paragraph continues on Descartes. For Descartes, it is not only a matter of this omission and thereby of a complete ontological indeterminacy of the *res cogitans sive mens sive animus*. Descartes carries through the fundamental reflections of his *Meditations* by transferring medieval ontology onto this being posited as *fundamentum inconcussum*. The *res cogitans* is ontologically determined as *ens*, and the meaning of Being of *ens* is, for medieval ontology, fixed in the understanding of *ens* as *ens creatum*. God as *ens infinitum* is the *ens increatum*. But createdness, in the broadest sense of produced-ness, is an essential structural moment of the ancient concept of Being. The apparent new beginning of philosophizing reveals itself as the planting of a fateful prejudice, on the ground of which the subsequent age neglected a thematic ontological analytic of the "mind" guided by the question of Being and at the same time carried out as a critical engagement with inherited ancient ontology.

The fourteenth paragraph adds that everyone acquainted with the Middle Ages can see that Descartes "depends" on medieval scholasticism and employs its terminology. But philosophically, nothing is gained by this "discovery" so long as it remains obscure what fundamental import this incursion of medieval ontology into the ontological determination (or non-determination) of the *res cogitans* has for the subsequent period. This import can be estimated only after the meaning and limits of ancient ontology have been exhibited from orientation toward the question of Being. In other words, the destruction faces the task of interpreting the ground of ancient ontology in the light of the problematic of Temporality. Here it becomes evident that the ancient interpretation of the Being of beings is oriented toward the "world" or "nature" in the broadest sense, and that it in fact gains its understanding of Being from "time." The external document for this — though only the external one — is the determination of the meaning of Being as *parousia* or *ousia*, which ontologically-temporally means "presence." Beings are grasped in their Being as "presence" — that is, they are understood with reference to a determinate mode of time: the "present." This is a fundamental claim: Greek ontology, and through it the entire Western metaphysical tradition, has understood Being in terms of *presence* — one particular temporal mode — without recognizing that it has done so.

The fifteenth paragraph states that the problematic of Greek ontology, like that of every ontology, must take its guiding thread from Dasein itself. Dasein — that is, the Being of the human — is, in the vulgar as well as the philosophical "definition," delimited as *zōon logon echon*: the living being whose Being is essentially determined by the ability to speak. The *legein* is the guiding thread for gaining the structures of Being of beings encountered in addressing and discussing. Therefore the ancient ontology forming itself with Plato becomes "dialectic." With the progressive working-out of the ontological guiding thread itself — i.e., the "hermeneutic" of *logos* — the possibility of a more radical grasp of the problem of Being grows. "Dialectic," which was a genuine philosophical embarrassment, becomes superfluous. For this reason Aristotle "no longer had any understanding" for it, because he placed it on a more radical ground and sublated (*aufhob*) it. The *legein* itself, or *noein* — the simple apprehending of something present-at-hand in its pure presence-at-hand, which Parmenides already took as the guiding thread for the interpretation of Being — has the temporal structure of the pure "making-present" (*Gegenwärtigen*) of something. The being that shows itself in and for this apprehending, and that is understood as the being proper, accordingly receives its interpretation with reference to *Gegen-wart* (present) — that is, it is grasped as *presence* (*ousia*).

The sixteenth paragraph notes that this Greek interpretation of Being takes place without any explicit knowledge of the guiding thread functioning therein, without acquaintance with or understanding of the fundamental ontological function of time, and without insight into the ground of the possibility of this function. On the contrary, time itself is taken as one being among others, and the attempt is made to grasp it in its structure of Being from the horizon of the understanding of Being that is implicitly and naively oriented toward time.

The seventeenth paragraph acknowledges a limit. Within the framework of the following fundamental working-out of the question of Being, the detailed temporal interpretation of the foundations of ancient ontology — above all its scientifically highest and purest stage in Aristotle — cannot be communicated. Instead, the treatise gives an interpretation of Aristotle's treatise on time (*Physics* IV.10–14), which can be chosen as the criterion for the basis and limits of the ancient science of Being.

The eighteenth paragraph states that the Aristotelian treatise on time is the first transmitted, detailed interpretation of this phenomenon. It has essentially determined all subsequent conceptions of time — Bergson's included. From the analysis of the Aristotelian concept of time it also becomes retroactively clear that Kant's conception of time moves within the structures worked out by Aristotle, which means that Kant's fundamental ontological orientation — despite all differences of a new mode of questioning — remains Greek.

The nineteenth paragraph concludes: only in carrying through the destruction of the ontological tradition does the question of Being gain its genuine concreteness. In it, it procures the full proof of the inescapability of the question of the meaning of Being and thus demonstrates the meaning of talk about a "repetition" of this question.

The twentieth and final paragraph adds a note of humility: every investigation in this field, where "the matter itself is deeply shrouded" (quoting Kant, *Critique of Pure Reason* B 121), will guard against overestimating its results. For such questioning constantly forces itself before the possibility of disclosing a still more primordial and more universal horizon from which the answer to the question "what does 'Being' mean?" might be drawn. Such possibilities can be seriously and productively discussed only when the question of Being has been awakened anew and a field of controllable engagement has been won.

### § 7. The Phenomenological Method of the Investigation

The first paragraph states that with the provisional characterization of the thematic object of the investigation (Being of beings, or the meaning of Being in general), its method also seems to be prescribed. The distinction of Being from beings and the explication of Being itself is the task of ontology. And the method of ontology remains highly questionable so long as one wishes to seek counsel from historically transmitted ontologies or the like. Since the term "ontology" is used in this investigation in a formally broad sense, the path of clarifying its method by tracing its history is ruled out.

The second paragraph adds that with the use of the term "ontology," no determinate philosophical discipline is being advocated that would stand in connection with others. It is not a matter of fulfilling the task of a pregiven discipline; rather, conversely, from the material necessities of determinate questions and from the treatment demanded by the "things themselves," a discipline may form itself.

The third paragraph identifies the method. With the guiding question of the meaning of Being, the investigation stands at the fundamental question of philosophy as such. The treatment of this question is phenomenological. Thereby, this treatise commits itself neither to a "standpoint" nor to a "direction," because phenomenology is neither and can never be either, so long as it understands itself. The expression "phenomenology" designates primarily a concept of method. It does not characterize the material *what* of the objects of philosophical research, but rather the *how* of that research. The more genuinely a concept of method takes effect and the more comprehensively it determines the fundamental thrust of a science, the more primordially it is rooted in engagement with the things themselves, and the farther it removes itself from what we call a technical device. The title "phenomenology" expresses a maxim that can be formulated as: "to the things themselves!" — against all free-floating constructions, accidental findings, against the adoption of merely apparently demonstrated concepts, and against the sham questions that often spread themselves through generations as "problems." This maxim might seem self-evident, but it concerns a "self-evidence" that needs to be brought closer.

The fourth paragraph notes that the expression has two components: *phainomenon* and *logos*; both go back to Greek terms. Taken externally, "phenomenology" is formed correspondingly to "theology," "biology," "sociology" — science of God, of life, of society. Phenomenology would then be the science of phenomena. The preliminary concept of phenomenology is to be exhibited through the characterization of what is meant by the two components, "phenomenon" and "*logos*," and through fixing the sense of the name composed of them.

#### A. The Concept of Phenomenon

The first paragraph traces the etymology. The Greek *phainomenon* derives from the verb *phainesthai*: to show itself. *Phainomenon* therefore means: that which shows itself, the self-showing, the manifest. *Phainesthai* itself is a middle-voice formation of *phainō* (to bring to daylight, to place in brightness), which belongs to the stem *pha-*, as does *phōs* (light, brightness) — that in which something can become manifest, visible in itself. The meaning of "phenomenon" is therefore to be retained as: the self-showing-itself-in-itself, the manifest. The *phainomena*, "phenomena," are then the totality of what lies in the light of day or can be brought to light, which the Greeks sometimes simply identified with *ta onta* (beings). But beings can show themselves from themselves in various ways, depending on the mode of access to them. The possibility even exists that a being shows itself as what it in itself is *not*. In this self-showing, the being "looks like..." Such self-showing we call *seeming* (*Scheinen*). And so in Greek, the expression *phainomenon* also has the meaning of "the apparent," "semblance": *phainomenon agathon* means a good that looks like one but "in reality" is not what it gives itself out to be. For further understanding of the concept of phenomenon, everything depends on seeing how what is named in the two meanings of *phainomenon* — "phenomenon" as the self-showing and "phenomenon" as semblance — is structurally connected. Only insofar as something in its very sense claims to show itself, i.e., to be a phenomenon, can it show itself as something it is not — can it "only look like..." In the meaning of *phainomenon* as semblance, the primordial meaning (phenomenon: the manifest) is already co-included as that which grounds the second. Heidegger assigns the title "phenomenon" terminologically to the positive and primordial meaning and distinguishes phenomenon from semblance as the privative modification of phenomenon. What both terms express, however, has initially nothing at all to do with what is called "appearance" (*Erscheinung*) or "mere appearance."

The second paragraph introduces "appearance" through the example of "symptoms of a disease." What is meant are occurrences in the body that show themselves and, in showing themselves, "indicate" something that does not show itself. The occurrence of such events, their self-showing, goes together with the presence of disturbances that themselves do not show. Appearance as appearance "of something" therefore precisely does not mean: showing-itself, but rather the announcing-of-itself — the self-announcing of something that does not show itself, through something that does show. Appearing is a not-showing-itself. But this "not" must not be confused with the privative not that determines the structure of semblance. What does not show itself in the way that the appearing entity does can also never seem (*scheinen*). All indications, presentations, symptoms, and symbols have this formal basic structure of appearing, though they differ among themselves.

The third paragraph clarifies the relation between appearance and phenomenon. Although appearing never is a showing-itself in the sense of phenomenon, appearing is possible only on the ground of a self-showing of something. But this self-showing that co-enables appearing is not the appearing itself. Appearing is the self-announcing through something that shows itself. When one says that with the word "appearance" we point to something in which something appears without itself being an appearance, then thereby the concept of phenomenon is not delimited but rather *presupposed* — a presupposition that remains concealed because in this determination of "appearance" the expression "to appear" is used ambiguously. That *in which* something "appears" means: that in which something announces itself — i.e., does not show itself. And in the phrase "without itself being an 'appearance,'" appearance means self-showing. But this self-showing essentially belongs to the "wherein" in which something announces itself. Phenomena are therefore never appearances, but every appearance is dependent upon phenomena. If one defines phenomenon with the help of a concept of "appearance" that is moreover itself unclear, then everything is turned on its head.

The fourth paragraph notes a further ambiguity. "Appearance" can itself mean two things: appearing in the sense of self-announcing as not-showing-itself, and the announcing entity itself — that which in its self-showing indicates something not-showing-itself. And finally, one can use "appearing" as the title for the genuine sense of phenomenon as self-showing. If one designates these three different states of affairs as "appearance," confusion is inevitable.

The fifth paragraph introduces yet another meaning. This confusion is essentially heightened by the fact that "appearance" can take on still another meaning. If one takes the announcing entity, which in its self-showing indicates the non-manifest, as that which occurs at or emanates from the non-manifest — in such a way that the non-manifest is thought as what is essentially never manifest — then "appearance" means as much as production or what is produced, which however does not constitute the authentic Being of the producer: appearance in the sense of "mere appearance." The produced announcing entity does show itself, but in such a way that as emanation of what it announces, it constantly *conceals* that thing in itself. But this concealing non-showing is in turn not semblance. Kant uses the term "appearance" in this coupling. For him, appearances are the "objects of empirical intuition" — what shows itself in empirical intuition. This self-showing (phenomenon in the genuine, primordial sense) is at the same time "appearance" as announcing emanation of something that conceals itself in the appearance.

The sixth paragraph draws the consequences. Since for "appearance" in the sense of self-announcing through a self-showing, a phenomenon is constitutive, and since this phenomenon can privatively modify itself into semblance, appearance too can become mere semblance. Phenomenon — the self-showing-itself-in-itself — means a distinguished mode of encounter. Appearance, by contrast, means an existing referential relation within beings, such that the referring (announcing) entity can fulfill its possible function only if it shows itself in itself, i.e., is a "phenomenon." Appearance and semblance are themselves in different ways founded in phenomenon. The confusing multiplicity of "phenomena" — phenomenon, semblance, appearance, mere appearance — can be disentangled only if the concept of phenomenon is understood from the beginning: the self-showing-itself-in-itself.

The seventh and final paragraph of subsection A introduces the formal, vulgar, and phenomenological concepts of phenomenon. If, in this conception, it is left undetermined which being is addressed as phenomenon, and if it remains entirely open whether what shows itself is ever a being or a character of Being of a being, then only the *formal* concept of phenomenon has been won. If what is understood by the self-showing is beings that are accessible, for example in the Kantian sense, through empirical intuition, then the formal concept of phenomenon comes to a legitimate application — the *vulgar* concept of phenomenon. But this vulgar concept is not the phenomenological concept. In the horizon of the Kantian problematic, what is phenomenologically grasped as phenomenon can be illustrated by saying: what in appearances — the vulgarly understood phenomenon — always already shows itself prior to and concomitantly, though unthematically, can be brought thematically to self-showing, and this self-so-showing (the "forms of intuition") are the phenomena of phenomenology. For evidently space and time must be able to show themselves, must be able to become phenomena, if Kant lays claim to a materially grounded transcendental statement when he says space is the a priori wherein of an ordering. The phenomenological concept of phenomenon, however, requires insight into the formal concept and its legitimate vulgar application as an indispensable presupposition.

#### B. The Concept of Logos

The first paragraph notes that the concept of *logos* in Plato and Aristotle is ambiguous, in a way that makes the meanings diverge without being positively guided by a basic meaning. This is in fact only a semblance that persists as long as interpretation fails to adequately grasp the basic meaning in its primary content. If we say the basic meaning of *logos* is "discourse" (*Rede*), then this literal translation becomes fully valid only through determining what discourse itself means. The later history of the word's meanings, and above all the manifold and arbitrary interpretations of subsequent philosophy, constantly conceal the authentic meaning of discourse, which lies openly enough in plain view. *Logos* is "translated" — i.e., always interpreted — as reason, judgment, concept, definition, ground, relation. But how can "discourse" modify itself so that *logos* means all of these things?

The second paragraph explains: *logos* as discourse means *dēloun* — making manifest that about which discourse speaks. Aristotle explicates this function more sharply as *apophainesthai*. *Logos* lets something be seen (*phainesthai*) — namely, that about which the discourse speaks, for the speaker (medium) or for those speaking with one another. Discourse "lets see" *apo* — from that very thing about which the discourse speaks. In discourse (*apophansis*), what is spoken should, if the discourse is genuine, be drawn from that about which the discourse speaks, so that the communicating discourse in what it says makes manifest what it speaks about and thereby makes it accessible to the other. This is the structure of *logos* as *apophansis*. Not every "discourse" possesses this mode of making-manifest in the sense of exhibiting letting-see. A prayer (*euchē*), for example, also makes manifest, but in a different way.

The third paragraph observes that in its concrete execution, discourse (letting-see) has the character of speaking — vocal utterance in words. *Logos* is *phōnē* — and indeed *phōnē meta phantasias*: vocal utterance in which something is always sighted.

The fourth paragraph explains *synthesis*. Only because the function of *logos* as *apophansis* lies in the exhibiting letting-see of something can *logos* have the structural form of *synthesis*. Synthesis does not here mean connecting and linking of representations, a handling of psychical occurrences regarding which the "problem" of how such inner connections agree with the physical outside is to arise. The *syn* has here a purely apophantic meaning: letting something be seen in its togetherness with something, letting something be seen as something.

The fifth paragraph addresses truth and falsehood. Because *logos* is a letting-see, it can therefore be true or false. Everything depends on freeing oneself from a constructed concept of truth in the sense of "correspondence." This idea is by no means primary in the concept of *alētheia*. The "being-true" of *logos* as *alētheuein* means: taking beings, about which the discourse speaks, out of their concealment in *legein* as *apophainesthai*, and letting them be seen as unconcealed (*alēthes*) — *discovering* them. "Being-false," *pseudesthai*, means correspondingly: deceiving in the sense of covering-up: putting something before something else (in the mode of letting-see) and thereby passing it off as something it is not.

The sixth paragraph draws a crucial consequence for the "locus" of truth. Because "truth" has this sense and *logos* is a determinate mode of letting-see, *logos* must precisely *not* be addressed as the primary "place" of truth. If one determines truth, as has today become thoroughly customary, as what "properly" belongs to judgment and appeals in this to Aristotle, then this appeal is without justification and the Greek concept of truth is misunderstood. "True" in the Greek sense, and indeed more primordially than the named *logos*, is *aisthēsis* — the simple, sensory apprehending of something. Insofar as an *aisthēsis* always aims at its *idia* — the being genuinely accessible only through it and for it (e.g., seeing aims at colors) — apprehension is always true. That means: seeing always discovers colors, hearing always discovers tones. In the purest and most primordial sense "true" — i.e., only discovering, such that it can never cover up — is pure *noein*, the simple beholding apprehension of the simplest determinations of Being of beings as such. This *noein* can never cover up, can never be false; it can at most remain an un-apprehending (*agnoein*), not sufficing for the simple, adequate access. This is a radical reinterpretation of the traditional concept of truth: truth is not primarily propositional correspondence but rather the more basic event of *uncovering* or *disclosure*.

The seventh paragraph states the consequence. What no longer has the form of pure letting-see, but in each case recurs to another in pointing out and thus lets something be seen as something — what takes on this synthesis-structure thereby takes on the possibility of covering-up. "Judgment-truth" is only the counter-case to this covering-up — i.e., a multiply founded phenomenon of truth. Realism and idealism both miss the sense of the Greek concept of truth with equal thoroughness.

The eighth and final paragraph of subsection B notes that because the function of *logos* lies in the simple letting-see, in letting beings be apprehended, *logos* can mean reason (*Vernunft*). And because *logos* is used not only in the meaning of *legein* but also of *legomenon* — what is pointed out as such — and because this is nothing other than the *hypokeimenon*, what for every addressing and discussing always already lies at the ground as present-at-hand, *logos* qua *legomenon* means ground (*Grund*), *ratio*. And finally, because *logos* qua *legomenon* can also mean what is addressed as something in its relation to something — what has become visible in its "relatedness" — *logos* acquires the meaning of relation (*Beziehung*) and ratio (*Verhältnis*). This interpretation of "apophantic discourse" may suffice for the clarification of the primary function of *logos*.

#### C. The Preliminary Concept of Phenomenology

The first paragraph states that with a concrete realization of what has been brought out in the interpretation of "phenomenon" and "*logos*," an inner connection between what is meant by these titles leaps to the eye. The expression "phenomenology" can be formulated in Greek: *legein ta phainomena*; and *legein* means *apophainesthai*. Phenomenology then says: *apophainesthai ta phainomena* — to let that which shows itself be seen from itself, just as it shows itself from itself. That is the formal meaning of the research that gives itself the name phenomenology. But this expresses nothing other than the maxim: "to the things themselves!"

The second paragraph notes that the title "phenomenology" is accordingly, with respect to its sense, different from designations like theology, etc. Those name the objects of the respective science in their material content. "Phenomenology" names neither the object of its research nor characterizes its material content. The word gives information only about the *how* of the mode of exhibition and treatment of what is to be dealt with in this science. Science "of" phenomena means: such a grasping of its objects that everything that stands to discussion about them must be treated in direct exhibition and direct legitimation. The same sense is expressed in the fundamentally tautological expression "descriptive phenomenology." Description here means not a procedure modeled on botanical morphology; the title again has a prohibitive sense: keeping away all non-legitimating determining.

The third paragraph poses the central question: with reference to what must the formal concept of phenomenon be de-formalized into the phenomenological concept, and how does this differ from the vulgar? What is it that phenomenology is to "let be seen"? What must be called "phenomenon" in a distinctive sense? What is by its essence necessarily the theme of an explicit exhibition? Manifestly, that which *proximally and for the most part precisely does not show itself*, which is concealed in contrast to what proximally and for the most part does show itself, but which at the same time essentially belongs to what proximally and for the most part shows itself, in such a way that it constitutes its meaning and ground. This is a paradoxical but decisive formulation: the genuine phenomenon of phenomenology is precisely what does *not* show itself — it must be wrested from concealment.

The fourth paragraph identifies what this concealed something is. What remains concealed in an eminent sense, or falls back into concealment, or shows itself only "in disguise," is not this or that being but, as the preceding considerations have shown, the *Being of beings*. It can be so extensively concealed that it is forgotten and the question of it and its meaning lapses entirely. What therefore demands, from its ownmost content, to become a phenomenon in a distinctive sense has been taken up thematically by phenomenology. Phenomenology is the mode of access to, and the legitimating mode of determination of, what is to become the theme of ontology. *Ontology is possible only as phenomenology.* The phenomenological concept of phenomenon means, as the self-showing, the Being of beings, its meaning, its modifications and derivatives. And the self-showing is not anything arbitrary, much less something like appearing. The Being of beings can least of all be something "behind which" something else stands, "something that does not appear."

The fifth paragraph addresses concealment. "Behind" the phenomena of phenomenology there is essentially nothing else; but what is to become a phenomenon can be concealed. And precisely because the phenomena are proximally and for the most part *not given*, phenomenology is needed. Concealedness is the counter-concept to "phenomenon." The kinds of possible concealedness are various. First, a phenomenon can be concealed in the sense that it is entirely undiscovered yet. Second, a phenomenon can be *buried over* — previously discovered but then falling back into concealment, either totally or (more commonly) still visible but only as semblance. This concealment as "disguise" is the most frequent and the most dangerous, because the possibilities of deception and misdirection are here especially obstinate. Available structures of Being and their concepts may perhaps claim their right within a "system," presenting themselves as clear and in no need of further justification on the basis of their constructive interlocking — and thereby serving as a point of departure for a progressive deduction.

The sixth paragraph adds that concealment has a twofold possibility: accidental and necessary. Every primordially drawn phenomenological concept and proposition, as a communicated assertion, stands in the possibility of degeneration. It is passed along in an empty understanding, loses its rootedness, and becomes a free-floating thesis. The possibility of hardening and loss of grip lies in the concrete work of phenomenology itself. The difficulty of this research consists precisely in making it critical of itself in a positive sense.

The seventh paragraph states that the mode of encounter of Being and the structures of Being in the mode of phenomenon must first be *wrested* from the objects of phenomenology. Therefore the point of departure of the analysis, as well as the access to the phenomenon and the passage through the prevailing concealments, require their own methodological securing. In the idea of "originary" and "intuitive" grasping and explication of phenomena lies the opposite of the naivety of a random, "immediate," and unreflective "gazing."

The eighth paragraph fixes the meaning of "phenomenal" and "phenomenological." What is given and explicable in the mode of encounter of a phenomenon is called "phenomenal"; hence the talk of phenomenal structures. "Phenomenological" is called everything that belongs to the mode of exhibition and explication and that constitutes the conceptuality required in this research.

The ninth paragraph explains why the vulgar concept of phenomenon also becomes phenomenologically relevant. Because phenomenon in the phenomenological understanding is always only what constitutes Being, and Being is always Being of beings, a right bringing-forth of the being itself is first needed for the aim of laying bare Being. This being must likewise show itself in the mode of access genuinely belonging to it. The prior task of a "phenomenological" securing of the exemplary being as the point of departure for the genuine analytic is always already prescribed from the goal of this analytic.

The tenth paragraph identifies phenomenology with ontology in content. Materially taken, phenomenology is the science of the Being of beings — ontology. In the given elucidation of the tasks of ontology, the necessity of a fundamental ontology arose — one that takes as its theme the ontologically-ontically distinguished being, Dasein, in such a way that it brings itself before the cardinal problem: the question of the meaning of Being in general. From the investigation itself it will emerge that the methodological sense of phenomenological description is *interpretation* (*Auslegung*). The *logos* of the phenomenology of Dasein has the character of *hermēneuein*, through which the authentic meaning of Being and the fundamental structures of Dasein's own Being are announced to the understanding of Being belonging to Dasein itself. Phenomenology of Dasein is *hermeneutics* in the primordial signification of the word, which designates the business of interpretation. Furthermore, insofar as by the uncovering of the meaning of Being and the fundamental structures of Dasein, the horizon is exhibited for every further ontological investigation of non-Dasein-like beings, this hermeneutics becomes at the same time "hermeneutics" in the sense of working out the conditions of the possibility of every ontological investigation. And finally, insofar as Dasein has the ontological priority over all beings — as a being in the possibility of existence — hermeneutics as interpretation of the Being of Dasein receives a specific third and, philosophically understood, primary sense: an analytic of the existentiality of existence. In this hermeneutics, insofar as it ontologically works out the historicality of Dasein as the ontic condition of the possibility of historiology, there is rooted what can only in a derivative way be called "hermeneutics": the methodology of the historical human sciences. Heidegger here develops a fourfold concept of hermeneutics: (1) the interpretation of the Being of Dasein, (2) the working-out of conditions for any ontological inquiry, (3) the analytic of the existentiality of existence, and (4) as the methodological ground for the human sciences.

The eleventh paragraph situates Being as *transcendens*. Being as the fundamental theme of philosophy is not a genus of a being, and yet it concerns every being. Its "universality" is to be sought higher. Being and the structure of Being lie beyond every being and every possible being-determination of a being. Being is the *transcendens* pure and simple. The transcendence of the Being of Dasein is a distinctive one, insofar as in it lies the possibility and necessity of the most radical individuation. Every disclosure of Being as the *transcendens* is transcendental cognition. Phenomenological truth (disclosedness of Being) is *veritas transcendentalis*.

The twelfth paragraph identifies philosophy itself with phenomenological ontology. Ontology and phenomenology are not two different disciplines alongside other philosophical disciplines. The two titles characterize philosophy itself with respect to its object and its mode of treatment. Philosophy is universal phenomenological ontology, proceeding from the hermeneutics of Dasein, which as the analytic of existence has fastened the end of the guiding thread of all philosophical questioning at the point from which it arises and to which it returns.

The thirteenth paragraph acknowledges the ground laid by Husserl's *Logical Investigations*, with which phenomenology achieved its breakthrough. Heidegger adds that the essential of phenomenology does not lie in being actual as a philosophical "direction." Higher than actuality stands *possibility*. The understanding of phenomenology lies solely in grasping it as a possibility.

The fourteenth and final paragraph of §7 offers a remark about expression. It is one thing to report about beings narratively, another to grasp beings in their Being. For the latter task, not only words but above all "grammar" are usually lacking. If a reference to earlier and incomparably high-level analyses of Being is permitted, one may compare ontological sections of Plato's *Parmenides* or the fourth chapter of the seventh book of Aristotle's *Metaphysics* with a narrative section of Thucydides, and one will see the enormity of the formulations that the Greeks were required to endure from their philosophers. And where the powers are essentially lesser and the domain of Being to be disclosed is ontologically far more difficult than what was given to the Greeks, the cumbersomeness of concept-formation and the harshness of expression will increase. This is Heidegger's preemptive defense of his own style.

### § 8. The Outline of the Treatise

The first paragraph states that the question of the meaning of Being is the most universal and emptiest; but at the same time it harbors the possibility of its ownmost individuation with respect to the respective Dasein. The winning of the basic concept "Being" and the prescribing of the ontological conceptuality demanded by it and its necessary modifications require a concrete guiding thread. The universality of the concept of Being does not conflict with the "speciality" of the investigation — i.e., the advance toward it by way of a special interpretation of a determinate being, Dasein, in which the horizon for the understanding and possible interpretation of Being is to be won. This being itself, however, is "historical" in itself, so that its ownmost ontological illumination necessarily becomes a "historical" interpretation.

The second paragraph states that the working-out of the question of Being thus divides into two tasks, to which corresponds the division of the treatise into two parts. Part One: The Interpretation of Dasein with Respect to Temporality, and the Explication of Time as the Transcendental Horizon of the Question of Being. Part Two: Basic Features of a Phenomenological Destruction of the History of Ontology with Orientation toward the Problematic of Temporality.

The third paragraph outlines Part One in three divisions: (1) The Preparatory Fundamental Analysis of Dasein; (2) Dasein and Temporality; (3) Time and Being.

The fourth and final paragraph outlines Part Two in three sections: (1) Kant's Doctrine of Schematism and Time as a Preliminary Stage of a Problematic of Temporality; (2) The Ontological Foundation of the "Cogito Sum" of Descartes and the Taking-Over of Medieval Ontology into the Problematic of the "Res Cogitans"; (3) The Treatise of Aristotle on Time as a Criterion of the Phenomenal Basis and the Limits of Ancient Ontology. As is well known, only Part One, Divisions One and Two were completed and published. Division Three of Part One ("Time and Being") and the entirety of Part Two were never published as parts of *Being and Time*, though the Kant-interpretation was later partially carried out in *Kant and the Problem of Metaphysics* (1929), and related material appeared in Heidegger's later works and lecture courses.


## Part One: The Interpretation of Dasein with Respect to Temporality and the Explication of Time as the Transcendental Horizon of the Question of Being

## First Division: The Preparatory Fundamental Analysis of Dasein

The brief introductory paragraph to Division One states that the primarily interrogated being in the question of the meaning of Being is the being of the character of Dasein. The preparatory existential analytic of Dasein requires, in keeping with its own distinctive character, a preliminary exposition and delimitation against apparently parallel investigations (Chapter 1). Holding to the fixed approach of the investigation, a fundamental structure of Dasein is to be laid bare: Being-in-the-world (Chapter 2). This "a priori" of Dasein-interpretation is not a pieced-together determinateness but a primordially and constantly whole structure. It does, however, afford various perspectives on the moments constituting it. With the constant keeping-in-view of the always prior whole of this structure, these moments are to be phenomenally distinguished. The objects of the analysis are thus: the world in its worldhood (Chapter 3), Being-in-the-world as Being-with and Being-a-self (Chapter 4), Being-in as such (Chapter 5). On the ground of the analysis of this fundamental structure, a provisional indication of the Being of Dasein becomes possible. Its existential meaning is care (Chapter 6). This passage is a compact roadmap for the entire first Division.

### First Chapter: The Exposition of the Task of a Preparatory Analysis of Dasein

### § 9. The Theme of the Analytic of Dasein

The first paragraph states that the being whose analysis is the task is in each case we ourselves. The Being of this being is in each case *mine* (*je meines*). In the Being of this being, this being itself comports itself toward its Being. As a being of this Being, it is delivered over to its own Being. Being is what is at issue for this being in each case. From this characterization of Dasein, a twofold result follows.

The second paragraph presents the first result: the "essence" of this being lies in its *having-to-be* (*Zu-sein*). The what-being (*essentia*) of this being must, insofar as one can speak of it at all, be grasped from its Being (*existentia*). It is precisely the ontological task to show that when we choose the designation "existence" for the Being of this being, this title does not and cannot have the ontological meaning of the traditional term *existentia*; *existentia* ontologically means as much as presence-at-hand (*Vorhandenheit*), a mode of Being that essentially does not belong to the being of the character of Dasein. Confusion is avoided by always using the interpretive expression "presence-at-hand" for the title *existentia* and assigning "existence" as a determination of Being solely to Dasein. The "essence" of Dasein lies in its existence. The characters exhibitable in this being are therefore not present-at-hand "properties" of a present-at-hand being that "looks" thus-and-so, but in each case possible ways for it to be, and only that. All so-being of this being is primarily Being. Therefore the title "Dasein," with which we designate this being, does not express its *what* — as do "table," "house," "tree" — but its *Being*. This is a radical departure from all substance-metaphysics: Dasein has no fixed nature. Its "what" just *is* its "how" — its manner of existing.

The third paragraph presents the second result: the Being that is at issue for this being in its Being is in each case *mine* (*je meines*). Dasein is therefore never to be grasped ontologically as a case or instance of a genus of beings as present-at-hand. For present-at-hand beings, their Being is "indifferent" to them — or more precisely, they "are" in such a way that their Being can be neither indifferent nor non-indifferent to them. The addressing of Dasein must, in accordance with the character of *mineness* (*Jemeinigkeit*) of this being, always include the personal pronoun: "I am," "you are."

The fourth paragraph adds that Dasein is mine in each case in this or that way of being. It has always already somehow decided in which way Dasein is in each case mine. The being for which its Being is at issue comports itself toward its Being as its ownmost *possibility*. Dasein is in each case its possibility and does not merely "have" it as a property of something present-at-hand. And because Dasein is essentially in each case its possibility, this being can in its Being "choose" itself, win itself; it can lose itself, or never and only "apparently" win itself. It can have lost itself only, and not yet have won itself only, insofar as it is by its essence possible authentic — that is, belonging to itself. The two modes of Being of *authenticity* (*Eigentlichkeit*) and *inauthenticity* (*Uneigentlichkeit*) — these expressions are terminologically chosen in their strict verbal sense — are grounded in the fact that Dasein is in general determined by mineness. But the inauthenticity of Dasein does not signify a "lesser" Being or a "lower" degree of Being. Inauthenticity can rather determine Dasein in its fullest concreteness — in its busyness, its excitement, its interestedness, its capacity for enjoyment. This point is essential for avoiding a moralistic misreading of the authentic/inauthentic distinction: inauthenticity is not a deficiency or failure but a fully concrete and positive mode of Being.

The fifth paragraph states that the two sketched characters of Dasein — the priority of *existentia* over *essentia*, and mineness — already indicate that an analytic of this being is placed before a peculiar phenomenal domain. This being never has the mode of Being of something merely present-at-hand within the world. It is therefore also not to be thematically given in the way of finding something present-at-hand. The right way of giving this being is so far from self-evident that its determination itself constitutes an essential piece of the ontological analytic. The possibility of bringing the Being of this being to understanding at all stands and falls with the secure execution of the right way of giving it.

The sixth paragraph specifies the starting point. Dasein determines itself as a being in each case from a possibility that it is and somehow understands in its Being. This is the formal meaning of the constitution of existence of Dasein. But in this lies the directive for the ontological interpretation of this being: to develop the problematic of its Being from the existentiality of its existence. This cannot mean constructing Dasein from a concrete possible idea of existence. At the outset of the analysis, Dasein is to be uncovered precisely not in the differentiation of a determinate existing but in its indifferent *initially-and-for-the-most-part*. This indifference of the everydayness of Dasein is not nothing but a positive phenomenal character of this being. Out of this mode of Being and back into it, all existing is as it is. We call this everyday indifference of Dasein *averageness* (*Durchschnittlichkeit*). The methodological decision is clear: the starting point is average, undifferentiated everydayness — not peak experiences, crises, or extraordinary moments.

The seventh paragraph observes that because average everydayness constitutes the ontic initially-and-for-the-most-part of this being, it was and always is again skipped over in the explication of Dasein. What is ontically nearest and best-known is ontologically farthest, most unknown, and constantly overlooked in its ontological significance. When Augustine asks, "What is closer to me than myself?" and must answer, "I labor here and labor in myself; I have become for myself a land of difficulty and of great sweat" (*Confessions* X.16), this applies not only to the ontic and pre-ontological opacity of Dasein but in an even heightened measure to the ontological task of not missing this being in its phenomenally nearest mode of Being but rather making it accessible in positive characterization.

The eighth paragraph adds that the average everydayness of Dasein must not be taken as a mere "aspect." In it too, and even in the mode of inauthenticity, the structure of existentiality lies a priori. In it too, Dasein is in a determinate way concerned with its Being, toward which it comports itself in the mode of average everydayness — even if only in the mode of flight from it and forgetting it.

The ninth paragraph clarifies that the explication of Dasein in its average everydayness does not yield merely average structures in the sense of a blurring indeterminacy. What is ontically in the mode of averageness can ontologically very well be grasped in pregnant structures that structurally do not differ from ontological determinations of, say, an authentic Being of Dasein.

The tenth paragraph introduces the key terminological distinction between *existentials* and *categories*. All explicata arising from the analytic of Dasein are won in view of its existence-structure. Because they are determined from existentiality, we call the characters of Being of Dasein *existentials* (*Existenzialien*). They are to be sharply separated from the determinations of Being of non-Dasein-like beings, which we call *categories* (*Kategorien*). The expression "category" is here taken and held fast in its primary ontological meaning. Ancient ontology had as the exemplary ground of its interpretation of Being the beings encountered within the world. As mode of access to these beings, *noein* or *logos* applies. In these, beings are encountered. But the Being of these beings must be graspable in a distinguished *legein* (letting-be-seen), such that this Being is understood in advance as what it is and what it already is in every being. The always-already-prior addressing of Being in the discussing (*logos*) of beings is *katēgoreisthai*. This originally means: to publicly accuse, to say something to someone's face before all. Used ontologically, the term means: to say to a being's face, as it were, what it already is as a being — i.e., to let it be seen in its Being for all. What is sighted and made visible in such seeing are the *katēgoriai*. They encompass the a priori determinations of beings addressable in *logos* in various ways. Existentials and categories are the two fundamental possibilities of characters of Being. The being corresponding to them demands a respectively different mode of primary interrogation: beings are a *who* (existence) or a *what* (presence-at-hand in the broadest sense).

The eleventh and final paragraph of §9 notes that the existential analytic of Dasein also advances a task whose urgency is scarcely less than that of the question of Being itself: the laying-bare of the a priori that must be visible if the question "what is the human being?" is to be philosophically discussed. The existential analytic of Dasein lies before all psychology, anthropology, and above all biology. Through delimitation against these possible investigations of Dasein, the theme of the analytic can receive a sharper circumscription.

### § 10. The Delimitation of the Analytic of Dasein against Anthropology, Psychology, and Biology

The first paragraph states that after an initial positive sketching of the theme, its prohibitive characterization always remains important, though discussions of what is not to be done can easily become unfruitful. What is to be shown is that previous investigations aimed at Dasein — for all their substantive yield — miss the genuinely philosophical problem. The delimitations of the existential analytic against anthropology, psychology, and biology concern only the fundamental ontological question. "Science-theoretically" they are necessarily insufficient already because the scientific structure of the named disciplines is today thoroughly questionable and requires new impulses that must spring from ontological problematic.

The second paragraph provides a historical illustration. Descartes, to whom the discovery of the *cogito sum* as the starting basis of modern philosophical questioning is attributed, investigated the *cogitare* of the *ego* — within certain limits. But he leaves the *sum* entirely undiscussed, although it is posited just as primordially as the *cogito*. The analytic poses the ontological question about the Being of the *sum*. Only when this is determined does the mode of Being of the *cogitationes* become graspable.

The third paragraph immediately qualifies this comparison. This historical exemplification is at the same time misleading. One of the analytic's first tasks will be to show that the approach from a primarily given I and subject fundamentally misses the phenomenal content of Dasein. Every idea of a "subject" still ontologically co-posits the *subjectum* (*hypokeimenon*) — however vehemently one may ontically defend oneself against "soul-substance" or the "reification of consciousness." Thinghood itself first requires an exhibition of its ontological provenance, so that one can then ask what is positively to be understood by the non-reified Being of the subject, soul, consciousness, spirit, or person. All these titles name determinate, "formable" phenomenal domains, but their use always goes together with a remarkable absence of any need to ask about the Being of the being so designated. It is therefore no willfulness in terminology when we avoid these titles, as well as the expressions "life" and "human being," for the designation of the being that we ourselves are.

The fourth paragraph addresses "philosophy of life." In the rightly understood tendency of all serious "Lebensphilosophie" — the word says about as much as "the botany of plants" — there lies the unexpressed tendency toward an understanding of the Being of Dasein. But it remains striking, and this is its fundamental deficiency, that "life" itself does not become ontologically problematic as a mode of Being. Dilthey's research was kept in motion by the constant question about "life." He sought to understand the "lived experiences" of this life from the whole of life itself, in their structural and developmental connection. The philosophically relevant point of his "human-scientific psychology" is not that it no longer orients itself by psychical elements and atoms, but that in all of this he was above all on the way to the question of "life." However, the limits of his problematic and conceptuality are also most strongly apparent here. These limits are shared with Dilthey by Bergson and all directions of "personalism" and tendencies toward philosophical anthropology determined by them.

The fifth paragraph turns to Husserl and Scheler. Even the fundamentally more radical and transparent phenomenological interpretation of personality does not reach the dimension of the question of the Being of Dasein. Despite all differences in questioning, execution, and worldview-orientation, the interpretations of personality in Husserl and Scheler agree in the negative: they no longer pose the question of "person-being" itself. Heidegger takes Scheler's interpretation as an example. Scheler insists that the person must never be thought of as a thing or substance; it is rather the immediately co-experienced unity of experiencing — not a merely thought thing behind or outside the immediately experienced. Person is no thing-like substantial Being. Furthermore, the Being of the person cannot consist in being a subject of rational acts of a certain lawfulness. Person is not a thing, not a substance, not an object. Acts are not objects; they are essentially lived only in their execution and given in reflection. Person exists only in the execution of intentional acts and is therefore essentially no object. Every psychical objectification, every grasping of acts as something psychical, is identical with depersonalization. But the critical question is: what is the ontological meaning of "execution"? How is the mode of Being of the person to be positively ontologically determined?

The sixth paragraph broadens the critique. The question is after the Being of the whole human being, which one is accustomed to grasping as a bodily-psychic-spiritual unity. Body, soul, and spirit may name phenomenal domains that can be thematically detached for certain investigations; within certain limits their ontological indeterminacy may not matter. But in the question of the Being of the human being, this Being cannot be summatively calculated from the modes of Being — themselves yet to be determined — of body, soul, and spirit. And even for an ontological attempt proceeding in this way, an idea of the Being of the whole would have to be presupposed. What blocks or misleads the fundamental question of the Being of Dasein is the pervasive orientation toward ancient-Christian anthropology, whose inadequate ontological foundations are also overlooked by personalism and philosophy of life. Traditional anthropology carries within it: (1) the definition of the human being as *zōon logon echon*, interpreted as *animal rationale*, rational living being — but the mode of Being of *zōon* is here understood as presence-at-hand and occurring, while *logos* is a higher endowment whose mode of Being remains equally obscure; (2) a theological guiding thread: "Let us make man in our image and likeness" (Genesis 1:26). Christian-theological anthropology wins from this, with co-adoption of the ancient definition, an interpretation of the being we call human. But just as the Being of God is ontologically interpreted with the means of ancient ontology, so too and all the more is the Being of *ens finitum*. The Christian definition was de-theologized in the modern period, but the idea of "transcendence" — that the human being is something reaching beyond itself — has its roots in Christian dogmatics, which one will hardly claim ever made the Being of the human being ontologically problematic.

The seventh paragraph states that these two guiding threads — the Greek definition and the theological — intertwine in modern anthropology with the methodological starting point of the *res cogitans*, consciousness, and experiential connection. But since the *cogitationes* also remain ontologically indeterminate, or are again unreflectively taken as something "given" whose "Being" is subject to no question, the anthropological problematic remains undetermined in its decisive ontological foundations. The same holds no less for "psychology," whose anthropological tendencies are unmistakable today. Nor can the missing ontological foundation be replaced by building anthropology and psychology into a general biology. In the order of possible grasping and interpretation, biology as "science of life" is *founded* in the ontology of Dasein, though not exclusively. Life is a mode of Being of its own, but essentially accessible only in Dasein. The ontology of life is accomplished by way of a *privative interpretation*: it determines what must be, so that something like mere-still-living can be. Life is neither pure presence-at-hand, nor is it Dasein. And Dasein in turn is never to be ontologically determined by positing it as life (ontologically indeterminate) and as something else in addition.

The eighth and final paragraph of §10 disclaims any judgment on the positive work of anthropology, psychology, and biology. But it must be brought to consciousness again and again that ontological foundations can never be retroactively inferred hypothetically from empirical material; they are always already "there" even when empirical material is merely collected. That positive research does not see these foundations and takes them for self-evident is no proof that they do not lie at the ground and are not problematic in a more radical sense than any thesis of positive science can ever be.

### § 11. The Existential Analytic and the Interpretation of Primitive Dasein. The Difficulties of Gaining a "Natural Concept of World"

The first paragraph states that the interpretation of Dasein in its everydayness is not identical with the description of a primitive stage of Dasein whose knowledge could be empirically mediated by anthropology. Everydayness does not coincide with primitivity. Everydayness is rather a mode of Being of Dasein even and precisely when Dasein moves in a highly developed and differentiated culture. On the other hand, primitive Dasein also has its possibilities of non-everyday Being; it has its specific everydayness. The orientation of the analysis of Dasein toward the "life of primitive peoples" can have positive methodological significance insofar as "primitive phenomena" are often less concealed and complicated by a far-reaching self-interpretation of the Dasein in question. Primitive Dasein often speaks more directly out of a primordial absorption in "phenomena" (taken in the pre-phenomenological sense). The conceptuality that may seem to us clumsy and crude can be positively helpful for a genuine bringing-out of the ontological structures of phenomena.

The second paragraph qualifies this. Our knowledge of "primitives" is provided by ethnology. But ethnology already moves, in its first "reception" of material, its sifting and processing, in determinate pre-concepts and interpretations of human Dasein in general. It is not settled whether the everyday psychology or even the scientific psychology and sociology that the ethnologist brings with them offer scientific guarantee of an adequate mode of access, interpretation, and transmission of the phenomena to be investigated. Ethnology itself already presupposes a sufficient analytic of Dasein as a guiding thread. Since the positive sciences neither "can" nor should wait for the ontological work of philosophy, the progress of research will not take place as "advance" but as repetition and ontologically more transparent purification of what has been ontically discovered.

The third and final paragraph of §11 addresses the concept of a "natural concept of world." However easy the formal delimitation of ontological problematic against ontic research may be, the execution and especially the *approach* of an existential analytic of Dasein is not without difficulties. In its task lies a desideratum that has long troubled philosophy but at whose fulfillment it has repeatedly failed: the working-out of the idea of a "natural concept of world." The presently available wealth of knowledge of the most manifold and remote cultures and forms of Dasein might seem favorable for a fruitful tackling of this task. But this is only semblance. At bottom, such superabundant knowledge is a temptation to misrecognize the actual problem. Syncretistic comparing-everything and typologizing does not of itself yield genuine essential knowledge. The manageability of the manifold in a table does not guarantee a genuine understanding of what lies ordered there. The genuine principle of ordering has its own material content, which is never found through ordering but is always already presupposed in it. Thus the ordering of worldviews requires the explicit idea of world as such. And if "world" is itself a constitutive element of Dasein, then the conceptual working-out of the world-phenomenon demands an insight into the fundamental structures of Dasein. Ontology can contribute only indirectly to the advancement of existing positive disciplines. It has for itself an independent aim, if the question of Being is indeed the spur of all scientific seeking over and above a mere taking-note of beings.


### Second Chapter: Being-in-the-World in General as the Fundamental Constitution of Dasein

### § 12. The Preliminary Sketch of Being-in-the-World from the Orientation toward Being-in as Such

The first paragraph recalls the preparatory discussions of §9 and restates the results: Dasein is a being that in its Being comports itself understandingly toward this Being. With this, the formal concept of existence is indicated. Dasein exists. Dasein is furthermore a being that I in each case myself am. To existing Dasein belongs mineness as the condition of the possibility of authenticity and inauthenticity. Dasein exists in each case in one of these modes, or in the modal indifference of them.

The second paragraph states that these determinations of Being of Dasein must now be seen and understood a priori on the ground of the constitution of Being that we call *Being-in-the-world* (*In-der-Welt-sein*). The right approach of the analytic of Dasein consists in the interpretation of this constitution.

The third paragraph analyzes the compound expression. "Being-in-the-world" already indicates in its coinage that a unitary phenomenon is meant. This primary finding must be seen as a whole. Its indissolubility into assemblable components does not exclude a manifoldness of constitutive structural moments. The phenomenal finding indicated by this expression in fact affords a threefold perspective. (1) The "in the world" — giving rise to the task of inquiring into the ontological structure of "world" and determining the idea of worldhood as such (Chapter 3). (2) The being that always is in the manner of Being-in-the-world — the "who?" of Dasein in the mode of average everydayness (Chapter 4). (3) Being-in as such — the ontological constitution of in-ness itself (Chapter 5). Each highlighting of one of these moments co-highlights the others. Being-in-the-world is an a priori necessary constitution of Dasein but by no means sufficient to fully determine its Being. Before the thematic individual analysis of the three highlighted phenomena, an orienting characterization of the last-named structural moment — Being-in as such — is to be attempted.

The fourth paragraph asks what "Being-in" means and immediately distinguishes it from the spatial "being inside" of one present-at-hand thing within another. We are inclined to understand "Being in the world" as "Being in..." — the way water is "in" a glass, a suit "in" a closet. With this term, the mode of Being of a being that is "in" another is named, as two spatially extended beings stand in a locational relation within space. Water and glass, suit and closet are both in the same way "in" space "at" a location. This relation can be extended — the bench in the lecture hall, the lecture hall in the university, the university in the city, and so on, up to "the bench in outer space." All these beings whose "in-one-another" can be so determined have the same mode of Being of presence-at-hand as things occurring "within" the world. This presence-at-hand "in" something present-at-hand, this co-presence-at-hand with something of the same mode of Being in the sense of a determinate locational relation, we call *categorial* ontological characters — those belonging to beings of a non-Dasein-like mode of Being.

The fifth paragraph provides the decisive contrast. Being-in, by contrast, designates a constitution of Being of Dasein and is an *existential*. One cannot think here of the presence-at-hand of a bodily thing (the human body) "in" a present-at-hand being. Being-in means a spatial "in-one-another" of present-at-hand entities as little as "in" originally designates a spatial relation of this sort at all. "In" stems from *innan-* — to dwell, *habitare*, to stay; "an" means: I am accustomed, familiar with, I tend to something; it has the meaning of *colo* in the sense of *habito* and *diligo*. The being to which Being-in in this meaning belongs we characterized as the being that I in each case myself am. The expression "bin" (am) is connected with "bei" (at, near); "I am" (*ich bin*) means in turn: I dwell, I stay near... the world as the familiar so-and-so. Being, as the infinitive of "I am" — understood as existential — means to dwell near..., to be familiar with... Being-in is accordingly the formal existential expression for the Being of Dasein, which has the essential constitution of Being-in-the-world. This is a fundamental etymological-phenomenological argument: the "in" of Being-in-the-world is not spatial containment but the existential structure of dwelling, familiarity, involvement.

The sixth paragraph introduces "Being-alongside" (*Sein-bei*) the world as a further existential founded in Being-in. This is the more specific sense of absorption in the world. Because these analyses aim at seeing a primordial structure of Being of Dasein — a structure whose phenomenal content must determine the articulation of the concepts of Being, and because this structure is fundamentally not graspable through inherited ontological categories — this "Being-alongside" must be further clarified. Heidegger again takes the path of contrast against a categorially different (i.e., ontologically essentially other) relation of Being.

The seventh paragraph insists that "Being-alongside" the world as an existential never means something like the co-presence-at-hand of occurring things. There is no such thing as a "side-by-side" of a being called "Dasein" with another being called "world." We do sometimes express the togetherness of two present-at-hand entities by saying "the table stands 'by' the door," "the chair 'touches' the wall." But of "touching" one can never strictly speak — not because upon close inspection a gap can always be found between chair and wall, but because the chair fundamentally cannot touch the wall even if the gap were zero. The precondition for this would be that the wall could *encounter* the chair. A being can only touch an intraworldly present-at-hand being if it has the mode of Being of Being-in — if with its Being-there a world is already discovered from which beings can reveal themselves in touching, so as to become accessible in their presence-at-hand. Two beings that are present-at-hand within the world and are moreover in themselves *worldless* can never "touch" each other; neither can "be alongside" the other. This is a powerful ontological argument: physical "contact" between worldless entities is a categorial relation entirely different from the existential "Being-alongside" that characterizes Dasein's involvement.

The eighth paragraph introduces *facticity* (*Faktizität*). The "factuality" of the fact of one's own Dasein is ontologically fundamentally different from the factual occurrence of a kind of stone. The factuality of the fact Dasein — which is what each Dasein in each case is — we call its facticity. The complex structure of this determination of Being can only become graspable as a problem in the light of the already worked-out existential fundamental constitutions of Dasein. The concept of facticity includes: the Being-in-the-world of an "intraworldly" being, such that this being can understand itself as bound up in its "destiny" with the Being of the beings that it encounters within its own world.

The ninth paragraph draws an initial boundary. For the present, the point is only to see the ontological difference between Being-in as an existential and the "insideness" of present-at-hand things among each other as a category. In delimiting Being-in in this way, not every kind of "spatiality" is thereby denied to Dasein. On the contrary: Dasein itself has its own "Being-in-space," but this is possible only on the ground of Being-in-the-world in general. Nor can Being-in be ontologically clarified through an ontic characterization — for example, by saying that Being-in a world is a spiritual property, while the "spatiality" of the human is a constitution of bodily nature always simultaneously "founded" by corporeality. In doing so one is back at a co-presence-at-hand of a spirit-thing and a body-thing, and the Being of the being so composed remains all the more obscure. Understanding Being-in-the-world as an essential structure of Dasein first enables insight into the existential spatiality of Dasein.

The tenth paragraph enumerates modes of Being-in. Being-in-the-world has with its facticity always already dispersed itself into determinate ways of Being-in. The manifold of such ways can be indicated exemplarily: having to do with something, producing something, attending to and tending something, making use of something, giving up and letting something be lost, undertaking, accomplishing, inquiring, questioning, considering, discussing, determining... These ways of Being-in have the mode of Being of *concern* (*Besorgen*), yet to be characterized in detail. Deficient modes of concern are also ways of concern: omitting, neglecting, renouncing, resting — all modes of "only still" with respect to possibilities of concern. The title "concern" initially has its pre-scientific meaning and can mean: to carry out, to settle, to "straighten out" something. It can also mean: to procure something for oneself. And we use it in a characteristic turn: "I am concerned that the undertaking will fail" — where concern means something like apprehension. Over against these pre-scientific ontic meanings, "concern" is used in the present investigation as an ontological term (existential) to designate the Being of a possible Being-in-the-world. The title was chosen not because Dasein is primarily economic and "practical," but because the Being of Dasein itself is to be made visible as *care* (*Sorge*). The expression has nothing to do with "tribulation," "melancholy," and "worries of life" that are ontically findable in every Dasein. Such things are ontically possible — just as "carefreeness" and "cheerfulness" are — because Dasein, ontologically understood, *is* care. Because Being-in-the-world essentially belongs to Dasein, its Being toward the world is essentially concern.

The eleventh paragraph insists that Being-in is not a "property" that Dasein sometimes has and sometimes does not. The human being does not "exist" and then additionally have a relation to the "world" that it occasionally takes on. Dasein is never "initially" a being-in-free being that sometimes has the inclination to take up a "relation" to the world. Such taking up of relations is only possible because Dasein, as Being-in-the-world, is as it is. Nor does this constitution of Being arise through the co-occurrence of other beings alongside Dasein. The commonly used phrase "the human has its environment" remains ontologically meaningless as long as this "having" remains undetermined. This "having" is grounded in the existential constitution of Being-in.

The twelfth paragraph addresses the predominance of negative characterization. The phenomenological exhibition of Being-in-the-world has the character of a *pushing back* of disguises and coverings-over, because this phenomenon is always already in every Dasein in a certain way "seen." It is so because, as a fundamental constitution of Dasein, it is always already disclosed with its Being for its understanding of Being. But the phenomenon is also at the same time mostly already just as thoroughly misinterpreted or ontologically insufficiently explicated. This "in a certain way seeing and yet mostly misinterpreting" is itself grounded in nothing other than this constitution of Being of Dasein itself: Dasein initially and for the most part understands itself and its Being-in-the-world from the being and the Being of the being that it itself is *not* — the being it encounters "within" its world.

The thirteenth paragraph states that in Dasein itself and for it, this constitution of Being is always already somehow known. When it is now to be *cognized*, the explicit cognition takes itself — as world-cognition — to be the exemplary relation of the "soul" to the world. Cognizing the world (*noein*), addressing and discussing the "world" (*logos*), therefore functions as the primary mode of Being-in-the-world, without Being-in-the-world as such being grasped. Because this structure of Being remains ontologically inaccessible while being ontically experienced as a "relation" between beings (world) and beings (soul), and because Being is initially understood in ontological dependence on beings as intraworldly beings, the attempt is made to grasp this relation on the ground of these beings and in terms of their Being — that is, as presence-at-hand. Being-in-the-world — though pre-phenomenologically experienced and known — is made invisible by way of an ontologically inappropriate interpretation. One then knows the constitution of Dasein only in the form of the inappropriate interpretation. In this way it becomes the "evident" starting point for the problems of epistemology or "metaphysics of cognition." For what is more self-evident than that a "subject" relates to an "object" and vice versa? This "subject-object relation" must be presupposed. But this remains — however unassailable in its facticity — a fateful presupposition when its ontological necessity and above all its ontological meaning are left in darkness.

The fourteenth and final paragraph of §12 announces the next step. Because world-cognition mostly and exclusively represents the phenomenon of Being-in exemplarily, and because through this priority of cognition the understanding of its ownmost mode of Being is misled, Being-in-the-world is to be brought out still more sharply with a view to world-cognition, and world-cognition is to be made visible as an existential "modality" of Being-in.

### § 13. The Exemplification of Being-in through a Founded Mode: World-Cognition

The first paragraph states that if Being-in-the-world is a fundamental constitution of Dasein in which it moves not just in general but preeminently in the mode of everydayness, then it must also always already be ontically experienced. A total remaining-concealed would be incomprehensible, given that Dasein has a self-understanding, however indeterminate. But as soon as the "phenomenon of world-cognition" was grasped, it also fell into an "external," formal interpretation. The index of this is the still-customary positing of cognition as a "relation between subject and object," which harbors as much "truth" as emptiness. Subject and object, however, do not coincide with Dasein and world.

The second paragraph undertakes a phenomenological re-description of cognition. Even if it were possible to determine Being-in ontologically primarily from cognizing Being-in-the-world, the first required task would still be the phenomenal characterization of cognizing as a Being in and toward the world. When one reflects on this relation of Being, what is first given is a being called nature as that which is cognized. Cognizing itself is not to be found in this being. If it "is" at all, it belongs solely to the being that cognizes. But even in this being — the human-thing — cognizing is not present-at-hand. At any rate, it is not externally ascertainable in the way bodily properties are. Since cognizing belongs to this being but is not an external characteristic, it must be "inside." The more definitively one holds that cognizing is primarily "inside" and indeed has nothing of the mode of Being of a physical or psychical being, the more presuppositionlessly one believes oneself to proceed in the question of the essence of cognition. For now a problem can arise: how does this cognizing subject get "out" of its inner "sphere" into an "other and outer" one; how can cognition have an object at all; how must the object itself be thought so that in the end the subject cognizes it without having to risk the leap into another sphere? In this variously elaborated approach, the question about the mode of Being of this cognizing subject is consistently *omitted* — even though one always already implicitly has its mode of Being as one's theme when one deals with its cognizing. One hears assurances that the "inside" and "inner sphere" of the subject is certainly not to be thought as a "box" or "housing." But what the "inside" of immanence positively means, and how the Being-character of this "being-inside" of cognizing is grounded in the mode of Being of the subject — about this there is silence.

The third paragraph states what is blindly passed over in this approach: with the most preliminary thematization of the phenomenon of cognition, something is already implicitly co-said — cognizing is a mode of Being of Dasein as Being-in-the-world; it has its ontic foundation in this constitution of Being. One might object that such an interpretation of cognizing destroys the problem of knowledge. But which authority decides whether and in what sense a problem of knowledge is to exist, if not the phenomenon of cognition itself and the mode of Being of the cognizer?

The fourth paragraph provides the positive phenomenological account. If we ask what shows itself in the phenomenal finding of cognition, we must hold fast that cognizing itself is grounded in an *already-Being-alongside-the-world* that essentially constitutes the Being of Dasein. This already-Being-alongside is initially not merely a rigid staring at something purely present-at-hand. Being-in-the-world, as concern, is absorbed by the world with which it is concerned. For cognition to be possible as a contemplative determining of what is present-at-hand, there is first required a *deficiency* of concerned having-to-do-with the world. In the refraining from all producing, handling, and the like, concern settles into the sole remaining mode of Being-in: simply-tarrying-alongside... On the ground of this mode of Being toward the world — which lets intraworldly beings be encountered only in their pure look (*eidos*) — an explicit looking-at what is so encountered becomes possible. This looking-at takes a determinate "point of view" from the encountered being. Such looking-at comes itself into the mode of an independent lingering alongside intraworldly beings. In this "lingering" — the refraining from all handling and use — the apprehending of what is present-at-hand takes place. Apprehending has the form of addressing and discussing something as something. On the ground of this interpreting in the broadest sense, apprehending becomes determining. What is apprehended and determined can be expressed in propositions, and as so asserted, retained and preserved. This retaining of an assertion about something is itself a mode of Being-in-the-world and may not be interpreted as a "process" through which a subject procures representations of something that then remain stored "inside," regarding which the question can then occasionally arise how they "correspond" with actuality. Heidegger is offering a genetic account of theoretical cognition as a *founded mode* — a derivative form arising from the more primordial involvement of concern through a process of deficiency and abstraction.

The fifth paragraph states the consequence. In directing itself toward and grasping, Dasein does not first go out of an inner sphere in which it is initially encapsulated. Rather, in its primary mode of Being, it is always already "outside" alongside a being of the already-discovered world. And the determining lingering alongside the being to be cognized is not a leaving of the inner sphere either; even in this "being-outside" alongside the object, Dasein is, in the rightly understood sense, "inside" — i.e., it itself is, as Being-in-the-world, that which cognizes. And again, the apprehending of what is cognized is not a returning with the gained booty into the "housing" of consciousness. Even in apprehending, preserving, and retaining, cognizing Dasein remains, as Dasein, *outside*. In "mere" knowing about a connection of Being of beings, in "only" representing it, in "merely" thinking about it, I am no less alongside beings outside in the world than in an originary grasping. Even forgetting — in which apparently every relation of Being to what was formerly cognized is extinguished — must be grasped as a modification of primordial Being-in; likewise all deception and error.

The sixth and final paragraph of §13 draws the overall conclusion. The exhibited foundational connection of the modes of Being-in-the-world constitutive of world-cognition makes clear: in cognizing, Dasein gains a new stance of Being toward the world always already discovered in Dasein. This new possibility of Being can develop independently, become a task, and as science take over the guidance of Being-in-the-world. But cognition does not first create a *commercium* of the subject with a world, nor does it arise from an influence of the world upon a subject. Cognizing is a mode of Dasein *founded* in Being-in-the-world. Therefore Being-in-the-world, as a fundamental constitution, demands a prior interpretation. The "problem of knowledge" is a pseudo-problem generated by failing to recognize the more primordial structure of Being-in-the-world.


### Third Chapter: The Worldhood of the World

### § 14. The Idea of the Worldhood of the World in General

The first paragraph opens by noting that Being-in-the-world is first to be made visible with respect to the structural moment "world." The task seems easy and trivial. What can it mean to describe "the world" as a phenomenon? To let be seen what shows itself as "beings" within the world. The first step would be an enumeration of what there is "in" the world: houses, trees, people, mountains, stars. We can describe the "look" of these beings and narrate the events involving them. But this is obviously a pre-phenomenological "business," phenomenologically irrelevant. The description sticks to beings; it is ontic. What is sought, however, is Being.

The second paragraph considers a more sophisticated approach. To describe the "world" phenomenologically would then mean: to exhibit the Being of beings present-at-hand within the world and to fix it conceptually-categorially. The beings within the world are things — natural things and "value-laden" things. Their thinghood becomes the problem, and insofar as the thinghood of value-laden things builds on natural thinghood, the Being of natural things — nature as such — is the primary theme. The Being-character of natural things that founds everything is substantiality. What constitutes its ontological meaning?

The third paragraph challenges this approach. But are we thereby asking ontologically about "the world"? The problematic is without doubt ontological. But even if the purest explication of the Being of nature succeeded, calibrated to the basic statements of mathematical natural science, this ontology would *never* hit upon the phenomenon "world." Nature is itself a being that is encountered *within* the world and is discoverable on various paths and stages. A pure ontology of nature — however successful — still misses the phenomenon of world.

The fourth paragraph considers an alternative. Should we then turn to the beings among which Dasein initially and for the most part lingers — "value-laden" things? Don't they show "the world" in which we live more forcefully? Perhaps they do indeed show something like "world" more urgently. But these things too are beings "within" the world. Neither the ontic description of intraworldly beings nor the ontological interpretation of the Being of these beings as such hits upon the phenomenon "world." In both modes of access to "objective Being," "world" is already, and in different ways, *presupposed*.

The fifth paragraph poses a series of questions. Can "world" perhaps not be addressed at all as a determination of the named beings? But we do call these beings *intraworldly*. Is "world" then a character of Being of Dasein? And does each Dasein then "initially" have its world? Does "world" then become something "subjective"? How is a "common" world possible in which we are? And if the question of "the world" is raised, which world is meant? Neither this one nor that one, but the *worldhood of world as such*. By what path do we encounter this phenomenon?

The sixth paragraph defines worldhood. "Worldhood" is an ontological concept and means the structure of a constitutive moment of Being-in-the-world. But Being-in-the-world we know as an existential determination of Dasein. Worldhood is accordingly itself an *existential*. When we ask ontologically about "the world," we by no means leave the thematic field of the analytic of Dasein. "World" is ontologically not a determination of the being that Dasein essentially is not, but rather a character of Dasein itself. This does not exclude that the path of the investigation of the phenomenon "world" must be taken by way of intraworldly beings and their Being.

The seventh paragraph disambiguates four senses of "world": (1) "World" as an ontic concept: the totality of beings that can be present-at-hand within the world. (2) "World" as an ontological term: the Being of the beings named under (1); "world" becomes the title of the region encompassing a manifold of beings — e.g., the "world" of the mathematician means the region of possible objects of mathematics. (3) "World" in an ontic but pre-ontologically existentiell sense: not the beings that Dasein essentially is not, but that "in which" a factical Dasein "lives" — the public We-world or the own, nearest (domestic) surrounding world. (4) "World" as the ontological-existential concept of *worldhood*. Worldhood is itself modifiable into the respective structural whole of particular "worlds" but includes in itself the a priori of worldhood as such. Heidegger terminologically claims "world" for meaning (3), marking meaning (1) with quotation marks when used. The derivative "worldly" (*weltlich*) designates a mode of Being of Dasein, never of intraworldly beings — the latter he calls "belonging to the world" (*weltzugehörig*) or "intraworldly" (*innerweltlich*).

The eighth paragraph observes that previous ontology's missing of the constitution of Being-in-the-world goes together with a skipping-over of the phenomenon of worldhood. Instead, the attempt is made to interpret the world from the Being of intraworldly beings — beings that are moreover not even initially discovered: from nature. Nature, understood ontologically-categorially, is a *limiting case* of the Being of possible intraworldly beings. Dasein can discover beings as nature only in a determinate mode of its Being-in-the-world — a mode with the character of a determinate *de-worlding* (*Entweltlichung*) of the world. "Nature" as the categorial epitome of structures of Being of a determinate intraworldly being can never make worldhood intelligible. Even the phenomenon "nature" in the Romantic sense is ontologically graspable only from the concept of world, i.e., from the analytic of Dasein.

The ninth paragraph states that traditional ontology, with regard to the problem of an ontological analysis of the worldhood of the world, is in a dead end. An interpretation of the worldhood of Dasein will have to show *why* Dasein, in the mode of Being of world-cognition, skips over the phenomenon of worldhood ontically and ontologically.

The tenth paragraph provides the methodological directive. Being-in-the-world and thus also the world are to become the theme of the analytic within the horizon of average everydayness as the nearest mode of Being of Dasein. The nearest world of everyday Dasein is the *surrounding world* (*Umwelt*). The investigation proceeds from this existential character of average Being-in-the-world to the idea of worldhood in general. We seek the worldhood of the surrounding world (environing worldhood, *Umweltlichkeit*) by way of an ontological interpretation of the nearest intraworldly beings encountered in the surrounding world. The expression "surrounding world" contains in its "surrounding" (*Um*) a reference to spatiality. But the "around" that is constitutive for the surrounding world does not have a primarily "spatial" meaning. The spatial character that undeniably belongs to a surrounding world is rather to be clarified first from the structure of worldhood. The analysis will proceed in three stages: A. Analysis of environing worldhood and worldhood in general. B. Contrasting the analysis of worldhood against Descartes' ontology of the "world." C. The aroundness of the surrounding world and the "spatiality" of Dasein.

#### A. The Analysis of Environing Worldhood and Worldhood in General

### § 15. The Being of Beings Encountered in the Surrounding World

The first paragraph states that the phenomenological exhibition of the Being of the nearest-encountered being is accomplished along the guiding thread of everyday Being-in-the-world, which we also call dealings in and with the intraworldly beings. Dealings have already dispersed themselves into a manifold of ways of concern. The nearest kind of dealing, as was shown, is not merely apprehending cognition but rather handling, using concern, which has its own "cognition." The phenomenological question initially concerns the Being of the being encountered in such concern. A methodological preparatory remark is needed to secure the required seeing.

The second paragraph makes the methodological point. In the disclosing and explicating of Being, beings are the pre- and co-thematic, while Being proper is the genuine theme. In the present analysis, the pre-thematic being is what shows itself in environmental concern. This being is not the object of a theoretical "world"-cognizing; it is the used, the produced, and the like. As so-encountered being, it comes pre-thematically into the view of a "cognizing" that as phenomenological primarily looks at Being and from this thematization of Being co-thematizes the respective being. This phenomenological interpreting is therefore not a cognizing of existing characteristics of beings but a determining of the structure of their Being. The phenomenologically pre-thematic being — the used, the being-in-production — becomes accessible through transposing oneself into such concern. Strictly speaking, this talk of "transposing oneself" is misleading, since we do not need to first transpose ourselves into this mode of Being of concerned dealing. Everyday Dasein is already always in this mode — for example, opening the door, I make use of the handle. Gaining phenomenological access to the so-encountered being consists rather in pushing back the intrusive and co-running interpretive tendencies that conceal the phenomenon of such "concern" and thereby all the more conceal the being as it encounters us from itself in concern.

The third paragraph asks which being is to become the pre-theme and be established as the pre-phenomenal ground. One answers: things. But with this self-evident answer, the sought pre-phenomenal ground may already be missed. For in this addressing of beings as "things" (*res*) lies an unexpressed, anticipatory ontological characterization. The analysis that inquires further from such beings toward Being hits upon thinghood and reality, then progressively finds characters of Being like substantiality, materiality, extendedness, alongside-one-another. But the being encountered in concern is in this Being also pre-ontologically initially *concealed*. In naming things as the "primarily given" being, one goes ontologically astray, even though one ontically means something different. What one actually means remains undetermined. Or one characterizes these "things" as "value-laden" things. But what does "value" mean ontologically? How is this "laden-ness" to be categorially grasped? Leaving aside the obscurity of this structure, does it capture the phenomenal character of Being of what is encountered in concerned dealing?

The fourth paragraph introduces *equipment* (*Zeug*). The Greeks had an appropriate term for "things": *pragmata* — that with which one has to do in concerned dealing (*praxis*). But they left the specifically "pragmatic" character of the *pragmata* ontologically in the dark and determined them "initially" as "mere things." We call the being encountered in concern *equipment* (*Zeug*). In dealing we find writing equipment, sewing equipment, tools, vehicles, measuring equipment. The mode of Being of equipment is to be brought out. This happens along the guiding thread of the prior delimitation of what makes equipment equipment: equipmentality (*Zeughaftigkeit*).

The fifth paragraph states a fundamental point: a single piece of equipment, strictly taken, never "is." To the Being of equipment there always already belongs an equipmental *totality* (*Zeugganzes*), in which it can be the equipment that it is. Equipment is essentially "something in order to..." (*etwas, um zu...*). The various modes of the "in-order-to" — serviceability, conduciveness, usability, handiness — constitute an equipmental totality. In the structure of the "in-order-to" lies a *reference* (*Verweisung*) of something to something. Equipment, in accordance with its equipmentality, always has its being from belonging to other equipment: writing equipment — pen, ink, paper, blotting pad, table, lamp, furniture, windows, doors, room. These "things" never show themselves initially by themselves, to then fill up a room as a sum of real things. What is nearest-encountered, though not thematically grasped, is the *room* — and this not as the "space between four walls" in a geometrical-spatial sense, but as *equipment for dwelling*. From it the "furnishing" shows itself, and in this the respective "individual" piece of equipment. Before this, an equipmental totality has always already been discovered. This passage is one of the most famous in *Being and Time*: the analysis of the equipmental totality as the primordial way beings show themselves in everyday life.

The sixth paragraph makes the crucial claim about *readiness-to-hand* (*Zuhandenheit*). The dealing cut to the measure of equipment — in which alone it can genuinely show itself in its Being — for example, hammering with the hammer — neither thematically grasps this being as an occurring thing, nor does the using know about the equipment-structure as such. Hammering does not merely still have knowledge of the tool-character of the hammer; it has appropriated this equipment in a way more appropriate than any other. In such using dealing, concern subordinates itself to the for-which constitutive of the respective equipment; the less the hammer-thing is merely gaped at, the more grippingly it is used, the more primordial the relation to it becomes, the more unveiled it is encountered as what it is — as equipment. Hammering itself discovers the specific "handiness" of the hammer. The mode of Being of equipment in which it manifests itself from itself we call *readiness-to-hand* (*Zuhandenheit*). Only because equipment has this "Being-in-itself" and does not merely occur is it handy in the broadest sense and available. The sharpest mere-looking at the "look" of things so-and-so constituted cannot discover anything ready-to-hand. The merely "theoretically" looking gaze at things lacks the understanding of readiness-to-hand. But using, handling dealing is not blind; it has its own kind of sight that guides the handling and gives it its specific "thingly" character. Dealing with equipment subordinates itself to the referential manifold of the "in-order-to." The sight of such fitting-in is *circumspection* (*Umsicht*). "Practical" comportment is not "atheoretical" in the sense of sightlessness, and its difference from theoretical comportment lies not merely in that one contemplates and the other acts; rather, contemplation is just as primordially a concern as action has its sight. Theoretical comportment is simply non-circumspect looking-at. This is one of the most consequential analyses in the entire work: the primacy of *Zuhandenheit* (readiness-to-hand) over *Vorhandenheit* (presence-at-hand). The practical, involved encounter with equipment is more primordial than detached theoretical observation.

The seventh paragraph states that the ready-to-hand is not at all first grasped theoretically, nor is it initially circumspectly thematic even for circumspection. The peculiarity of the proximally ready-to-hand is to withdraw, as it were, in its readiness-to-hand in order to be properly ready-to-hand. What everyday dealing initially lingers with are not the tools themselves but the *work* — what is in each case to be produced — is the primary object of concern and therefore also ready-to-hand. The work carries the referential totality within which the equipment is encountered.

The eighth paragraph traces the referential connections further. The work as the for-which of hammer, plane, and needle has for its part the mode of Being of equipment. The shoe to be produced is for wearing (shoe-equipment); the manufactured clock is for reading time. The work primarily encountered in concerned dealing — what is in production — always already co-encounters the for-which of its usability. The commissioned work is in its turn possible only on the ground of its use and the referential connection of beings discovered in use.

The ninth paragraph identifies further references embedded in the work. The work to be produced is not merely usable for something; producing itself is in each case a using of something for something. In the work lies at the same time the reference to "materials." It is dependent on leather, thread, nails, etc. Leather is in turn produced from hides, taken from animals that are bred by others. Animals also occur within the world without breeding, and even in breeding this being in a certain way produces itself. In the surrounding world, beings that are in themselves in no need of production — already always ready-to-hand — also become accessible. Hammer, tongs, and nails in themselves refer to — consist of — steel, iron, ore, rock, wood. In the used equipment, "nature" is co-discovered through use — "nature" in the light of natural products. But nature must not here be understood as the merely present-at-hand, nor as natural power. The forest is timber, the mountain a quarry, the river water-power, the wind is wind "in the sails." With the discovered "surrounding world," the so-discovered "nature" is encountered. Its mode of Being as ready-to-hand can be disregarded, and it can be discovered and determined purely in its presence-at-hand. But to this nature-discovery, nature also remains concealed as that which "weaves and strives," overwhelms us, captivates us as landscape. The botanist's plants are not the flowers on the hedgerow; the geographically fixed "source" of a river is not the "spring in the ground."

The tenth paragraph identifies the final direction of reference. The produced work refers not only to the for-which of its usability and the from-which of its material; in simple craft conditions it also contains the reference to the wearer and user. The work is cut to fit them; they "are" present in the arising of the work. In the production of mass goods this constitutive reference is by no means absent; it is merely indeterminate, pointing to anyone, to the average. With the work, then, not only beings that are ready-to-hand are encountered, but also beings with the mode of Being of Dasein — those for whom the product is ready-to-hand in their concern.


### § 16. The Worldly Character of the Surrounding World as it Announces Itself in Intraworldly Beings

The first paragraph states the fundamental puzzle: world is not itself an intraworldly being, and yet it determines intraworldly beings so thoroughly that they can encounter us and discovered beings can show themselves in their Being only insofar as there "is" world. But how "is there" world? If Dasein is ontically constituted through Being-in-the-world, and if an understanding of Being — however indeterminate — essentially belongs to its Being, then does it not have an understanding of world, a pre-ontological understanding that can and may lack explicit ontological insight? Does something like world show itself for the concerned Being-in-the-world along with the intraworldly beings encountered — along with their intraworldliness? Does this phenomenon not come into a pre-phenomenological view? Does Dasein itself, within the sphere of its concerned absorption in ready-to-hand equipment, have a possibility of Being in which the worldhood of the concerned intraworldly being in a certain way *lights up* for it?

The second paragraph answers affirmatively: if such possibilities of Being of Dasein can be exhibited within concerned dealing, then a path opens to pursue this lighting-up phenomenon and to try to "corner" it and interrogate it regarding the structures showing themselves in it.

The third paragraph identifies the relevant phenomena. Modes of concern belong to everyday Being-in-the-world that let the concerned being be encountered in such a way that the *worldly character* (*Weltmäßigkeit*) of the intraworldly comes to the fore. The nearest ready-to-hand being can be encountered in concern as *unusable* — not prepared for its determinate use. A tool turns out to be damaged; the material proves unsuitable. Equipment is in each case still ready-to-hand. But what discovers the unusability is not a looking-at that ascertains properties but the *circumspection* of using concern. In such discovering of unusability, equipment becomes *conspicuous* (*auffällig*). Conspicuousness gives the ready-to-hand equipment in a certain un-readiness-to-hand. In this lies the following: the unusable merely lies there — it shows itself as an equipment-thing that looks thus-and-so and was, in its readiness-to-hand, constantly also present-at-hand as so looking. Pure presence-at-hand announces itself in the equipment, only to withdraw again into the readiness-to-hand of the concerned — i.e., what is being repaired. This presence-at-hand of the unusable does not yet altogether lack all readiness-to-hand; the so-present-at-hand equipment is not yet just some randomly occurring thing.

The fourth paragraph identifies a second mode of disturbance. Concerned dealing encounters not only the unusable within what is already ready-to-hand; it also finds what is *missing* — what is not only not "handy" but altogether not "to hand." Such a missing discovers the ready-to-hand, in finding the un-ready-to-hand, in a certain mere-presence-at-hand. The ready-to-hand comes, in the noticing of un-readiness-to-hand, into the mode of *obtrusiveness* (*Aufdringlichkeit*). The more urgently the missing thing is needed, the more authentically it is encountered in its un-readiness-to-hand, the more obtrusive the ready-to-hand becomes — to the point that it seems to lose the character of readiness-to-hand. It reveals itself as merely present-at-hand, something that cannot be moved along without the missing piece. The helpless standing-before discovers, as a deficient mode of concern, the mere-presence-at-hand of the ready-to-hand.

The fifth paragraph introduces a third mode. In dealing with the concerned world, un-ready-to-hand beings can be encountered not only in the sense of the unusable or the altogether missing, but as un-ready-to-hand things that precisely do not fail and are not unusable but that "get in the way" of concern. What concern cannot turn to, what it has "no time" for, is un-ready-to-hand in the mode of not-belonging, of unfinished business. This un-ready-to-hand *disturbs* and makes visible the *obstinacy* (*Aufsässigkeit*) of what must first and foremost be concerned with. With this obstinacy, the presence-at-hand of the ready-to-hand announces itself in yet another way.

The sixth paragraph draws the crucial conclusion about the function of these three modes. The modes of conspicuousness, obtrusiveness, and obstinacy have the function of bringing to the fore the character of presence-at-hand in the ready-to-hand. But in doing so, the ready-to-hand is not yet merely contemplated and stared at as present-at-hand; the presence-at-hand that announces itself is still bound up in the readiness-to-hand of the equipment. Equipment does not yet disguise itself as mere things. In such a tendency to push away, however, the ready-to-hand shows itself as still ready-to-hand in its persistent presence-at-hand.

The seventh paragraph asks what this analysis yields for the clarification of the world-phenomenon. Even with this analysis, we still stand at the Being of the intraworldly; we have not come nearer to the world-phenomenon. But we have now brought ourselves into the possibility of getting the phenomenon into view. In conspicuousness, obtrusiveness, and obstinacy, the ready-to-hand in a certain way *loses* its readiness-to-hand. But readiness-to-hand is itself understood in dealing with the ready-to-hand, though unthematically. It does not simply disappear; rather, in the conspicuousness of the unusable it takes its leave, as it were. Readiness-to-hand shows itself once more — and precisely here the *worldly character* of the ready-to-hand also shows itself.

The eighth paragraph explains how. The structure of the Being of the ready-to-hand as equipment is determined by references. The peculiar and self-evident "in-itself" of the nearest "things" is encountered in using concern that does not explicitly attend to them. When concern encounters the unusable, a constitutive reference — the in-order-to toward a for-which — is *disturbed*. The references themselves are not contemplated but "there" in the concerned fitting-in under them. In a disturbance of the reference — in unusability for something — the reference becomes explicit. Not yet as an ontological structure, but ontically for the circumspection that bumps into the damage. With this circumspect awakening of the reference to the respective for-which, this for-which itself comes into sight, and with it the work-connection — the whole "workshop" — as that in which concern always already lingers. The equipment-connection lights up not as something never before seen but as a whole already constantly sighted in advance in circumspection. *With this whole, the world announces itself.* This is one of the most important claims of the chapter: the world becomes phenomenally visible precisely when the smooth functioning of equipment breaks down, because the breakdown makes visible the referential totality that was always already there but unnoticed.

The ninth paragraph generalizes. Likewise, the absence of something ready-to-hand, whose everyday being-at-hand was so self-evident that we did not even take notice of it, is a breach of the referential connections discovered in circumspection. Circumspection hits the void and only now sees for-what and with-what the missing thing was ready-to-hand. Again the surrounding world announces itself. What thus lights up is itself neither a ready-to-hand entity among others nor a present-at-hand entity that perhaps founds the ready-to-hand equipment. It is in the "there" prior to all ascertaining and contemplating. It is itself inaccessible to circumspection insofar as circumspection always bears upon beings, but it is for circumspection always already *disclosed*.

The tenth paragraph notes the correlation between the lighting-up of the world and the de-worlding of the ready-to-hand. That the world does not "consist of" the ready-to-hand is shown by the fact that with the lighting-up of the world in the interpreted modes of concern, a de-worlding (*Entweltlichung*) of the ready-to-hand occurs, so that mere-presence-at-hand comes to the fore. For the ready-to-hand equipment to be encountered in its "in-itself-being" in everyday concern with the "surrounding world," the references and referential totalities in which circumspection "is absorbed" must remain unthematic both for circumspection and even more for a non-circumspect, "thematic" grasping. The *non-announcing* of the world is the condition of the possibility of the non-emerging of the ready-to-hand from its inconspicuousness. And therein the phenomenal structure of the in-itself-being of this being is constituted.

The eleventh and final paragraph states that the privative expressions — inconspicuousness, unobtrusiveness, non-obstinacy — designate a positive phenomenal character of the Being of the proximally ready-to-hand. These "un-"s designate the character of holding-itself-in of the ready-to-hand: what we have in view with the "in-itself" that we characteristically ascribe "initially" to the present-at-hand as what can be thematically ascertained. In the primary and exclusive orientation toward the present-at-hand, the "in-itself" cannot be ontologically clarified at all. The in-itself-being of intraworldly beings is ontologically graspable only on the ground of the world-phenomenon.

### § 17. Reference and Sign

The first paragraph recalls that in the preliminary interpretation of the structure of Being of the ready-to-hand (equipment), the phenomenon of *reference* (*Verweisung*) became visible, though only so roughly that the necessity of uncovering this phenomenon with respect to its ontological provenance was simultaneously emphasized. It also became clear that reference and referential totality would in some sense be constitutive for worldhood itself.

The second paragraph takes the departure again from the Being of the ready-to-hand, now with the aim of grasping the phenomenon of reference itself more sharply. For this purpose, an ontological analysis of such equipment is attempted in which "references" can be found in a manifold sense. Such "equipment" is found in *signs* (*Zeichen*). The word names many things — not only various kinds of signs, but the being-a-sign-for... can itself be formalized into a universal kind of relation, so that the sign-structure itself furnishes an ontological guiding thread for a "characterization" of all beings whatsoever.

The third paragraph states that signs are themselves equipment whose specific equipment-character consists in *showing* (*Zeigen*). Such signs include road markers, boundary stones, the storm-ball for shipping, signals, flags, signs of mourning, and the like. Showing can be determined as a "kind" of referring. Referring, taken in an extremely formal way, is a relating (*Beziehen*). But relation does not function as the genus for "species" of references that might differentiate themselves into signs, symbols, expressions, meanings. Relation is a formal determination directly readable, by way of "formalization," from any kind of connections of whatever material content and mode of Being. Every reference is a relation, but not every relation is a reference. Every "showing" is a reference, but not every referring is a showing. For the investigation of the phenomena of reference, sign, or even meaning, nothing is gained by a characterization as "relation."

The fourth paragraph takes the automobile turn-signal as an example. A red, rotating arrow is attached to automobiles, whose position shows, for example at a street intersection, which way the car will go. Its position is regulated by the driver. This sign is equipment ready-to-hand not only in the driver's concern (steering). Non-passengers too — and precisely they — make use of this equipment, in the mode of getting out of the way or standing still. This sign is intraworldly ready-to-hand in the whole of the equipmental connection of traffic means and traffic regulations. As equipment, this showing-equipment is constituted through reference. It has the character of the in-order-to, its determinate serviceability: it is for showing. This showing of the sign can be grasped as "referring." But note: this "referring" as showing is not the ontological structure of the sign as equipment. The "referring" as showing is rather grounded in the structure of Being of equipment — in serviceability. Serviceability does not make something a sign. The hammer too is constituted through serviceability, but this does not make it a sign. The "reference" as showing is the ontic concretion of the for-which of serviceability and determines equipment to be this particular equipment. The reference "serviceability for" is by contrast an ontological-categorial determinateness of equipment as equipment.

The fifth paragraph asks what the showing of a sign means. The answer can only be gained by determining the appropriate mode of dealing with showing-equipment. In orientation to the example (the arrow): the corresponding comportment to the encountered sign is "getting out of the way" or "standing still" vis-à-vis the oncoming car bearing the arrow. Getting out of the way belongs, as striking out in a direction, essentially to the Being-in-the-world of Dasein. Dasein is always somehow oriented and underway; standing and remaining are only limit-cases of this oriented "being-underway." The sign addresses itself to a specifically "spatial" Being-in-the-world. The sign is precisely *not* genuinely "grasped" when we stare at it, ascertaining it as an occurring showing-thing. Even if we follow the pointing direction of the arrow with our gaze and look at something present-at-hand in the region toward which the arrow points, even then the sign is not genuinely encountered. It addresses itself to the circumspection of concerned dealing, such that circumspection, following its directive, brings the respective aroundness of the surrounding world into an explicit "survey" (*Übersicht*). The circumspective surveying does not grasp the ready-to-hand; it rather gains an *orientation* within the surrounding world. Signs of the described kind let ready-to-hand beings be encountered — more precisely, they let a connection of such beings become accessible in such a way that concerned dealing gives itself and secures an orientation. A sign is not a thing that stands in a showing-relation to another thing but rather an equipment that expressly raises an equipmental totality into circumspection, so that in one stroke the worldly character of the ready-to-hand announces itself.

The sixth paragraph discusses the "establishing" (*Stiftung*) of signs. It takes place in and out of a circumspect foresight that needs the ready-to-hand possibility of at any time having the respective surrounding world announce itself for circumspection through a ready-to-hand entity. But to the Being of the proximally ready-to-hand belongs the described character of self-holding, non-emerging. Therefore circumspect dealing needs ready-to-hand equipment that takes on the "work" of letting the ready-to-hand become conspicuous. Hence the production of such equipment (signs) must be planned with a view to their conspicuousness. But one also places such conspicuous signs in a determinate way with a view to easy accessibility.

The seventh paragraph discusses a more primordial mode of sign-establishing. Signs also arise through the *taking-as-a-sign* of something already ready-to-hand. In this mode, sign-establishing reveals a still more primordial meaning. Showing does not merely procure the circumspectly oriented availability of a ready-to-hand equipmental totality and the surrounding world in general; sign-establishing can even *first discover*. What is taken as a sign is first made accessible through its readiness-to-hand. For example, in agriculture, the south wind "counts" as a sign of rain — but this "counting" or "value" is not an addition to something already present-at-hand, an air current of a particular geographical direction. As this mere occurrence, which may be meteorologically accessible, the south wind is never initially present-at-hand to then occasionally assume the function of a forecast. Rather, the circumspection of agriculture first discovers the south wind in its Being, in the mode of reckoning with it.

The eighth paragraph addresses the objection that what is taken as a sign must have been previously accessible. Certainly, it must have been in some way encountered. The question remains only how in this prior encountering the being is discovered — as a pure occurring thing, or not rather as ununderstood equipment, as something ready-to-hand with which one "didn't know what to do," which accordingly still concealed itself from circumspection.

The ninth paragraph discusses signs in primitive Dasein. For the primitive, the sign coincides with what is shown. The sign itself can represent the shown — not only as a substitute, but in such a way that the sign *is* always the shown. This remarkable coincidence of sign and shown does not lie in the fact that the sign-thing has already undergone a certain "objectification," experienced as a pure thing and placed with the shown into the same Being-region of the present-at-hand. The "coincidence" is not an identification of what was previously isolated but a *not-yet-becoming-free* of the sign from the signified. Such sign-use still entirely dissolves into Being toward the shown, so that a sign as such cannot yet detach itself at all. The coincidence is grounded not in a first objectification but in the *complete absence* of one. This means that signs are not at all discovered as equipment, and perhaps the intraworldly "ready-to-hand" does not have the mode of Being of equipment at all in primitive existence. Perhaps even the ontological guiding thread of readiness-to-hand and equipment achieves nothing for interpreting the primitive world.

The tenth paragraph summarizes the threefold relation between sign and reference: (1) Showing, as a possible concretion of the for-which of serviceability, is founded in the equipment-structure as such, in the in-order-to (reference). (2) The showing of the sign, as equipment-character of a ready-to-hand entity, belongs to an equipmental totality, to a referential connection. (3) The sign is not only ready-to-hand with other equipment; in its readiness-to-hand, the surrounding world becomes in each case explicitly accessible for circumspection. A sign is an ontically ready-to-hand entity that, as this determinate equipment, simultaneously functions as something that indicates the ontological structure of readiness-to-hand, referential totality, and worldhood. Therein lies the privilege of this ready-to-hand entity within the circumspectly concerned surrounding world. Reference itself, therefore — if it is to be the ontological foundation for signs — cannot itself be grasped as a sign. Reference is not the ontic determinateness of a ready-to-hand entity, since it constitutes readiness-to-hand itself.

### § 18. Involvement and Significance; the Worldhood of the World

The first paragraph opens with the guiding question. Ready-to-hand beings are encountered intraworldly. The Being of this being — readiness-to-hand — therefore stands in some ontological relation to world and worldhood. World is always already "there" in all the ready-to-hand. It is prior to all encountering, already, though unthematically, discovered. How can world let the ready-to-hand be encountered? The foregoing analysis showed that intraworldly beings are *released* for the concerned circumspection in their Being. What does this prior releasing mean, and how is it to be understood as an ontological distinction of the world?

The second paragraph introduces the concept of *involvement* (*Bewandtnis*). The equipment-constitution of the ready-to-hand was indicated as reference. The Being of the ready-to-hand has the structure of reference — it has in itself the character of *being-referred*. Beings are discovered as being, as the beings they are, referred to something. With them, something has its involvement at something. The character of Being of the ready-to-hand is involvement. In involvement lies: letting something be involved with something at something. The relation of the "with... at..." is indicated by the term reference.

The third paragraph explains involvement further. Involvement is the Being of intraworldly beings, to which they are in each case proximally released. As beings, they in each case *have* an involvement. That they have an involvement is the ontological determination of the Being of this being, not an ontic assertion about the being. The *at-which* (*Wobei*) of involvement is the for-which of serviceability, the for-which of usability. With the for-which of serviceability, there can in turn be its involvement: for example, with this ready-to-hand entity that we therefore call a hammer, its involvement is *at* hammering; with hammering, its involvement is at fastening; with fastening, at protection against storm; this "is" *for the sake of* sheltering Dasein — that is, for the sake of a possibility of its Being. What involvement a ready-to-hand entity has is in each case prescribed from the *totality of involvements* (*Bewandtnisganzheit*). The totality of involvements that constitutes, for example, the ready-to-hand in a workshop in its readiness-to-hand is "earlier" than the individual piece of equipment, likewise that of a farmstead with all its equipment and properties. The totality of involvements itself goes back ultimately to a for-which at which there is no further involvement — a being that is not a being with the mode of Being of the ready-to-hand within a world, but rather a being whose Being is determined as Being-in-the-world and to whose constitution of Being worldhood itself belongs. This primary for-which is not a for-this as a possible at-which of an involvement. The primary "for-which" is a *for-the-sake-of-which* (*Worum-willen*). The "for-the-sake-of" always concerns the Being of Dasein, for which in its Being this Being itself is essentially at issue. This is a key structural point: all chains of involvement (equipment → work → purpose → further purpose) ultimately terminate in the Being of Dasein — in a for-the-sake-of-which. Dasein is the terminal point of all referential chains, the being for whose sake the entire equipmental world exists.

The fourth paragraph clarifies "letting-be-involved" (*Bewendenlassen*) ontologically. Ontically, it means: within factical concern, letting a ready-to-hand entity be so-and-so as it now is, and so that it is so. We grasp this ontic sense fundamentally ontologically. We thereby interpret the meaning of the prior releasing of the proximally intraworldly ready-to-hand. Prior "letting-be" does not mean first bringing something into its Being and producing it; it means in each case discovering "beings" in their readiness-to-hand and thus letting them be encountered as the beings of this Being. This "a priori" letting-be-involved is the condition of the possibility for ready-to-hand entities to be encountered, so that Dasein, in its ontic dealing with so-encountered beings, can let them be in the ontic sense.

The fifth paragraph identifies letting-be-involved as an a priori perfect characterizing the mode of Being of Dasein itself. The ontologically understood letting-be-involved is the prior releasing of beings to their intra-environmental readiness-to-hand. From the at-which of the letting-be-involved, the with-which of the involvement is released. For concern, it encounters as this ready-to-hand entity. Insofar as a being shows itself to concern at all — insofar as it is discovered in its Being — it is always already environmentally ready-to-hand and precisely not "initially" merely present-at-hand "world-stuff."

The sixth paragraph elevates the analysis to the level of worldhood. Involvement itself, as the Being of the ready-to-hand, is always discovered only on the ground of the *pre-discovery* of a totality of involvements. In discovered involvement — in the encountered ready-to-hand — there lies pre-discovered what we called the worldly character of the ready-to-hand. This pre-discovered totality of involvements harbors within itself an ontological relation to world. The letting-be-involved that releases beings toward a totality of involvements must itself have already somehow disclosed *that toward which* it releases. This toward-which — toward which environmentally ready-to-hand beings are released so that they first become accessible as intraworldly beings — cannot itself be grasped as a being of this discovered mode of Being. It is essentially not discoverable, if we henceforth retain "discoveredness" as a term for a possibility of Being of all non-Dasein-like beings. What must be prior to all discovering of beings is *disclosed* (*erschlossen*) — and this disclosing belongs to Dasein.

The seventh paragraph connects this to Dasein's understanding of Being. If Being-in-the-world essentially belongs to Dasein, then the understanding of Being-in-the-world belongs to the essential content of its understanding of Being. The prior disclosing of that toward which the releasing of intraworldly encountered beings occurs is nothing other than the *understanding of world*, toward which Dasein as a being always already comports itself. The prior letting-be-involved with... at... is grounded in an understanding of such things as letting-be-involved, at-which, with-which. All of this must be in a certain intelligibility prior to any encountering. That in which Dasein pre-ontologically understands itself as Being-in-the-world is the *wherein of self-referential understanding as the toward-which of letting-beings-be-encountered in the mode of involvement* — and this is the phenomenon of world. The structure of that toward which Dasein refers itself is what constitutes the *worldhood of the world*.

The eighth paragraph introduces *significance* (*Bedeutsamkeit*). That with which Dasein is in each case already familiar is the wherein of self-referral. This familiarity with the world does not necessarily require a theoretical transparency of the relations constituting world as world. But the possibility of an explicit ontological-existential interpretation of these relations is grounded in the world-familiarity constitutive of Dasein, which for its part co-constitutes Dasein's understanding of Being. The understanding holds the indicated relations in a prior disclosedness. In the familiar maintaining-itself-therein, it holds these before itself as that in which its referring moves. Understanding lets itself be referred in and from these relations. The relational character of these relations of referring we grasp as *signifying* (*bedeuten*). In familiarity with these relations, Dasein "signifies" to itself; it originally gives itself its Being and its ability-to-be to understand with respect to its Being-in-the-world. The for-the-sake-of-which signifies an in-order-to; this signifies a for-this; this signifies an at-which of letting-be-involved; this signifies a with-which of involvement. These relations are among themselves clamped together as a primordial totality; they are what they are as this *signifying*, in which Dasein gives itself its Being-in-the-world to understand in advance. The relational totality of this signifying we call *significance* (*Bedeutsamkeit*). It is what constitutes the structure of world — of that wherein Dasein as such already always is. Dasein, in its familiarity with significance, is the ontic condition of the possibility of the discoverability of beings that are encountered in a world in the mode of Being of involvement (readiness-to-hand) and can thus announce themselves in their in-itself.

The ninth paragraph connects significance to language. Significance harbors in itself the ontological condition of the possibility for understanding Dasein, as interpreting, to be able to disclose something like "meanings" (*Bedeutungen*), which in turn ground the possible Being of word and language.

The tenth paragraph addresses the objection that the analysis dissolves the "substantial Being" of intraworldly beings into a system of relations. Within the present field of investigation, the repeatedly marked differences of structures and dimensions of ontological problematic must be fundamentally kept apart: (1) the Being of the proximally encountered intraworldly being (readiness-to-hand); (2) the Being of beings (presence-at-hand) that becomes discoverable and determinable through an independent passage through the proximally encountered beings; (3) the Being of the ontic condition of the possibility of the discoverability of intraworldly beings in general — the worldhood of world. The last-named is an existential determination of Being-in-the-world, i.e., of Dasein. The first two are categories concerning beings of a non-Dasein-like mode of Being. The referential connection that as significance constitutes worldhood can be formally grasped as a system of relations. But such formalizations level the phenomena to such an extent that genuine phenomenal content is lost. These "relations" of the in-order-to, for-the-sake-of, and with-which of an involvement resist in their phenomenal content any mathematical functionalization; they are also not something thought, something first posited in "thinking," but rather relations in which concerned circumspection as such always already dwells. This "system of relations" as constitutive of worldhood so little dissipates the Being of the intraworldly ready-to-hand that on the ground of the worldhood of the world, this being is first discoverable in its "substantial" "in-itself." Only when intraworldly beings can be encountered at all does the possibility exist of making accessible, within the field of these beings, the merely present-at-hand. Function-concepts of the mathematical sort are ontologically possible only with respect to beings whose Being has the character of pure substantiality. Function-concepts are always only possible as formalized substance-concepts.


#### B. The Contrasting of the Analysis of Worldhood against Descartes' Interpretation of the World

The brief introductory paragraph to subsection B states that the investigation can only gradually secure the concept of worldhood and the structures enclosed in this phenomenon. Because the interpretation of the world initially sets out from intraworldly beings and then fails to get the phenomenon of world into view at all, Heidegger proposes to ontologically clarify this approach in what is perhaps its most extreme execution. He will not only briefly present the basic features of Descartes' ontology of the "world" but will ask about its presuppositions and attempt to characterize them in the light of what has been gained so far. Descartes sees the fundamental ontological determination of the world in *extensio*. Since extension co-constitutes spatiality (and for Descartes is identical with it), and spatiality remains in some sense constitutive for the world, the discussion of the Cartesian ontology of the "world" simultaneously provides a negative reference-point for the positive explication of the spatiality of the surrounding world and of Dasein itself. Three topics will be treated: (1) the determination of the "world" as *res extensa* (§19); (2) the foundations of this ontological determination (§20); (3) the hermeneutical discussion of the Cartesian ontology of the "world" (§21).

### § 19. The Determination of the "World" as Res Extensa

The first paragraph states that Descartes distinguishes the *ego cogito* from the *res corporea*. This distinction henceforth ontologically determines that of "nature and spirit." However much this opposition may be ontically fixed in content-variations, the unclarity of its ontological foundations and of the opposing members themselves has its nearest root in the distinction carried out by Descartes. Within which understanding of Being did he determine the Being of these beings? The title for the Being of a being that is in itself is *substantia*. The expression means sometimes the Being of a being that is a substance — substantiality — and sometimes the being itself — a substance. This ambiguity of *substantia*, already carried by the ancient concept of *ousia*, is not accidental.

The second paragraph takes up the ontological determination of *res corporea*. It requires the explication of substance — the substantiality of this being as a substance. What constitutes the authentic in-itself-being of the *res corporea*? How is a substance as such — that is, its substantiality — graspable at all? Heidegger quotes Descartes (*Principia* I.53): a substance is cognized through any of its attributes, but each substance has one principal property constituting its nature and essence, to which all others are referred. Substances become accessible in their "attributes," and each has a distinguished property from which the essence of the substantiality of a given substance is readable. Which property is this for the *res corporea*? Extension in length, breadth, and depth constitutes the nature of corporeal substance — what we call "world." What gives *extensio* this distinction? Because everything else attributable to a body presupposes extension. Extension is the constitution of Being of the being in question that must already "be" before all other determinations of Being so that these can "be" what they are. Extension must be primarily "assigned" to the bodily thing.

The third paragraph traces the proof. The demonstration that extension characterizes the substantiality of the "world" proceeds by showing that all other determinations of this substance — principally *divisio*, *figura*, *motus* — can be grasped only as *modi* of *extensio*, while conversely *extensio* remains understandable *sine figura vel motu*. A body can, while retaining its total extension, change the distribution of that extension across various dimensions and present itself in manifold shapes as one and the same thing. Heidegger quotes Descartes in Latin: one and the same body, retaining the same quantity, can be extended in many different ways — now more in length and less in breadth or depth, and shortly after more in breadth and less in length.

The fourth paragraph discusses how shape (*figura*) is a *modus* of *extensio*, as is motion. Motion is grasped only if we think of nothing but local motion and do not inquire about the force by which it is excited. If motion is a property of *res corporea*, then it must, to be experienceable in its Being, be grasped from the Being of this being itself — from *extensio* — that is, as pure change of place. Something like "force" contributes nothing to the determination of the Being of this being. Determinations like *durities* (hardness), *pondus* (weight), *color* can be removed from matter, and it still remains what it is. These determinations do not constitute its authentic Being, and insofar as they are, they prove to be *modi* of *extensio*.

The fifth paragraph gives Descartes' argument about hardness in detail, quoting the *Principia* II.4. Hardness is experienced in touching. What does touch "tell" us about hardness? The parts of the hard thing "resist" the movement of the hand — for instance, an attempt to push them away. If hard — i.e., unyielding — bodies were to change their location at the same speed at which the hand "approaching" them changes its location, then touching would never occur, hardness would never be experienced, and consequently would never exist. But it is in no way intelligible why bodies yielding at such speed should thereby lose anything of their corporeal being. Retaining their corporeal being even without hardness, it follows that hardness does not constitute the nature of body. In the same way — Descartes concludes — it can be shown that weight, color, and all other such qualities can be removed from corporeal matter or can be absent, while it remains in its full being (*ipsa integra remanente*). Hence the nature of corporeal substance depends on none of these qualities. This argument rests on a prior ontological decision: the Being of bodies is fully determined by extension alone, and all sensible qualities are modes of this extension or dispensable accidents.

The sixth and final paragraph of §19 concludes that what constitutes the Being of the *res corporea* is the *extensio*: the *omnimodo divisibile, figurabile et mobile* — what can change in every way of divisibility, shape, and motion — the *capax mutationum* — what persists (*remanet*) through all these changes. What in the bodily thing satisfies the demand for such constant remaining is the genuinely being thing in it — that which characterizes the substantiality of this substance.

### § 20. The Foundations of the Ontological Determination of the "World"

The first paragraph identifies the idea of Being underlying the ontological characterization of the *res extensa*: substantiality. Heidegger quotes Descartes' definition (*Principia* I.51): by substance we can understand nothing other than a being that so exists that it needs no other being in order to exist (*per substantiam nihil aliud intelligere possumus, quam rem quae ita existit, ut nulla alia re indigeat ad existendum*). The Being of a "substance" is characterized by needlessness (*Unbedürftigkeit*). What is absolutely needless of another being satisfies the idea of substance in the proper sense — this being is the *ens perfectissimum*. The substance that is in no way in need of any other is unique: God. "God" functions here as a purely ontological title when understood as *ens perfectissimum*. At the same time, what is "self-evidently" co-meant with the concept of God enables an ontological interpretation of the constitutive moment of substantiality — needlessness.

The second paragraph draws out the implication. All beings that are not God need the concurrence (*concursus*) of God for their existence — they need production in the broadest sense and preservation. Production into presence-at-hand and needlessness-of-production constitute the horizon within which "Being" is understood. Every being that is not God is *ens creatum*. Between the two beings there is an "infinite" difference of their Being, and yet we address the created as well as the creator as beings. We thus use "Being" in a breadth that embraces an "infinite" difference. We can therefore with a certain right also call created beings substances. This being is indeed in need of production and preservation relative to God, but within the region of created beings — the "world" in the sense of *ens creatum* — there are beings that, relative to creaturely producing and maintaining (for instance, human producing), are "needless of another being." Such substances are two: the *res cogitans* and the *res extensa*.

The third paragraph identifies the fundamental ontological problem. The Being of the substance whose principal *proprietas* is *extensio* is therefore ontologically determinable in principle when the meaning of Being "common" to the three substances — the one infinite and the two finite — is clarified. But Descartes himself observes that the name "substance" does not apply to God and to creatures univocally (*nomen substantiae non convenit Deo et illis univoce*). Here Descartes touches on a problem that occupied medieval ontology extensively: the question of how the meaning of Being signifies (*bedeutet*) the respective being that is addressed. In the statements "God is" and "the world is," we assert Being. But this word "is" cannot mean the respective being in the same sense (*univoce*), given the infinite difference of Being between them. If the signification of "is" were univocal, then the created would be meant as uncreated or the uncreated would be brought down to something created. But "Being" does not function as a merely homonymous name either; in both cases "Being" is understood. Scholasticism grasps the positive sense of the signification of "Being" as *analogous* signification, as distinct from univocal or merely homonymous signification. Various modes of analogy were fixed in connection with Aristotle, in whom the problem was prefigured. Descartes falls far behind scholasticism with respect to the ontological working-through of this problem — indeed, he evades the question. No distinct meaning of the name "substance" can be understood that would be common to God and creatures.

The fourth paragraph draws the decisive conclusion. This evasion means that Descartes leaves the meaning of Being enclosed in the idea of substantiality, as well as the character of the "universality" of this meaning, entirely undiscussed. Medieval ontology, to be sure, inquired as little as ancient ontology into what Being itself means. It is therefore not surprising that a question like the manner of signification of Being cannot advance so long as it is discussed on the ground of an unclarified meaning of Being that the signification "expresses." The meaning remained unclarified because it was held to be "self-evident." This is the fundamental diagnosis: the concept of substance, which does all the heavy ontological lifting in Descartes' system, is itself ontologically opaque.

The fifth paragraph notes that Descartes does not merely evade the ontological question of substantiality; he expressly emphasizes that substance as such — its substantiality — is inaccessible in advance and in itself. Heidegger quotes *Principia* I.52: substance cannot first be noticed merely from the fact that it is an existing thing, because this alone does not "affect" us (*nos non afficit*). "Being" itself does not affect us; therefore it cannot be apprehended. "Being is no real predicate," as Kant says — merely repeating Descartes' proposition. With this, the possibility of a pure problematic of Being is fundamentally renounced, and an alternative path is sought: Being is expressed through existing determinations of the respective being — attributes. But not through arbitrary attributes: through those that most purely satisfy the meaning of Being and substantiality that is nonetheless unspokenly presupposed. In the *substantia finita* as *res corporea*, the primarily necessary "assignment" is *extensio*. Substantiality is *ratione tantum* — not *realiter* — separable and findable like the substantial being itself.

The sixth and final paragraph of §20 summarizes. The ontological foundations of the determination of the "world" as *res extensa* have thus become clear: the idea of substantiality, not only unclarified in its meaning of Being but declared to be in principle unclarifiable, represented by way of the most distinguished substantial property of the respective substance. In the determination of substance through a substantial being lies also the ground of the ambiguity of the term *substantia*: what is intended is substantiality, but what is understood is an existing characteristic of a substance. Because the ontological is underwritten by the ontic, the expression *substantia* functions now in an ontological, now in an ontic, but mostly in a blurring ontic-ontological meaning. Behind this apparently minor ambiguity lurks the unmastered fundamental problem of Being.

### § 21. The Hermeneutical Discussion of the Cartesian Ontology of the "World"

The first paragraph raises the critical question: does this ontology of the "world" seek the phenomenon of world at all? If not, does it at least determine an intraworldly being far enough that its worldly character can be made visible? Both questions must be answered in the negative. The being that Descartes tries to grasp ontologically-fundamentally through *extensio* is rather one that is first discoverable only through a passage through a proximally ready-to-hand intraworldly being. But even granting this, and even if the ontological characterization of this particular intraworldly being (nature) — both the idea of substantiality and the meaning of the *existit* and *ad existendum* taken into its definition — leads into darkness, there still remains the possibility that through an ontology grounded in the radical separation of God, I, and "world," the ontological problem of the world might be posed and advanced in some sense. But if even this possibility does not exist, then the express proof must be furnished that Descartes does not merely give a wrong ontological determination of the world but that his interpretation and its foundations led to *skipping over* the phenomenon of world as well as the Being of the proximally ready-to-hand intraworldly being.

The second paragraph identifies the decisive question about access. In the critical examination of the Cartesian approach, we must ask: which mode of Being of Dasein is fixed as the adequate mode of access to the being whose Being as *extensio* Descartes equates with the Being of the "world"? The sole and genuine access to this being is cognition — *intellectio* — specifically, mathematical-physical cognition. Mathematical cognition counts as the mode of grasping beings that can at all times be certain of the secure possession of the Being of the beings grasped in it. What in its mode of Being is such that it satisfies the Being accessible in mathematical cognition is in the proper sense. This being is what always is what it is; therefore what constitutes the authentic Being of the experienced being of the world is that of which it can be shown to have the character of constant remaining — as *remanens capax mutationum*. What is genuinely is the permanently persisting. Such is known by mathematics. What is accessible through mathematics about a being constitutes its Being. Thus, from a determinate idea of Being enclosed in the concept of substantiality, and from the idea of a cognition that cognizes beings so constituted, the "world" has its Being, as it were, dictated to it. Descartes does not let the Being of intraworldly beings be given to him by these beings themselves; rather, on the ground of an idea of Being whose origin is unexposed and whose justification is undemonstrated (Being = constant presence-at-hand), he prescribes to the world its "authentic" Being. It is therefore not primarily the reliance on a particular, highly esteemed science — mathematics — that determines the ontology of the world, but the fundamental ontological orientation toward Being as constant presence-at-hand, the grasping of which mathematical cognition preeminently satisfies. Descartes thus carries out philosophically and explicitly the switching of the effect of traditional ontology onto modern mathematical physics and its transcendental foundations.

The third paragraph observes that Descartes does not need to pose the problem of adequate access to intraworldly beings. Under the unbroken dominance of traditional ontology, the genuine mode of grasping the authentically being is decided in advance. It lies in *noein* — "intuition" in the broadest sense — of which *dianoein* ("thinking") is only a founded mode of execution. And from this fundamental ontological orientation, Descartes provides his "critique" of the still-possible intuitive-apprehending mode of access to beings — *sensatio* (*aisthēsis*) — as against *intellectio*.

The fourth paragraph exhibits Descartes' dismissal of sensory access. Descartes knows very well that beings do not initially show themselves in their authentic Being. What is "initially" given is this determinately colored, tasting, hard, cold, sounding wax-thing. But this and everything the senses give remains ontologically irrelevant. Heidegger quotes *Principia* II.3: the perceptions of the senses refer only to the conjunction of the human body with the mind and ordinarily exhibit what external bodies can benefit or harm. The senses do not teach us what bodies are in themselves (*qualia in seipsis existant*). Through the senses we receive no disclosure about beings in their Being. Only when we recognize that the nature of matter does not consist in being hard, heavy, colored, or otherwise affecting the senses, but solely in being extended in length, breadth, and depth, do we grasp the Being of the corporeal.

The fifth paragraph provides a critical analysis of Descartes' interpretation of hardness, connecting back to §19. Hardness is grasped as resistance. But resistance, like hardness, is not understood in a phenomenal sense — as something experienced in itself and determinable in such experience. For Descartes, resistance means: not yielding one's place — not undergoing change of location. Resisting means remaining at a determinate location relative to another thing that is changing location, or changing location at such a speed that one can be "overtaken." Through this interpretation of the experience of hardness, the mode of Being of sensory apprehending — and thereby the possibility of grasping the being encountered in such apprehending in its Being — is extinguished. Descartes translates the mode of Being of apprehending something into the only mode he knows: apprehension becomes a determinate alongside-one-another-presence-at-hand of two present-at-hand *res extensae*. The movement-relation between the two is itself grasped in the mode of *extensio*. Hardness and resistance do not show themselves at all unless a being exists with the mode of Being of Dasein or at least of a living being. This is a crucial ontological point: sensory qualities like hardness are not properties of present-at-hand things but modes of encounter presupposing a being with the existential structure of Being-in-the-world.

The sixth paragraph draws the broader consequence. The idea of Being as constant presence-at-hand not only motivates an extreme determination of the Being of intraworldly beings and its identification with the world as such; it also prevents bringing comportments of Dasein ontologically adequately into view. Thereby the path is completely blocked to seeing the founded character of all sensory and intellectual apprehending and understanding them as a possibility of Being-in-the-world. The Being of "Dasein," to whose fundamental constitution Being-in-the-world belongs, Descartes grasps in the same way as the Being of the *res extensa*: as substance.

The seventh paragraph considers a possible defense of Descartes. One might object that a task is being foisted on Descartes and then "demonstrated" as unsolved by him — a task entirely outside his horizon. How can Descartes identify a determinate intraworldly being and its Being with the world if he does not know the phenomenon of world and therefore intraworldliness at all? But in fundamental-ontological engagement, one must not confine oneself to doxographically graspable theses; one must take the material tendency of the problematic as orientation, even if it does not get beyond a vulgar formulation. That Descartes intended not merely to pose but to radically solve the problem of "I and world" with the *res cogitans* and *res extensa* is clear from his *Meditations* (especially I and VI). That the ontological orientation toward tradition — lacking all positive critique — made it impossible for him to lay bare a primordial ontological problematic of Dasein, had to block his view of the phenomenon of world, and could press the ontology of the "world" into the ontology of a determinate intraworldly being — this is what the preceding discussions were meant to demonstrate.

The eighth paragraph considers and refutes a further defense. One might counter that even if the problem of the world and the Being of the environmentally nearest-encountered being remain concealed, Descartes has nevertheless laid the ground for the ontological characterization of the intraworldly being that founds all others in its Being: material nature. Upon this fundamental layer, the remaining layers of intraworldly actuality are built up. In the extended thing as such, determinations that show themselves as qualities but are "at bottom" quantitative modifications of the *modi* of *extensio* are first grounded. Upon these still-reducible qualities rest the specific qualities like beautiful, ugly, fitting, unfitting, useful, useless — value-predicates by which the initially merely material thing is stamped as a good. With this layering, the analysis arrives at the being we ontologically characterized as ready-to-hand equipment. The Cartesian analysis of the "world" thus first makes possible the secure construction of the structure of the proximally ready-to-hand; it needs only the easily accomplished supplementation of the natural thing into the full use-thing.

The ninth paragraph demolishes this defense. But is the Being of the proximally encountered intraworldly being ontologically reachable on this path? With material thinghood, a Being — constant thing-presence-at-hand — is unspokenly posited, which receives so little ontological supplementation through the subsequent equipping of beings with value-predicates that these value-characters themselves remain merely ontic determinations of a being that has the mode of Being of a thing. The addition of value-predicates cannot furnish the slightest new disclosure about the Being of goods; it merely presupposes again the mode of Being of pure presence-at-hand. Values are present-at-hand determinations of a thing. Values have in the end their ontological origin solely in the prior positing of thing-actuality as the fundamental layer. But even pre-phenomenological experience shows in the thingly-intended being something that is not fully intelligible through thinghood. So the thingly Being requires supplementation. But what does the Being of values or their "validity" mean ontologically — validity that Lotze grasped as a mode of "affirmation"? What does this "adhering" of values to things mean ontologically? As long as these determinations remain in the dark, the reconstruction of the use-thing from the natural thing is an ontologically questionable undertaking — quite apart from the fundamental perversion of the problematic. And does not such reconstruction always already need the prior positive view of the phenomenon whose wholeness is to be reconstructed? If its ownmost constitution of Being is not previously adequately explicated, then the reconstruction builds without a blueprint.

The tenth paragraph concludes the Descartes discussion. Descartes sharpened the narrowing of the question of the world to the question of natural thinghood as the primarily accessible intraworldly being. He solidified the opinion that the supposedly most rigorous ontic cognition of a being is also the possible access to the primary Being of the being discovered in such cognition. But at the same time it must be seen that the "supplements" to thing-ontology move on fundamentally the same dogmatic basis as Descartes. It was already indicated (§14) that the skipping over of the world and the nearest-encountered being is not accidental, not an oversight easily corrected, but grounded in an essential mode of Being of Dasein itself. Only when the analytic of Dasein has made the most important main structures of Dasein transparent and the horizon of possible intelligibility has been assigned to the concept of Being as such — so that readiness-to-hand and presence-at-hand also first become ontologically primordially intelligible — can the critique of the Cartesian and fundamentally still prevailing world-ontology be set in its philosophical right.

The eleventh and final paragraph states that the considerations on Descartes were meant to bring the insight that the seemingly self-evident starting point from the things of the world, as well as the orientation toward the supposedly most rigorous cognition of beings, does not guarantee the winning of the ground on which the nearest ontological constitutions of the world, of Dasein, and of intraworldly beings can be phenomenally encountered. Heidegger adds, however, that Descartes' radical positing of *extensio* as the *praesuppositum* for every determinateness of the *res corporea* has worked toward the understanding of an a priori whose content Kant then fixed more insistently. The positing of *extensio* as the fundamental determination of the "world" has its phenomenal right, even though in returning to it neither the spatiality of the world, nor the initially discovered spatiality of the being encountered in the surrounding world, nor the spatiality of Dasein itself can be ontologically grasped. This acknowledgment of the partial validity of *extensio* sets up the transition to subsection C on spatiality.


#### C. The Aroundness of the Surrounding World and the "Spatiality" of Dasein

The brief introduction to subsection C states that in the explication of the worldhood of the surrounding world, the discussion touched on the spatiality of the ready-to-hand and of Dasein itself. The existential-ontological exposition of this must now be given. The question is: in what sense is space constitutive for the world? Space cannot initially be conceived in the sense of pure extended dimensionality (*extensio*); rather, spatiality must be shown from the structures of worldhood. The spatiality of Dasein is grounded in Being-in-the-world, not the reverse: the world is not present-at-hand in space. The investigation proceeds through three stages: (1) the spatiality of the innerworldly ready-to-hand (§22), (2) the spatiality of Being-in-the-world (§23), (3) the spatiality of Dasein and space (§24).

### § 22. The Spatiality of the Innerworldly Ready-to-Hand

The first paragraph observes that if space in a sense yet to be determined constitutes the world, it is not surprising that we had to keep the intraworldly in view as something inner-spatial in the preceding ontological characterization. But the spatiality of the ready-to-hand was not yet expressly grasped phenomenally or exhibited in its connection with the structure of Being of the ready-to-hand. That is now the task.

The second paragraph asks how we already encountered the spatiality of the ready-to-hand. We spoke of the *proximally* ready-to-hand. This means not only the being first encountered before others, but also the being "nearby." The ready-to-hand of everyday dealing has the character of *nearness* (*Nähe*). Precisely considered, this nearness is already intimated in the term expressing its Being — in "readiness-to-hand" (*Zuhandenheit*: literally, "to-handness"). What is "to hand" has in each case a different nearness not established by measuring distances. This nearness is regulated by circumspectively "reckoning" handling and use. The circumspection of concern simultaneously fixes what is near in this way with respect to the *direction* in which the equipment is at all times accessible. The directed nearness of equipment means that it does not merely have its position somewhere present-at-hand in space, but as equipment is essentially placed, set up, installed, laid out. Equipment has its *place* (*Platz*), or else it "lies around" — which is fundamentally to be distinguished from a pure occurring at an arbitrary spatial location. The respective place determines itself as the place of this equipment *for*... from a whole of mutually oriented places of the environmentally ready-to-hand equipmental connection. Place and the manifold of places must not be interpreted as the where of an arbitrary presence-at-hand of things. Place is in each case the determinate "there" and "here" of the *belonging* of equipment. The respective belonging corresponds to the equipment-character of the ready-to-hand — its involvement-related membership in an equipmental totality.

The third paragraph introduces the concept of *region* (*Gegend*). The placeable belonging of an equipmental totality has as the condition of its possibility the whither-in-general into which an equipmental connection is assigned its totality of places. This whither of possible equipment-belonging, held circumspectly in advance in concerned dealing, we call the *region*. "In the region of" means not only "in the direction toward" but also "in the vicinity of" something lying in that direction. The place constituted by direction and remoteness — nearness is only a mode of this — is already oriented toward and within a region. Something like a region must be previously discovered if the assigning and finding of places of a circumspectly available equipmental totality is to become possible. This regional orientation of the manifold of places of the ready-to-hand constitutes the *aroundness* — the around-us — of the environmentally encountered being.

The fourth paragraph states that a "pure manifold of three dimensions" is never found first. Spatiality is always already articulated through region and place. The specific spatiality of equipment is not an objective spatial property; it is disclosed through circumspective concern. The sun, whose light and warmth are everyday ready-to-hand, has its own circumspectly discovered "places" — sunrise, noon, sunset, midnight — which are not astronomical positions but regions of concern. The house has its sunny side and its weather side; the rooms are arranged with reference to these, and within them equipment is placed according to its use. Churches and graves, for instance, are oriented toward sunrise and sunset. These spatial orientations of the ready-to-hand are not arbitrary assignments of significance to indifferent spatial positions; they articulate the primordial spatiality of the surrounding world as it is discovered in concern.

The fifth and final paragraph of §22 observes that neither the multiplicity of places of the equipmental totality nor the totality of the regional manifold is ever present-at-hand in the pure multiplicity of the positions of arbitrary things. These spatial determinations are in each case motivated from the significance-structure of the world. The spatiality of the ready-to-hand is not an incidental feature of its presence; it belongs to its Being as ready-to-hand within an environmentally articulated world.

### § 23. The Spatiality of Being-in-the-World

The first paragraph turns from the spatiality of the ready-to-hand to the spatiality of Being-in-the-world itself. If the ready-to-hand of the surrounding world has an environmentally articulated spatiality, then Being-in-the-world — as concerned dealing with the ready-to-hand — must itself be spatial. What constitutes the spatiality of Dasein?

The second paragraph introduces *de-severance* (*Ent-fernung*) as the first existential character of Dasein's spatiality. De-severance means making distance disappear — bringing something near. Dasein is essentially de-severant: it always tends to bring beings close. This de-severing is not a matter of bridging measurable physical distances; it is the circumspective concern that discovers beings in their nearness. The "near" and "far" of everyday Dasein do not coincide with measurable distances. The glasses on one's nose are, in Dasein's sense of distance, farther away than the picture on the opposite wall at which one gazes. The street along which one walks is more closely proximate than the ground beneath one's feet. Nearness is regulated by circumspective concern — by what is "to hand" — not by the geometry of bodies in space.

The third paragraph introduces *directionality* (*Ausrichtung*) as the second existential character. Every bringing-near has already taken a direction. Dasein as Being-in-the-world always orients itself in a directed way. The directions of left and right are not first determined by the subject's body as a present-at-hand thing and then projected onto the world. Rather, Dasein finds itself always already in a directed, oriented surrounding world. The regions from which the ready-to-hand approaches are disclosed through the significance-structure of the world — not through a coordinate system imposed on neutral space.

The fourth paragraph addresses the relation between Dasein's existential spatiality and physical-geometrical space. De-severance and directionality are existential characters of Being-in-the-world. Dasein's spatiality is not that of a body-thing occupying a location. Dasein does not "fill up" a bit of space the way a real thing does. Dasein takes up space — it "makes room" (*räumt ein*) — by giving space to the encountered beings, letting them be encountered in their places and regions. This making-room constitutes Dasein's existential spatiality.

The fifth and final paragraph of §23 states that only because Dasein is spatial in the manner of de-severance and directionality can the encountered ready-to-hand be encountered in its environmentally articulated spatiality. Dasein's spatiality is neither a property of a body-thing nor a projection of consciousness; it is the existential condition of the possibility of the spatial encounter with intraworldly beings.

### § 24. The Spatiality of Dasein and Space

The first paragraph addresses the relationship between Dasein's existential spatiality and space as the formal structure described by geometry or physics. Dasein's spatiality, with its characters of de-severance and directionality, is the existential ground from which alone something like "space" in the pure, three-dimensional sense can be discovered. Pure space — the homogeneous three-dimensional extension of mathematics — is won only through a specific "de-worlding" (*Entweltlichung*) of the environmentally near. When the involvement-laden places and regions of the surrounding world are progressively stripped of their equipment-character, what remains is the pure dimensionality of positions that can be occupied by anything.

The second paragraph argues that Descartes' *extensio* represents precisely this de-worlded space — space from which all involvement, all aroundness, all reference to Dasein's concern has been removed. Mathematical space is not the primary phenomenon; it is a *derivative* — the result of a specific abstractive operation carried out upon the primordial existential spatiality of Being-in-the-world. The homogeneous, infinite, directionless space of geometry is the end-product of a progressive de-worlding of the surrounding world's spatiality.

The third paragraph insists that space is neither "in" the subject nor is the world "in" space. Space is rather "in" the world — insofar as the Being-in-the-world constitutive of Dasein has disclosed space. Space is not first found by contemplation and then subjected to arrangement; rather, Dasein has always already made room for space in its concerned dealings. This does not mean that space is merely subjective; it means that the condition of the possibility of encountering space lies in the existential constitution of Dasein as Being-in-the-world. Beings of non-Dasein-like character can be discovered as spatial only because Dasein itself is spatial in the existential sense.

The fourth paragraph addresses possible misunderstandings. To say Dasein is spatial does not mean the soul has a location in a body; nor does it mean that a consciousness floats above bodies and accidentally relates to extension. It means that Dasein, as Being-in-the-world, has always already disclosed space by making room for beings within the significative referential structure of its world. Dasein can comport itself "spatially" only because it is existentially spatial.

The fifth and final paragraph of §24 concludes the entire Third Chapter. The investigation of worldhood has shown that the phenomenon of world is constituted by a referential totality of significance grounded in Dasein's Being. The world is not a container, not a collection of things, not pure extension; it is the horizon of meaningfulness within which beings can first be encountered. The spatiality of the world derives from the existential spatiality of Dasein, not from the mathematical properties of extension. With this, the analysis of the first structural moment of Being-in-the-world — the world in its worldhood — is provisionally complete. The investigation now turns to the second structural moment: the "who" of everyday Dasein.


### Fourth Chapter: Being-in-the-World as Being-with and Being-a-Self. The "They"

The introductory paragraph to Chapter 4 states that the existential analytic now turns to the question of the "who" of Dasein — specifically, who is Dasein in everydayness? All structures of Being of Dasein, including the phenomenon answering this who-question, are modes of its Being; their ontological characterization is existential. The inquiry into this phenomenon leads to structures of Dasein equally primordial with Being-in-the-world: *Being-with* (*Mitsein*) and *Dasein-with* (*Mitdasein*). In this mode of Being is grounded the mode of everyday Being-one's-self, whose explication makes visible what we may call the "subject" of everydayness: the *They* (*das Man*). The chapter has three sections: (1) the approach to the existential question of the who of Dasein (§25); (2) the Dasein-with of Others and everyday Being-with (§26); (3) everyday Being-one's-self and the They (§27).

### § 25. The Approach to the Existential Question of the Who of Dasein

The first paragraph recalls that the answer to who this being (Dasein) in each case is was apparently already given in the formal indication of Dasein's basic determinations (§9). Dasein is a being that I myself in each case am; Being is in each case mine. This determination indicates an ontological constitution — but only that. It also contains the ontic (though rough) indication that in each case an I is this being and not Others. The who answers itself from the I itself, the "subject," the "self." The who is what maintains itself as identical through the change of comportments and experiences and relates itself to this manifold. Ontologically, we understand it as what is already always and constantly present-at-hand in a closed region, lying at the ground in a preeminent sense: the *subjectum*. As the selfsame in the manifold of otherness, it has the character of the self. One may reject soul-substance, thingness of consciousness, and objectivity of person, but ontologically one remains with the positing of something whose Being, explicitly or not, retains the meaning of presence-at-hand. Substantiality is the ontological guiding thread for the determination of the being from which the who-question is answered. Dasein is unspokenly grasped in advance as something present-at-hand.

The second paragraph raises the central challenge. The ontic self-evidence of the statement that I am the one who in each case is Dasein must not mislead us into thinking that the path of ontological interpretation of the so "given" is unambiguously prescribed. It remains questionable whether even the ontic content of this statement adequately reproduces the phenomenal findings of everyday Dasein. It could be that the *who* of everyday Dasein is precisely *not* in each case I myself. This is one of the most striking and counterintuitive claims in *Being and Time*: the who of everyday Dasein may not be the I at all.

The third paragraph insists that if phenomenal exhibition from the mode of Being of the being itself is to retain priority over the most self-evident and time-honored answers, then the phenomenological interpretation of Dasein must be preserved from a perversion of the problematic. What if the approach from the givenness of the I were a *temptation* — one grounded in the Being of Dasein itself? Perhaps Dasein, in first addressing itself, always says "I am it" — and says this most loudly precisely when it "is not" this being. What if the constitution of Dasein — that it is in each case mine — were the *ground* for the fact that Dasein is initially and for the most part *not* itself? What if the existential analytic, with the approach from the givenness of the I, were to walk into a trap set by Dasein itself?

The fourth paragraph considers the counterargument from method. Does it not contravene the rules of all sound methodology when the approach does not hold to the evident givennesses of the thematic domain? What is more indubitable than the givenness of the I? Does this givenness not contain the directive to disregard everything else "given" — not only a being "world" but also the Being of other "I's"? Perhaps what this mode of givenness — the simple, formal, reflective I-apprehension — gives is indeed evident. This insight even opens access to an independent phenomenological problematic that, as "formal phenomenology of consciousness," has its fundamental, framework-giving significance. Heidegger does not deny the legitimacy of this formal approach but insists that it cannot serve the existential analytic of factical Dasein.

The fifth paragraph poses the decisive series of questions. In the present context, the question arises whether the named mode of givenness of the I discloses Dasein in its everydayness — if it discloses it at all. Is it a priori self-evident that access to Dasein must be a simply apprehending reflection on the I of acts? What if this mode of "self-givenness" were a *temptation* for the existential analytic — one grounded in the Being of Dasein itself? Perhaps Dasein in its nearest addressing of itself always says "I am it" — and says this most loudly precisely when it "is not" this being. What if the constitution of Dasein — that it is in each case mine — were the *ground* for the fact that Dasein is initially and for the most part *not* itself? What if the existential analytic, with the approach from the givenness of the I, were to walk into a trap laid by Dasein itself?

The sixth paragraph draws the methodological conclusion. One can always ontically legitimately say of this being that "I" am it. But the ontological analytic that makes use of such statements must place them under fundamental reservations. The "I" may be understood only in the sense of a nonbinding formal indication of something that may reveal itself, in the respective phenomenal context, as its "opposite." "Not-I" does not mean a being essentially lacking I-hood but a determinate mode of Being of the "I" itself — for example, self-lostness.

The seventh paragraph recalls that the positive interpretation of Dasein already forbids the starting point of the formal givenness of the I. The clarification of Being-in-the-world showed that initially no bare subject without a world "is" or is given. And so equally a bare I without the Others is never initially given. If "the Others" are always already co-there in Being-in-the-world, then this phenomenal finding must not tempt us into holding the ontological structure of the so "given" as self-evident. Just as the ontic self-evidence of the in-itself of intraworldly beings tempts one to overlook the phenomenon of the world, so too the self-evidence that Dasein is in each case mine harbors a temptation regarding its ontological interpretation.

The eighth and final paragraph of §25 states that the existential-analytic answering of the who-question does have a guiding thread. Of the formal indications given in §§9 and 12, the relevant one is not so much the one just discussed but rather the indication that the "essence" of Dasein is grounded in its existence. If the "I" is an essential determination of Dasein, then it must be interpreted existentially. The who is then answerable only through the phenomenal exhibition of a determinate mode of Being of Dasein. If Dasein is in each case only its self by existing, then the constancy of the self requires, just as much as its possible "inconstancy," an existential-ontological questioning as the sole adequate access. The "substance" of the human being is not spirit as the synthesis of soul and body, but *existence*.

### § 26. The Dasein-with of Others and Everyday Being-with

The first paragraph states that the answer to the who-question is to be won from the analysis of the mode of Being in which Dasein initially and for the most part maintains itself. The investigation takes its orientation from Being-in-the-world. If we rightly said that the preceding explication of the world had already brought the other structural moments of Being-in-the-world into view, then the answering of the who-question should also be in a certain way prepared by it.

The second paragraph recalls the analysis of the surrounding world. The "description" of the nearest surrounding world — for example, the work-world of the craftsman — yielded that with the equipment in production the Others "co-encounter," those for whom the "work" is destined. In the mode of Being of this ready-to-hand — its involvement — lies an essential reference to possible wearers, for whom the product should be "cut to fit." Likewise, in the used material the producer or "supplier" is encountered as one who "serves" well or poorly. The field along which we walk "outside" shows itself as belonging to so-and-so, kept in good order by them; the book we use was bought at such-and-such a shop, given by so-and-so; the boat anchored at the shore refers to an acquaintance who undertakes his trips in it, or even as a "strange boat" it shows Others. These Others encountered in the ready-to-hand environmental equipmental context are not thought-added to a merely present-at-hand thing; these "things" encounter us *from the world* in which they are ready-to-hand for the Others, which world is also always already mine.

The third paragraph clarifies the ontological character of the encountered Others. The world of Dasein thus releases beings that are not only different from equipment and things in general but that, in accordance with their mode of Being as Dasein itself, are "in" the world in the manner of Being-in-the-world — the world in which they are at the same time encountered intraworldly. This being is neither present-at-hand nor ready-to-hand but is so as the releasing Dasein itself — it also is, and is co-there. The "Others" does not mean everyone else besides me from whom the I distinguishes itself; the Others are rather those from whom one mostly does not distinguish oneself, among whom one also is. This also-Being-there with them does not have the ontological character of a co-presence-at-hand within a world. The "with" is Dasein-like; the "also" means the equality of Being as circumspectly-concernfully Being-in-the-world. "With" and "also" are to be understood existentially, not categorially. On the ground of this co-existing Being-in-the-world, the world is always already the one I share with Others. The world of Dasein is *Mitwelt* (with-world). Being-in is Being-with Others. The intraworldly in-itself-being of Others is *Mitdasein* (Dasein-with).

The fourth paragraph insists on the primacy of worldly encounter over the theoretical construction of Other minds. The Others do not encounter us in a prior distinguishing grasp that first takes a present-at-hand subject and then looks for further subjects alongside it. They encounter from the world in which concernfully circumspective Dasein essentially dwells. Against the easily intrusive theoretically devised "explanations" of the presence-at-hand of Others, the exhibited phenomenal finding of their environmental encounter must be held fast. This nearest and elementary worldly mode of encounter goes so far that even one's own Dasein is first "findable" by itself in what it pursues, uses, expects, guards against — in the environmentally ready-to-hand that is proximally concerned with. Dasein finds "itself" initially in what it does, uses, expects, averts — in the ready-to-hand of the surrounding world that it concerns itself with. Even when Dasein expressly addresses itself as "I-here," this personal-local determination must be understood from the existential spatiality of Dasein: this I-here does not mean a distinguished point of an I-thing but is understood as Being-in from the there of the ready-to-hand world, alongside which Dasein dwells as concern.

The fifth paragraph introduces the formal ontological structures. Being-with is an existential that constitutes Being-in-the-world. The phenomenological assertion that Dasein is essentially Being-with has an existential-ontological sense. It does not wish to ontically establish that I am factically not alone, that rather more of my kind occur. If what the proposition meant were something like that, then Being-with would not be an existential determination that belongs to Dasein from itself out of its mode of Being, but a characteristic that arises in each case on the basis of the occurring of Others. Being-with determines Dasein existentially even when an Other is factically not present-at-hand and perceived. Even the Being-alone of Dasein is Being-with in the world. The Other can be lacking only in and for a Being-with. Being-alone is a deficient mode of Being-with; its possibility is proof of Being-with. On the other hand, factical Being-alone is not remedied by the fact that a second specimen of human being occurs "beside" me, or perhaps ten such. Even when these and still more are present-at-hand, Dasein can be alone. Being-with and the facticity of Being-with-one-another are therefore not grounded in a co-occurring of several "subjects." Being-alone "among" many does not mean that these many are merely present-at-hand. Even in Being "among them" they are co-there; their Dasein-with is encountered in the mode of indifference and foreignness.

The sixth paragraph introduces *solicitude* (*Fürsorge*) as the existential term for Dasein's Being toward Others, distinguishing it from *concern* (*Besorgen*), which is Being toward ready-to-hand equipment. The being toward which Dasein as Being-with comports itself does not have the mode of Being of ready-to-hand equipment — it is itself Dasein. This being is not concerned-for but stands in solicitude. Even the "concern" for food and clothing, the tending of the sick body, is solicitude. "Solicitude" as a factual social institution (welfare) is grounded in the constitution of Being of Dasein as Being-with. Its factual urgency is motivated in the fact that Dasein initially and for the most part maintains itself in the deficient modes of solicitude. Being-for, -against, -without-one-another, passing-one-another-by, not-mattering-to-one-another are possible modes of solicitude. And precisely the last-named modes of deficiency and indifference characterize everyday and average Being-with-one-another.

The seventh paragraph describes the two extreme positive possibilities of solicitude. First, *leaping in* (*Einspringen*): solicitude can take away the "care" from the Other, as it were, substituting itself for them in their concern. The Other is thereby thrown out of position, steps back, and later takes over the finished product as ready-available, or completely disburdening themselves of it. In such solicitude the Other can become dependent and dominated, even if this domination is tacit and remains hidden from the dominated. This leaping-in solicitude that takes over concern from the Other determines Being-with-one-another to a great extent and mostly concerns the concern with ready-to-hand equipment. Second, *leaping ahead* (*Vorausspringen*): solicitude that leaps ahead of the Other in their existential ability-to-be, not in order to take away their "care" but to give it back to them authentically. This solicitude, which essentially concerns the Other's authentic existence rather than a what that the Other is concerned with, helps the Other become transparent in their own care and free for it. Between these two extremes — the leaping-in that dominates and the leaping-ahead that liberates — everyday Being-with-one-another maintains itself and displays manifold mixed forms.

The eighth paragraph observes that Being-with-one-another is often grounded initially and exclusively in what is commonly concerned with. A Being-with-one-another arising from pursuing the same thing usually keeps within external limits, maintaining distance and reserve. The Being-with-one-another of those employed in the same affair often nourishes itself only on distrust. Conversely, a common commitment to the same cause is determined from each one's individually seized Dasein. This authentic bond first makes possible the right objectivity that releases the Other in their freedom for themselves.

The ninth paragraph connects the analysis to the broader question. Because Being-with remains existentially constitutive of Being-in-the-world, it must be interpreted from the phenomenon of care, which determines the Being of Dasein as such (Chapter 6). Solicitude is guided by *considerateness* (*Rücksicht*) and *forbearance* (*Nachsicht*) — just as concern with equipment is guided by circumspection. These can run through the corresponding deficient and indifferent modes, down to inconsiderateness and the indulgence that guides indifference. The world releases not only the ready-to-hand as intraworldly beings but also Dasein — the Others in their Dasein-with. Because Being-with belongs to the constitution of Dasein, and because the understanding of the Being of Others lies already in the understanding of Being belonging to Dasein (since its Being is Being-with), this understanding is not a cognition grown from knowledge but a primordially existential mode of Being that first makes knowledge and acquaintance possible.

The tenth and final paragraph of §26 concludes with the transition to the who-question proper. In absorbed concern with the world — simultaneously in Being-with-Others — Dasein is not itself. Who is it, then, that has taken over Being as everyday Being-with-one-another?

### § 27. Everyday Being-One's-Self and the They

The first paragraph states that the ontologically relevant result of the preceding analysis of Being-with lies in the insight that the "subject-character" of one's own Dasein and that of the Others is existentially determined — from certain modes of Being. In environmentally concerned dealing, the Others are encountered as what they are; they are what they pursue. In concern with what one has taken up with, for, and against the Others, there rests constantly the care about a *difference* vis-à-vis the Others — whether to even out the difference, or to catch up with the Others, or, being ahead, to keep them down. Being-with-one-another is — hidden from itself — disquieted by the care about this difference. Existentially expressed, it has the character of *distantiality* (*Abständigkeit*). The more inconspicuous this mode of Being is to everyday Dasein itself, the more stubbornly and primordially it works itself out.

The second paragraph draws the consequence. In this distantiality belonging to Being-with, Dasein stands as everyday Being-with-one-another in the subjection of the Others. Not it itself is; the Others have taken its Being from it. The discretion of the Others disposes over the everyday possibilities of Being of Dasein. These Others are not determinate Others. On the contrary, every Other can represent them. What is decisive is only the inconspicuous, unawares-already-taken-over dominion of the Others. One oneself belongs to the Others and entrenches their power. "The Others," whom one so names in order to conceal one's own essential belonging to them, are those who in everyday Being-with-one-another are there initially and for the most part. The who is not this one and not that one, not oneself and not some and not the sum of all. The "who" is the neuter: *das Man* — the They.

The third paragraph specifies how the They functions. In the use of public means of transport, in the use of the information services (newspapers), every Other is like every other. This Being-with-one-another dissolves one's own Dasein entirely into the mode of Being of "the Others," such that the Others in their distinguishability and expressness vanish all the more. In this inconspicuousness and unascertainability the They unfolds its authentic *dictatorship*. We enjoy and have fun as *one* enjoys; we read, see, and judge literature and art as *one* sees and judges; but we also withdraw from the "great mass" as *one* withdraws; we find "outrageous" what *one* finds outrageous. The They, which is no determinate one and which is all — though not as a sum — prescribes the mode of Being of everydayness.

The fourth paragraph identifies the They's own modes of Being. The mentioned tendency of Being-with — distantiality — is grounded in the fact that Being-with-one-another as such is concerned with *averageness* (*Durchschnittlichkeit*). Averageness is an existential character of the They. The They is in its Being essentially concerned with it. Therefore it factically maintains itself in the averageness of what is proper, what one lets count, what one grants success to, what one denies success to. This averageness, in its prescribing of what may and should be ventured, keeps watch over every exception that presses forward. Every priority is noiselessly suppressed. Everything primordial is overnight smoothed down as long familiar. Everything won through struggle becomes something to be handled. Every mystery loses its power. The care of averageness reveals another essential tendency of Dasein: the *leveling down* (*Einebnung*) of all possibilities of Being.

The fifth paragraph identifies *publicness* (*Öffentlichkeit*). Distantiality, averageness, and leveling down — as modes of Being of the They — constitute what we know as "publicness." Publicness initially controls every interpretation of the world and of Dasein and is in every case right. And this not on the basis of a distinguished and primary relation of Being to "things," not because it has at its disposal an explicitly appropriated transparency of Dasein, but on the basis of not-getting-into-the-matter — because it is insensitive to all differences of level and genuineness. Publicness darkens everything and then passes off what is so concealed as well-known and accessible to everyone.

The sixth paragraph introduces *disburdening* (*Entlastung*) and *accommodation* (*Entgegenkommen*). The They is everywhere on hand, yet in such a way that it has always already slipped away when Dasein presses toward a decision. Because, however, the They presents every judgment and decision as its own, it takes the responsibility from the respective Dasein. The They can, as it were, afford to have one constantly appeal to it. It can most easily answer for everything because no one is the one who needs to stand for anything. It "was" always the They, and yet it can be said that "no one" was it. In the everydayness of Dasein, most things happen through that of which we must say: no one did it. The They thus disburderns the respective Dasein in its everydayness. Not only that: with this disburdening of Being, the They accommodates Dasein insofar as in it lies the tendency to take things lightly and make things easy. And because the They constantly accommodates the respective Dasein in this way, it retains and entrenches its stubborn dominion.

The seventh paragraph states the central ontological finding: everyone is the Other, and no one is himself. The They, with which the question of the who of everyday Dasein is answered, is the *Nobody* (*Niemand*) to which every Dasein has always already surrendered itself in Being-among-one-another. In the exhibited characters of the everyday Being-among-one-another — distantiality, averageness, leveling down, publicness, disburdening, and accommodation — lies the nearest "constancy" of Dasein. This constancy concerns not the perpetual presence-at-hand of something but the mode of Being of Dasein as Being-with. Being in the named modes, the self of one's own Dasein and the self of the Other have not yet found themselves — or have lost themselves. One is in the manner of non-self-constancy and inauthenticity. This manner of Being signifies no diminishment of the facticity of Dasein, just as the They as the Nobody is not nothing. On the contrary, in this mode of Being, Dasein is an *ens realissimum* — if "reality" is understood as Dasein-like Being.

The eighth paragraph insists that the They is not present-at-hand in the way a stone is. The more obviously the They comports itself, the more ungraspable and hidden it is — but the less it is nothing for all that. To the unprejudiced ontic-ontological "seeing," it reveals itself as the "most real subject" of everydayness. One must neither hastily decree that this They is "really" nothing nor harbor the opinion that the phenomenon is ontologically interpreted when one "explains" it as the subsequently assembled result of the co-presence-at-hand of several subjects. The They is not a "universal subject" hovering over several individuals. It is not the genus of the respective Dasein, nor is it to be found as a lasting characteristic of this being. The They is an *existential* and belongs as a primordial phenomenon to the positive constitution of Dasein. It has itself various possibilities of its Dasein-like concretion. The penetration and explicitness of its dominion can vary historically.

The ninth paragraph introduces the *They-self* (*Man-selbst*). The self of everyday Dasein is the They-self, which we distinguish from the authentic — that is, specifically seized — self. As the They-self, the respective Dasein is dispersed into the They and must first *find* itself. This dispersal characterizes the "subject" of the mode of Being we know as concerned absorption in the nearest surrounding world. If Dasein is familiar with itself as the They-self, this also means that the They prescribes the nearest interpretation of the world and of Being-in-the-world. The They-self, for the sake of which Dasein is in everyday life, articulates the referential connection of significance. The world of Dasein releases the encountered beings upon an involvement-totality familiar to the They and within the limits set by the averageness of the They. Initially, factical Dasein is in the averagely discovered with-world. Initially, "I" am not, in the sense of my own self, but the Others in the manner of the They. From this and as this I am first "given" to "myself." Initially Dasein *is* the They and for the most part remains so.

The tenth paragraph describes how authenticity is won. When Dasein expressly discovers the world and brings it near to itself, when it discloses to itself its own authentic Being, this discovering of "world" and disclosing of Dasein is always accomplished as a clearing-away of concealments and darkenings, as a breaking-up of the disguises with which Dasein barricades itself against itself. Authentic self-being does not rest on an exceptional state of the subject detached from the They; it is an *existentiell modification* of the They as an essential existential. The selfsameness of the authentically existing self is then ontologically separated by a chasm from the identity of the I maintaining itself through the manifold of experiences.

The eleventh and final paragraph of §27, which also closes Chapter 4, draws the overall conclusion. With the interpretation of Being-with and Being-one's-self in the They, the question of the who of everyday Being-with-one-another has been answered. These considerations have also achieved a concrete understanding of the fundamental constitution of Dasein. Being-in-the-world has been made visible in its everydayness and averageness. Everyday Dasein draws its pre-ontological interpretation of its Being from the nearest mode of Being of the They. The ontological interpretation initially follows this interpretive tendency: it understands Dasein from the world and finds it as an intraworldly being. Not only that: even the meaning of Being on the basis of which these being "subjects" are understood, the "nearest" ontology of Dasein lets itself be prescribed by the "world." Because in this absorption in the world the world-phenomenon itself is skipped over, intraworldly present-at-hand entities — things — take its place. The Being of the being that is co-there is grasped as presence-at-hand. So the exhibition of the positive phenomenon of the nearest-everyday Being-in-the-world enables insight into the root of the missing of the ontological interpretation of this constitution of Being. It itself, in its everyday mode of Being, is what initially misses and covers over itself.


### Fifth Chapter: Being-in as Such

### § 28. The Task of a Thematic Analysis of Being-in

The first paragraph states that the existential analytic has so far directed its phenomenal characterization of Being-in-the-world at the structural moment of the world and the answering of the question of the who of this being in its everydayness. But already in the first characterization of the tasks of a preparatory fundamental analysis, an orientation concerning Being-in as such was sent ahead (§12) and demonstrated in the concrete mode of world-cognition (§13). Now the interpretation must be led back to the phenomenon of Being-in, with the goal of grasping not only the structural totality of Being-in-the-world more securely but also paving the way for grasping the primordial Being of Dasein itself: *care* (*Sorge*).

The second paragraph asks what more can be shown about Being-in-the-world beyond the essential relations of Being-alongside-the-world (concern), Being-with (solicitude), and Being-a-self (the who). There remain tasks of extending the analysis, but the present investigation has a fundamental-ontological aim. When we thematically inquire into Being-in, we cannot wish to destroy the primordiality of the phenomenon through derivation from other phenomena. But the non-derivability of a primordial phenomenon does not exclude a manifoldness of constitutive characters of Being. If such show themselves, they are existentially *equiprimordial* (*gleichursprünglich*). The phenomenon of equiprimordiality of constitutive moments has often been disregarded in ontology due to a methodologically unbridled tendency to trace everything back to a simple "primal ground." This principle of equiprimordiality is one of Heidegger's most important methodological commitments: the constitutive structures of Dasein are not reducible to a single foundation but stand in an irreducible structural unity.

The third paragraph identifies the direction of analysis. Being-in, distinguished from the spatial "insideness" of one present-at-hand thing in another, is an essential mode of Being of this being itself. What presents itself with this phenomenon is not the present-at-hand *commercium* between a present-at-hand subject and a present-at-hand object. One might come closer by saying: Dasein *is* the Being of this "between." But even this remains misleading, because it smuggles in the ontologically undetermined positing of the beings between which the "between" obtains. The between is already grasped as the result of the coming-together of two present-at-hand entities. The ontologically decisive thing is to prevent the blasting apart of the phenomenon in advance — to secure its positive phenomenal content.

The fourth paragraph introduces the concept of the "there" (*Da*). The being that is essentially constituted through Being-in-the-world is itself in each case its "there." The existential spatiality of Dasein, which determines its "place," is itself grounded in Being-in-the-world. "Here" and "there" are possible only in a "there" — only when a being that, as the Being of the "there," has disclosed spatiality. This being carries in its ownmost Being the character of *un-closedness*. The expression "there" means this essential *disclosedness* (*Erschlossenheit*). Through it, this being (Dasein), together with the Being-there of a world, is "there" *for itself*. Dasein is its disclosedness.

The fifth paragraph explains the image of the *lumen naturale*. The ontically metaphorical talk of the *lumen naturale* in the human being means nothing other than the existential-ontological structure of this being: that it is in the mode of being its there. Dasein is "illuminated" — not by another being but in such a way that it *itself is the clearing* (*Lichtung*). Only for a being existentially so cleared can present-at-hand entities become accessible in the light, concealed in the dark. Dasein brings its there with it from the start; lacking it, Dasein would not only factically not be but would not be this being at all. Dasein *is* its disclosedness.

The sixth paragraph outlines the chapter's structure. Since the essence of this being is existence, the existential proposition "Dasein is its disclosedness" means at the same time: the Being at issue for this being is to be its "there." The two equiprimordial constitutive ways of being the there are *state-of-mind* (*Befindlichkeit*) and *understanding* (*Verstehen*); their analysis receives phenomenal confirmation through the interpretation of a concrete and important mode of each. State-of-mind and understanding are equiprimordially determined through *discourse* (*Rede*). Part A treats the existential constitution of the there; Part B treats the everyday Being of the there and the falling of Dasein. Under A: §29 (state-of-mind), §30 (fear), §31 (understanding), §32 (understanding and interpretation), §33 (assertion), §34 (discourse and language). Under B: §35 (idle talk), §36 (curiosity), §37 (ambiguity), §38 (falling and thrownness).

#### A. The Existential Constitution of the There

### § 29. Dasein as State-of-Mind

The first paragraph introduces what Heidegger ontologically indicates with the title *Befindlichkeit* (state-of-mind, or attunement): ontically it is the best-known and most everyday thing — *mood* (*Stimmung*), being-attuned. Before all psychology of moods — which moreover lies entirely fallow — this phenomenon must be seen as a fundamental existential and outlined in its structure.

The second paragraph exhibits the universality and inescapability of mood. The undisturbed equanimity of everyday concern, its inhibited ill-humor, the gliding from one to the other, the falling into bad moods — these are ontologically not nothing, even though they may be disregarded as the supposedly most indifferent and fleeting things in Dasein. That moods can be corrupted and can shift only says that Dasein is always already attuned. Even the often sustained, even, and pallid un-attunedness — not to be confused with ill-humor — is far from nothing; in it, Dasein becomes weary of itself. Being has been revealed *as a burden*. Why, one does not know. And Dasein cannot know this, because the disclosive possibilities of cognition reach far too short compared to the primordial disclosing of moods, in which Dasein is brought before its Being as there. Mood makes manifest "how one is and is getting along." In this "how one is," being-attuned brings Being into its "there."

The third paragraph introduces *thrownness* (*Geworfenheit*). In being-attuned, Dasein is always already disclosed as the being to which it was delivered over in its Being — as the Being it has to be in existing. "Disclosed" does not mean "known as such." Precisely in the most indifferent and innocuous everydayness, the Being of Dasein can break forth as the naked "that it is and has to be." The pure "that it is" shows itself; the whence and whither remain in darkness. This character of Dasein's Being — concealed in its whence and whither but in itself all the more unconcealed — we call the *thrownness* of this being into its there. The expression "thrownness" is meant to intimate the facticity of being delivered over. Thrownness is not the factuality of the occurrence of a present-at-hand entity; it is the facticity of Dasein's Being handed over to itself. As a first essential character of state-of-mind: state-of-mind discloses Dasein in its thrownness, and initially and for the most part in the mode of evasive turning-away.

The fourth paragraph identifies the second essential character. Mood is so far from something like the finding of a psychical state that it has the character not of a turning-around-and-back-to-grasping but rather: all immanent reflection can only "find" "experiences" because the there is already disclosed in state-of-mind. "Bare mood" discloses the there more primordially than any cognition, but it also closes it off correspondingly more stubbornly than any not-perceiving. Mood always already discloses Being-in-the-world *as a whole* and first makes possible a directing-oneself-toward. Being-attuned does not initially relate to something psychical; it is not a state "inside" that then mysteriously gets outside and colors things and persons. The second essential character: state-of-mind is an existential fundamental mode of the equiprimordial disclosedness of world, Dasein-with, and existence, because existence itself is essentially Being-in-the-world.

The fifth paragraph identifies a third essential character. State-of-mind contributes to a deeper understanding of the worldhood of the world. What was previously shown (§16) was that the world already disclosed in Being-in-the-world lets intraworldly beings be encountered. This prior disclosedness of the world belonging to Being-in is co-constituted by state-of-mind. In letting-be-encountered, what is first "given" circumspectively is the ready-to-hand within the surrounding world, not "mere sense-data." The threatening first discovers itself circumspectively; a pure observer would never discover threat. Mood has its own disclosive function for the world: it opens up the world as that from which beings press upon us in their involvement-character.

The sixth paragraph concludes §29. State-of-mind discloses Dasein in three ways: (1) in its thrownness (facticity), (2) as the always-already-disclosed whole of Being-in-the-world, (3) as the prior openness to the affecting (*Angänglichkeit*) of intraworldly beings. Mood is never something merely subjective projected outward; it is an existential mode of disclosure of Being-in-the-world.

### § 30. Fear as a Mode of State-of-Mind

The first paragraph treats fear as a concrete phenomenon exemplifying state-of-mind. The analysis is threefold: the *before-which* of fear (*das Wovor*), fearing itself (*das Fürchten*), and the *about-which* of fear (*das Worum*).

The second paragraph analyzes the before-which. That before which we fear is always an intraworldly being — something threatening that approaches from a definite region and comes within the range of nearness. What is detrimental has not yet reached a controllable nearness but is approaching. In approaching, the detrimental radiates a threatening quality. The threatening being has the character of threateningness (*Bedrohlichkeit*). The before-which of fear is encountered as something ready-to-hand or present-at-hand within the world, or as Dasein-with — never as something indeterminate.

The third paragraph analyzes fearing itself. Fearing is not a theoretical ascertaining of something approaching but a mode of state-of-mind that *discovers*. It first discovers the threatening as threatening. Fearing is a circumspective concernful discovering of the threatening that approaches from within the world. In fear, what is environmentally ready-to-hand is found in the mode of not-being-relevant (*Unbewandtnis*) — in an upset bewilderment. Fear disorients circumspection.

The fourth paragraph analyzes the about-which. That about which fear fears is the fearful being itself — Dasein. Only a being for which its Being is an issue can fear. Fear discloses Dasein primarily in its endangeredness, in its being-delivered-over to itself. Fear reveals Dasein as factically *being-in-the-world* in a world where threatening things can approach.

The fifth paragraph briefly notes variations of fear: alarm (*Erschrecken*), horror (*Grauen*), terror (*Entsetzen*) — modifications with respect to the suddenness, unfamiliarity, or inescapability of what threatens. Fear will later be contrasted with *anxiety* (*Angst*) in §40: unlike fear, anxiety has no determinate intraworldly being as its before-which.

### § 31. Dasein as Understanding

The first paragraph states that state-of-mind always has its understanding, and understanding always has its mood. Understanding is the other equiprimordial existential constitutive of the disclosedness of the there. If we interpret understanding as a fundamental existential, this shows that it is conceived as a fundamental mode of Dasein's Being — not as a particular act of cognition among others.

The second paragraph introduces the fundamental existential concept. In state-of-mind, Dasein is disclosed in its thrownness. In understanding, Dasein is disclosed as *ability-to-be* (*Seinkönnen*). Dasein is in each case what it can be and how it is its possibility. The essential possibility of Dasein concerns the modes of concern with the world, of solicitude with Others, and of Being toward itself that have been characterized. Dasein always understands itself from its possibilities. Understanding is the existential Being of Dasein's ownmost ability-to-be, in such a way that this Being in itself discloses what its Being is about. The formal existential structure of understanding shows that it is not a kind of cognition among others but the primordial mode of Dasein's Being.

The third paragraph connects understanding to *projection* (*Entwurf*). Understanding has the existential structure of *projection*. It projects Dasein upon its for-the-sake-of-which just as primordially as upon significance as the worldhood of its current world. The projective character of understanding constitutes Being-in-the-world with respect to the disclosedness of its there as the there of an ability-to-be. Projection is the existential constitution of the leeway (*Spielraum*) of Dasein's factical ability-to-be. As thrown, Dasein is thrown into the mode of Being of projecting. Dasein is always already beyond itself — not toward another being but toward its own possibilities. This Being-structure of the essential "it is about something" we call *projection*. Projection always concerns the full disclosedness of Being-in-the-world; as thrown, Dasein projects itself and is as long as it is in projecting. Projection has nothing to do with relating oneself to a thought-out plan according to which Dasein would arrange its Being; Dasein has in each case already projected itself and is projecting as long as it is. The thrown-projecting character of Dasein — its thrown projection — is the ontological foundation for what will later be thematized as the temporality of Dasein.

The fourth paragraph introduces *sight* (*Sicht*). Understanding, as the disclosure of the for-the-sake-of-which and of significance, constitutes the *sightedness* of Dasein. Circumspection is the sight of concern; considerateness (*Rücksicht*) is the sight of solicitude; transparency (*Durchsichtigkeit*) is the self-understanding of Dasein regarding its own existence. All sight is grounded in understanding. Sight is an existential, not a sensory faculty.

The fifth paragraph concludes with the connection between understanding and the interpretation of Being. As constituting the disclosedness of the there, understanding always concerns the whole basic constitution of Being-in-the-world. As ability-to-be, Being-in is in each case *possible* Being-in-the-world. Not only is the world open as possible significance; the releasing of intraworldly beings also releases them upon their possibilities. The ready-to-hand is discovered as such in its serviceability, usability, detrimentality. The totality of involvements is revealed as the categorial whole of a possibility of the interconnection of the ready-to-hand. But even the "unity" of the manifold of the present-at-hand, of nature, is discoverable only on the basis of a disclosed possibility of it. Understanding, as the projection upon possibilities, is also what first lets the Being of intraworldly beings be discoverable.

### § 32. Understanding and Interpretation

The first paragraph states that understanding, as disclosive projection, constitutes the existentiality of the there. As ability-to-be, Dasein can in its understanding "devote" itself to the world, to Others, or to itself. Understanding can be developed — and this development we call *interpretation* (*Auslegung*). In interpretation, understanding *appropriates* what it understands. Interpretation does not transform understanding into something else; it lets understanding become itself.

The second paragraph explains the structure of interpretation through the ready-to-hand. In dealing with equipment, circumspective interpretation takes what is in each case ready-to-hand as *something* — the "as" (*Als*). In interpretive projection, the ready-to-hand is expressly understood in terms of its in-order-to — i.e., it is taken *as* something. The circumspective "seeing-as" is interpretation. What is encountered ready-to-hand in concern is understood with respect to what it is — i.e., with respect to its involvement. This "with-respect-to-what" (*Woraufhin*) is not first stated propositionally; it is the understanding of involvement within which circumspective concern always already moves.

The third paragraph introduces the three structural moments of interpretation: *fore-having* (*Vorhabe*), *fore-sight* (*Vorsicht*), and *fore-conception* (*Vorgriff*). Every interpretation operates within a previously understood totality of involvements (fore-having); it is guided by a point of view from which what is to be interpreted is approached (fore-sight); and it works with a definite conceptuality toward which what is to be interpreted is pushed (fore-conception). Interpretation is never a presuppositionless grasping of something given. All interpretation is grounded in these three fore-structures.

The fourth paragraph derives the concept of *meaning* (*Sinn*) from interpretation. When intraworldly beings are discovered with the Being of Dasein — i.e., when they have come to be understood — we say they have meaning. But what is understood, strictly taken, is not the meaning but the being, or Being itself. Meaning is that wherein the intelligibility of something maintains itself. What can be articulated in understanding — the *articulable* as such — we call meaning. The concept of meaning embraces the formal framework of what necessarily belongs to what understanding interpretation articulates. Meaning is an existential of Dasein, not a property attached to beings, not something floating behind or hovering above beings. Only Dasein "has" meaning, insofar as the disclosedness of Being-in-the-world can be "filled in" by the beings discoverable in it.

The fifth paragraph addresses the *hermeneutical circle*. All interpretation moves within the fore-structure described; all understanding has the character of projection. The "circle" of understanding is not a *circulus vitiosus* to be avoided but belongs to the structure of meaning itself. What is decisive is not to get out of the circle but to come into it in the right way. The circle is not to be degraded to the level of a vicious circle, not even a tolerated one. In it is hidden a positive possibility of the most primordial kind of knowing. The fulfillment of the fundamental conditions of possible interpretation lies in not allowing fore-having, fore-sight, and fore-conception to be presented by fancies and popular conceptions, but in securing the scientific theme from the things themselves. This is the methodological foundation of all hermeneutics.

### § 33. Assertion as a Derivative Mode of Interpretation

The first paragraph introduces the question of the relationship between interpretation and assertion (*Aussage*). Assertion or statement is a derivative mode of interpretation. Three meanings of assertion are distinguished: (1) pointing-out (*Aufzeigung*) — letting a being be seen from itself; (2) predication — the narrowing of what is shown to a determinate "as what"; (3) communication (*Mitteilung*) — letting another person see what has been pointed out and predicated.

The second paragraph analyzes the first meaning. In assertion as pointing-out, the "subject" of the assertion is not a representation or concept but the being itself — the hammer, the door. Assertion lets the being be seen from itself.

The third paragraph analyzes predication. In predication, what is pointed out is narrowed to a determinate respect. "The hammer is heavy" does not first grasp a pure subject-thing and then add a predicate; rather, the hammer already understood in its ready-to-hand involvement is now made visible in a determinate regard. Predication is grounded in pointing-out, and pointing-out is grounded in interpretation.

The fourth paragraph draws the crucial distinction between the "as" of interpretation and the "as" of assertion. In interpretation — circumspective understanding — the ready-to-hand is understood in terms of its involvement: the *hermeneutical "as"* (*hermeneutisches Als*). In assertion, the being is determined as having certain properties: the *apophantical "as"* (*apophantisches Als*). The apophantical "as" is a *derivative modification* of the hermeneutical "as." In the transition from circumspective interpretation to determining assertion, the ready-to-hand becomes something that can be pointed at and talked about. The referential structure of "in-order-to" is leveled down to the "about-which" of a proposition. The entity is no longer understood as equipment in an involvement-context but as a thing with properties. This leveling-down — the shift from the hermeneutical to the apophantical "as" — is the existential-ontological foundation for the possibility of propositional logic.

The fifth paragraph concludes: assertion is not a free-floating structure but is grounded in and derived from the more primordial disclosive activity of understanding and interpretation. The traditional logic of propositions, which takes assertion as its starting point, has cut itself off from its existential-ontological foundations.

### § 34. Dasein and Discourse. Language

The first paragraph states that the third fundamental existential constitutive of the disclosedness of the there — equally primordial with state-of-mind and understanding — is *discourse* (*Rede*). Discourse is the articulation of intelligibility. The intelligibility of Being-in-the-world — already articulated in understanding — expresses itself as discourse. The totality of meanings (*Bedeutungsganze*) of intelligibility comes to word. To meanings, words are added; words do not first get supplied with meanings.

The second paragraph identifies the structural moments of discourse: the *about-which* of discourse (what is talked about), the *said-as-such* (what is said in the discourse), *communication*, and *making-known* (*Bekundung*). These are not empirical properties picked up from language but existential characters rooted in the constitution of Being of Dasein. Discourse is the existential-ontological foundation of language.

The third paragraph introduces *hearing* (*Hören*) and *keeping-silent* (*Schweigen*) as essential possibilities of discourse. Hearing is constitutive for discourse — not as acoustic perception but as the existential openness of Dasein *to* Others and *to* the world. We initially and for the most part hear what is said "about" something, the equipment, the wind, the creaking door — not pure noise. Keeping silent is another essential possibility of discourse: it does not mean merely being dumb but actively articulating intelligibility in a mode that can say more than a flood of words. In keeping silent, Dasein can give others something to understand more authentically than in chatter.

The fourth paragraph summarizes. Discourse is the articulation of the disclosedness of the there; it is equiprimordial with state-of-mind and understanding. The existential-ontological foundation of language is discourse. Language is the expressed articulation of discourse — the totality of words as a being "within-the-world." Language, as an intraworldly being, can be present-at-hand; but its existential ground lies in discourse. With this, the analysis of the existential constitution of the there (Part A) is complete.

#### B. The Everyday Being of the There and the Falling of Dasein

### § 35. Idle Talk

The first paragraph introduces *idle talk* (*Gerede*) as the everyday mode of discourse. Discourse has the tendency to communicate. In communication, what is talked about in discourse becomes accessible to Others. But in everyday communication, what is talked about need not be directly understood from the things themselves; rather, it is passed along. Talk spreads. The primary relation of Being toward the being talked about is lost; understanding becomes groundless. One has "heard" that things are thus-and-so. Idle talk is discourse that has lost its primary relationship to the being talked about.

The second paragraph states that idle talk does not restrict itself to oral gossip. It spreads also in writing, as *scribbling* (*Geschreibe*). Idle talk is the mode in which average understanding takes up and passes along what has been said, written, or otherwise articulated — without genuine appropriation of the matter. It controls what can be said, how it should be said, and even what one should think about it. The public interpretation effected by idle talk decides in advance what is "understood" and what is not.

The third paragraph insists that idle talk is not a pejorative term. It designates a positive phenomenon constituting the mode of Being of everyday Dasein's understanding and interpretation. Idle talk is the way in which Dasein, as Being-with, initially and for the most part "understands" its world, Others, and itself. It provides a prior understanding — however groundless — without which no further understanding could take place. But it also closes off the possibility of genuine understanding by making everything seem already understood.

### § 36. Curiosity

The first paragraph introduces *curiosity* (*Neugier*) as the everyday mode of the sight belonging to understanding. Understanding has its own kind of sight (§31). In everydayness, this sight takes the form of curiosity — a looking that is not directed toward understanding but toward *mere seeing*. Curiosity does not tarry alongside what is near in order to understand it but is always already abandoning it in favor of the next thing.

The second paragraph identifies three constitutive characters of curiosity: (1) *not-tarrying* (*Unverweilen*) — curiosity does not dwell alongside what is present but is constantly distracted by what is newer; (2) *dispersion* (*Zerstreuung*) into ever-new possibilities — curiosity seeks novelty for its own sake; (3) *never-dwelling-anywhere* (*Aufenthaltslosigkeit*) — curiosity is everywhere and nowhere. Curiosity is uprooted from any genuine place and wanders restlessly. It provides the semblance of a universal understanding of everything while understanding nothing.

### § 37. Ambiguity

The first paragraph introduces *ambiguity* (*Zweideutigkeit*) as the everyday mode of the interpretedness belonging to understanding. When everyone can say everything about everything and when the idle-talk interpretation has decided in advance what counts as genuine understanding, then it is no longer possible to decide what is disclosed in genuine understanding and what is not. This impossibility of distinguishing pertains not only to the world but also to Dasein-with and to Being toward oneself.

The second paragraph observes that ambiguity extends especially to Being-with-one-another. Everyone keeps watch on everyone; everyone knows best what ought to happen, what everyone "would have said." But as soon as something is actually done, ambiguity ensures that the interest of the They has already moved on. Ambiguity constitutes a mode of the They's Being in which everything seems genuinely understood, genuinely grasped, and genuinely expressed — and yet at bottom is not.

### § 38. Falling and Thrownness

The first paragraph introduces the concept of *falling* (*Verfallen*). Idle talk, curiosity, and ambiguity characterize the way in which Dasein is its "there" — its disclosedness — in everydayness. These characters constitute a fundamental mode of Being-in-the-world that Heidegger calls *falling*. The term does not express any negative evaluation; it designates an existential mode of Being-in-the-world in which Dasein is absorbed by the world and the They-interpretation of the world.

The second paragraph specifies the existential-ontological meaning of falling. Falling is Dasein's absorption in the They and the "world" of its concern. Dasein has initially and for the most part fallen away from itself as authentic ability-to-be-a-self and has fallen into the "world." This falling-into (*Verfallenheit an*) is not a fall from a higher, purer state. Dasein does not first exist authentically and then fall. Falling is an essential existential structure: Dasein is initially and for the most part *alongside* the world of its concern.

The third paragraph introduces three characters of the movement of falling: *temptation* (*Versuchung*) — the They-interpretation, with its idle talk, curiosity, and ambiguity, tempts Dasein into falling; *tranquilization* (*Beruhigung*) — the semblance of universal understanding created by idle talk and curiosity leads Dasein to suppose that everything is in the best order; *alienation* (*Entfremdung*) — falling closes off from Dasein its ownmost Being-possibilities and authentic understanding; *plunge* (*Absturz*) — in the pull of the movement of falling, Dasein is dragged along in a self-intensifying momentum. These characters constitute the *turbulence* (*Wirbel*) of falling — the movement in which Dasein is always already caught up in everydayness.

The fourth paragraph draws the fundamental-ontological consequence. Falling reveals a fundamental character of Dasein's Being. In falling, Dasein is delivered over to itself as thrown — but this thrownness takes the form of absorption in the world and the They. Falling is not an incidental event but an essential structure. In falling, Dasein flees from itself — specifically, from the uncanniness (*Unheimlichkeit*) of thrownness. Dasein flees from its ownmost Being-toward-death, from the "not-at-home" character of its existence, into the comfortable familiarity of the They-world. This flight from uncanniness is what drives the movement of falling. The phenomenon of *anxiety* (§40) will later make this structure fully visible.

The fifth and final paragraph of §38 connects falling to the full phenomenon of Being-in-the-world. Being-in-the-world, as concerned-falling Being-alongside the nearest-encountered world, is factically disclosed in state-of-mind, understanding, and discourse. The full constitutive structure of Being-in-the-world has now been exhibited. The analysis has shown: (1) worldhood (the referential totality of significance), (2) the who (the They), and (3) Being-in as such (state-of-mind, understanding, discourse, falling). With this, the preparatory analysis of Being-in-the-world as a whole is completed — provisionally. The next chapter will draw these results together in a unified existential determination of the Being of Dasein as *care* (*Sorge*).


### Sixth Chapter: Care as the Being of Dasein

### § 39. The Question of the Primordial Totality of the Structural Whole of Dasein

The first paragraph recalls that Being-in-the-world is a primordially and constantly whole structure. In the preceding chapters (Division One, Chapters 2–5) it was phenomenally clarified as a whole and, always on this ground, in its constitutive moments. The initially given preview of the whole phenomenon has now lost the emptiness of its first general sketch. But the phenomenal manifoldness of the constitution of the structural whole and its everyday mode of Being can easily obstruct the unitary phenomenological view of the whole as such. This view must be kept all the more free and ready as we now pose the question toward which the preparatory fundamental analysis of Dasein as a whole is striving: how is the totality of the exhibited structural whole to be determined existentially-ontologically?

The second paragraph restates the problem. Dasein exists factically. The question is after the ontological unity of existentiality and facticity — or the essential belonging-together of the latter with the former. Dasein, on the ground of its essentially belonging state-of-mind, has a mode of Being in which it is brought before itself and disclosed to itself in its thrownness. But thrownness is the mode of Being of a being that in each case *is* its possibilities, in such a way that it understands itself in and from them (projects itself upon them). Being-in-the-world, to which Being-alongside the ready-to-hand and Being-with-Others equiprimordially belong, is in each case for the sake of itself. But the self is initially and for the most part inauthentic — the They-self. Being-in-the-world is always already fallen. The average everydayness of Dasein can accordingly be determined as: *fallingly-disclosed, thrown-projecting Being-in-the-world*, for which in its Being-alongside-the-"world" and in Being-with-Others, its ownmost ability-to-be itself is at issue.

The third paragraph poses the guiding question. Can this structural whole of everydayness be grasped in its totality? Can the Being of Dasein be brought out so unitarily that from it the essential equiprimordiality of the exhibited structures becomes intelligible together with the existential modification-possibilities belonging to them? Is there a way to gain this Being phenomenally on the ground of the present approach of the existential analytic?

The fourth paragraph rules out the negative approach. The totality of the structural whole is not to be reached phenomenally by assembling elements — for that would require a blueprint. The Being of Dasein that ontologically carries the structural whole becomes accessible in a full seeing-through of this whole toward a primordially unitary phenomenon that already lies within the whole so that it ontologically founds each structural moment in its structural possibility. The "summarizing" interpretation therefore cannot be a gathering-up of what has been gained so far. Everyday environmental experience, which remains directed ontically and ontologically at intraworldly beings, cannot primordially present Dasein ontically for ontological analysis. Equally, the immanent perception of experiences lacks an ontologically sufficient guiding thread. On the other hand, the Being of Dasein should not be deduced from an idea of the human being.

The fifth paragraph asks what access Dasein itself demands. To the ontological structure of Dasein belongs understanding of Being. Being, it is disclosed to itself in its Being. State-of-mind and understanding constitute the mode of Being of this disclosedness. Is there a *understanding state-of-mind* in Dasein in which it is disclosed to itself in a distinctive way? If the existential analytic is to maintain fundamental clarity about its fundamental-ontological function, then it must seek one of the most far-reaching and most primordial possibilities of disclosing that lies in Dasein itself. The mode of disclosing in which Dasein brings itself before itself must be such that Dasein becomes accessible in a certain simplification. With what is disclosed in this mode, the structural totality of the sought Being must come elementarily to light.

The sixth paragraph identifies anxiety (*Angst*) as the phenomenon meeting these methodological requirements. The working-out of this fundamental state-of-mind takes its departure from the phenomenon of falling and delimits anxiety against the previously analyzed related phenomenon of fear. Anxiety, as a possibility of Being of Dasein, gives — together with the Dasein disclosed in it — the phenomenal ground for the explicit grasping of the primordial Being-totality of Dasein. This Being reveals itself as *care* (*Sorge*). The ontological working-out of this existential ground-phenomenon demands delimitation against phenomena that might initially be identified with care — will, wish, tendency, and urge. Care cannot be derived from these, because they themselves are founded in care.

The seventh paragraph addresses the relation between ontological interpretation and pre-ontological understanding. The ontological interpretation of Dasein as care, like every ontological analysis, lies far from what is accessible to the pre-ontological understanding of Being or even to the ontic acquaintance with beings. That ontological findings seem strange to common understanding — given that only the ontically familiar is known to it — should not be surprising. But even the ontic approach of the here-attempted ontological interpretation of Dasein as care may appear contrived and theoretically fabricated, to say nothing of the supposed violence in setting aside the traditional and proven definition of the human being. Hence a *pre-ontological confirmation* of the existential interpretation is needed — the demonstration that Dasein has early on interpreted itself as care (*cura*), though only pre-ontologically.

The eighth and final paragraph of §39 outlines the remainder of Chapter 6: §40 (the fundamental state-of-mind of anxiety), §41 (the Being of Dasein as care), §42 (confirmation from pre-ontological self-interpretation), §43 (Dasein, worldhood, and reality), §44 (Dasein, disclosedness, and truth).

### § 40. The Fundamental State-of-Mind of Anxiety as a Distinctive Disclosedness of Dasein

The first paragraph introduces anxiety in contrast to fear and to falling. In the analysis of falling, the fleeing of Dasein from itself was exhibited. But not every shrinking-back from something is fear. Fear was analyzed (§30) as a mode of state-of-mind in which the threatening approaches from within the world — from determinate intraworldly beings. But from what does Dasein flee in falling? Not from intraworldly beings; on the contrary, falling is precisely an absorption *in* these. What does falling flee from? It must be something that has the character of threat. That before which Dasein flees is not grasped thematically; it is not encountered as a determinate intraworldly being. But if falling is flight from something, then the turning-toward intraworldly beings that characterizes falling is a turning-*away* from something. What is this something?

The second paragraph introduces the phenomenon of anxiety proper. Anxiety is to be distinguished from fear. The *before-which* (*Wovor*) of anxiety is not a determinate intraworldly being. Nothing that is ready-to-hand or present-at-hand within the world functions as that before which anxiety is anxious. The totality of involvements discovered in circumspection — the significance-structure of the world — collapses into insignificance. The world can offer nothing, and neither can Dasein-with-Others. The intraworldly ready-to-hand sinks into irrelevance. The "world" in which I exist has sunk into insignificance, and the world disclosed in this mode presses upon me with an emptiness that characterizes the *nothing and nowhere*. This "nothing and nowhere" does not mean nihilistic emptiness but rather that intraworldly beings as such are *not relevant*. They are not annihilated but become matters of indifference. In this insignificance of the intraworldly, the world in its worldhood obtrudes itself.

The third paragraph identifies what is disclosed in the before-which of anxiety. That before which anxiety is anxious is *Being-in-the-world itself*. Anxiety is anxious in the face of the world as such. What threatens cannot be identified with any intraworldly thing because the threatening is not something determinate but rather the sheer fact of Being-in-the-world — of being delivered over to a disclosed world. Anxiety differs from fear in that fear is always fear of a definite intraworldly being, while anxiety has no such object. In anxiety, the utter insignificance of intraworldly beings pushes itself upon us, and what oppresses us is not this or that particular being but the world as such in its worldhood. The "nothing" of anxiety — the fact that no particular intraworldly being serves as its object — is itself grounded in the primordially positive: in the world as the world.

The fourth paragraph identifies the *about-which* (*Worum*) of anxiety. That about which anxiety is anxious is *Dasein's own ability-to-be-in-the-world*. Anxiety throws Dasein back upon that about which it is anxious — its authentic ability-to-be-in-the-world. Anxiety individualizes Dasein and discloses it as *solus ipse* — but not as a worldless subject cut off from everything. This existential "solipsism" brings Dasein before its world as world and thereby before itself as Being-in-the-world. In anxiety, Dasein is disclosed to itself in its ownmost ability-to-be — freed from the disguises of the They and from the familiarity of the everyday world. Anxiety discloses Dasein as *possible Being* — as what it can be in its ownmost self, unrelieved by the interpretation of the They.

The fifth paragraph introduces *uncanniness* (*Unheimlichkeit*). In anxiety, Dasein finds itself in the mode of the *un-homelike* (*un-zuhause*). The everyday familiarity of the world — the tranquilized, familiar, at-home-ness constituted by the They — collapses. In the state-of-mind of anxiety, Dasein is "not-at-home." The not-at-home must be conceived existentially-ontologically as the more primordial phenomenon. It is only because Dasein is essentially un-homelike — only because it is in its thrownness exposed to the uncanniness of its finite Being-in-the-world — that it flees into the familiar public They-world. Falling is ontologically flight from the uncanniness of thrownness. Everyday familiarity is the covering over of the essentially uncanny character of Dasein's existence. Anxiety undoes this covering-over and brings Dasein back before the uncanniness of its thrown Being-in-the-world.

The sixth paragraph distinguishes anxiety from the everyday experience of the uncanny. Uncanniness is not first constituted by anxiety; the reverse is the case. Anxiety is the fundamental state-of-mind in which Dasein is brought before its uncanniness. The uncanniness lies always at the ground — even when Dasein is in everyday familiarity. But it is covered over by the everyday absorption in the They-world. The "not-at-home" must be existentially-ontologically grasped as the more primordial phenomenon: Dasein is initially and for the most part not at home, though it covers this over. Anxiety merely strips away the covering.

The seventh paragraph connects anxiety to the phenomenon of falling. The analysis of falling (§38) had exhibited a fleeing of Dasein from itself. Now it becomes clear what it is that Dasein flees from: the uncanniness that lies in Dasein as thrown, as delivered over to itself in its finitude. Falling is a mode of Being-in-the-world that is absorbed in the world and the They — and this absorption is motivated by the flight from the uncanniness of existence. The average everyday "at-home-ness" of Dasein is ontologically the *not-primordial* mode; the uncanny "not-at-home" is the existentially more original.

The eighth paragraph connects anxiety to the question of the totality of Dasein's Being. Anxiety as a fundamental state-of-mind discloses Dasein in its full structural totality: in its thrown Being-alongside (falling), in its projected ability-to-be, and in its thrownness as such. What anxiety makes transparent is the Being of Dasein in the unity of existentiality, facticity, and falling. What is thus disclosed in its totality is the Being of Dasein as *care*. The formal existential structure of care can be read off from what anxiety discloses.

### § 41. The Being of Dasein as Care

The first paragraph formulates the definition of care. The Being of Dasein has been disclosed in anxiety as: *ahead-of-itself-Being-already-in-(the-world-) as Being-alongside (intraworldly beings encountered)* — *Sich-vorweg-schon-sein-in-(der-Welt-) als Sein-bei (innerweltlich begegnendem Seienden)*. This Being fulfills the meaning of the title *care* (*Sorge*). The formal existential totality of Dasein's ontological structural whole is: care.

The second paragraph unpacks the structure. "Ahead-of-itself" (*Sich-vorweg*) captures the existential structure of projection — Dasein's essential Being-toward-possibilities, its existence. "Already-Being-in" (*Schon-sein-in*) captures the existential structure of thrownness — Dasein's facticity. "Being-alongside" (*Sein-bei*) captures the existential structure of falling — Dasein's absorption in the world of concern. Care is the unity of existentiality, facticity, and falling.

The third paragraph clarifies that care is not to be understood as a particular ontic comportment like worrying or caring-for. Care is the formal ontological term for the totality of Dasein's Being. All concrete modes of Dasein's Being — wishing, willing, urging, inclination — are founded in care and are modifications of it. The priority of care over these specific modes is not a priority of praxis over theory; care is prior to and encompasses both.

The fourth paragraph insists that the definition of Dasein's Being as care is not an arbitrary construction but has its phenomenal ground in the disclosiveness of anxiety. Anxiety, as the fundamental state-of-mind, first makes transparent the primordial structural totality of Dasein. Care is not a property added to Dasein but the most basic existential determination of what Dasein *is*.

### § 42. The Confirmation of the Existential Interpretation of Dasein as Care from the Pre-ontological Self-Interpretation of Dasein

This section provides a corroboration of the interpretation through an ancient fable. Heidegger cites the story of *Cura* (Care) from Hyginus. In the fable, *Cura* fashions a figure from clay and asks Jupiter to give it spirit. A dispute arises about who should name the creature. Saturn decides: Jupiter gave spirit, so he shall receive it at death; Earth (Tellus) gave the body, so she shall receive it at death; but since *Cura* first fashioned the being, she shall possess it as long as it lives. And the being shall be called *homo*, because it was made from *humus* (earth).

Heidegger reads this as a pre-ontological testimony that the being we call human is, for as long as it lives, in the possession of care. The pre-ontological self-interpretation of Dasein has always already understood the human being as defined by care. This does not "prove" the existential analysis but shows that it corresponds to Dasein's own deepest self-understanding.

### § 43. Dasein, Worldhood, and Reality

This lengthy section, divided into three subsections, addresses the traditional "problem of reality" — the question whether an "external world" exists and how its existence can be proved. The fundamental thesis is that the problem is misconceived because it presupposes the very subject-object split that the analysis of Being-in-the-world has shown to be derivative.

**a) Reality as Problem of Being and the Provability of the "External World."**

The first paragraph of subsection (a) states the issue. With the question of reality (*Realität*), the attempt is made to give an ontological characterization of the intraworldly being that is present-at-hand. But the question has traditionally been entangled with the epistemological question whether the "external world" can be proved to exist. Both questions — the ontological and the epistemological — must be disentangled.

The second paragraph takes up the demand for a proof. If Dasein is understood correctly as Being-in-the-world, then the question of whether the world "really exists" is senseless. It is senseless not because it cannot be answered but because Dasein, as Being-in-the-world, always already is alongside intraworldly beings. The demand for a proof presupposes that Dasein is initially a worldless subject that must somehow reach out to an external world. But this presupposition has been shown to be ontologically groundless. Kant called it a "scandal of philosophy" that no satisfactory proof of the existence of the external world had yet been given. Heidegger counters that the real scandal consists not in the absence of a proof but in the fact that such proofs are *expected and attempted again and again*. The demand itself testifies to the unclarity of the ontological foundations.

The third paragraph insists that what needs to be shown is not that and how an "external world" exists but rather that and why Dasein, as Being-in-the-world, has the tendency to *bury* the "external world" in nullity epistemologically — first to prove it and then, failing that, to take it "on faith." The reason lies in the falling of Dasein: in falling, Dasein is absorbed in intraworldly beings and understands its own Being from these beings, specifically in the mode of presence-at-hand. The subject-object schema is the ontologically inadequate product of this falling self-interpretation.

**b) Reality as an Ontological Problem.**

The first paragraph of subsection (b) states that the question of what "reality" or "the real" means in terms of mode of Being is legitimate. Reality (*Realität*) in the traditional sense — the Being of intraworldly beings grasped as *res* — is itself in need of ontological clarification. If reality is equated with Being in general, then the question of Being becomes the question of reality. But this equation covers over the multiplicity of modes of Being and the ontological difference.

The second paragraph reviews the positions of realism and idealism. Realism holds that reality is independent of consciousness; idealism holds that reality is constituted by consciousness. But both remain within the framework that the analysis of Being-in-the-world has shown to be derivative. Realism tries to explain the reality of the "real" but fails because it leaves the meaning of Being unclarified. Idealism, insofar as it holds that Being is constituted by consciousness, has an advantage in recognizing that Being cannot be explained by beings — but it too fails to pose the question of Being as such. Both realism and idealism operate with unclarified concepts of Being and of the subject.

The third paragraph states the positive thesis. If "idealism" amounts to the understanding that Being can never be explained by beings but is always already the "transcendental" for every being, then in idealism lies the only right possibility of philosophical problematic. Aristotle was no less an "idealist" than Kant in this sense. But if idealism means the reduction of all beings to a subject or consciousness whose Being remains undetermined and is at best characterized negatively as "not a thing," then this idealism is methodologically no less naive than the most crude realism.

**c) Reality and Care.**

The first paragraph of subsection (c) draws the consequence. Reality, as a mode of Being of intraworldly beings, is accessible only to a being whose Being has the character of care. Beings are uncovered as real only on the ground of a world already disclosed. The independence of the "real" from experience and cognition does not mean independence from care-structured Being-in-the-world. It means that beings can be discovered *within* the disclosed world as what they are "in themselves." The in-itself of the real — the fact that beings are encountered as having their own Being independent of our noticing them — is itself ontologically grounded in the disclosedness of Being-in-the-world.

The second paragraph addresses the question of whether beings "are" even when no Dasein exists. Beings can be as they are only if there "is" something like understanding of Being — and understanding of Being is a mode of Being of Dasein. Beings can be without Dasein; but *Being* — the intelligibility of what it means to be — "is" only so long as Dasein is. If no Dasein exists, there is no Being — which does not mean that beings would not exist, but that there would be nothing like the disclosure of Being. This is not a form of subjective idealism: Heidegger does not deny that beings exist independently of Dasein. What he denies is that the *meaning* of Being — what it means for beings to be — can be intelligible independently of a being for whom Being is an issue.

The third and final paragraph of §43 states that the question of reality, in the sense of the ontological question about the meaning of Being of intraworldly beings, has been answered: reality is a mode of Being that is grounded in the disclosedness of Being-in-the-world. The epistemological "problem" of the external world dissolves when Being-in-the-world is recognized as Dasein's fundamental constitution.

### § 44. Dasein, Disclosedness, and Truth

This section, divided into three subsections, provides the existential-ontological foundation for the phenomenon of truth. It is one of the most consequential sections in the entire work, as it reconceives truth from the ground up.

**a) The Traditional Concept of Truth and its Ontological Foundations.**

The first paragraph identifies the three theses defining the traditional concept of truth: (1) the "locus" of truth is the proposition (judgment); (2) the essence of truth lies in the "correspondence" (*adaequatio*) of the judgment with its object; (3) Aristotle, the "father of logic," assigned truth to the judgment as its primordial locus and set the definition of truth as correspondence in motion. Heidegger will call all three theses into question.

The second paragraph traces the concept of correspondence. *Adaequatio intellectus et rei* — correspondence of intellect and thing. This formula functions in both directions: the truth of the thing (its conformity to its idea or essence) and the truth of knowledge (its conformity to the thing). Both senses presuppose a relation between two beings (intellect and thing) that are to "agree." But what kind of agreement is this? A coin "agrees" with another coin when they look the same. But knowledge does not "look like" its object. The relation of correspondence is therefore not a relation of likeness between two entities. What does it mean for a statement to be "true" — to correspond to its object?

The third paragraph clarifies through a phenomenological example. A statement — "the picture on the wall is hanging askew" — is confirmed when one turns to the wall and sees the picture hanging askew. What is confirmed is not a relation between a representation "inside" and a thing "outside" but rather that the asserting — the Being-toward the thing asserted about — is a *discovering* of the being as it is in itself. The assertion is true not because it matches an inner representation to an outer object but because it *discovers* the being in the way it is. Truth is *uncovering* (*Entdecken*) — letting the being be seen as it is from itself. The truth of a proposition is grounded in a more primordial phenomenon: the discoveredness (*Entdecktheit*) of beings.

The fourth paragraph locates the foundation. Beings can be discovered — shown in their uncoveredness — only by a being that is itself disclosed (*erschlossen*). Discoveredness (the being-uncovered of beings) is grounded in disclosedness (the being-disclosed of Dasein). Disclosedness, however, is constituted by state-of-mind, understanding, and discourse. The primordial "place" of truth is therefore not the proposition but the *disclosedness of Dasein* — the being-opened-up of Being-in-the-world. Propositional truth is *derivative* from this primordial disclosedness.

**b) The Primordial Phenomenon of Truth and the Derivativeness of the Traditional Concept.**

The first paragraph of subsection (b) returns to the Greek concept of truth as *alētheia* — *unconcealment* (*Unverborgenheit*). To say that a proposition is true is to say that it discovers the being it is about — it takes the being out of concealment and lets it be seen as unconcealed (*alēthes*). Uncovering is a mode of Being of Dasein — specifically, of Being-in-the-world. Dasein, as Being-in-the-world, is essentially *disclosive* — it is in the truth.

The second paragraph formulates the thesis: *Dasein is "in the truth."* This is an existential-ontological statement, not a moral or epistemological claim. It means that to the constitution of Being of Dasein — as Being-in-the-world that understands and is attuned — there belongs disclosedness. In disclosedness, there lies: (1) the disclosedness of Being-in-the-world as such — the disclosure of world, Being-with, and existence; (2) the discoveredness of intraworldly beings; (3) the disclosure of Dasein's own Being. All three are constitutive of "being in the truth."

The third paragraph adds the equally primordial counter-thesis: *Dasein is equally primordially "in untruth."* Disclosedness always co-includes closedness-off. To the constitution of Dasein belongs falling — the tendency to cover over, to close off, to misinterpret. Idle talk, curiosity, and ambiguity are modes of closing-off that belong to Dasein's everydayness. Because Dasein is essentially falling, it is initially and for the most part in untruth — in the mode of covering-over, disguise, and closedness. Dasein is in the truth *and* in untruth equiprimordially. Truth must be *wrested* from beings — torn from concealment. All uncovering is a counter-movement to the essential tendency of Dasein to cover over.

The fourth paragraph discusses the primordial Greek understanding. The Greeks understood truth as something *stolen* from concealment — as *alētheia*, literally "un-concealment." This implies that beings are initially and for the most part concealed or disguised, and that truth is an *achievement* against the grain. Heraclitus's *physis kryptesthai philei* — "nature loves to conceal itself" — testifies to this primordial understanding. The pre-Socratic insight was progressively covered over as the concept of truth migrated from unconcealment to correspondence, from the disclosedness of Being-in-the-world to the correctness of propositions.

The fifth paragraph addresses assertion and its derivative truth. Assertion (*Aussage*), as analyzed in §33, is a derivative mode of interpretation and thereby of understanding. The assertion's truth — its letting-be-seen of the being as it is — is a derivative mode of uncovering, founded in the more primordial disclosedness of Being-in-the-world. Propositional truth is not the primary phenomenon; it is a narrowing and leveling-down of the more originary disclosive event. When assertion becomes the dominant mode of truth, and when propositional correspondence becomes the definition, the primordial phenomenon of truth as unconcealment is covered over.

**c) The Mode of Being of Truth and the Presupposition of Truth.**

The first paragraph of subsection (c) states the ontological thesis about truth: truth "is" only insofar as and as long as Dasein is. Before all Dasein was, and after all Dasein will be, there were and will be no "truths" — not because beings would not be, but because truth, as unconcealment and uncovering, is a mode of Being of Dasein. This does not mean that truth is "subjective" in the sense of being arbitrary or dependent on the whim of a subject. It means that the *Being* of truth — the happening of unconcealment — requires a being for whom Being is disclosed.

The second paragraph addresses an apparent difficulty. Newton's laws were not "true" before they were discovered — but they were not false either. Before Newton, there were no truths about these laws; but the beings that these laws disclose — and the relations between them — *were* as they were discovered to be. The beings themselves are independent of Dasein; but truth as unconcealment is not. What Newton discovered was already "there" in the sense that beings comported themselves in accordance with these laws. But the uncovering of these laws — their coming-to-truth — required a being with the existential structure of Being-in-the-world.

The third paragraph addresses the "presupposition" of truth. That there "is" truth belongs to the conditions of Dasein's Being. Truth is presupposed because Dasein, as Being-in-the-world, essentially involves understanding of Being, and understanding is always projection upon possibilities. Because Dasein exists — because there is a being for which its Being is an issue — truth "is." The presupposition of truth is not a logical assumption or methodological postulate; it is an existential-ontological structure. Truth is presupposed in the very Being of Dasein as care.

The fourth paragraph draws the final consequence. Every truth is relative to the Being of Dasein. This does not mean relativism — it does not mean that truth is "merely subjective" or that one opinion is as good as another. It means that the mode of Being of truth — as uncovering and unconcealment — is existentially constituted. The question of the "eternal truths" is not answered but *displaced*: it can be legitimately discussed only when it is shown that and why truth can maintain a supra-temporal validity even though its mode of Being is existentially temporal. The full answering of this question requires the clarification of the meaning of Being in general — which is the task of the never-completed Third Division.

This concludes Division One. The entirety of the preparatory fundamental analysis of Dasein has now been accomplished: Dasein has been exhibited as Being-in-the-world, whose Being is care, whose constitutive structures are existentiality (projection), facticity (thrownness), and falling, whose "there" is disclosed through state-of-mind, understanding, and discourse, and whose being "in the truth" is always accompanied by being "in untruth." Division Two will repeat these analyses at a deeper level by exhibiting the meaning of care as *temporality*.


## Second Division: Dasein and Temporality

### § 45. The Result of the Preparatory Fundamental Analysis of Dasein and the Task of a Primordial Existential Interpretation of this Being

The first paragraph recapitulates. What was found: the fundamental constitution of Dasein — Being-in-the-world — whose essential structures center in disclosedness. The totality of this structural whole revealed itself as *care*. In care, the Being of Dasein is enclosed. What is sought: the answer to the question of the meaning of Being in general, and before that, the possibility of a radical working-out of this fundamental question of all ontology. The laying-bare of the horizon in which something like Being in general becomes intelligible amounts to the clarification of the possibility of the understanding of Being that itself belongs to the constitution of Dasein.

The second paragraph raises the question of primordiality. May we claim the ontological characterization of Dasein as care as a *primordial* interpretation? Every interpretation has its fore-having, fore-sight, and fore-conception. An original ontological interpretation demands not only a phenomenally secured hermeneutical situation but also assurance that the whole of the thematic being has been brought into the fore-having. The fore-sight on Being must hit upon Being with respect to the unity of the relevant structural moments. Only then can the question of the *meaning* of the unity of the Being-totality of the whole being be posed and answered with phenomenal certainty.

The third paragraph identifies two deficiencies in the analysis so far. First, the interpretation has so far been conducted from the starting point of average everydayness — the analysis of indifferent or inauthentic existing. While concrete determination of existentiality was reached, the ontological characterization of the constitution of existence remained afflicted with an essential deficiency: existence means ability-to-be, but also *authentic* ability-to-be. As long as the existential structure of authentic ability-to-be is not incorporated into the idea of existence, the fore-sight guiding the interpretation lacks primordiality.

The fourth paragraph identifies the second deficiency: the question of Dasein's *totality*. The analysis has exhibited Dasein's structures but has not yet grasped Dasein as a *whole*. As long as Dasein exists, something always "stands out" — Dasein is always still ahead of itself, still underway. Dasein never achieves its wholeness while existing — or so it seems. The question is: can Dasein's Being be grasped as a whole at all? This question forces the analysis to confront *death* as the existential phenomenon that first makes the question of Dasein's totality possible.

The fifth paragraph outlines the plan of Division Two. Chapter 1 (§§46–53): the possible whole-Being of Dasein and Being-toward-death. Chapter 2 (§§54–60): the Dasein-attestation of an authentic ability-to-be — conscience and resoluteness. Chapter 3 (§§61–66): the authentic whole-ability-to-be of Dasein and temporality as the ontological meaning of care. Chapters 4–6 (§§67–83): temporality and everydayness, historicality, and within-time-ness.

### First Chapter: The Possible Whole-Being of Dasein and Being-toward-Death

### § 46. The Seeming Impossibility of an Ontological Grasping and Determination of the Dasein-like Whole-Being

The first paragraph states that the inadequacy of the hermeneutical situation from which the preceding analysis of Dasein sprang is to be overcome. With respect to the necessarily to-be-won fore-having of the *whole* of Dasein, the question must be asked whether this being as existing can become accessible in its whole-Being at all. Weighty grounds seem to speak for the impossibility of the demanded fore-giving, grounds lying in the constitution of Being of Dasein itself.

The second paragraph identifies the apparent contradiction. Care, which forms the totality of the structural whole of Dasein, seems in its ontological meaning to contradict a possible whole-Being of this being. The primary moment of care — the "ahead-of-itself" — means that Dasein exists in each case for the sake of itself. "As long as it is," until its end, it comports itself toward its ability-to-be. Even when, still existing, it has nothing more "before it" and has "settled its accounts," its Being is still determined by the "ahead-of-itself." Hopelessness, for example, does not tear Dasein away from its possibilities but is itself a mode of Being toward these possibilities. The disillusioned "being-prepared-for-everything" equally harbors the "ahead-of-itself." This structural moment of care says unambiguously that in Dasein there is always still something *outstanding* that has not yet become "actual" as an ability-to-be of its own. In the essence of the fundamental constitution of Dasein lies a constant *incompleteness*. The un-wholeness signifies an outstanding of ability-to-be.

The third paragraph sharpens the difficulty. As soon as Dasein "exists" in such a way that absolutely nothing more stands out in it, then it has also at one stroke become a *no-longer-Being-there*. The removal of the Being-outstanding amounts to the annihilation of its Being. As long as Dasein is as a being, it has never attained its "wholeness." If it gains wholeness, this gaining becomes the utter loss of Being-in-the-world. As a being, it can then never more be experienced.

The fourth paragraph locates the ground of this impossibility. The ground of the impossibility of ontically experiencing Dasein as a being-whole and consequently of ontologically determining it in its whole-Being lies not in an imperfection of our cognitive faculty. The hindrance lies on the side of the Being of this being. What can never even be as that which an experiencing of Dasein pretends to grasp is in principle withdrawn from experienceability. But Heidegger then raises critical questions against his own argument: was the "not-yet" grasped in a genuinely existential sense? Was "end" and "totality" discussed in phenomenal conformity to Dasein? Did "death" have a biological or existential-ontological meaning? These questions demand answers before the problem of Dasein's totality can be dismissed as futile.

### § 47. The Experienceability of the Death of Others and the Possibility of Grasping a Whole Dasein

The first paragraph considers whether the death of Others might provide the needed access to Dasein's wholeness. The attaining of the wholeness of Dasein in death is at the same time the loss of the Being of the there. The transition to no-longer-Being-there lifts Dasein precisely out of the possibility of experiencing this transition and understanding it as experienced. Such may indeed be denied to the respective Dasein regarding itself. But the death of Others is all the more penetrating. A termination of Dasein becomes thereby "objectively" accessible. Dasein, since it is essentially Being-with-Others, can gain an experience of the death of Others.

The second paragraph analyzes what this experience actually gives. The Other who has died has left "our" world and left behind those who remain. But the deceased is accessible from this world only as an entity that has become no longer Dasein — as something present-at-hand (a corpse) or as the departed who is still an object of concerned solicitude (funeral rites, mourning, commemoration). But precisely in this mourning and commemoration, we do not experience the dying of the Other. We are at best alongside the death of the Other. We are never able to genuinely experience the Other's loss-of-Being. The dying of the Other is not something we undergo — it is at most something we observe. And even this observation gives us only the transition from a being having the mode of Being of Dasein to a no-longer-Being-there (or a being-still-present-at-hand of a mere material thing).

The third paragraph draws the critical conclusion. The attempt to substitute the experience of the death of Others for the existential analysis of one's own Being-toward-death fails. No one can take away another's dying. Someone can "go to death for another" — but this always means: sacrificing oneself for the other *in a determinate affair*. Such dying *for* the Other can never mean that the Other has thereby been in the least relieved of death. Dying is something that each Dasein itself must take upon itself in each case. Death is, insofar as it "is," essentially in each case mine. No one can relieve me of my dying. The death of the Other is not accessible as a substitute for an existential analysis of Dasein's own Being-toward-death.

### § 48. Outstanding, End, and Totality

The first paragraph recalls the apparent difficulty: as long as Dasein is, something is always still outstanding — an ability-to-be that has not yet become "actual." It seems that the "not-yet" belongs to Dasein as a missing piece. But is this the right way to understand the not-yet? Heidegger examines several models to clarify what "outstanding," "end," and "totality" mean when applied to Dasein.

The second paragraph considers the model of the outstanding debt (*Ausstand*). A debt is an outstanding sum that is "not yet" paid. The totality is reached when the sum is brought together — when the last partial payment is made. The whole is the "togetherness" of the parts. But Dasein's not-yet is not like an outstanding payment that, once made, "fills up" the whole. Dasein is not a sum of moments; its not-yet is not a piece that is somewhere else and merely not yet brought together with the rest.

The third paragraph considers the model of the unripe fruit. The fruit "is" not yet ripe. But the not-yet of unripeness belongs to the fruit itself as a constitutive moment. The fruit does not first exist and then have unripeness added to it; rather, the fruit *is* its unripeness. It is on the way to ripeness, and this being-on-the-way belongs to its very being. Similarly, Dasein does not first exist and then "also" have a not-yet; the not-yet belongs constitutively to Dasein as long as it is. As long as Dasein is, it *is* its not-yet. But the analogy has limits: the fruit achieves its ripeness by arriving at its end; for Dasein, "arriving at its end" means ceasing to be.

The fourth paragraph addresses the concept of *ending* (*Enden*). Ending does not necessarily mean fulfilling or completing. Rain ends — but that does not mean the rain has thereby reached its fulfillment. A road ends — but the end of the road does not constitute the road's completion. Ending can mean simply ceasing. Death as the "end" of Dasein must not be understood as the completing or fulfilling of a process but as the existential possibility that limits Dasein's Being from the outset. The not-yet of Dasein is not like an outstanding piece that will eventually arrive; it is the way Dasein exists — as finite, as Being-toward-the-end.

The fifth and final paragraph of §48 draws the conclusion. The "not-yet" that belongs to Dasein is not to be grasped as a piece still outstanding to be added to a whole already present-at-hand. Rather, Dasein, as the being it is, is always already its not-yet. Likewise, "ending" must not be interpreted as completion but as a limit. And "totality" does not mean the togetherness of all pieces. These existential-ontological clarifications prepare the ground for a genuine phenomenology of death.

### § 49. The Delimitation of the Existential Analysis of Death against Possible Other Interpretations

This section distinguishes the existential analysis from biological, medical, theological, and biographical approaches to death. Biology thematizes demise (*Ableben*) — the ending of a living organism. Theology interprets death within the framework of sin and salvation. Biography narrates the factual circumstances of a particular dying. None of these approaches reaches the existential-ontological phenomenon of death as a structure of Dasein's Being. Existential death is not the event of demise but the structure of Being-toward-the-end that belongs to Dasein as long as it exists.

### § 50. The Preliminary Sketch of the Existential-Ontological Structure of Death

The first paragraph formulates the existential concept of death. Death is the *possibility of the absolute impossibility of Dasein* — the possibility of no-longer-being-able-to-be-there. Death is not something that will happen "someday" but a possibility that Dasein carries with it always. As this ownmost possibility, death is *certain* yet *indefinite* as to its "when." Death is *non-relational* (it cannot be taken over by another), *unsurpassable* (there is no getting past it), and *certain*.

The second paragraph states the full existential determination: death is the ownmost, non-relational, certain, and yet indefinite, unsurpassable possibility of Dasein. Being-toward-death is an essential structure of Dasein's Being — not an attitude adopted toward the event of dying but the way Dasein exists as finite.

### § 51. Being-toward-Death and the Everydayness of Dasein

This section analyzes how everyday Dasein relates to death. The They covers over death through the everyday interpretation: "one dies" (*man stirbt*). The "one" distributes death — anyone can die, but for the time being, not I. The They transforms death from an ownmost, non-relational possibility into an anonymous, public event that "has not yet happened" to me. This covering-over is not an error but a structural feature of everyday Being-toward-death: falling is flight from the uncanniness of Being-toward-death. The everyday interpretation provides tranquilization: death is "known" but its existential significance is evaded.

### § 52. Everyday Being-toward-the-End and the Full Existential Concept of Death

This section consolidates the full concept. Everyday Being-toward-death has the characters of fleeing concealment, evasive certainty, and indeterminate "not yet." The existential-ontological concept of death is: *Death, as the end of Dasein, is the ownmost, non-relational, certain, and as such indefinite, unsurpassable possibility of Dasein.* Death, as the end of Dasein, *is* in the Being of this being toward its end.

### § 53. Existential Projection of an Authentic Being-toward-Death

This section asks: what would an *authentic* Being-toward-death look like? Authentic Being-toward-death does not mean "thinking about death" or brooding over it. It means *running ahead* (*Vorlaufen*) into death as possibility — letting death stand as a possibility without weakening, covering over, or fleeing from it. Running ahead into death discloses: (1) the ownmost character of the possibility — death is *mine* in an unreplaceable way; (2) the non-relational character — in the face of death, all relations to Others become irrelevant in a certain sense; Dasein is individuated; (3) the unsurpassable character — no possibility lies beyond death; (4) the certainty — death is the most certain possibility; (5) the indefiniteness — death can come at any moment. Running ahead into the unsurpassable possibility wrests Dasein from the They and brings it before itself as finite ability-to-be. It opens up the authentic existential understanding of Dasein as a whole. Running ahead is not a morbid preoccupation; it is the existential condition for grasping Dasein in its totality and freeing it for authentic existence.

### Second Chapter: The Dasein-Attestation of an Authentic Ability-to-Be and Resoluteness

### § 54. The Problem of the Attestation of an Authentic Existentiell Possibility

The first paragraph raises the problem. The existential projection of an authentic Being-toward-death (§53) showed that running ahead into death discloses Dasein in its ownmost, non-relational, unsurpassable, certain and indefinite possibility. But this authentic Being-toward-death was won existentially-ontologically — it was projected as an existential possibility. The question arises: is this existential possibility also attested (*bezeugt*) in Dasein itself? Is there a way in which Dasein *gives itself to understand* from its ownmost existing that authentic existence is not merely a conceptual construction but a real possibility rooted in Dasein's own Being?

The second paragraph specifies the requirements. What is sought is an *attestation* (*Bezeugung*) — not a theoretical proof but a disclosure in which Dasein shows itself in its authentic ability-to-be. This attestation must come from Dasein itself. It must disclose Dasein in its ownmost self against the dispersal into the They. And it must do so in a way that is itself a mode of Dasein's Being — not a theoretical observation from outside.

The third paragraph identifies the phenomenon: *conscience* (*Gewissen*). The existential-ontological analysis of conscience is not a moral theory. It aims at uncovering the ontological structure of conscience as a mode of disclosedness belonging to Dasein.

### § 55. The Existential-Ontological Foundations of Conscience

The first paragraph states that conscience gives something to understand — it discloses. It must therefore be analyzed in terms of the constitutive structures of disclosedness: state-of-mind, understanding, discourse, and falling. Conscience is a mode of *discourse* (*Rede*): specifically, it has the character of a *call* (*Ruf*). The analysis must determine who calls, who is called, what the call says, and how the called one hears.

The second paragraph answers: what is called is Dasein itself — the They-self, absorbed in the public world of concern. The call reaches Dasein in its everyday lostness and calls it back. But the call does not address Dasein in a public, social manner; it passes over the They-self and strikes Dasein in its ownmost self.

### § 56. The Call-Character of Conscience

The first paragraph describes the mode of the call. The call of conscience reaches Dasein in its everyday Being-alongside and Being-lost-in-the-They. It summons Dasein out of the They and calls it to its ownmost ability-to-be. The call passes over all public self-interpretation. It does not discuss anything; it does not give information; it does not narrate; it does not prescribe norms. The call is discourse in the mode of *keeping-silent* (*Schweigen*).

The second paragraph asks about the content of the call. What does the call say? Taken strictly: *nothing*. The call does not give Dasein something to calculate with; it does not propose an ideal of existence. It calls Dasein to itself — to its ownmost ability-to-be — and that is all. The call is "silent" — it has no propositional content. But in this silence it speaks more powerfully than any public discourse: it calls Dasein before itself as thrown projection.

The third paragraph asks who the caller is. The caller is not another person, not a social institution, not the "voice of God." The caller is *Dasein itself* — but not the They-self. The caller is Dasein in its *uncanniness*: thrown, individuated, not-at-home. In the call, Dasein calls itself from its uncanny thrownness and Being-toward-death to its ownmost ability-to-be. The call comes from Dasein and yet from beyond Dasein's everyday self — it comes from the uncanniness that lies at the ground of Dasein's Being and is covered over by falling.

### § 57. Conscience as the Call of Care

The first paragraph identifies what calls in conscience: *care* itself. Care, as the Being of Dasein, constitutes the unity of existentiality, facticity, and falling. In the call of conscience, care summons Dasein from its absorption in the They-world back to its ownmost ability-to-be. The caller — Dasein in its uncanniness — is Dasein as thrown (facticity), and what is called is Dasein as able-to-be (existentiality). The call occurs from falling and calls Dasein away from falling. Conscience is thus care calling Dasein to its own finitude.

The second paragraph notes that the call comes from the *not-at-home* — from the uncanniness that Dasein flees in falling. In conscience, the covering-over effected by the They is pierced. The "voice" of conscience is the silent call of care, arising from the depths of Dasein's thrown Being-in-the-world.

### § 58. Understanding the Call and Guilt

The first paragraph identifies the appropriate response to the call: *wanting-to-have-a-conscience* (*Gewissen-haben-wollen*). This is not a moral resolution to follow rules but the readiness to let oneself be called back from the They to one's ownmost ability-to-be. It is the existential openness to the call.

The second paragraph introduces the concept of *guilt* (*Schuld*). Understanding the call means understanding oneself as *guilty*. But "guilty" here does not mean moral guilt — transgression of a law, indebtedness to another, being the "cause" of a lack in another's existence. These ontic meanings of guilt presuppose a more primordial existential concept of guilt.

The third paragraph formulates the existential concept. Dasein is guilty in the sense of being the *ground of a nullity* (*Grundsein einer Nichtigkeit*). This has a double aspect: (1) Dasein, as thrown, has not itself laid its own ground. It is delivered over to its existence without having chosen it. It finds itself *already in* its there — already having possibilities, already in a world, already with a past — none of which it has itself originated. Dasein is the ground of its own Being, but a ground that it has not posited and can never get behind. (2) Dasein, as projecting, is always choosing *some* possibilities and thereby *not*-choosing others. Every projection involves a *not* — a closing-off of possibilities not seized. Dasein, as thrown projection, is the null ground of nullity: it is a ground it has not laid, and a projecting that always involves exclusion and negation.

The fourth paragraph draws the consequence. This existential guilt — being the null ground of a nullity — is not something that Dasein sometimes is and sometimes is not. It belongs to the structure of care itself. As thrown projection, Dasein is *essentially* guilty. The call of conscience calls Dasein to take over this essential guilt — to understand itself as the finite, null ground of its own existence. Understanding the call means: understanding oneself as essentially finite, as never fully in command, as always already behind oneself.

### § 59. The Existential Interpretation of Conscience and the Vulgar Interpretation

The first paragraph contrasts the existential interpretation with the "vulgar" or ordinary interpretation of conscience. The vulgar interpretation sees conscience as a faculty of moral judgment — a voice that condemns wrong actions and approves right ones. This interpretation takes conscience as a present-at-hand capacity of the subject that occasionally makes itself felt through feelings of guilt or approval. Heidegger does not deny the ontic phenomena the vulgar interpretation addresses. But he insists that these ontic phenomena are founded in the more primordial existential structure now exhibited.

The second paragraph argues that the vulgar interpretation operates within the horizon of the They. The "bad conscience" that follows a transgression is interpreted from the standpoint of public norms — what "one" should or should not have done. The "good conscience" is the tranquilized reassurance that one is in conformity with the They. Both are modes of the They's interpretation of conscience and cover over the more primordial call. The vulgar interpretation is not wrong but *derivative* — a falling-level interpretation of a more primordial phenomenon.

The third paragraph insists that the existential interpretation does not seek to correct or improve the moral concept of conscience. Its aim is different: to exhibit the ontological structure of the phenomenon that makes the moral interpretation — and every other interpretation — possible. The existential analysis is not a substitute for ethics but a laying-bare of the existential foundations upon which any ethics must rest.

### § 60. The Existential Structure of the Authentic Ability-to-Be Attested in Conscience

The first paragraph draws together the results. The attestation of an authentic ability-to-be — what is disclosed in wanting-to-have-a-conscience — has now been exhibited in its existential structure. This existential structure Heidegger calls *resoluteness* (*Entschlossenheit*).

The second paragraph defines resoluteness. Resoluteness is the *wanting-to-have-a-conscience* that understands itself as called to its ownmost ability-to-be. It is the reticent self-projection upon one's ownmost Being-guilty — reticent because it does not chatter about guilt but silently takes it over; self-projecting because it projects Dasein upon its ownmost possibilities rather than upon the possibilities prescribed by the They. Resoluteness is not a particular decision about a particular course of action. It is the *disclosedness* of Dasein in which it opens itself to its ownmost *situation* (*Situation*) — the existential "there" that calls for a response.

The third paragraph distinguishes *situation* from mere circumstance. The They knows no situation — only generic occurrences. Resoluteness first discloses the situation as the concrete, individualized "there" in which Dasein finds itself and to which it must respond from its ownmost ability-to-be. In resoluteness, the They-self is taken back and Dasein stands in its own finitude. But resoluteness does not detach Dasein from the world; it first gives Dasein its genuine Being-alongside-the-world and its authentic Being-with-Others. Only in resoluteness can Dasein genuinely encounter Others — not as objects of the They's concern but as co-existing Dasein to be met with liberating solicitude.

The fourth paragraph states the connection to Being-toward-death. Resoluteness, as here exhibited, is not yet linked to the full phenomenon of authentic Being-toward-death. That linkage is the task of Chapter 3, which will unite running-ahead-into-death and resoluteness in the phenomenon of *anticipatory resoluteness* (*vorlaufende Entschlossenheit*) — the authentic whole-Being of Dasein from which temporality as the meaning of care will be exhibited.

### Third Chapter: The Authentic Whole-Ability-to-Be of Dasein and Temporality as the Ontological Meaning of Care

### § 61. Preliminary Sketch of the Methodological Step from the Delimitation of the Authentic Dasein-like Whole-Being to the Phenomenal Laying-Bare of Temporality

This section states the task: to bring together the authentic whole-Being of Dasein — running ahead into death (Chapter 1) and resoluteness (Chapter 2) — into a unitary phenomenon. This unitary phenomenon is *anticipatory resoluteness* (*vorlaufende Entschlossenheit*): resoluteness that holds itself open for death, that projects itself upon its ownmost ability-to-be while understanding itself as finite. Through anticipatory resoluteness, the ontological meaning of care must be laid bare.

### § 62. The Existentielly Authentic Whole-Ability-to-Be of Dasein as Anticipatory Resoluteness

Anticipatory resoluteness is the authentic mode of Dasein's Being as a whole. In it, running ahead into death and resoluteness are united. Dasein understands itself from its ownmost, non-relational, unsurpassable, certain and indefinite possibility (death) and takes over its thrownness in a resolute way. The whole structure of care — ahead-of-itself-already-Being-in-the-world-as-Being-alongside — is held together in anticipatory resoluteness. With this, the hermeneutical situation is secured for the question of the meaning of care.

### § 63. The Hermeneutical Situation Attained for the Interpretation of the Meaning of Care and the Methodological Character of the Existential Analytic in General

This section reflects on the hermeneutical situation. The fore-having is the whole of Dasein as exhibited through the analysis. The fore-sight is directed at the unity of the existential structural moments. The fore-conception uses existential-ontological concepts. The question now is: what is the *meaning* (*Sinn*) of the totality of the structural whole of care?

### § 64. Care and Selfhood

This section examines the relationship between care and the self. The self is not a core-subject behind or beneath experiences. The self is constituted by care: the who of Dasein is determined through existentiality (projection), facticity (thrownness), and falling. The self is its self-constancy or its self-dispersal. Authentic selfhood is constituted through anticipatory resoluteness; inauthentic selfhood through the They. In both cases, selfhood is a mode of care.

### § 65. Temporality as the Ontological Meaning of Care

The first paragraph poses the question directly: what makes the totality of the structural whole of care possible? What is the *meaning* (*Sinn*) of care? Meaning was defined (§32) as the upon-which of a projection in terms of which something becomes intelligible as something. The meaning of care is that upon which the projectedness of care — in the mode of the understanding of Being belonging to Dasein — is projected. The question asks: what makes possible the articulated structural totality of care in the unity of its articulation?

The second paragraph approaches the answer through anticipatory resoluteness. Anticipatory resoluteness was determined as the authentic Being-toward-death — the authentic whole-Being of Dasein. In anticipatory resoluteness, Dasein comes toward itself from its ownmost, unsurpassable possibility. This coming-toward-itself is the primordial phenomenon of the *future* (*Zukunft*). The German *Zukunft* is to be understood literally: *Zu-kunft* means coming-toward — Dasein comes toward itself from its ownmost possibility. This coming-toward-itself is not the awaiting of a not-yet-present event; it is the existential structure of Dasein's Being as projection upon its ownmost ability-to-be.

The third paragraph identifies the second ecstasis. In anticipatory resoluteness, Dasein takes over its thrownness — it comes back to what it already is. This coming-back-to is the primordial phenomenon of *having-been* (*Gewesenheit*). The German *Gewesenheit* does not simply mean the past (*Vergangenheit*) — something gone and no longer present — but rather the *having-been* that still determines Dasein's Being. Dasein can come toward itself from its ownmost possibility (future) only by coming back to what it has been (having-been). Authentic existing is always simultaneously a retrieving of thrownness.

The fourth paragraph identifies the third ecstasis. In resoluteness, Dasein discloses its situation — it makes present (*gegenwärtigen*) the beings it is alongside in concern. This making-present is the primordial phenomenon of the *present* (*Gegenwart*). The present is not the "now" as a moment in a time-sequence but the existential structure of Dasein's Being-alongside beings — its letting beings be encountered within the disclosed situation. Authentic making-present is the *moment of vision* (*Augenblick*) — the resolute disclosure of the situation.

The fifth paragraph states the answer: the meaning of care is *temporality* (*Zeitlichkeit*). The three structural moments of care are grounded in three temporal *ecstases*: (1) *Existentiality* (ahead-of-itself) is grounded in the *future* — Dasein comes toward itself from its ownmost possibility. (2) *Facticity* (already-Being-in) is grounded in *having-been* — Dasein is always already its past, always coming back to its thrownness. (3) *Falling* (Being-alongside) is grounded in the *present* — Dasein makes present the beings it is concerned with. These three ecstases — future, having-been, present — constitute the primordial unity of temporality. Temporality is the primordial "outside-itself" (*Außer-sich*) in and for itself. The ecstases are not three isolated phases but the unitary phenomenon of a future-that-has-been-and-makes-present.

The sixth paragraph insists that temporality is not a being, not something that "is" and then flows or passes. Temporality *temporalizes* itself (*zeitigt sich*) — it is the primordial self-unifying of the three ecstases. There is no temporality that first exists and then begins to temporalize. Temporality "is" temporalizing — it is a verb, not a noun. The primacy among the ecstases belongs to the *future*: Dasein is primordially ahead-of-itself, and it is from the future that the past and present are drawn together. Authentic temporality is *finite*: the future is primordially the running-ahead into death — the coming-toward-oneself from one's ownmost unsurpassable possibility. The finitude of temporality means: the ecstases do not extend into infinity. Primordial temporality is the finite temporality of mortal Dasein.

The seventh paragraph addresses the connection between authentic and inauthentic temporality. Inauthentic temporality also has the structure of future-having-been-present, but in modified forms: the inauthentic future is *awaiting* (*Gewärtigen*) — expectation directed toward what is to come within the world; the inauthentic having-been is *forgetting* (*Vergessen*) — the covering-over of thrownness; the inauthentic present is the *making-present* (*Gegenwärtigen*) of what is nearest and most urgent in concern. Inauthentic temporality is not without temporality; it is a modification — specifically, the modification in which the primacy of the future is suppressed and the present dominates.

The eighth and final paragraph of §65 states that with temporality as the meaning of care, the fundamental-ontological question can now be asked: does temporality as the meaning of Dasein's Being provide the horizon for the understanding of Being in general? This question — which would have been answered in the projected but never published Third Division — remains open in the published text. But what has been achieved is the demonstration that the Being of Dasein — care — has temporality as its meaning, and that all the structures of Division One can and must be reinterpreted as temporal phenomena.

### § 66. The Temporality of Dasein and the Tasks Arising from it for a More Primordial Repetition of the Existential Analysis

This section states that with temporality as the meaning of care, the existential analysis must now be *repeated* — all the structures of Division One are to be reinterpreted in terms of their temporal constitution. The following chapters carry out this task: Chapter 4 (temporality and everydayness), Chapter 5 (temporality and historicality), Chapter 6 (temporality and within-time-ness as the origin of the vulgar concept of time).

### Fourth Chapter: Temporality and Everydayness

### § 67. The Basic Content of the Existential Constitution of Dasein and the Preliminary Sketch of its Temporal Interpretation

This section outlines the program: the structures exhibited in Division One — understanding, state-of-mind, falling, discourse, concern, solicitude, spatiality — are now to be exhibited in their temporal constitution.

### § 68. The Temporality of Disclosedness in General

**a) The Temporality of Understanding.** Understanding is primordially grounded in the *future*. As projection upon possibilities, understanding is essentially futural — it comes toward itself from its ability-to-be. Authentic understanding projects itself upon the ownmost possibility (death) and is thus grounded in the authentic future (anticipation). Inauthentic understanding projects upon immediate concernful possibilities and is grounded in the inauthentic future (awaiting, *Gewärtigen*).

**b) The Temporality of State-of-Mind.** State-of-mind is primordially grounded in *having-been*. Mood discloses thrownness — Dasein's "that it is and has to be." This disclosure of facticity is a bringing-back to what one already is. Fear, for example, is grounded in the future (the threatening thing approaches) but primarily discloses the vulnerability of Being-in-the-world — the having-been of thrownness. Anxiety discloses the "nothing" of the world and throws Dasein back upon its naked thrownness.

**c) The Temporality of Falling.** Falling is primordially grounded in the *present*. The making-present (*Gegenwärtigen*) of what is nearby and ready-to-hand drives the movement of falling. Curiosity, as never-tarrying, is the restless making-present of the ever-new. In falling, Dasein loses itself in the present — it forgets its having-been (thrownness) and closes off its future (authentic possibility).

**d) The Temporality of Discourse.** Discourse as such has no single ecstatic grounding but temporalizes itself in the unity of all three ecstases.

### § 69. The Temporality of Being-in-the-World and the Problem of the Transcendence of the World

**a) The Temporality of Circumspective Concern.** The unity of the temporal ecstases constituting concern is the existential-temporal condition of the possibility of the ready-to-hand being encountered in its involvement-structure.

**b) The Temporal Meaning of the Modification of Circumspective Concern into Theoretical Discovery.** The transition from practical circumspection to theoretical contemplation is a modification of temporality: a shift from the making-present guided by the retaining-awaiting of concerned circumspection to the making-present of a "pure beholding" (*reines Hinsehen*) that lets the ready-to-hand show itself as merely present-at-hand.

**c) The Temporal Problem of the Transcendence of the World.** The "transcendence" of the world — the fact that Dasein is always already "beyond" itself toward the world — is grounded in the ecstatic-horizonal constitution of temporality. Each ecstasis has its *horizon*: the future has the horizon of the for-the-sake-of-which; having-been has the horizon of the before-which of thrownness; the present has the horizon of the in-order-to. The unity of these horizons constitutes what Heidegger calls the *horizonal schema* of temporality. This horizonal unity is the existential-temporal condition of the possibility of world as such.

### § 70. The Temporality of the Dasein-like Spatiality

Dasein's existential spatiality — de-severance and directionality — is grounded in the making-present that belongs to the retaining-awaiting of concern. Space is "given" in the ecstatic temporality of Dasein's Being-in-the-world.

### § 71. The Temporal Meaning of the Everydayness of Dasein

Everydayness is not a merely ontic category but a temporal phenomenon: the indifferent constancy of the way Dasein maintains itself in its average self-understanding. The temporality of everydayness is characterized by the dominance of the present and the leveling-down of the ecstatic structure.

### Fifth Chapter: Temporality and Historicality

### § 72. The Existential-Ontological Exposition of the Problem of History

The question of historicality (*Geschichtlichkeit*) arises from the temporal constitution of Dasein. If Dasein's Being is temporality, and if Dasein stretches between birth and death, then Dasein is essentially *historical*. Historicality is not first derived from historiography (*Historie*) but is the existential-ontological ground of the possibility of historiography.

### § 73. The Vulgar Understanding of History and the Happening of Dasein

The vulgar understanding of history sees it as the past — as what is "over and done with." But Dasein's past is not simply gone; it is constitutive of its present Being. Dasein does not "have" a history like a thing has properties; Dasein *is* its history. The happening (*Geschehen*) of Dasein between birth and death constitutes its historicality.

### § 74. The Basic Constitution of Historicality

Authentic historicality is grounded in *anticipatory resoluteness*. In running ahead into death and thereby taking over its thrownness, Dasein *hands itself down* (*überliefert sich*) to its ownmost inherited possibilities. This handing-down constitutes authentic *heritage* (*Erbe*). Authentic historicality is *fate* (*Schicksal*): the self-determination of Dasein in its resolute taking-over of its thrown inheritance. When Dasein exists communally, fate becomes *destiny* (*Geschick*) — the happening of community or generation.

### § 75. The Historicality of Dasein and World-History

World-historical beings are encountered within the world: tools, works, institutions. They become historical because they belong to the world of a historical Dasein. The historicality of intraworldly beings is derivative of the historicality of Dasein.

### § 76. The Existential Origin of Historiology from the Historicality of Dasein

Historiology (*Historie*) — the science of history — is grounded in Dasein's historicality. Authentic historiology is not mere antiquarian interest or monumental celebration but the "repetition" (*Wiederholung*) of past possibilities — the productive appropriation of heritage.

### § 77. The Connection of the Foregoing Exposition of the Problem of Historicality with the Researches of W. Dilthey and the Ideas of Count Yorck

This section acknowledges Dilthey's contribution to the problem of historicality and the correspondence between Count Yorck von Wartenburg and Dilthey, in which the distinction between the "ontic" and the "historical" was worked out in a proto-existential way. Heidegger sees his own analysis as the ontological radicalization of their insights.

### Sixth Chapter: Temporality and Within-Time-ness as the Origin of the Vulgar Concept of Time

### § 78. The Incompleteness of the Foregoing Temporal Analysis of Dasein

The temporal analysis so far has exhibited temporality as the meaning of care and reinterpreted the structures of Division One temporally. But one dimension remains unthematized: the time "in which" intraworldly beings are encountered — the *within-time-ness* (*Innerzeitigkeit*) from which the vulgar concept of time arises.

### § 79. The Temporality of Dasein and the Concern with Time

Dasein, as concerned Being-in-the-world, "reckons with time." It says "now," "then," "at-that-time." This public, datable, significant time is the time of concern — not the temporality of care in the strict existential sense, but the "world-time" that Dasein encounters in its dealings.

### § 80. Concerned Time and Within-Time-ness

Concerned time has the characters of *datability* (the "now" is always a "now that..."), *spannedness* (time stretches between "then" and "now"), *publicness* (the "now" is the time of everyone), and *significance* (the "now" is always a "now for..." — a time to do something). Within-time-ness is the way beings are encountered "in" time — as processes, events, occurrences. This within-time-ness is the ground from which the vulgar concept of time — the infinite, homogeneous, directionless "now-sequence" — is abstracted.

### § 81. Within-Time-ness and the Genesis of the Vulgar Concept of Time

The vulgar concept of time is an infinite sequence of now-points, each "in itself" empty and without significance. This concept arises through the progressive stripping of datability, spannedness, publicness, and significance from the time of concern. What remains is the pure now-sequence — the time of clocks and physics. This concept of time was first explicitly articulated by Aristotle (*Physics* IV.10–14) and has dominated Western thought since. The now-point time is a "leveled-off" derivative of the more primordial temporality that is the meaning of Dasein's Being.

### § 82. The Contrast of the Existential-Ontological Connection of Temporality, Dasein, and World-Time against Hegel's Conception of the Relationship between Time and Spirit

**a) Hegel's Concept of Time.** Heidegger examines Hegel's treatment of time in the *Philosophy of Nature* and shows that Hegel's concept of time remains within the vulgar framework: time as the "intuited becoming" of abstract points. Hegel determines time through its relation to space (negation of space) and thus remains within the Aristotelian horizon.

**b) Hegel's Interpretation of the Connection between Time and Spirit.** For Hegel, spirit "falls into time" — spirit's externalization in nature involves temporal existence. Heidegger argues that this formulation, despite its profundity, reverses the proper ontological order. It is not that spirit falls into time; rather, factical existence falls *out of* primordial temporality into the "world-time" from which the vulgar now-concept is abstracted. Hegel's account, for all its power, presupposes the vulgar concept of time that the existential analysis has shown to be derivative.

### § 83. The Existential-Temporal Analytic of Dasein and the Fundamental-Ontological Question of the Meaning of Being in General

The final section of *Being and Time* restates the guiding question: what is the meaning of Being in general? The existential-temporal analytic of Dasein has shown that temporality is the meaning of the Being of Dasein (care). But Dasein is the being for which Being is an issue — the being through which the question of Being is posed. The horizon for the understanding of Being in general must therefore be sought in the temporal constitution of Dasein's Being. Is there a way from primordial time to the meaning of Being? Does time itself manifest itself as the horizon of Being?

With these questions, *Being and Time* ends. The projected Third Division ("Time and Being") was to have answered them by exhibiting the Temporality (*Temporalität*) of Being itself — not merely the temporality (*Zeitlichkeit*) of Dasein. This Division was never published. The path from Dasein's temporality to the meaning of Being as such — the ultimate goal of the entire work — remains a task that Heidegger pursued by other means in his later thought, but that *Being and Time* itself leaves open.
