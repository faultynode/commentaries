# Lectures on the Theory of Meaning, Summer Semester 1908 (Husserliana XXVI)

## Commentary

-----

## Introduction

### § 1. On the Topic of the Lecture

Husserl opens by acknowledging that the lectures will not entirely correspond to the title announced in the printed course catalogue. He had originally intended to pursue the general idea of a *Wissenschaftslehre* (theory of science) and to demarcate the various theory-of-science disciplines falling under that broad heading, characterizing the domains and problem-groups proper to each. The first of these disciplines was to have been formal analytic logic — understood as the highest and ultimately philosophical logic — along with the phenomenology of cognition essentially connected to it. He notes that in an earlier semester he had already sketched out such an introduction to the theory of science, as his closer students well know. That attempt revealed that the relevant determinations of domains and problems present considerable difficulties for understanding and scientific formulation, and that their fully adequate execution points back to a series of highly important investigations of fundamental interest not only for logic but for philosophy as a whole. He now intends to restrict himself in the present lectures to the sphere of the logic of analytic thinking — so-called formal logic — to whose essential characterization he had already devoted much effort in the *Logical Investigations*.

Husserl continues by stressing that clarifying the essence and distinctive domain of formal logic is one of the chief and foundational tasks of epistemology, as is readily apparent from the all-encompassing scope of purely logical concepts and the normative application of formal-logical laws. Formal-logical thinking, analytic thinking in the most pregnant sense, is — according to the *Logical Investigations* — a thinking on the basis of mere meanings. It relates to all and every objectuality (whether real or not) because objects in general are objects for thinking only through its meaning-bestowing activity, and because laws grounded in the essence of meanings as such, i.e., in their essential kinds or forms, must necessarily hold for all objectualities grasped in a meaning-determined way.

Thus the concept of meaning confronts us at the very outset — a concept that can itself “mean” quite various things and whose clarification presents the greatest difficulties. Husserl announces that the lectures will devote comprehensive investigations to this concept and to the concepts and phenomena connected with it. Such investigations are, he adds, independently of the interest in clearly determining the sense of formal logic, of self-evident and paramount importance for logic itself and for the critique of cognition. Various closely connected interests can thus find their satisfaction. He notes that he has all the more reason for this choice of topic because he knows of the efforts his young friends in the Philosophical Society have devoted to meaning-problems in their discussions over the preceding two semesters. It was above all to meet these currently lively interests that he made the topic of the lectures less general than originally planned. Since he has the good fortune of finding not only listeners but active co-workers, he has attached the philosophical exercises this semester to the lecture course, so that the students will have the opportunity to voice their own observations, objections, and concerns. He also reserves the right, as in previous years and in keeping with the intimate character of these lectures, to occasionally let the lecture turn into discussion.

### § 2. The Relation of the Lecture to the Logical Investigations

Husserl states that he naturally does not intend merely to repeat what he said in the *Logical Investigations*. In the years since that work appeared, he has not stood still. The domains it treated were almost entirely uncharted — a whole world of problems opened up, a dark continent barely explored along its coasts — when he resolutely adopted the standpoint he calls the purely phenomenological one. Cognition must be investigated in itself and according to its own essence, not in relation to a pregiven actual world and not as a fact within that world, but in pure immanence, as absolute givenness in which every actual world first “constitutes” itself (the word to be understood in a definite, proper sense). The actual world is the world that is represented, judged, cognized, and only in this correlation is it “actual world” for cognition. If one wants to bring the “possibility” of cognition to fully satisfying reflective clarity and learn to understand how objectuality constitutes itself in cognition — what it means for an objectuality to be able to stand there in cognition as perceived, remembered, intuitively represented, thought, cognized — then one must interrogate cognition itself, study these correlations in the manner here in question, i.e., according to their immanent essence. Every existential presupposition of an actual world and the construal and scientific investigation of cognitive phenomena as occurrences of actuality, in relation to the other occurrences of actuality, constitutes an evident *metabasis*, a confusion of essential clarification with natural (naturalistic, psychological) explanation. This is also the fundamental error of psychologism: it operates with cognitive experiences as experiences of human beings and other psychic individuals of the actual world — i.e., with psychic experiences in the proper sense.

Husserl then reflects that this was indeed the standpoint of the *Logical Investigations*. It was, despite some vacillations in expression, methodically carried through in a purely phenomenological manner, although the account given in the Introduction itself regarding this standpoint and its distinctive character left much to be desired. A first exploration of an endlessly extending dark continent never constitutes, in every point, a definitive investigation, however faithful the description of what has been seen. New voyages of discovery reveal new connections and let even the old appear in a new light. What was taken to be one thing, seemingly seen as such, dissolves into a multiplicity. Unaccustomed to the plenitude of ever-new distinctions that have always been overlooked, one does not immediately dare — even where one has seen them — to assert them decisively, and one errs through a striving toward the most far-reaching simplification of descriptions. Naturally this happened to him too in the *Logical Investigations*. One finds in them some timid half-measures, some self-contradictions, some oversights due to the lingering effects of inherited prejudices.

This holds, he says, also for the theory of meaning and for the connected interpretations of pure logic, especially insofar as the First Investigation of Volume II is concerned. The Fifth and Sixth Investigations, to which he has always directed his students as the most mature, already contain in germ the worked-out fundamental materials for improvement and require only energetic and consistent further development. Nevertheless, the *Logical Investigations* must still form the starting point for new investigations, since the treatments of the same problems that have appeared in the meantime have drawn no or no sufficient profit from the comprehensive preparatory work. The latter applies to the works of Meinong and his school. Husserl criticizes the haste with which Meinong threw himself upon the problems in question, which did not allow him to familiarize himself with the whole standpoint required or with the deep difficulties of the subject matter. Despite his honest efforts to learn from these writings — though he approached their study with some reluctance, repelled by the dishonest concealment of the identity of Meinong’s much-lauded “discoveries” with ideas Husserl had already developed — he finds everywhere astounding unclarities and inadequacies that could have been avoided through more careful study of what was already available.

## Chapter 1. Word-Sound Consciousness and Meaning-Consciousness

### § 3. Contrasting Word-Sound Consciousness and Meaning-Consciousness

#### a) First Distinctions: Indicative and Meaningful Signs. Physical and Psychic Sides of the Expression. Intimation and Understanding of Intimation

Husserl begins by saying that it is necessary to start with the presentation as given in the *Logical Investigations*, in order to connect to it and eventually to improve upon it. What first comes into question is the presentation of the First Investigation. There he began with the distinction between merely indicative signs and signs that are meaningful in a more proper sense — expressions. Expressions are signs, but not all signs have the character of expressions in the sense relevant here. Every sign designates something, but not every sign does so in the way that words and equivalent signs (e.g., algebraic signs) do. When we call fossil bones signs of the existence of prehistoric animals, or old architectural monuments signs of past cultures, such signs are obviously not expressions in the way that speech is. Mnemonic signs, identifying marks, indications of every sort are excluded. In their case, empirical objects or states of affairs whose existence points, projects, or refers back to the existence of certain other objects or states of affairs are at issue. That is to say: the cognizance of the being of such an objectuality functions, in the suitably disposed person, as an empirical motive for the conviction or conjecture of the actual (present, past, or future) being of the other things for which it functions as an “indication.”

A linguistic expression, by contrast, does not essentially function in this way. Its essential function is not to indicate — through its own existence — the existence of other objects, although it does sometimes exercise this function, as everywhere in communicative speech. The essential function of the expression is to mean something in the pregnant sense, and this meaning-function as essential belongs to it even where it indicates nothing. Husserl also emphasizes that the so-called involuntary “expressions” of our mental life — such as facial expressions and gestures — belong to the excluded sphere, even though ordinary speech permits us to say of them, just as of linguistic expressions, that their meaning is understood. It is clear that involuntary gestures only acquire the character of signs for the onlooker, namely as indications that the onlooker interprets as pointing to mental occurrences of the gesticulating person. In themselves, however, they do not have the character of expressions in the sense of speech, either for the onlooker or for the person involuntarily expressing themselves. Genuine gestural speech of the deaf-mute is, of course, a different matter.

Restricting ourselves to genuine expressions, Husserl notes that one customarily distinguishes, on the objective side (as with all signs), sign and signified; and on the subjective side, in respect of the phenomena, two things: (1) the physical side of the expression — the sensory appearance of the word-sound, the phenomenon of the sensory sign on the paper, etc.; and (2) the psychic side — certain psychic experiences that, as is usually said, are associatively linked to the word-sound and lend it meaning. These attached psychic experiences are typically designated straightforwardly as the meanings or sense of the expression. Husserl declares this entirely inadequate. Better, though also not entirely sufficient, is Brentano’s distinction between what a name intimates (*kundgibt*), what it means, and what it names, a distinction easily extended to all expressions.

Husserl then explains the communicative function: the speaker communicates something with speech, the hearer takes up the communication and understands it. The speaker’s articulation of thoughts about this or that subject-matter takes place in certain psychic experiences; insofar as the speaker is understood, these experiences are intimated (*kundgegeben*) to the hearer. This means nothing other than that the hearer can only understand the speaker insofar as the hearer apperceives — better: represents — the speaker as someone performing just such psychic functions, as wanting to communicate this or that. Conversely, these apperceiving psychic functions on the hearer’s side constitute the content of the *Kundnahme* (understanding of the intimation). One of the concepts of expression is thereby determined: one says of the speaker that he or she “expresses” something, in the sense that something is intimated.

A further distinction is then made between experiences intimated in the narrower sense and those in the broader sense. In the narrower sense, Husserl means specifically the experiences in which meaning is enacted, which constitute the meaning-bestowing acts, as distinct from the otherwise interwoven experiences co-apperceived by the hearer, such as the wanting-to-communicate and the like. However, the specific functions of intimation and understanding of intimation do not further concern the investigation. Linguistic expressions remain expressions even where communication and the relationship between speaker and addressee are not in question, as in solitary thinking and speaking. The focus is on what belongs essentially to the expression, however it may function — whether communicatively or not.

#### b) The Expression as Mere Sensory Sign (Word-Sound) and the Expression-Appearance as Foundation for Meaning-Bestowing Acts

Since it is meaning and meaning-bestowal that interest us, expressions in their communicative functions need not further concern us. Husserl directs attention to what belongs to an expression insofar as it is genuinely a meaningful sign. In this regard, the First Investigation from §9 onward presents a series of preliminary considerations and indicates a number of distinctions. One might initially want to distinguish the expression itself — i.e., apart from its sense — and the sense itself, the meaning. But the matter is not so simply dispatched. Phenomenologically and in pure essential consideration, one must distinguish the expression-appearance and the expression itself that appears. By “expression” one must here understand the mere sensory sign, normally appearing in sensory form — the word-sound. This represents for us everything sensory at issue: the spoken sound, the acoustic-motor aspect, the written or printed character, etc. This appears, and one must distinguish the word-sound that appears and the appearing itself — the experience of the perceptual appearance in which the word-sound, the printed character on the paper, is present, and through which it is something present for the speaker and hearer.

It must immediately be added, Husserl says, that the act in which the word-sound is constituted for consciousness is not entirely determined in its qualitative character. It does not matter whether it is a perception, a memory, or some other intuition — including a mere phantasy-presentation. The word-sound can stand there as actually existing, as presumably existing, and the like, but need not do so; being or non-being is simply irrelevant here. In inner thinking and speaking, a flow of phantasy-presentations takes place, but the phantasied word-sounds are not assigned any place in the actual world; they appear but count neither as existent nor as non-existent. Nevertheless they are word-sounds — namely sounds that carry word-function, that appear as word-signs for meanings. Correlatively, these mere phantasy word-sound appearances without positional commitment serve as foundations for meaning-bestowing acts just as perceptual appearances do in the cases of actual speech.

Normally one finds some kind of sensory intuitions on the word-sound side. Husserl says “normally” because, as occasionally noted in a later context in the *Logical Investigations*, non-intuitive presentations — more precisely, mere empty presentations — can function in the same way. In the *Logical Investigations* he had spoken throughout of “signitive” or “symbolic” presentations. It was namely the empty presentations bound to expressions on the meaning-side that first captured his interest and led him to comprehensive investigations. In the process, it became clear that the binding to sensory intuitions of signs and symbols is not essential to the empty intention. The terminus “symbolic presentation” is therefore not really appropriate as a terminus for the whole class of empty presentations. In his lectures he now uses instead the expression “empty presentations” in contrast to the full ones, i.e., intuitions. In the case at hand, it seems to him that in inner speaking, when “words are lacking,” the word-presentations as empty presentations can nonetheless be present and indeed are present. He suggests verifying this by starting from cases where one thinks of a person and inwardly makes various statements about them while the person’s name, which belongs in the statements, is missing — and perhaps no substitute occurs. He does not wish to dwell on this but also does not want to dismiss the possibility that empty presentations directed at a determinate word-sound, which as empty do not bring it to appearance-givenness in consciousness, may be connected with meaning-bestowing acts. The situation is remarkable: words are sensory objectualities, and words that neither exist nor even truly appear can nonetheless mean something. The word-appearances (and more generally the word-presentations) are carriers of meaning-acts.

#### c) The Acts of Meaning This or That with the Word-Sound. Meaning-Bestowing and Meaning-Fulfilling Acts

Husserl turns to the meaning-side. The presented — for convenience let us say the intuited, appearing, “ideated” — word means something, signifies something, and thereby refers to a meant, signified objectuality. In the case of names: a named objectuality. On account of this reference, the word-sound is more than a mere sound, more than any arbitrary sensory object. Not every sensory object is a word, a meaningful sign. So we express ourselves in the objectual attitude, looking toward the appearing, merely presented object “word-sound” and the relation to the named, meant attributed to it. In doing so, we carry out a reflection and possibly create a circumscribing expression that modifies, through an altered apprehension, the state of affairs we actually wanted to present. For we must on the one hand distinguish the way in which the appearing word, the meant subject-matter, and the relation or connection between them stand intentionally in the normal functioning of the expression — in normal speech, inner or outer — from the way they stand when we speak about word and word-meaning and the word’s referring to the meant objectuality, think about it, judge about it, and thus make them into “objects-about.” Husserl notes that he here goes beyond the description in §9 of the First Investigation, because it is obviously useful and necessary to point out these states of affairs from the outset.

Taking the original case to which the circumscription was meant to point, we have as objectually “standing there,” as presented, a certain connection of word-sound and meant. In the case of intuitive presentation (intuitive word-sound appearance and intuitive appearance of the named), the objectual connection stands intuitively there, as one of its own peculiar kind. But this does not suffice for description, and when one delves into it, various difficulties emerge — to be made up later. For now it suffices that here a peculiar subject-matter going beyond the word-sound appears or is in some way constituted for consciousness. This “constituted for consciousness” points to the experience-side, the consciousness-side, which is precisely what makes up the standing-there of the relevant objectualities.

Reflecting on this, we can say from the outset: with the word-sound consciousness, through which the mere word-sound is objectual, we do not yet have the meaning-consciousness — all of what goes beyond the mere establishing of word-sound objectuality. New acts enter into unity with word-sound consciousness: the acts of “meaning this and that with the word.” An objectual something — e.g., the thing named by the name — becomes conscious, and this consciousness can apparently be various. In naming, the named thing may stand before our eyes in perception; or a memory or other intuitive presentation may function similarly; or the expression can be a meaningful expression without any intuition of the named or more generally of the meant object. Husserl speaks of a merely symbolic function of expressions: they are understood, meaningfully used without illustrating, “fulfilling,” clarifying intuition. The expressions continue to refer to their objectuality, they name it, mean it; but this relation is not “realized.”

On the side of the constituting consciousness, a distinction is visible. With identical underlying word-sound consciousness and constant same-sense meaning — referring to the same objectuality meant in the same sense — the consciousness of this objectuality is sometimes realized, sometimes unrealized, sometimes full, sometimes empty, or more or less full, more or less empty. We customarily say: “with the expression something is meant” or “in expressing, in naming, something is meant, but sometimes it is merely meant and other times it is at once meant and intuitively given.” Yet these are dangerous expressions, since givenness can be a mere phantasy-givenness in the manner of a mere fiction, and “meant” has many significations that are all the more dangerous because they all hang together. In the First Investigation Husserl had also said that when the initially empty meaning-intention fulfills itself, the objectual reference realizes itself, the naming becomes an actual relation between name and named. Here too one must be cautious — first, with the expression “intention,” which is the foreign word for “meaning” (*Meinung*) and for which what was said above also holds; Husserl says he would now strike the word from most places in the *Logical Investigations* and delimit its sense anew and more narrowly. The talk of “actual relation between name and named” is also better avoided, since too much could be read into it.

What remains important, however, is the distinction between meaning-bestowing acts and meaning-fulfilling acts. It was incautious, Husserl notes, to designate the fulfillment-unity — the phenomenon of the fulfilling that establishes itself between the one and the other acts in fulfillment-consciousness — as a “cognitive unity” (*Erkenntniseinheit*), against which much can already be read out of the Fifth and especially the Sixth Investigation. From the outset one must sharply distinguish two things that only gradually come to light there: (1) fulfillment as visualization (*Veranschaulichung*), as filling out of an empty presentation with intuitive fullness — where, however, quasi-visualization must be separated from truly fulfilling visualization (merely “forming an image”); and (2) fulfillment in the sense of confirmation, corroboration of a meaning in the sense of a *doxa*, a belief, a conviction, a conjecture, and more specifically an authentication through evidence-producing (corresponding!) “intuition” that itself has the character of belief and transforms the *doxa* into *epistēmē*. If one speaks in both cases of “meaning” or “intention,” that is disturbing and dangerous. A mere empty presentation passes, in a covering, into a full one — say, into a phantasy — without any “intention” being fulfilled in the manner of a corroboration or evidencing. The moment of judging positional commitment is out of play there. In the other case, it is precisely this moment that essentially matters: the belief confirms itself, legitimates itself. One must attend to these differences from the outset. Of course, the first kind of fulfillment plays an essential role in the second. As for the relation of meaning-bestowing and meaning-fulfilling acts, nothing can be said about it prior to closer acquaintance, and it is better not to let oneself be prejudiced by the indications given in the First Investigation. The actual execution — the investigation of what kind of acts are meaning-bestowing and can be so, and further, how “fulfillment” is to be thought — follows in the Fifth and Sixth Investigations, which are themselves, however, only first beginnings beyond which one must essentially go in many respects.

### § 4. Phenomenological Characterization of the Distinctive Kind of Connection between Word-Sound Consciousness and Meaning-Consciousness

Before pursuing such questions further, Husserl supplements the rough outer contours with a few words about the connection between word-sound consciousness and meaning-consciousness. They are obviously not merely given together as a mere sum; they do not simply form a unity in the way that different data of consciousness in a total consciousness, at the same phenomenological now-point and in the flow of actual phenomenological time-consciousness, are one. Rather, beyond this, there is a phenomenologically distinctive, essentially grounded connection to the unity of an act-consciousness. Meaning-consciousness builds itself upon word-sound consciousness. In their unity, each has a different position, a different function. Since the whole is an act-unity and as a whole constitutes a correlative objectuality (makes it present in an originary way), it is understandable that the respective objectualities on both sides acquire positional — and differentiated positional — characters relative to one another. It is an asymmetrical connection: word-sound and named objectuality cannot arbitrarily exchange their positions. Word is word and thing is thing — and precisely for consciousness.

We observe, Husserl continues, that in the connection the two acts set into unity also have a certain inequality. Take the word-sound phenomenon as an intuitive one — say, as a perception. The printed character that we read stands before our eyes on the paper. But in the normal consciousness of word-meaning (here of reading), it does not have our “interest.” We look at it, and yet we do not perceive it in the familiar pregnant sense in which we are turned toward an object as a perceived one, as something itself presently grasped and posited. One feels perhaps pushed to say: we see the printed character, we even fixate it, but we do not mean it, we are not aiming at it. Rather, apprehending it and thus following with our gaze the complicated configurations of successive or interwoven written characters, we mean something quite different — we mean the signified objectualities, we “live” in the meaning-consciousness. These are distinctive phenomenological relations.

#### a) The Functions of Attention: Primary Noticing and Thematic Meaning

To clarify these relations, Husserl distinguishes two elective functions that tend to run together under the title “attention.” He first establishes generally that the title “attention” in normal speech concerns consciousness in the intentional sense — the sense in which an objectuality is conscious. Attending is attending to some subject-matter. Noticing and attending differ in degree: an objectuality can be conscious in various ways, and these various ways can concern different kinds of “acts” (experiences to whose essence it belongs to be “directed” toward something objectual). But crossing this distinction is the difference designated by the various modes of noticing and attending. Holding the character of the intentional experience fixed — the act remaining otherwise the same (e.g., a perception, even one of the same content, i.e., the same sense: the same objects appearing as themselves present, in the same determinations, from the same side, etc.) — there may be a preferential turning toward this object over against other simultaneously perceived or otherwise conscious objects. Or it may be that the primary, preferential turning belongs to another object, while the object in question is indeed perceived but is not the object of primary turning — it is still noticed, but only secondarily, on the side. A third level may also be conceded: while one object or a unified group primarily stands out, others receive the favor of noticing only secondarily, and then there remains an objectual background from which the primarily and secondarily conscious is, as it were, picked out or against which it stands off. This background is not entirely unnoticed, but it is noticed in a different mode — the mode of the background.

These differences, apprehended as differences in the way objects are conscious — how they stand out, are favored or set back for the turning — constitute themselves naturally in differences of consciousness itself, of the act-phenomena themselves. Husserl notes that he cannot here investigate whether the relevant phenomenological differences affect all act-types in the same way or whether, e.g., emotional acts possess them only by virtue of the intellective (i.e., “objectifying”) acts that found them. With the shifting of this elective function — i.e., with the change in the relevant consciousness-characteristic — content-differences normally also go hand in hand. Perception, for instance, takes up new differences in fixation: the object becomes determinate where it was previously indeterminate, is seen more distinctly, and is naturally presented with other sensation-contents and apprehension-components and possibly positional functions. But analysis shows that these differences, which normally go hand in hand with the attention-differences, run independently of them and cross them.

Husserl then makes a fundamental point that has been constantly overlooked in the doctrine of attention: turning to the object does not mean turning to the contents of consciousness — whether these be the presenting sensations that really inhere in perceptual consciousness, or the phantasied sensory contents through which phantasied objects are represented and which do not themselves really inhere in phantasy-consciousness. Turning to contents would presuppose perceiving or presenting acts directed at these contents. One should also note that perceptual consciousness phenomenologically changes when we primarily notice the perceived rather than merely secondarily noticing it. This phenomenological change seizes also upon the interwoven sensations, the lived color-contents and the like. These phenomenological differences of attention, as real differences of consciousness, are not differences of attention to the lived color-contents, to sensations and the like, but rather they constitute the differences of attention to the appearing colored object vis-à-vis other objects.

Having discussed differences of preferential turning in a general way, Husserl now makes a crucial split. He distinguishes (1) the distinctive and perhaps primary *noticing* from (2) attending in a somewhat different and very important sense: being-turned-toward-an-object (1) is to be distinguished from being-occupied-with-it, having-one’s-sights-set-on-it, being-turned-toward-it-as-a-theme, meaning-it-in-this-sense (2). One may also speak here of “interest.”

He illustrates with two examples. (1) We survey a landscape. Our gaze slides from object to object. The specially perceived object, typically fixated, has a certain privilege in noticing. What was just fixated has not vanished from consciousness — it is still presented, perhaps still actually perceived, but lacks the privilege of being primarily noticed. The secondarily noticed can still have a privilege over other co-perceived things, over the “background” from which everything privileged stands out. (2) Now suppose the surveying of the landscape is an interested observation. While we live in this observation, various unrelated notions, someone speaking, the words being apprehended and noticed, the pressure and pull of clothing momentarily making itself felt, etc., intrude. But none of this has the “interest,” none of it has the kind of turning that would, as it were, make it the theme in perception and other acts. The “interest” here means a special privilege. We do not only perceive the landscape objects — we “live” in these perceptions. Likewise we can live in memory, in phantasy, in thinking, in theoretical consideration, etc. A landscape may float before us in phantasy: we move about observing in it, we follow processes playing themselves out in phantasy. Meanwhile actual reality intrudes — a wagon passes, a bird sings. We notice it but are not occupied with it in the special sense. We are not attentive to it in the specific sense, even though it is temporarily primarily noticed.

Something new thus comes to light: a being-attentive in a special sense. When a teacher says “Be attentive to the subject-matter,” he means this attending. He does not mean primary turning in the sense of primary noticing, but rather living-in-the-turning-toward-the-things, or better, making-them-the-theme. For this it is not enough to direct the “gaze” at the things, to notice them preferentially, even primarily. They must be the theme of occupation, of interested turning, of preferential meaning. The shift of preferential noticing is not a shift in thematic noticing in which interest lives. And the difference obviously does not lie in the merely empirical-objective fact that certain groups of things are factually privileged — that primary noticing, though occasionally directed elsewhere, always returns to these things. The constant return to a disturbing melody, a street-tune that pursues us, does not make it our theme. Husserl emphasizes that he is not speaking of psychological facts but of phenomenological differences.

#### b) Application of the Distinction to Verbal Consciousness

Husserl now applies these distinctions to clarify normal verbal consciousness. In verbal consciousness, the two functions are intertwined in an interesting way. The word-sound appears, it is in one way or another presentive, objectual. But it is not meant. We are attentive to it and not attentive to it, depending on the sense given to the word “attention.” We are attentive in one sense: an elective function, that of primary noticing, distinguishes the word-sound. We read, say. We see the book, all sorts of things around us. We are turned toward the word. The word-perception has the distinction that constitutes a primarily noticing perception, but not that of a thematic perception. The printed character is not an object of “interest.” It is not our theme. Interwoven with word-sound consciousness is meaning-consciousness, the sense-bestowing consciousness. Here is the theme. Meaning-consciousness is not merely given together with word-sound consciousness; in the normal functioning of the word, and hence in the case of all verbally enacted thinking and cognizing, meaning-consciousness is the carrier of a thematic distinction. With the meant, we are occupied in this specific sense; we are turned toward it, meaning it. Word-sound consciousness has the function of not holding fast the primary noticing enacted in it, but rather of transitioning it onto the co-excited meaning-consciousness.

But not only that. Insofar as word-sound consciousness carries a pointing-tendency belonging to its phenomenological essence — a *Sollen* (an “ought”) that points to the meant and terminates in it — it also confers upon the meant the dignity of the theme, of the specifically meant or to-be-meant. The word palpably points to the subject-matter; we are supposed to live in meaning-consciousness and thereby be attendingly occupied. This *Sollen*, the function of pointing, is something phenomenologically findable. Attached to the word — not, of course, in a sensory-apparitional way — is a tendency to direct our attending to the meant objectuality. The word pushes interest away from itself and urges it toward the meant. In itself it has the character of irrelevance, and in meaning-consciousness, through which the pointing arrow projects, we terminate and look upon the relevant as such. This becomes clearly apparent when we force an attending gaze to fixate on the word-sound. We experience the resistance in the turning away from our theme to something foreign and experience the forwarding *Sollen*, the being-urged-toward the objectualities to which we are, through the medium of meaning, pointed from the word-sound.

Husserl concludes that what is at issue here is not merely psychological facts — not a mere fact of being driven from one thing to another that could obtain without any consciousness of it — but rather immanent moments in the experience of the normally functioning word, of the word in its verbal presentational and thinking function. There obtains a distinctive phenomenological unity between word-sound consciousness and meaning-consciousness. In this unity there are grounded essential possibilities of modification: for instance, the possibility of reversing the interest, of resistance against the tendency toward the meaning-theme, whereby the word, under an altered phenomenological total character, loses its normal function. To that function belongs not only that word and thing are both at once presentive, each in its own way, through its own presentational function, but that “with the word the thing is meant” — that the primary noticing of the word passes over, indeed passes over in fulfillment of a tendency, of a *Sollen*, into the thematic meaning of the thing, and specifically through the medium of meaning-consciousness. The word stands there as the pointing indicator; from it radiates a pointing arrow of meaning that terminates in the meant thing. The “verbal intention” fulfills itself. This verbal intention is a tendency, and its self-fulfilling is analogous to that of other tendencies, e.g., tendencies of the will. The verbal tendency is fulfilled when the word exercises its normal function.

## Chapter 2. Meaning and Signification

### § 5. Rejection of the Psychologistic Concept of Meaning

Husserl begins by noting that we must distinguish between meaning (*Bedeutung*) and meaning-bestowal (*Bedeuten*). “Meaning-bestowal” can — given what we have learned — refer either to the acts endowed with the pointing-tendency or to the sense-bestowing or sense-fulfilling acts. It has not seldom happened that such acts — especially the latter — were designated as meanings, without all the distinctions we have drawn being made. This was connected with the psychologistic tendencies to which so many analyses of the time succumbed, and according to which — here as everywhere — ideal objectualities were replaced by psychic experiences. To designate psychic acts carried out in expressing as “meanings” contradicts the clear sense of the universally customary talk of sense and meaning, which orients itself toward something entirely different from the psychological reflections so remote from natural thinking. The respective psychic acts are flowing and fleeting appearances; meanings, on the other hand, are identical unities standing over against these manifolds. In normal speech, no one understands by the meaning of the expression “the King of Prussia” the fleeting experiences in speaking or understanding the expression — otherwise one could not say that the same expression, used repeatedly and by different people, means (through all such multiplication of meaning-experiences) constantly and identically the same thing, that the meaning of the expression “the King of Prussia” is one, in the strictly identical sense.

What, then, is this objectuality called meaning? And how does it constitute itself: in the sense-bestowing acts or on the basis of them? Is it an analogous ideal unity like the unity of the expression? Or does the word “meaning” in customary usage have multiple significations, depending on the context, all relating to verbal presenting and thinking, which must be distinguished by us and investigated according to their phenomenological sources? This is in fact the case.

### § 6. The Meant Objectuality and the “Stated As Such”

Husserl identifies a first sense of “meaning” determined by the meant objectuality. Earlier logic, in its very meager discussions of the relation of expression, meaning, and meant-object, tended to look only at names, thus attending only to named objectualities. It did not come to consciousness that in carrying out a whole statement — and corresponding to it as a whole — an objectuality is conscious, though in a quite different way than, say, the objectuality named in the subject-name. It did not emerge that in a certain sense every self-enclosed expression refers as an expression to an objectuality, and further, eventually every expression-part that still has the character of an expression insofar as it is “meaningful.” An arbitrary sound or syllable contained in an expression and discernible within it is a part of the word-sound, but not a part of the expression in the full sense. There are, however, parts that as such carry meaning-function: meaningful parts that are themselves to be designated as expressions, even if perhaps as dependent expressions. Husserl has generally distinguished between composite and simple, and again between independent and dependent expressions. To the latter belong, e.g., particles, inflections, etc. The total meaning of an independent expression encompasses the meanings of its dependent parts; and just as a meant objectuality corresponds to the whole, so too does a meant objectual something correspond in a certain way to each dependent expression-part — though one cannot straightforwardly say, at least not without dangerous misunderstanding, that a part of the object corresponds to the expression-part that is meant in the whole expression.

Husserl adds a parenthetical note on the intimation-function (*Andeutungsfunktion*) of grammatical expression-forms and the way they awaken a meaning-consciousness of a conceptually shaped matter. The nominal subject-form, the verbal form, calls out to us in understanding — mediating the understanding of the meaning, as it were: “This is the subject-object! — the object spoken of, about which something will further be predicated.” The predicate-form of the expression calls: “This is asserted of that subject,” and so on. Behind the properly so-called meanings — subject-meanings, predicate-meanings, etc. — there are secondary indications and secondary “meanings” (opinions): those that we circumscriptively express through the deictic expressions “this is meant to be a subject-meaning, that a predicate-meaning,” etc. The presupposition for carrying through this view is that the word “objectuality” be taken in an extraordinarily wide sense — the widest thinkable — that permits even dependent moments, indeed even categorial forms, to be designated as objectualities.

With the construction of unified expressions from partial expressions stands the fundamental fact (not an empirical fact) that differently constructed expressions — say, two composite expressions not built from identically the same expressions in the same form — can refer to one and the same objectuality. Likewise a simple and a composite expression, e.g., “Napoleon” and “the victor at Jena,” or “the victor of Jena” and “the vanquished of Waterloo”; “the conic sections,” “the curve of the second order,” “the curve of the second class.” This leads to several distinctions. We call two expressions inessentially or, more clearly, tautologically different when what differentiates them lies solely in the words, in the mere words — above all purely linguistic, purely idiomatic differences, e.g., “der König” and “le roi.” The essential lies on the thematic side, in the meaning and not in the mere word-consciousness. Disregarding tautological differences, we carry out a new identification or objectivation: we simply regard tautologically different expressions as the same expression. The possibility discussed above of meaning the same objectuality in different expressions is not affected by the exclusion of tautological differences.

If we now find the same objectuality “meant” in two different expressions — in the case of nominal expressions: named — and want to seek the meaning in the direction of the objectuality, we must at once attend to the distinction between the objectuality that is meant and the objectuality in the manner in which it is meant. A new sense of “meaning” thereby confronts us. Tautological expressions say one and the same thing; non-tautological expressions always say something different. The object is the same whether I say “Napoleon” or “the victor of Jena.” But the two expressions say something different. In the second we do not say “Napoleon,” and this concerns not merely the word-sound, for what the word says does not occur in the other expression at all. In the one, the talk is of Jena and of being-a-victor. Expressions that say different things can refer to one and the same objectuality; names saying different things can name the same person or thing. We can thus distinguish the thing itself that is named, and the same thing taken in the manner in which it is here stated. What we clarify here under the title of “different stating” or different manner of assertion yields the new sense of meaning. Every expression has a meaning; non-tautological expressions have different meanings, tautological ones one and the same. Every expression also has an object (taken in the widest sense) to which it refers. Meaning and object are now distinguished, for expressions of different meaning can refer to the same object, insofar as we understand by “meaning” that “stated as such.”

### § 7. Propositional Meaning, State of Affairs, and Situation of Affairs

With all expressions and meanings, Husserl says, we must distinguish between the meaning itself and the objectuality to which it refers. In a categorical proposition such as “S is p,” we must distinguish not only on the side of the subject-meaning between the subject-object and the subject-meaning itself (and similarly for all nominal presentations occurring in the sentence, say within the predicate), but no less for the whole sentence. It says that the subject-object has such and such a property. This is an objectuality — precisely this state of affairs (*Sachverhalt*): that that object has such and such a property. This state of affairs remains the same so long as the object is the same, the property the same, and it remains the case that the object has this property. But this is something objectually common to propositions of different meaning. The same subject-object can be presented in meaning-different ways, and likewise the same property. We must therefore distinguish the asserted state of affairs from the respective manner in which it comes to assertion — namely, in the form of the different meanings of the statements that, saying different things, nonetheless assert the same property of the same subject.

Husserl notes that in the First Investigation the concept of *Sachverhalt* is construed differently, though with the same intent of distinction. In the later Investigations, the usage agrees much more with the present exposition. To distinguish propositional meaning and state of affairs, he had pointed in the First Investigation to examples of immediately equivalent propositions, such as a>b and b<a. In both cases, something is asserted in different ways, but the matter, the state of affairs, is the same. For this concept of state of affairs he now uses the terminus *Sachlage* (situation of affairs). In both cases we have different subjects and predicates — thus in the previously discussed sense different states of affairs — but the situation of affairs is the same. The two states of affairs, immediately conditioning one another, have the same situation of affairs at their basis, which is merely categorially grasped in different ways. This different manner of categorial apprehension naturally suggests saying that it is one objectual something that presents itself in meaning-different ways. On the other hand, it is clear that here in yet another sense one can speak of an objectuality of the sentence that presents itself in meaning-different ways: namely such that of the same nominal objects the same thing is asserted. Something analogous is to be carried out for all types of judgment, not only the categorical.

### § 8. The Phanological (Phansical) and the Phenomenological (Ontic) Concept of Meaning

The meaningfulness of the expression — its property of stating thus and so — appears to be its primary function vis-à-vis its function of referring to something objectual. Through the fact that the expression means, it refers to something objectual. The expression is an expression by virtue of its meaning-function, and it is from this that it says something of objectualities. For it can only say something of objectualities by stating in a certain way; and to be referred to objects in a direct or indirect, determinate or indeterminate manner, by means of these or those inner or outer characteristic-concepts — expressing these different ways (carrying out sense-bestowing acts with the expressions) — is precisely what constitutes the difference of meaning-bestowal.

Although meaning and objectual reference have been distinguished, Husserl says, it has not yet been clarified what meaning is in this distinction, how meaning and meaning-bestowal are to be separated and understood in their relation to one another, and again how this meaning-function is united with the objectuality-function — whether it merely links up with it or essentially carries it within itself, and how on the correlative side the object meant in expressing and the meaning are to be set in the right relation. The question now is: what is meaning?

#### a) Meaning as the Specific of Meaning-Bestowal

“Meaning” is, as we know and must keep firmly in view, an equivocal name. To separate and clarify its various interconnected significations is our endeavor. The present direction seeks a concept of meaning (or possibly connected concepts) circumscribed by the phrase: the expression says something, and this “what” is distinguished from the object to which it refers. Is meaning something to be sought in the sense-bestowing acts?

The answer is evidently no. More precisely, meaning (in the sense now before us) is no act and no real (*reell*) moment in an act. The expression “says” this and that, and this constitutes its meaning; insofar as it is an expression at all, it has its meaning, for that is what constitutes its essence. Equivocal expressions are really several expressions. Just as we regard tautologically different expressions as one and the same expression, so we regard synonymous or better tautonymous and heterological expressions as different — e.g., “Hahn” (rooster/tap). When it is said that the expression has its meaning, there is obviously no talk of the respective experience — neither the word-constituting nor the sense-constituting one, neither the acts in which the word appears nor those in which word-understanding, or actual asserting, naming, judging, etc., takes place. The expression, the one and same ideal unity — e.g., the German word “Löwe” — means, says something, and what it says is an identical something over against the manifold of acts of speaking and meaning. The expression “a lion” can be spoken innumerable times and understood in the same sense. The sense, the meaning, is one. The meaning-bestowing acts are fleeting experiences; meaning itself is an ideal unity, atemporal, self-identical like every idea. Meaning can also of course be no experience-moment, which would be flowing and fleeting like the whole experience.

If meaning is nothing psychologically individual — no psychic act and no moment therein — and equally from the phenomenological standpoint no phenomenologically singular datum, then it could perhaps be grasped as a specific unity that particularizes itself in the act of meaning in a way the same as or similar to the species Red in singular red-moments. The distinguishing talk of the object that is meant and the manner in which it is meant seems to point in this direction. In actual expressing or the meaning-bestowal enacted therein, something objectual becomes conscious — it is named, asserted — but one and the same objectual thing can be meant in different ways, presented in different ways. The different manners of meaning or presenting (words that here say the same) correspond to different meanings. It is not the individual actual presentations that are the meanings — these are ideal unities — but certain species that particularize themselves in them. In the specific “nature,” the essence of the presentations — more precisely, of the meaning-presentations at issue in expressing — lies what their “referring to objects” means. And it likewise lies in the nature of presentations that they can combine into judgment-unity in such a way that we have consciousness that these phenomenologically different presentations refer to the same objectual something. If this lies in the general essence of these presentations as so characterized, then a specifically universal something stands out in the consciousness of universality, in the consciousness of ideation. The presentations, however they may individually differ as singular particulars, carry in themselves certain common moments that, specifically grasped, yield the meanings.

Husserl illustrates: what is the meaning of the assertive sentence “Socrates was the teacher of Plato” or “The angle-sum—”? Obviously not the act of judging. This meaning exclusively determines what in logico-scientific speech is straightforwardly called the proposition (*Satz*). For the word-sound and the linguistically various word-sounds do not matter. When we call the proposition a truth, and a truth that was once discovered but is not something first brought into being by the discovery, or when we speak of the same proposition whoever may have thought it, we obviously grasp the proposition as an ideal unity and not as a contingent act of presenting, judging, or asserting. On the other hand, what kind of ideal unity is this that we call the meaning of the assertive sentence? We can also say: the judgment. But “judgment” does not mean the contingent act of judging, but a judgment in specie — the general essence that particularizes itself in all judgment-acts of this same content, of this same inner character. The judgment “The triangle has as its angle-sum two right angles” is one, and the judgment “Socrates was—” is another. Each is an ideal unity. Likewise we distinguish the presentation “a triangle,” the presentation “Socrates,” etc., as presentations in the ideal logical sense from presentations in the empirical sense. In this way, Husserl argues, we can satisfy all the demands that Bolzano raised with his fundamental conceptions of “proposition in itself” (*Satz an sich*) and “presentation in itself” (*Vorstellung an sich*). Bolzano did not articulate this interpretation, but isolated remarks point in this direction.

If someone were to object that the proposition as propositional meaning does not coincide with the judgment in the specifically ideal sense — since we can understand an assertive sentence without ourselves judging and yet have its meaning, and this meaning would be the same if we were to judge — then one could respond that this meaning (understood as an ideal species) is indeed not the idea “judgment of such and such content” nor “presentation of such and such content,” but the common content itself. This common content is to be grasped as a common ideal moment on both sides. What is specifically common on both sides would then constitute what is distinctive about propositional meaning vis-à-vis judgment in specie or mere understanding in specie. Meaning would still be a specific that particularizes itself in acts or in certain moments of them. In this specific lies the reference to objectuality, which can, however, be different even with identical objectuality. It must be added that the word “meaning” can possibly be delimited in two ways: one can call the judgment in specie a meaning, but also the common element of judgment in specie and understanding “of the same content” in specie.

For the sphere of names in Mill’s widest sense, the distinction discussed here would correspond to the distinction between nominal positing and the nominal “mere presentations” without positing — as when we say “our Emperor!” speaking naturally in belief, versus cases where we operate with conceptual presentations whose validity is uncertain, like a regular decahedron. But regardless of whether the name is used in positing-consciousness or in some other thinking-consciousness, it “means one and the same thing.” Within this meaning-bestowal, which is neutral with respect to the differences of positing and non-positing, all difference in the manner in which the objectual is conscious — in which determinations, in which relations, whether in any at all, or whether it is rather thought directly, without attributive mediation, in the form of a proper name, etc. — runs its course.

After these discussions, the right of a concept of meaning that draws its sense from a specific in meaning-bestowal seems secured. Depending on the specific character of meaning-consciousness, we are referred in different ways to objectualities — possibly the same objectualities — and this is why we must distinguish between the object simpliciter that is meant and the object as it is meant. “Meaning” is here regarded as the specific of meaning-bestowal insofar as it establishes reference to objectuality and in the manner in which it establishes it. This is the concept of meaning with which the *Logical Investigations* operate.

#### b) Meaning as the Categorial Objectuality Distinguished from the Meant Objectuality Simpliciter

Without attacking the right of the foregoing concept, Husserl says, it seems also possible — and indeed imperative in the direction our starting point was moving — to conceive yet another concept. The word “meaning” can apparently also have a sense that hits upon neither an act nor a specific drawn from an act, but rather something correlatively standing over against it on the objectual side. One speaks often of the intentional object as such or the meant object as such. This “as such” concerns not merely an insensitivity to being and non-being (which already holds for the named or meant object simpliciter), but also the “how” of the categorial apprehension, the “how” of being-determined, of being-meant in general.

To make this clear, Husserl proposes the following reflection. In the assertion “the victor of Jena,” I think and posit precisely: the victor of Jena! What am I directed toward, what stands as it were before my eyes, what do I mean? Well: the, victor, of, Jena. Does not each word, each step-by-step apprehended expression-part, contain a determinacy of that toward which I am directed? But does anything other than something objectual occur here — objectual as opposed to the meaning-bestowal, which is indeed enacted but in which we live and upon which we are therefore not directed? We are not engaged in an ideating abstraction that specifically grasps some moment from the acts. We perform no reflection and hence no ideation presupposing the same or similar direction of gaze. We say “the victor” and mean “the victor,” and when we further say “of Jena,” we mean this determinacy, and therein Jena occurs — we mean the city — and all of this we mean in a unity in which a person is posited who was victorious, and indeed victorious at Jena. If on the other hand it says “the vanquished of Waterloo,” we mean the same person but not the same thing, or the same person not in the same way. We now mean not “the victor” but “the vanquished,” and we refer not to Jena but to Waterloo. On both sides the theme standing before consciousness is a different one, encompassing different things — now a relation to this city, now to that, now being-a-victor, now being-vanquished, etc. Not the object simpliciter is the theme; the object itself never comes before our eyes. A theme comes before our eyes — now this one, now that one. But manifold themes can stand in such relation that we say it is the same — the same person, say — that is meant, once so and the other time otherwise signified, categorially grasped. Meaning would thus be this standing-before-our-eyes, the object as such grasped or thought in such and such a “manner” — where, however, the expression must not mislead as though the object first stood before our eyes and then a manner of thinking it were added, but simply in the way we have it when, living in the understanding of the expression, we are conscious of: the victor of Jena. This is what one wanted to designate under the title “intentional object as such,” and more specifically, “the meant object as such in the manner in which.”

Husserl connects this to the doctrine of categorial objects in the *Logical Investigations*, where he had discussed how objects are categorially grasped through forms such as “and,” “is,” “if,” “but,” etc., and formed into new higher-level objects. There a sharp distinction was already drawn, e.g., between the colligating act belonging to the speech “A and B” in the collective assertion, and the objectual aggregate — the objectual correlate of the former — constituting the total object of the expression “A and B.” When we utter a sentence and live in propositional consciousness, an objectuality constitutes itself, and to every meaningful word and word-part there corresponds something objectual, possibly a categorial and dependent objectual moment. For every expression — especially for every self-enclosed name and every self-enclosed assertion — a twofold objectuality emerges. The difference lies not merely in the fact that one and the same objectuality can be conscious in different manners of meaning-bestowal, in certain specific characters of the same objectual thing, but rather that with the alteration of the manner of consciousness that we call different manners of meaning, there goes hand in hand an alteration in an objectuality to be constituted thereby. If, contrasting the object simpliciter, we speak of the object taken in the manner in which it is meant or thought, then not only the manner of thinking makes the difference, but in the different manner of thinking we “intuit,” we have as theme, a different manner of the thought as such. And thereby there arises indeed a new concept of meaning, which we acknowledge through the parallel expression “categorial objectuality” of the relevant assertion (or assertion-part), in distinction from the meant objectuality simpliciter.

Here too we make ourselves independent of the difference of belief or non-belief, of all differences of positional commitment however broadly the word may be taken, and likewise of the differences of clarity and distinctness. It is to be a pure correlation to the concept of meaning in the sense of the species we discussed in the last lecture. Strictly considered, the present concept of meaning is the earlier one, according to which we only then orient the other. If we distinguish, as in the recent exercises, between phanological (phansical) and phenomenological (ontic) — such that all concepts originating in the act-side, the experience-side in general, and all investigations remaining purely in this sphere, are designated phanological, while the correlate side, that of the constituted objectualities, is designated phenomenological in the narrower sense — then we would have a phanological concept of meaning and a phenomenological one. The latter is the guiding one insofar as we are naturally directed at the objectualities, while we arrive at the phanological concept by reflecting on the experiences and saying: the identity of objects comes to givenness for us in an identity-consciousness, and if this is the case as it is with the identity of meaning, then we must acknowledge that the acts on both sides entering into the unity of identity-consciousness have something in common in their essence — and we give account of this through the talk of identical meaning-bestowal (meaning in the phanological sense).

## Chapter 3. The Sense of the Talk of the Objectual Reference of Presentations

### § 9. Difficulties

#### a) The Problem of So-Called “Objectless Presentations”

Now all sorts of difficulties arise. On the one hand, there is the general difficulty connected with the talk of the object in the case of every presentation, and hence also in the case of meaning — whether we understand presentation and meaning in the sense of acts or of ideal unities of whatever kind. Every presentation is supposed to have an object, to refer to an object, regardless of whether conviction accompanies it or not, and regardless of whether the conviction is correct, i.e., the relevant existential judgment valid or not. The presentation “a centaur” presents a centaur; the presentation “a quadrilateral that is round” presents a quadrilateral, namely one that is round. Of presentations like these we say that no object corresponds to them. They do present an object, but the object in truth does not exist. How is this to be understood? How can one say in one breath “the presented object” and “this very object does not exist”? How can one say “there is an object that the presentation presents” and at the same time “this object in truth does not exist”?

However we may resolve this difficulty, the resolution must proceed in judgments, and if we are to be entitled to such judgments, we must be able to trace them back to insightful, evident judgments in which the relevant situations come to adequate givenness. It is thus clear that it will come down to establishing judgment-connections — specifically evident ones — in which the reference to something objectual that belongs to the essence of all presenting demonstrates itself, and again those in which the sense of the existential judgment (in which the existence of the presented objects is affirmed or denied with respect to some presentation) demonstrates itself. In the first direction, every presentation is supposed to belong to an ideal closed group of presentations that, though differing in content, “present one and the same objectual something.” This points to certain judgment-connections — certain identity-judgments. We must study and clarify these in order to make intelligible in them the sense of this identification. Then we understand what the identical is here — this reference to something objectual. In the other direction, the talk says: “The object of a presentation exists or does not exist.” Here the other-type judgments called existential judgments are to be studied and thereby the sense of this “existence” clarified. It must emerge that in the context of judging, presentations can function in different ways and that accordingly objectual reference is to be ascribed to them in different senses. Ultimate resolution and clarification of these and similar paradoxes lead very deep into judgment-analyses.

#### b) How Can Thematically Different Meanings Refer to the Same Object?

Closer at hand for us now is another series of difficulties. How can an expression in one sense refer at once to two objectualities — two objectualities that are so strangely intertwined? The expression “the victor of Jena” means precisely “the victor of Jena” and something different from “the vanquished of Waterloo.” In the one and the other case, we said, we have something different before our eyes, a different theme as it were; on the other hand, the named object, the object simpliciter that is presented on both sides, is the same. We judge, with good reason: “The victor of Jena is the same as the vanquished of Waterloo.” On the one hand we see a difference (and not merely of acts) and say: “The theme, the meaning here and there is different”; and then we set the meanings into unity and say: “the same object.” Closely connected is the question: what is it, actually, to which we are turned living in the presentation? Surely “its object.” We are turned toward the victor of Jena, we present him, and this is Napoleon; so we present Napoleon. But the same holds for the vanquished of Waterloo — so on both sides it is the same. Yet we said that what stands before our eyes is actually not Napoleon but the victor of Jena. If the talk says “the vanquished of Waterloo,” then again the talk is not of Jena and being-a-victor-at-Jena, but of Waterloo and being-vanquished. On both sides we have something different before our eyes.

How, then, are the object simpliciter and the meaning (ontically) conscious in meaning? If we look toward the meanings, how can we carry out the identity-judgment and say: “The victor of Jena is the same as the vanquished of Waterloo”? We would have to say, rather, that the one and the other are different. But if one says we look toward the objects and not toward the meanings, we feel the difficulty. For are the objects something alongside the meanings such that, as with simultaneously given objects of our visual field, we could attend to some and ignore the others? Is it not evident that we cannot be directed toward the named objects without being turned toward them in such and such a meaning? The person presented now as “Napoleon,” now as “the victor of Jena,” and potentially through innumerable other presentations — we cannot get hold of this person without some such presentation, i.e., without some determinate manner of meaning. Are we then not in each case turned toward it alone, as the only truly standing-before-our-eyes? But then the question returns: the meaning is always something different, so we would have to judge accordingly — that the victor of Jena and Napoleon are not identical but different. And furthermore, through the meaning we say something about the object. How is that possible and to be understood?

#### c) A First Attempt at Resolution

To resolve these difficulties — which seem to challenge the right of our last conception of the concept of meaning — Husserl proposes the following reflection. We start, say, from the identity-judgment “A is the same as B; the victor of Jena is the same as the vanquished of Waterloo.” This judgment-consciousness is consciousness of a self-same. Through this judgment, the two presentations we have when we say first A and then B in the assertion are brought to a unity; they are components of the judgment and linked by the overarching consciousness of “is identical.” In this case we say correctly: in the judgment, in this conviction, the thing presented in presentation A and the thing presented in presentation B are posited, and posited as the same, as an identical, as one object; the two presentations present the same object, and it holds in this conviction as the same.

Similarly for a correspondingly constructed diversity-judgment “A is not the same as B.” Wherever the judgment is the same, the self-same persists: the object judged and presented in the underlying nominal presentations remains the same. The sameness of the judgment points not merely to judgments identical in phanological essence but to those that, despite possibly considerable phanological variation, share a certain essence that we have in view when speaking of “the same judgment.” This concerns identity of sense — that selfsameness which obtains whether we carry out the judgment “The victor of Jena etc.” intuitively or non-intuitively, distinctly or indistinctly, holding fast only its sense and the conviction.

Husserl asks: how does it stand with the turning toward meanings? Are meanings also objects? Can it be said in the same sense that we are turned toward them in meaning? Obviously not. Meanings can indeed be grasped as objects, distinguished from others, and possibly set as identical. One can say: I think the victor of Jena, and therein I think a victor and I think Jena, etc. And this, what I think, is different from what I think with the words “vanquished of Waterloo.” But when I carry out such diversity-judgments, what I objectually set as different is something different from what I had before. To express what we now mean, we must not say: “The victor of Jena is not identical with the vanquished of Waterloo.” The two presentations as straightforward components in a judgment-unity fit truthfully only into an identity-judgment, not into a diversity-judgment. We could retain the wording in written expression, but we would have to put the expressions “victor of Jena” etc. in quotation marks — expressing thereby that the nominal presentations, and thus also the objects, have changed. Better still, we say: “Not A is different from B, but the meaning A is different from the meaning B.” This implies that meanings can indeed be objects of presentation and judgment, but only in the way other objects can — namely, through presentations referred to them, hence through (vis-à-vis themselves) new meanings. And evidently, the consciousness corresponding to the expression “the victor of Jena,” wherever this expression functions in normal asserting, is not an object-consciousness of the meaning. Our object is the victor of Jena — that which turns out to be identical with Napoleon etc. — not the meaning “the victor of Jena.” But is not the meaning conscious and necessarily conscious?

### § 10. Closer Working-Out of the Difficulty of How the Relation of Meaning and Object Simpliciter Is to Be Understood

Husserl recapitulates the difficulty found at the close of the previous lecture: that one and the same expression, taken in the same sense, is supposed to refer to different objectualities, of all of which it is said that it means, presents them. Not only did we have in view that parts of an expression, insofar as they are themselves expressions, refer to something objectual — generally to something other than the whole expression — and that the whole expression also co-presents the objectual of its parts; we also had in view that one speaks of the whole expression as having a unified meaning to which all expression-parts contribute. Under “meaning” one can understand on the one hand a specific of meaning-bestowal, on the other the meant objectuality as such.

How is the relation of these two objectualities — meaning and object simpliciter, categorial and predicated objectuality — to be understood? Can it be meant in the proper sense that the object simpliciter itself takes on various forms, categorial forms? If a house exists, a real thing exists. If this house takes on forms and connects itself through forms with other actualities, must the resulting new thing again be in real actuality? Do we find in actuality not only houses simpliciter but also categorially formed houses? One would hardly want to say so. On the other hand, how can one avoid saying that the same object presents itself in categorially different ways? And one must consider that there are evidently objects that contain in themselves categorial formations and that can in turn be thought in different ways, so that a distinction always re-emerges between the object itself and the categorially different ways in which it is meant. Perhaps there are no objects at all that do not in some way encompass thinking-forms, excepting only the objects at the last boundary of objectual determination — the purely phanological data.

We must not be misled, Husserl warns, by the — when properly understood, not illegitimate — talk that it is thinking which lends the objects their categorial form. The naive and truly difficult-to-overcome thought must be kept firmly at bay: that the object is what it is in itself, that thinking can unfortunately only get hold of it through the medium of presenting, through acts of perceiving, remembering, expecting, believing, etc. — above all through thinking in the pregnant sense — and that the object is thus a thought object with the thinking’s own forms through which the relation to the objectual necessarily takes place. We are long since clear, Husserl says, that we cannot begin with the psychologically natural opposition of object on one side and thinking that contingently thinks the object and unfortunately reaches it only through the psychological medium on the other. In thinking itself, and only in thinking, can the distinction between the object itself and the thinking of the object a priori be carried out. “Object itself” points to certain unities of identification to be carried out in identifying thinking. Furthermore, thinking-forms understood as forms psychologically or phanologically inhering in thinking cannot serve our purposes. For the presented object is not a component in thinking that is then clothed by further components — the so-called forms. Presenting a house, I have a consciousness. In this consciousness a house is presented and nothing else; and this presented object, which is a house, is no psychic phenomenon, no real consciousness-moment. The categorial form “a house” is no immanent phanological or psychological form. Meaning in the sense of categorial objectuality in its full categorial apprehension is no experience and no piece of an experience, nothing phanologically discoverable and also no abstraction therefrom — no phanological species.

When we carry out an assertion — whether expressing a more or less composite nominal presentation or completing a full assertion — we live in the subject-matters; we carry out no reflection, we do not look at acts and act-moments. Apprehending word for word in the given grammatical context, we have constantly something objectual before our eyes; everything has objectifying function. The objectual stands before us in these and those forms — say a deictically indicated and conceptually grasped-as-a-human subject, of which this and that is asserted, and the relation to other thus and so conceptually grasped and posited objects. All this stands before the thinking-eyes.

In connection with the question of how meaning as categorial objectuality — and specifically as the objectuality determining the object simpliciter, grasping it thus and so — stands in its distinctive relation to the object simpliciter, there stands the phenomenological question about the manner of consciousness. Carrying out a meaning-bestowal, we mean the objectual. In a certain sense it is said that we mean the object simpliciter, and in a certain sense that we mean the meaning. Do we look at two things? Is it like cases where several objects in our perceptual field compete for the privilege of attention? Obviously not. The objects are not in meaning-bestowal something alongside the meanings. It is evident that we can be turned toward objects only by being turned toward them as so-and-so-determined, so-and-so grasped in meaning. In asserting we are turned toward a certain person — this person is our object — either directly (through the meaning-apprehension the proper name stamps out, through an “own-meaning”) or in the form “the victor of Jena,” etc. There is no meaning-bestowal without a determinate manner of meaning, and so the object stands there precisely in this manner.

### § 11. The Synthesis of Identification

To gain clarity in the main lines, Husserl now proceeds from the acts. We want to see how cases stand in which we say in one sense and the other that we have something “before our eyes” — now the objectual thing that is predicated, now the meaning that must first mean the objectual. Take the asserting judging, say the identifying judgment “A is the same as B; the victor of Jena is the same as the vanquished of Waterloo.” We understand the words and the whole assertion “normally,” and we not merely understand but judge. This judgment-consciousness is consciousness of a self-same, and thereby a fixed sense of an identical objectual is determined, which is posited in the judgment and posited as identical. Through this judgment, the two presentations that we have when we first say A and then say B are brought to unity. They are components of the judgment and linked by the overarching consciousness of “is identical.”

Wherever the judgment is the same, the self-same persists. The sameness of the judgment points us not merely to judgments identical in phanological essence but to those that share a certain essence — what we have in view when speaking of “the same judgment.” It concerns identity of sense. The same kind of essential identity runs through the parts of the judgment, hence the presentations A and B occurring therein. Not of the fluctuating acts but of the presentations in this identical sense do we say that they have reference to the same object in the judgment and both present one and the same in the objectual sense.

Wherever a presentation appears — in whatever judgment-unity it may help build up the total meaning, provided it straightforwardly and directly fits into it — it remains the case that it presents the same object. And indeed, the one judgment and the other can always and with evidence be linked through an identification. If it is said once “A is B” and another time “A is C,” then we can say “A is B, and the same is C.” Object is an identical, an identical of the presentation; object of the asserting judgment is an identical of the judgment — that which brings the presentations occurring in the judgment to identity-unity, which through the connection and positing in this and other judgments comes and can always come to judgmental identity-positing, that which receives and preserves its identity in the synthesis of presentations. But the talk of that which is identical does not point to a something alongside presentation and judgment, but to the synthesis of identification into which presentations enter by their essence — i.e., as those specific essences.

Husserl adds: if the judgment is false, the identity of the object does not obtain — namely as so-determined as the judgment sets it. The judgment is conviction of identity; it obtains or does not obtain depending on whether the judgment is true or false. On the other hand, it belongs to the essence of every presentation that it can enter into certain valid identity-judgments; and in these, when they are evident, the evident consciousness of the selfsameness of the object of the presentations obtains. The identification can have its various modes depending on the character of the underlying presentations — they can be carried out in the character of positing or without positing, a difference that also carries over into the specific. Accordingly the object of both presentations counts as existing, and the judgment posits the existing object as the same; or the object is presented as merely thought but not in being-mode, and the identity-consciousness is identity-consciousness of one and the same, differently presented but not as-existing posited object.

Moving from judgment to non-convictional thinking — mere understanding of an assertion without judging — Husserl distinguishes between presentation as components of judgments (to which object-consciousness was ascribed with reference to possible judging) and presentation in the sense of a whole assertion that is not carried out in belief. If from two arbitrary nominal presentations we compose an assertion without conviction — “Bismarck was the greatest French statesman” — the presentations are brought to unity not in a judgment but in mere presentation. The asserted identity is not in the real sense asserted, not believed, merely thought. This propositional thought of identity thinks the object of both presentations as the same, but it is not thereby the same; it would be if the assertion were a true one. Even though arbitrary presentations can be brought to mere presentational unity through such mere identity-thoughts, this does not disturb the firm talk of the object of each presentation. For this talk has its root not in arbitrary identity-thoughts but in certain true and ultimately evident judgments. Every presentation, by its essence, admits not only incorporation into identity-thoughts and possible identity-judgments but into true identity-judgments. The principles of these identity-syntheses belong to the sphere of pure meaning-logic.

### § 12. Supplements

#### a) Presented Presentations Do Not Objectify

Husserl clarifies a point requiring further attention. The reference to an object as a self-identical is ascribed to the presentation insofar as it is a possible member of a valid judgment-unity in which it can unfold its objectifying function and carry it through in identifications. It may occur outside judgment as an uncompleted asserting, alive in understanding, but it must be understood exactly as it would function as a judgment-member, only apart from the further connective functions. Within the judgment too we must be attentive to what therein is really objectifying — i.e., is a presentation — and what is not. Certain meaning-modifications are possible with unchanged expressions, and the presentations change accordingly. If it says “The Emperor has returned to Berlin,” the expression “the Emperor” functions as a presentation, and an object — an identical — is presented, which can also be presented through other presentations such as “Wilhelm II,” “the present King of Prussia,” etc. But if we say “The presentation ‘the Emperor’ is a nominal presentation,” the same expression “the Emperor” occurs, but this expression now does not function in its nominal meaning. No presentation of the Emperor corresponds to it; rather, a distinctive presentation of a presentation is involved. The expression “the Emperor” is now a proper name for the presentation “the Emperor,” which does not, however, appear as a presentation in the judgment-context and does not function objectifyingly. A presentation that enters into being-presented has undergone a modification. Indirect presentations — presentations that refer to objects through the medium of other presentations — must be carefully separated from presentation-presentations. The latter are directed solely at certain presentations and in no way at their objects.

#### b) Position and Function of Nominal Presentations in the Propositional Whole

Under the title “presentation,” one may have thought exclusively of nominal presentations, and indeed these first come to prominence in their objectifying function. Restricting ourselves expressly to this group, we must note the various functional turns that a nominal presentation, preserving its own essence, can undergo in the encompassing thinking-context. A nominal presentation can function as a full subject-presentation; it can also present a relational point on the predicate side to another subject; it can appear within an attributive presentation that transfers the relational presentation into the subject itself, etc. Linguistically, Husserl recalls the different case-forms of the noun. A change has evidently occurred when a presentation has passed from one position and function into another — a change that has its objectifying significance in the wider context.

More generally: as it belongs to the essence of a whole that its parts have their connective function, so too here. If it concerns a kind of whole whose essence permits composition from like parts, the exchange of such parts within the whole again yields a whole of this kind. But the same part, the same in content, has in the new whole a different position and function, and precisely thereby the whole itself is altered within the overall type. Assertions, judgments, and mere thoughts belong here. They can contain several nominal presentations. An exchange of these in the propositional whole yields a new proposition. The same name at a new position accomplishes something new in thought. In itself the nominal presentation is the same and means the same, but in the whole it has a new function. The functional form is something that fuses with the presentation’s own content; the name must always function in some way, and interwoven with its own presentational content it has a functional characteristic pointing to the encompassing presentational whole. We have here throughout a distinction between content and form. The latter stamps itself more or less clearly in the grammatical form of the expression, pointing beyond to the broader presentational context and the presentational function to be performed therein. We recognize throughout the identical content in which in a certain way constantly the same is presented. We ascribe to the nominal presentation, independently of positional function and grammatical form, reference to the same objectual something — and the sense of this talk rests on identifying judgments and truths.

## Chapter 4. The Constitution of the Objects-About

### § 13. Status of the Investigation

How far have we come? Husserl reviews. We wanted to make clear what it is: the reference of the presentation to a presented objectuality. The presentations in question are meaning-bestowing acts — acts through which we refer, in asserting, to what is asserted, and in naming, to what is named. This reference is to hold whether it is a matter of actual asserting (i.e., judging, conviction-borne asserting) or not — where conviction is absent — and for full assertions as well as for possible or actual assertion-parts. A name names the same and a nominal presentation presents the same analyses of the time were subject, according to which ideal objectualities were everywhere replaced by psychic experiences. To designate psychic acts enacted in expression as meanings contradicts the clear sense of the commonly accepted way of speaking of sense and meaning, which is oriented by something quite different from the psychological reflections so remote from natural thinking. The respective psychic acts are flowing and fleeting appearances; meanings, by contrast, are identical unities over against these manifolds. In normal speech, no one understands by the meaning of the expression “the king of Prussia” their fleeting experiences in speaking or understanding the expression; otherwise one could not say that the same expression — used repeatedly and by different people — means (even with the multiplication of meaning-experiences) identically the same thing, that the meaning of “the king of Prussia” is one, and in the strictly identical sense.

What then, Husserl asks, is this objectuality called meaning? And how does it constitute itself: in the sense-bestowing acts, or on their basis? Is it an analogous ideal unity like the unity of the expression? Or does the word “meaning” in customary usage have, depending on different contexts, multiple meanings — all referring to verbal presenting and thinking — that must be distinguished and investigated according to their phenomenological sources? This, he says, is indeed the case.

### § 6. The Meant Objectuality and the “Said” as Such

Husserl identifies a first sense of “meaning” determined by the meant objectuality. Earlier logic, in its very meager discussions of the relation of expression, meaning, and meaning-bestowal, looked only at names and thus noticed only named objectualities. It did not come to awareness that in the performance of a whole assertion, and corresponding to it as a whole, an objectuality is conscious — though in a quite different way from the objectuality named in the subject-name. It did not come to light that in a certain sense every self-enclosed expression relates as an expression to an objectuality, and further, ultimately every expression-part that still has the character of an expression, insofar as it is “meaningful.” An arbitrary sound or syllable contained in an expression and distinguishable in it is a part of the word-sound but not a part of the expression in the full sense. There are, however, parts that as such carry meaning-function: meaningful parts that are themselves to be designated as expressions, even if perhaps as non-independent expressions. Husserl distinguishes between composite and simple, and again between independent and non-independent expressions. Among the latter belong particles, inflections, etc. The total meaning of an independent expression encompasses the meanings of its non-independent parts; and just as to it as a whole there corresponds a meant objectuality, so too to every non-independent expression-part there corresponds in a certain way a meant objectual something, though one cannot without dangerous misunderstandings simply say that to the expression-part there corresponds a part of the object meant in the expression-whole.

He notes parenthetically the “indication function” (*Andeutungsfunktion*) of grammatical expression-forms and how they awaken the meaning-consciousness of a thought-matter formed in this or that way. The nominal subject-form, the verbal form, calls to us in understanding: “It is the subject-object! The object about which there is talk and about which something is to be predicated.” The predicate-form of the expression calls: “This is asserted of that subject,” and so on. In the background, over against the properly so-called meanings (subject-meanings, predicate-meanings, etc.), there stand secondary indications and secondary “meanings” (opinions).

The presupposition for carrying through this conception is that the word “objectuality” be taken in an extraordinarily wide, indeed the widest conceivable sense — a sense that permits even non-independent moments, and even categorial forms, to be designated as objectualities.

Husserl then identifies a fundamental fact (not an empirical one) connected with the building up of unified expressions from partial expressions: differently constructed expressions — e.g., two composite expressions not built from identically the same expressions in the same form — can refer to one and the same objectuality. Likewise a simple expression and a composite one, e.g., “Napoleon” and “the victor at Jena” and again “the victor of Jena” and “the vanquished of Waterloo”; “the conic sections,” “the curve of the second order,” “the curve of the second class.” This leads to some distinctions. Two expressions are called extrinsically or, more clearly, tautologically different when what distinguishes them lies only in the words, in the mere words — above all, merely linguistic, merely idiomatic differences, e.g., “der König” and “le roi.” The essential lies on the thematic side, in the meaning and not in the mere word-consciousness. We abstract from tautological differences: we simply treat tautologically different expressions as the same expression.

If we find the same objectuality “meant” in two different expressions — in the case of nominal expressions: named — and wish to seek the meaning in the direction of the objectuality, we must forthwith attend to the distinction between the objectuality that is meant and the objectuality in the way it is meant. A new sense of “meaning” thereby confronts us. Tautological expressions say one and the same; non-tautological expressions always say something different. The object is the same whether I say “Napoleon” or “the victor of Jena.” But the two expressions say different things. In the second, we do not say “Napoleon,” and this concerns not merely the word-sound, since what the one word says does not occur in the other expression at all. In the latter there is talk of Jena and of being-a-victor. Differently saying expressions refer to one and the same objectuality; differently saying names name the same person or thing. We can thus evidently distinguish the thing itself that is named and the same thing taken in the way in which it is here said. What we here make clear to ourselves under the title of “different saying” or different manner of assertion gives us the new sense of meaning.

It is merely a different expression when we now choose the words: every expression has a meaning; non-tautological expressions have different meanings, tautological ones one and the same meaning. Every expression also has an object (taken in the widest sense) to which it refers. But meaning and object are now distinguished, for expressions of different meaning can refer to the same object, insofar as we understand by “meaning” that “said as such.”

### § 7. Propositional Meaning, State of Affairs, and Situation of Affairs

For all expressions and meanings, Husserl continues, we must distinguish between meaning itself and the objectuality to which it refers. In a categorial proposition, say “S is p,” we must distinguish not only on the side of the subject-meaning between the subject-object and the subject-meaning itself, and likewise for all nominal presentations occurring in the proposition (e.g., within the predicate), but equally for the whole proposition. It asserts that the subject-object has such-and-such a property. This is an objectuality — precisely this state of affairs (*Sachverhalt*): that such-and-such an object has such-and-such a property. This state of affairs remains the same as long as the object is the same and the property the same and it remains the case that the object has this property. But this is an objectual commonality across propositions differing in meaning: the same subject-object can be presented in meaningfully different ways, and likewise the same property. We must therefore distinguish the asserted state of affairs from the respective way it comes to assertion — namely, in the form of the different meanings of assertions that, saying different things, nonetheless assert the same property of the same subject.

Husserl notes for those reading the *Logical Investigations* in parallel that the concept of “state of affairs” (*Sachverhalt*) is differently conceived in the First Investigation, though with the same intent of distinction. In the later Investigations, the usage agrees much more with the present account. He had pointed, to distinguish propositional meaning and state of affairs, to examples of immediately equivalent propositions such as a > b and b < a. In both cases, something is asserted in different ways, but the subject matter, the state of affairs, is the same. For this concept of state of affairs he now uses the term “situation of affairs” (*Sachlage*). In both cases we have different subjects and predicates, hence in the previously discussed sense different states of affairs, but the situation of affairs is the same. The two states of affairs, mutually conditioning each other, have the same situation of affairs as their basis, which is only categorially apprehended in different ways. This different manner of categorial apprehension naturally suggests saying that there is something objectual that presents itself in meaningfully different ways. On the other hand, it is clear that here a further sense of objectuality of the proposition can be meant — one in which the same nominal objects are spoken about and the same thing is asserted of them.

### § 8. The Phanological (Phansic) and the Phenomenological (Ontic) Concept of Meaning

#### a) Meaning as the Specific of Meaning-Bestowal

Husserl observes that the meaningfulness of the expression — its property of saying thus and so — seems to be its primary function over against its function of referring to something objectual. Through the fact that the expression means, it refers to something objectual. The expression is expression by virtue of its meaning-function, and it is this function that makes it say something of objectualities. For it can say something of objectualities only by saying in a certain way; and to be referred to objects in this or that manner — directly or indirectly, determinately or indeterminately, by means of these or those inner or outer characteristic-concepts — this is precisely what constitutes the diversity of meaning-bestowal.

Although meaning and objectual reference have been distinguished, it has not yet been clarified what meaning is in this distinction, how meaning and meaning-bestowal are to be separated and understood in their relation; nor how the meaning-function is united with the objectuality-function — whether they are merely conjoined or how meaning essentially carries objectual reference within itself, and how, on the correlative side, the object meant in expression and the meaning are to be placed in the right relation. The immediate question is: what is meaning?

Husserl then observes that “meaning” is an equivocal name. To sort out and clarify its various interconnected meanings is the task. The present direction seeks a concept of meaning (or possibly connected concepts) circumscribed by the locution: the expression says something, and this “what” is distinguished from the object to which it refers. Is meaning something to be sought in the sense-bestowing acts? The answer is evidently no. More precisely, meaning (in the presently envisaged sense) is no act and no real moment in an act. The expression “says” this and that, and this constitutes its meaning; and insofar as it is an expression at all, it has its meaning, for that is what makes its essence. Equivocal expressions are really multiple expressions. The expression, the one and the same, the ideal unity — e.g., the German word “Löwe” — means, it says something, and what it says is an identical something over against the manifold of acts of speaking and meaning. The expression “a lion” can be spoken countless times and understood in the same sense. The sense, the meaning, is one. The meaning-bestowing acts are fleeting experiences; meaning itself is an ideal unity, atemporal, self-identical like every idea.

Naturally, meaning can also not be a moment of experience, which would be flowing and fleeting like the whole experience. If meaning is nothing psychologically singular, no psychic act and no moment therein — and equally, from the phenomenological standpoint, no phenomenologically singular datum — then it could perhaps be conceived as a specific unity that singularizes itself in the act of meaning in the same or a similar way as the species Red singularizes itself in singular red-moments. This seems to be indicated by the distinguishing talk of the object that is meant and the way in which it is meant. In actual expression and the meaning-bestowal enacted therein, something objectual becomes conscious — it is named, asserted; but one and the same objectual thing can hereby be meant in different ways, presented in different ways. And to the different ways of meaning or presenting (words that here say the same) there correspond the different meanings. Not the individual actual presentations are the meanings — these are ideal unities — but certain species singularizing themselves in them. In the specific “nature,” in the essence of the presentations — more specifically, of the meaning-presentations relevant to expression — lies what their “referring to objects” means; and again it lies in the nature of presentations that they can join into judgment-unity in such a way that we thereby have the consciousness that these presentations — phenomenologically different presentations — refer to the same objectual something. If this lies in the general essence of these presentations as so constituted, then a specifically universal something lifts itself out in the consciousness of universality, in the consciousness of ideation. The presentations, however they may individually differ as singular particularities, carry within themselves certain common moments that, taken specifically, yield the meanings.

Husserl illustrates: what is the meaning of the assertoric proposition “Socrates was the teacher of Plato” or “the angle-sum…”? Not the act of judging. This meaning is determined exclusively by what in logico-scientific speech is straightforwardly called the “proposition” (*Satz*). For the word-sound and the linguistically different word-sounds do not matter. When we call the proposition a truth and a truth that was once discovered but that is therefore not something first arising in the discovery, or when we speak of the same proposition regardless of who thought it or whether anyone thought it at all, we evidently conceive the proposition as an ideal unity and not as a contingent act of presenting, judging, or asserting.

On the other hand, this ideal unity — the meaning of the assertoric proposition — can be called “the judgment.” But judgment here means not the contingent judgment-act but a judgment in specie, the universal essence that singularizes itself in all judgment-acts of this same content, of this same inner character. Each such judgment is an ideal unity. Likewise we distinguish the presentation “a triangle,” the presentation “Socrates,” etc. — the presentation in the ideal logical sense from the presentation in the empirical sense. In this way, Husserl says, we can satisfy all the requirements that Bolzano raised with his fundamental conceptions of “proposition in itself” (*Satz an sich*) as distinguished from the thought, verbally formulated, actually executed proposition, and “presentation in itself” (*Vorstellung an sich*) as opposed to the actual presentation. The word “meaning” in the generic sense generalizes so that not merely the specific universal of the evident judgment and of the intuitively executed judgment are equated, but also the empty and the full, the non-intuitive and the intuitive. Husserl adds that meaning could be doubly delimited: one can call the judgment in specie “meaning,” but also the common element of judgment in specie and understanding “of the same content” in specie. For the sphere of names in Mill’s widest sense, the corresponding distinction would be between nominal positing and nominal “mere presentations” without positing.

After the given discussions, the right of a concept of meaning that draws its sense from a specific in meaning-bestowal seems secured. Depending on the specific character of meaning-consciousness, we are related in different ways to objectualities — and possibly the same objectualities — and this is why we must distinguish between the object simpliciter that is meant and the object as it is meant. “Meaning” is thereby regarded as the specific of meaning-bestowal insofar as it establishes reference to objectuality and in the manner in which it establishes it. The manner of meaning-bestowal is in each case some determinate one: if something objectual is to be meant at all, it must be meant in some way. Taking meaning-bestowal and grasping in it the specific essence by virtue of which the objectual thing comes not merely to expression at all but in this and that particular way, this specific essence is meaning in the ideal sense. This is the concept of meaning with which the *Logical Investigations* operate, whose interest is everywhere primarily directed at the essence of acts insofar as they are constitutive for objectivity.

#### b) Meaning as the Categorial Objectuality Distinguished from the Meant Objectuality Simpliciter

Without attacking the right of the foregoing concept, it seems possible and even called for — precisely in the direction of the initial approach — to conceive yet another concept. The word “meaning” can, it seems, also have a sense that targets not an act, nor anything specific extracted from the act, but rather something correlatively standing over against it on the objectual side. One often speaks of the intentional object “as such” or the meant object “as such.” This “as such” here concerns not only an indifference to being and non-being (which already holds for the named or meant object simpliciter) but also aims at the how of categorial apprehension, the how of being-determined, of being-meant in general. We ourselves speak, in distinction from the object simpliciter, of the object as it is meant. This “how” certainly points, with respect to act-experiences, to certain modes of the same that can be specifically grasped; but correlatively something also corresponds to them on the objectual side.

Husserl illustrates: in the assertion “the victor of Jena,” I think and posit: the victor of Jena! What am I directed toward, what stands before my eyes, what do I mean? Well: “the,” “victor,” “of,” “Jena.” Does not each word, each step-by-step apprehended expression-part, contain a determinateness of what I am directed toward? Does anything other than something objectual come into play here? Something objectual over against the meaning-bestowal, which is indeed enacted but in which we live — toward which we are therefore not directed. And naturally we are not occupied with an ideating abstraction that specifically grasps some moment from the acts. We perform no reflection and thus also no ideation presupposing a similar direction of gaze. So we say “the victor” and mean “the victor,” and when we further say “of Jena,” we mean this determinateness, and therein Jena occurs — we mean the city — and all of this we mean in a unity in which a person is posited who was victorious, and indeed victorious at Jena. When, on the other hand, it says “the vanquished of Waterloo,” we mean the same person but not the same thing, or the same person not in the same way. We now mean not “the victor” but “the vanquished,” and we relate not to Jena but to Waterloo. On both sides, one might say, the theme before consciousness is a different one, and it is something that in the manner of the theme encompasses different things — now a relation to this city, now to that, now being-a-victor, now being-vanquished. Not the object simpliciter is the theme. The object itself never comes before our eyes. A theme comes before our eyes — now this one, now that. But manifold themes can stand in such a relation to one another that we say it is the same, the same person perhaps, that is meant — once grasped conceptually in this way and another time in that. Meaning would thus be this that stands before our eyes, this object apprehended or thought in this and that “way” — as such. But the expression must not mislead, as though first the object stood before one’s eyes and then a way of thinking it were added.

In the *Logical Investigations*, Husserl notes, there was often and extensively talk of categorial objects — of the way objects are categorially apprehended through the forms “and,” “is,” “if,” “but,” etc., and formed into new objects of higher level. There was already a sharp distinction, e.g., between the colligating act belonging to the expression “A and B” in a collective assertion and the objectual aggregate — the objectual correlate of the former — that constitutes the total object of “A and B.” When we utter a proposition and live in propositional consciousness, an objectuality constitutes itself, and to every meaningful word and word-part there corresponds something objectual — possibly a categorial and non-independent objectual moment. Thus also to a propositional subject, say “the victor of Jena,” there corresponds something objectual that in a certain way includes the objectual Jena, the determinateness of being-a-victor, etc. This categorial objectuality is then naturally a different one in the case where we would take the other subject “the vanquished of Waterloo,” even though in both cases the talk may hold that one and the same person is merely meant through different categorial formations.

For every expression, especially for every closed name and every closed assertion, a twofold objectuality results. And the difference lies not merely in the fact that one and the same objectuality can be conscious in different ways of meaning-bestowal, in certain specific characters of the same objectual thing; rather, with the change in the manner of consciousness that we call different manner of meaning-bestowal, a change in a correlatively constituted objectuality goes hand in hand. Speaking of the object taken in the way in which it is meant or thought, not only the manner of thinking makes the difference, but in the different manner of thinking we “intuit” — we have as theme — a different manner of the thought as such. And precisely thereby a new concept of meaning indeed arises, which we acknowledge through the parallel expression “categorial objectuality” of the relevant assertion (or assertion-part), in distinction from the meant objectuality simpliciter.

Here too we make ourselves independent of the difference between belief or non-belief, of all differences of positional commitment however broadly taken, and equally of the differences of clarity and distinctness. It is to be a pure correlation to the concept of meaning in the sense of that species discussed in the preceding lecture. Precisely considered, the present concept of meaning is the one oriented before the other. Distinguishing, as in the preceding exercises, between phanological (phansic) and phenomenological (ontic) — so that all concepts having their origin in the act-side, in the experience-side generally, and all investigations remaining purely in this sphere are designated as phanological, while the correlative side, that of constituted objectualities, is designated as phenomenological in the narrower sense — we would have a phanological concept of meaning and a phenomenological one. The latter is the guiding one insofar as we are naturally directed at objectualities, while we arrive at the phanological concept by reflecting on the experiences and saying to ourselves: the identity of objects comes to givenness for us in an identity-consciousness, and if that is the case — and specifically as it is the case with identity of meaning — then we must acknowledge that the respective acts entering into the unity of identity-consciousness share something in their essence, and we take account of this through the talk of identical meaning-bestowal (meaning in the phanological sense).

## Chapter 3. The Sense of the Talk of the Objectual Reference of Presentations

### § 9. Difficulties

#### a) The Problem of So-Called “Objectless Presentations”

Now, Husserl says, all manner of difficulties arise. On the one hand, there is the general difficulty connected with the talk of the object that belongs, as with every presentation, so also with meaning — whether we understand “presentation” and “meaning” in the sense of acts or in the sense of ideal unities of whatever sort. Every presentation is supposed to have an object, to refer to an object, regardless of whether conviction accompanies it or not, and regardless of whether the conviction is correct or not, whether the relevant existential judgment is valid or not. The presentation “a centaur” presents a centaur; the presentation “a quadrangle that is round” presents precisely a quadrangle, one that is round. Of such presentations we say that no object exists corresponding to them. They do present an object, but the object in truth is not. How is this to be understood? How can I say in one breath “the presented object” and “that very object does not exist”?

However we may resolve this difficulty, the resolution must occur in judgments, and if we are to have a right to such judgments, we must be able to trace them back to insightful, evident judgments in which the relevant situations come to adequate givenness. It will thus depend on establishing judgment-connections — evident ones — in which, on the one hand, the reference to something objectual that belongs to the essence of all presenting is demonstrated, and on the other hand, those in which the sense of the existential judgment is demonstrated — in which, in relation to some presentation, the existence of the presented objects is affirmed or denied. In the first direction, every presentation is supposed to belong to an ideal closed group of presentations that, though differing in content, nonetheless “present one and the same objectual thing.” This points to certain judgment-connections, to certain identity-judgments. We must study and clarify these to make the sense of this identification intelligible. Then we understand what the identical something is — this reference to something objectual. In the other direction: “The object of a presentation exists or does not exist.” Here the different sorts of judgments called existential judgments are to be studied and thereby the sense of this “existence” clarified.

#### b) How Can Thematically Different Meanings Refer to the Same Object?

Another series of difficulties stands closer at hand. How can an expression in one sense refer at once to two objectualities — two objectualities that are so strangely intertwined? The expression “the victor of Jena” means precisely “the victor of Jena” and something different from “the vanquished of Waterloo.” In the one and the other case, we said, we have something different before our eyes, a different theme as it were; on the other hand, the named object, the object simpliciter that is presented in both cases, is the same. We judge, and with good reason: “The victor of Jena is the same as the vanquished of Waterloo.” At once we see a difference (and not merely one of acts) and say: “The theme, the meaning here and there, is different”; and then we set the meanings together and say: “the same object.” And closely connected: what is it, really, to which we are turned in living in the presentation? To “its object.” To the victor of Jena we are turned — him we present, and this is Napoleon — so we present Napoleon; and the same holds for the vanquished of Waterloo. On the other hand, we said that what stands before our eyes is really not Napoleon but the victor of Jena. And if the speech reads “the vanquished of Waterloo,” then again Jena and being-a-victor at Jena are not what is spoken of, but Waterloo and being-vanquished. We thus have something different before our eyes in each case.

How then are the object simpliciter and the meaning (ontically) conscious in meaning-bestowal? Looking within at the meanings, how can we perform the identity-judgment “the victor of Jena is the same as the vanquished of Waterloo”? We would have to say, rather, that the one and the other are different. If one says we look at the objects and not at the meanings, we feel the difficulty. For are the objects something alongside the meanings, so that we could attend to some and ignore the others as in the case of co-given objects in our visual field? Is it not evident that we cannot be directed at the named objects without being turned toward them in this or that meaning? The person presented now as “Napoleon,” now as “the victor of Jena,” and possibly through countless other presentations — we cannot get hold of her without some such presentation, i.e., without some determinate manner of meaning. Are we then each time turned toward the meaning, and toward it alone as the only thing truly standing before our eyes? But then the question returns: the meaning is everywhere something different, so we would have to judge correspondingly — not that the victor of Jena and Napoleon are identical, but different.

#### c) A First Attempt at Resolution

To bring these difficulties to resolution — difficulties that seem to challenge the right of our latest conception of meaning — Husserl proposes the following consideration. We start from the identity-judgment “A is the same as B, the victor of Jena the same as the vanquished of Waterloo.” This judgment-consciousness is consciousness of something that is the same. Through this judgment, the presentations of A and of B are brought to a unity; they are components of the judgment, connected by the overarching consciousness of “identical.” In this case we correctly say that in the judgment the two presentations are referred to the same object. The same presentations can occur in other predicating judgments, even when these are not pure identity-judgments. In each case, wherever the same presentation fits itself straightforwardly into a judgment, it presents the same object. Naturally the judgment can also be false — then the identity of objects was merely supposed. But to every presentation, evident identity-judgments can be produced; this belongs to the essence of the presentation as such, and so the talk of the identity of the presented object with respect to multiple presentations can always be carried through in the sense of believed, possibly true and evident, identity.

How does it stand with the turning toward meanings? Are meanings also objects? Not in this sense, and one cannot say that we are turned toward them in meaning-bestowal. To be sure, they can be grasped as objects, distinguished from others, and possibly set as identical. One can say: I think the victor of Jena, and therein I think a victor and I think Jena, etc. And this that I think is something other than what I think with the words “the vanquished of Waterloo.” But when I perform such difference-judgments, what I there objectually set as different is precisely something different — different from the earlier case, insofar as I hold fast under “object” precisely what I earlier defined. To express what we now mean, we must not assert: “the victor of Jena is not identical with the vanquished of Waterloo.” The two presentations, as straightforward components in a judgment-unity, fit truthfully only into an identity-judgment, not a difference-judgment. Meanings can indeed be objects of presentation and judgment, but only in the way other objects can — i.e., by means of presentations referred to them, hence by means of new meanings (different from themselves). The expression “meaning A” that occurs in the difference-judgment has a different meaning from the expression A that occurs in the identity-judgment.

### § 10. Closer Elaboration of the Difficulty of How the Relation of Meaning and Object Simpliciter Is to Be Understood

Husserl recapitulates the difficulty: one and the same expression, taken in the same sense, is supposed to refer to different objectualities, of all of which it is said that it means, presents them. He distinguishes again the objectuality simpliciter — the identical objectuality that not only this expression but possibly countless other meaning-different expressions present (the object simpliciter, the predicated object) — from the meant objectuality as such. The identical object simpliciter is once meant in this way, once in that, but always in some way. Taking the object in and with this apprehension, in this manner of being-thought, being-meant, we have the categorial object or meaning in a distinctive sense.

How is the relation of these two objectualities — meaning and object simpliciter, categorial and predicated objectuality — to be understood? Can it be meant in the proper verbal sense that the object simpliciter takes on categorial forms? A real object like a house: if it exists, a real thing exists. If this house takes on forms and connects through forms with other actualities, must the resulting new thing also be in real actuality? Do we find in actuality not only houses simpliciter but also categorially formed houses? One will hardly want to say so. On the other hand, how can one avoid the talk of the same object presenting itself in categorially different ways? There are objects that in themselves already contain categorial formations and that can in turn be thought in various ways, so that a distinction always arises between the object itself and the categorially different ways in which it is meant.

One must not be misled by the not-illegitimate talk that thinking is what confers categorial form on objects. We must keep at arm’s length the dangerous, naive, and indeed hard-to-overcome thought that the object is what it is in itself, and that thinking can unfortunately get hold of it only through the medium of presenting — through acts of perceiving, remembering, expecting, believing, etc. — and supremely through acts of thinking in the pregnant sense. The object is thus thought, and thinking naturally has its own forms with which the relation to the objectual necessarily occurs. But we have long been clear that we cannot begin with the psychologically so plausible opposition of object on one side and thinking that contingently thinks the object on the other. In thinking itself, and only in thinking, can the separation between the object itself and the thinking of the object be enacted a priori. “Object itself” points to certain unities of identification to be performed in identifying thinking. Moreover, thinking-forms understood as forms psychologically or phanologically inhering in thinking cannot serve us for our questions here. The presented object is not a component in thinking that is then clothed by further components — the so-called forms. Nor should we take the naive view that correlating to the object-in-itself in thinking-consciousness is a little picture, the supposed presented object, with the categorial forms being a psychological filigree woven around this picture. Presenting a house, I have a consciousness. In this consciousness a house is presented and nothing else; and this presented object, which is a house, is no psychic phenomenon, no real moment of consciousness. And the categorial form “a house” is no immanent phanological or psychological form.

Meaning in the sense of categorial objectuality in its full categorial apprehension is therefore no experience and no piece of an experience, nothing phanologically demonstrable and also no abstraction therefrom — i.e., no phanological species. When we perform an assertion — whether expressing a more or less complex nominal presentation or completing a full assertion — we live in the things; we perform no reflection, we do not look at acts or act-moments. Word by word, apprehending in the given grammatical connection, we continually have something objectual before our eyes; everything has objectifying function, and the objectual stands before us in the relevant forms.

In connection with the question of how meaning as categorial objectuality — and indeed as the objectuality determining, apprehending the object simpliciter — is to be understood in this distinctive relation to the object simpliciter, there stands the phenomenological question about the manner of consciousness. Performing a meaning-bestowal, we mean the objectual. In a certain sense it is said we mean the object simpliciter, and in a certain sense we mean the meaning. Do we look in two directions at once? Do we have two things before our eyes, as when several objects in our perceptual field compete for the privilege of attention? That obviously will not work. The objects are in meaning-bestowal not something alongside the meanings. It is evident that we can only be turned toward objects by being turned toward them as so-and-so determined, so-and-so grasped in meaning. There is no meaning-bestowal without a determinate manner of meaning, and so the object stands precisely in this manner.

### § 11. The Synthesis of Identification

To achieve clarity on the main lines, Husserl proceeds from the acts. Starting from assertoric judging — say, the identifying judgment “A is the same as B, the victor of Jena is the same as the vanquished of Waterloo” — the judgment-consciousness is consciousness of something that is the same, and thereby a fixed sense of an identical objectual something, posited in the judgment and posited as identical, is determined. Through this judgment, the two presentations are brought to unity; they are components of the judgment, connected by the overarching consciousness of “is identical.” In this case it means, in a concrete and determinate sense, that in the judgment, in this conviction, what is presented in presentation A and what is presented in presentation B is posited, and posited as the same, as one identical thing, as one object.

Wherever the judgment is the same, the self-same remains: the object that is judged and presented in the underlying nominal presentations remains the same. The sameness of the judgment points not merely to judgments phanologically identical in essence, but to those that, with possibly considerable phanological content, share a certain essence — the identity of sense, that selfsameness that obtains whether we perform the judgment “the victor of Jena…” intuitively or non-intuitively, distinctly or indistinctly, only holding fast its sense and the conviction.

Wherever a presentation occurs — namely, in whatever judgment-unity it may help build the total meaning, provided it fits straightforwardly into that unity — it remains the case that it presents the same object. One judgment and the other can always be connected by an identification with evidence. “A is B” and “A is C” — we can forthwith say “A is B, and the same is C.” Object is what is identical, the identical of presentations; and object of the assertoric judgment is the identical of the judgment — that which brings the presentations occurring in the judgment to identity-unity, what through the connection and positing in this and other judgments comes and can always come to judgmental identity-positing, what maintains and preserves its identity in the synthesis of presentations. The talk of what is there identical does not point to something alongside presentation and judgment, but to the synthesis of identification into which presentations by their essence enter, i.e., enter as those specific essences. In the respective judging, nothing else is present than a certain presentation occurring therein, at best a second presentation of different content combining with it in identity-synthesis. But in addition, it belongs to the essence of such presentations that valid judgment-connections obtain in which the same presentations logically function in the same objectification — i.e., that overarching identity-syntheses have validity that ever again establish identity-unity between the relevant presentations and exclude non-identity. Identity-unity, established between presentations and established in validity — that means: the object is identical, or the presentations present the same something, the same objectual thing. And naturally this does not mean the presentations themselves are identical, since identity-unity (where it is not tautological) is unity of presentations of different content.

If the judgment is false, the identity of the object does not obtain — namely, as so determined as the judgment has set it. The judgment is conviction of identity; it obtains or does not, depending on whether the judgment is true or false. On the other hand, it belongs to the essence of every presentation as such that it can enter into certain valid identity-judgments; and in these, when they are evident, the evident consciousness of the selfsameness of the object of the presentations obtains. The identification can have its different modes depending on the character of the underlying presentations — they can be enacted in the character of positing or without positing, a difference that also transfers into the specific.

Moving from judgment to conviction-less thinking — the mere understanding of an assertion without judging — we speak of “mere presentation,” but one must not confuse presentation in the sense of those components of judgments to which objectual consciousness was just ascribed, and presentation in the sense of a whole assertion performed without belief. When we arbitrarily and without conviction compose an assertion from two nominal presentations — “Bismarck was the greatest French statesman” — the presentations here are brought to unity not in judgment but in mere presentation. The propositional thought of identity thinks the object of the one and other presentation as the same, but therefore the object is not yet the same; it would be so if the assertion were true, if a judgment of this content could be validly performed. But even though arbitrary presentations can be brought into mere presentational unity through such mere identity-thoughts, this does not disturb the firm talk of the object of each presentation. For this has its root not in arbitrary identity-thoughts but in certain true and ultimately evident judgments. Every presentation, by its essence, admits an ordering into true identity-judgments, and every thought containing a presentation A admits an ideal closed group of transformations all connected by evident identity-syntheses. The principles of these syntheses belong to the sphere of pure meaning-logic.

### § 12. Supplements

#### a) Presented Presentations Do Not Objectify

Husserl clarifies that the reference to an object as something self-identical is ascribed to a presentation insofar as it is a possible member of a valid judgment-unity in which it can develop its objectifying function and carry it through in identifications. It may occur outside a judgment as an incomplete assertion, living in understanding; but it must then be understood exactly as it functions as a judgment-member, only apart from the further connective functions. Within judgment too, one must be attentive to what therein is really objectifying — i.e., a presentation — and what is not. One must not overlook certain meaning-modifications possible with unchanged expressions, in accordance with which the presentations themselves change. “The emperor has returned to Berlin” — here “the emperor” functions as a presentation, and what is presented is an object, something identical. But “the presentation ‘the emperor’ is a nominal presentation” — here the same expression “the emperor” occurs, but it does not function in its nominal meaning; no presentation of the emperor corresponds to it. Rather, it contains a proper presentation of a presentation; the expression “the emperor” is now a proper name for the presentation “the emperor,” which now does not function as a presentation in the judgment-connection and does not objectify.

One might say that in the total act in which we presentingly relate to the presentation and look directly at it, the presentation is really present, and like every presentation it refers to something objectual — the emperor. But this would be to lose sight of the essential. The presentation of the presentation that functions in the judgment alone exercises truly presenting function. When a presentation enters into being-presented, it has undergone a modification, and at most alongside the presentation of the presentation there may be another actual presentation. Indirect presentations — presentations that refer to objects through the medium of other presentations — are carefully to be separated from presentation-presentations. The latter direct themselves solely at certain presentations and not at all at their objects.

#### b) Position and Function of Nominal Presentations in the Propositional Whole

The various functional turns that a nominal presentation can undergo while retaining its own essence in the encompassing thought-connection are to be noted. A nominal presentation can function as a full subject-presentation; it can also present a reference-point on the predicate-side; it can occur within an attributive presentation, etc. Linguistically, Husserl reminds us of the different case-forms of the noun. When a presentation passes from one position and function to another, a change has entered that has its objectifying significance in the broader connection.

More generally, just as it belongs to the essence of a whole that its parts as such have their connective function, so here. When a whole of a certain kind admits composition from parts of the same sort, the exchange of such parts in the whole yields again a whole of this kind. But the same part, the same in content, has in the new whole a different position and function, and precisely thereby the whole itself is altered within its general kind. Assertions, judgments, and mere thoughts can contain several nominal presentations. An exchange of these in the propositional whole yields a new proposition. The same name at a new position achieves something new in thought. In itself the nominal presentation is the same, and it means the same, but in the whole it has a new function; and since the whole is a meaning-whole, a presenting whole, we must speak of a new presentational function. The functional form fuses with the proper content, for the name must always function in some way, and interwoven with its proper presentational content it has a functional characteristic that points to the encompassing presentational whole. We thus have everywhere a distinction between content and form. The latter expresses itself more or less distinctly in the grammatical form of the expression; this points outward to the more encompassing presentational connection and the presentational function to be achieved therein. We recognize throughout the identical content, in which in a certain way the same is always presented. We ascribe to the nominal presentation, independently of its positional function, its grammatical form, reference to the same objectual thing, and naturally this rests on identifying judgments and truths.

## Chapter 4. The Constitution of Objects-About

### § 13. State of the Investigation

Husserl reviews the ground covered. The aim was to make clear what it means for a presentation to refer to a presented objectuality — specifically, presentations in the sense of meaning-bestowing acts, i.e., acts by virtue of which we refer in asserting to what is asserted, in naming to what is named. This reference is supposed to hold whether the asserting is a genuine one (i.e., a judging one, carried by conviction) or not, and likewise for possible or actual assertion-parts. A name names the same, and a nominal presentation presents the same, whether performed in belief or not. It names the same regardless of the intuitiveness or non-intuitiveness of the presentations and regardless of whether, if it is a belief-consciousness, it can demonstrate its right in evidence or not. Accordingly, it is not now in question whether the named or otherwise asserted objectuality in truth is or is not. Every asserting presenting — every verbal one — refers to something objectual even if it were established that no such thing exists or obtains.

### § 14. Fixing of Terminology

Husserl fixes the terminology. By assertoric judging or predicating he understands the self-enclosed assertion-consciousness that something is or is not, is so-and-so constituted or not — excluding the acts in which word-sound consciousness, indication-consciousness, or intimation in the broader sense is enacted. It concerns purely the conviction of such-and-such being or non-being that comes to expression with the words. Opposed to actual predicating is the exactly corresponding thinking-without-believing — “predicative thinking” (mere presentation as opposed to judgment). He also introduces broader terms: “propositional acts” that are either being-positing or non-positing; “predicative acts” encompassing all acts belonging or capable of belonging to predication, whether full propositional acts or immediate partial acts; “presentations” for the relevant acts insofar as the reference to objectuality is being considered. Predicative acts that are not full propositional acts can themselves be positing or non-positing — so nominal acts are either nominal positings or nominal “mere” presentations, a nominal thinking. All predicative acts are categorial, but categorial acts can also be non-predicative. Relational apprehending and recognizing on the basis of mere perception, without words and word-meaning, encompasses certain categorial act-forms that are not predicative.

### § 15. Identity-Predication Brings the Sense of Objectual Reference to Its First Expression

With this terminology, the account can proceed more simply. Husserl focuses on nominal presentations as predicative acts — those that, connected with nominal expressions, function as immediate parts of the predicative act. To understand what it means for a nominal presentation to refer to an objectuality, we are referred to the function of this presentation in predicative connections. This path seems contrived at first. It seems natural to say that the reference to the object is primarily provided by the intuitions interwoven with the words. And even if it is recognized that interlacing with intuitions does not necessarily belong to predicative objectual consciousness — that there are also intuition-less predicative acts — one might seek the objectual reference in the possible adaptation to intuition. But the reference to absurd presentations, to which a corresponding intuition is necessarily denied, suffices to show that the objectual reference ascribed to every presentation, even every absurd one, cannot be clarified by recourse to visualization. Moreover, closer investigation shows that the difficulties besetting us in the predicative sphere return mutatis mutandis in the intuitive sphere as well.

Returning to the predicative sphere: for nominal presentations, in every predicative context they present something — one and the same. To be sure, we can perform such presentations *kata symplokēn* (outside a predicative connection), but then the presentation points through an interwoven functional thought back to some predicative connection, and in any case draws its originarily presenting property only from its predicative function. It is solely on account of what it is able to demonstrate there, on the basis of its specific essence, that objectual reference is ascribed to it also in isolation. The predicative basic form that brings the sense of objectual reference to its first expression is the form of identity-predication: “A is the same (the same object) as B.” Two presentations are united to a full propositional act that means and says: “A is the same as B.”

### § 16. The Question of the Truth of the Predicative Identification-Thoughts

Husserl deepens the analysis. One might think: what good is the possibility — indeed grounded in the essence of nominal presentations — of inserting them pairwise and quite arbitrarily into the form of predicative identification-thoughts, if in general no truth corresponds to such a thought? What if there were a nominal presentation insertable into no valid propositional identity-act? Could one still say this presentation presents something, has a presented, named object? Every nominal presentation can be made into a subject-presentation in any predicative thought and thus linked with any predicate. But perhaps no single such predicative thought corresponds to a correct judgment. Would the presentation then still present something in all these contexts?

Husserl makes clear that the assertion that a nominal presentation presents a so-and-so determined object necessarily has its validity if we fill it with appropriate content, and that when we analyze the sense of such an assertion, we arrive at identifications and other predications in which the presentation enters as a member — predications that, analogously to those discussed earlier, furnish the origin for the reflective speech in which we make presentations into objects of assertion and assert of them reference to these and those objectualities. For example: what does it mean that the presentation “centaur” presents an object — namely, an animate being, something between human and horse? This assertion about the presentation “centaur” refers us back to assertions of the type: “a centaur is (by its essence) something between human and horse.” Such judgments belong to every presentation, and they can be performed with evidence, they have their evident correctness. The judgment about centaurs does not posit any actuality of centaurs but only lays out the essence of the centaur in clarification. But it is a judgment, and a true one. There are thus true judgments into which every arbitrary presentation can be fitted, and each must thereby become the continuous member of various connecting identifications in which the identity of the object maintains itself as essential identity and the object itself lays out its essence. Certain identifications and attributions thus belong, in true judgments, to the essence of every presentation.

### § 17. The Determination of Presenting Acts through Their Capacity to Enter into Identifying Synthesis

Husserl summarizes: meaning-bestowing acts are predicative acts — acts that are either full predications or can be inserted as immediate and proper components into predications. Only in their predicative function do they demonstrate their objectual reference. All acts we call “presentations” are analogous. The proper objectifying functions are those in which something objectual is conscious as self-identical. We are thus led back to the acts of unity-consciousness and of the articulating identity-consciousness intimately connected with it. All acts that can be distinguished as members or phases in these — the acts that found unity-consciousness or enter as members into an identifying synthesis — are called presenting acts on account of this capacity: on account of their capacity to fuse or connect in selfsameness-consciousness with their likes and so to make something self-same conscious. Continuous perceptual consciousness is a unity-consciousness; the perceptual phases are presentations; because they can be so united, by their essence, they present something identical.

Within predication, the predicative basic function is a unity-function — namely, identification. Predication is at bottom nothing but identification and what is essentially referred back to identification and in any case transformable back into identification in unity-consciousness. Sometimes there are simple and straightforward identifications, sometimes distinctions (which by their essence and sense refer back to identification). Sometimes identifications are independent, sometimes under assumption. And wherever a unified predication is given, it can itself be transformed into an identification, and this transformation means a new act that unites with the original in a unity-consciousness.

### § 18. The Nominal Acts as the Properly Object-Giving Acts. The Possibility of Nominalization

Within predication, nominal acts have a distinguished position. They are presenting in a pregnant sense. They constitute in predicative consciousness the objects-about — above all as subject-acts, in which objects stand there as things “about which something is asserted.” But even when nominal acts are not at the subject-position, they exercise the pregnant presenting function we designate by the word “object-about.” Adjective predicates are different: saying of a tree that it is green, the green is not objectual in the same way as the tree, though we also say the adjectival predicate presents something — namely, the color green. It is evidently two different things: to present green as in “the tree is green” and to present it as in “the color green.” But one connects with the other. We can assert: “the tree is green — this color green” or “this green is the same as that green of such-and-such a shrub.” To the essence of the adjectival presentation belongs the possibility of nominalization, through which “the same green” constitutes itself as an object-about.

Husserl further notes that of the predicate “green” it is said both that it presents green and that it refers to the tree. The unity of predication relates the green-being to the subject tree, and so one can say that the green presents something belonging to the tree. In the predication, the green-presentation “covers” (*deckt sich*) the tree-presentation — it presents something the tree has, its property; the determination covers the determined, brought to synthesis in unity-consciousness. But this is not identification in the sense where two nominal presentations connect in consciousness as “this and that is one and the same.”

He adds that not only partial acts of predications but whole predications count as presentations. To a whole proposition we ascribe an objectuality distinguished from the objects that are objectual in it by virtue of its partial presentations. We are again referred to the possibility of nominalization that transforms the full predication in selfsameness-consciousness into a nominal presentation that can in turn function as a subject-presentation in new assertions. E.g., “S is P — this is a very pleasant fact.” “This” — that is, “this, that S is P.” Even in the simple hypothetical judgment, presupposition and consequence were thought, but not nominally.

### § 19. The Nominal Presentation Is a Basic Function within the Full Assertion

From these considerations it follows that nominal acts occupy a privileged position. They are the properly presenting acts. Though they are properly presenting only by virtue of their predicative performance — they make something into an object-about within predication, they constitute the substrates for all determinations and identifications — this requires predication. Not everything within predications is a nominal act. And yet we also speak of presenting and its objectuality outside the nominal — even for whole predications, which are not nominal. But this happens because we can reach into any predicative partial act and, as it were, look into it and extract from it something objectual — namely, through nominalization.

Something is really objectual, objectual in the proper sense — that means: we have a certain nominal consciousness. When we say of a consciousness that something is objectual in it although it is not a nominal consciousness, this implies that this consciousness by its essence allows a nominalization that lets us look out from it at what it does not itself look at, and lets us extract and present an object that is implied in it but not in the proper sense presented. All objectifying occurs in unity- and identity-consciousness, but therein in the proper sense “object,” “counter-standing,” explicitly, is only what is thought in nominal presentations. Every extracting or making-objectual where no nominal presentation is present requires new levels of predications with new nominal presentations referred back to and made possible by the lower levels. No thinking that would not be thinking about something; hence no asserting thinking without nominal presentations through which the assertion is assertion about this and that. On the other hand, no thinking is merely nominal presentation. Nominal presentation designates a basic function within the full assertion.

A special difficulty lies in the fact that the nominal presentation itself allows a double direction of gaze: the one in which its object constitutes itself, and the one in which its object is taken precisely as the so-and-so thought, so-and-so determined and not otherwise determined, and is distinguished from the same but otherwise determined. Phanologically speaking: at one time we assert and, living in the assertion-consciousness, identify “the victor of Jena” with “the vanquished of Waterloo”; at another time we say that the nominally so-and-so determined object is indeed the same on both sides, but the meaning and the meant object as such are different.

### § 20. Recapitulation of the Preceding Considerations

Husserl recapitulates. The preceding lectures dealt with the reference to objects ascribed to predicative acts. We were referred back to the unity of cognition, to unity-connections of judging and ultimately of correct judging. The privileged status of nominal presentations as the primarily presenting, object-opposing functions emerged. In the proper sense objectual, actually objectual, is the objectual of nominal presentations. Objectual is the identical to which nominal presentations in the context of judging
refer, of which as the same this or that predicates are asserted, or which as the same is asserted in the manner of total identification. Object is what determines itself ever anew as the same in the context of cognition, enters into new selfsameness-relations, takes on now these, now those relative attributes, and so stands forth continually as an identical unity-point for the mutually intertwining, sometimes conditioned and sometimes unconditioned predications.

It is thus clear: to speak of an object means to perform a nominal presentation and to live in it in the direction of gaze that makes it a genuine predicative act or at least qualifies it to be one, so that the predicative thought-connection can then unfold — laying out or ever newly and further determining what constitutes itself as the identical of this thought-connection with this nominal presentation. Nominal presentations admit various turns, and one must note that these turns signify essential modifications through which the nominal presentation ceases to be and achieve what it is and achieves in its originary state — what it is and achieves as an actual predicative act. For example, “the emperor” is a genuine predicative act where it directly and itself is a member of a predication (“the emperor made an outing”). But “the presentation ‘the emperor’” or “the concept ‘the emperor’” — here the same expression occurs but the nominal presentation “the emperor” does not predicatively function. In judgments about presentations, the judged presentations do not themselves function as presentations in the predicative sense.

Where a nominal presentation genuinely predicatively functions, it lays the ground for a selfsameness-consciousness that unfolds as such in the connection of attributions and identifications and their interweavings through ever new acts of unity-consciousness. With the nominal act, a self-same is established — an object — not as something alongside the determinations allotted to it in predication, but rather the self-same is precisely the same so-and-so-determined, standing in such-and-such relations, etc.

### § 21. True and Merely Presented Objectual Reference. Judging under Assumption

The objectual reference ascribed to nominal acts and then further to predicative acts in general is now either true or merely supposed objectual reference. We say distinguishingly that the presented object is in truth, or that it is merely presented but in truth is not; or that the object is presented but it is not yet decided whether it is in truth or not.

This distinction leads us back to certain differences in the judgment-unity in which the nominal presentations function. If the judgment-unity is a unity of correct judgments, the nominal presentations can be performed in validity-consciousness, as positing. The presented is then not merely presented — it stands as being, it counts; and under the presupposition of correct judgments, it is in truth, truly being. The nominal presentation can even be expressly connected with the existential predication: “an A exists.” On the other hand, a nominal presentation can occur in the connection of judging and have predicative function without this validity-consciousness. To this belongs all judging under assumption (*Assumtion*). One judges under the assumption of A, possibly expressly under the formulated existential assumption “suppose there were A.” In the hypothetical judgment, one judges: “if A were B” — under the assumption that A is B. In the nominalization we grasp the state of affairs, but as assumed, not as counting or posited in validity-consciousness.

In the unity of such cognition we naturally find the persistent identifications for the sake of which it is said that a self-same is presented and the presenting refers to such-and-such objects, states of affairs. But this self-same or objectual that constitutes itself does not stand in the mode of being — the identity is identity not simpliciter but only under assumption. We therefore speak, with reference to the fact that the consciousness has the character of selfsameness-consciousness, of the presentation presenting something objectual; but with reference to the fact that it has the mode of mere assumption or of validity under assumption, we say the presenting merely presents something objectual — namely, that of its own accord it does not confer the value of being upon it.

Nevertheless, if not every presentation refers to a true object, it remains that of every presentation one can unconditionally say it “presents an object” — insofar as it belongs to the essence of every presentation to be capable of being assumptively taken and then functioning in truth as a member of hypothetical judgments (and related assumptive judgments), so that truthful assertions arise about what truly pertains to the “object” of the presentation — not as posited in being, but as assumed.

If we ask what the correctness of presentations or judgments means and how it demonstrates itself, we are referred back from the further predicative sphere to the intuitions that give predicative acts evidence; and within the intuitive acts we notice that the same distinctions mutatis mutandis recur. Intuitions can be complete or incomplete, and only completeness of intuition yields true evidence for truth and possibility. The intuition is either of the character of “perception” — not only seeing but positing the object, being-giving — or of the character of modified intuition — not being-giving but merely presenting being in intuitive fashion (representing). In the latter case it yields the possibility of the object, the objectual essence.

Now it is time to approach the question of the predicative (more generally, categorial) objects and the question of meanings in the phenomenological sense, distinguished from the objects-about. Every nominal presentation not only presents the object simpliciter but also has a “meaning” — it presents an object by presenting it in this or that way, e.g., as so-and-so determined, standing in such-and-such relations to other objects. One can take the object in and with its manner of being-determined, as so-and-so conceived and only as such: “the presented object as such,” as so-and-so presented. But all this is still unclear, and essential distinctions must now be made.

## Chapter 5. The Categorial Objects

### § 22. Simple — Categorial — Verbal Presenting. Categorial Form and Categorial Matter

Husserl begins with the problem of categorial objects. Every nominal presentation not only presentingly names an object — the object-about — but also has a meaning in various senses. An object of which we speak, which is thought in our thinking, is naturally always thought in some way. Every presentation entering into the unity of cognition has its categorial form. The object may initially be presented in a simple presentation, in a continuous unity-consciousness, prior to all category. This simple presentation may be empty or intuitive. Only when the presentation takes on categorial forms does thinking in the pregnant sense occur. Simple perceptions flowing into one another in unity-consciousness pass into discursive intuition: a this-consciousness makes the appearing into an object-about, and parts or marks are apprehended — “this has such-and-such a property” etc. All this before verbal expressions. The discursive or categorial thinking brings its wealth of forms, a certain articulation; each categorial partial act has its determinate position and function and in that its determinate form.

Verbal thinking mirrors all these articulations and forms in the word-presentations. Just as simple presenting can be full or empty, intuitive or non-intuitive, so too can categorial presenting, and in its way also verbal presenting. The latter as merely symbolic carries, in proper connection with its word-constituting acts, empty categorial acts. When symbolic thinking “takes on” intuitive fullness, we have intuitive thinking with verbal expression.

Husserl notes the need to distinguish between categorial form and categorial matter. To the essence of thinking as *katēgoria* in the widest sense belong certain basic forms of thinking-apprehension — the commonly so-called logical forms. These forms are abstractively distinguished; they have a changing content. Predicative acts of the same form differ by their content, which is itself something abstract. Presentations like “something that is a” and “something that is b” have the same form but different content. This distinction of form and matter in the categorial pregnant sense must be distinguished from that already offered by simple intuition.

### § 23. The Distinction of Object-About and Categorial Object. Nominalization and Categorial Reflection

The question is what the categorial object is supposed to be in distinction from the object-about. How is a double objectuality supposed to be objectual in the categorial act? The named object, the object-about, is the identical to which the manifold determinations refer in the determining judgment-connection in which the nominal presentation predicatively functions. In thinking, judging, asserting about things, they stand as the bearers of determinations, as the identical. But in thinking, with a different direction of gaze, we find something further. “The mill by the brook” — there is a this that is grasped as a mill, and the mill is the one by the brook. We can see this when we enter into perceptual connections in which this object first presents itself simply and unitarily, and then upon the connections of discursive and conceiving acts in which it demonstrates itself as the subject of manifold determinations.

The categorial reflection is not a phanological reflection (which would make the acts themselves into objects) but a “looking into” the act-formation — into the nominal presentation — without making it into an object. We find therein a givenness: not the object simpliciter (which is the object before this reflection, in judgment-consciousness, when we naively live in determining), but rather the “so-and-so thought object as such.” Taking a whole judgment — “this is a” — we perform a certain reflection: instead of the “this” that in the judgment is object in the proper sense, we make objectual “this is a.” Not the judgment do we make objectual, but the state of affairs, and precisely as what is there asserted. The total categorial object, the state of affairs extractable from the respective judgment, contains as a component the categorial object extractable from the nominal presentation. Husserl designates these objects as *Kategorialien* and speaks of the *Propositionale* as the categorial belonging to the full judgment.

### § 24. Categorial Reflection Is a Giving Act When the Underlying Judging Is Evident

If judging occurs “on the basis of intuition” — originarily, in the simplest cases on the basis of perception — and every categorial act-form is genuinely enacted and not replaced by empty pieces of judgment-intention, then it belongs to the essence of this intuitive, “evident” judging that it admits “categorial reflection,” and that the categorial is “given.” That reflecting seeing is a seeing, a giving and at the same time objectifying act, when the underlying judging is carried out in a perceptual and generally “insightful” way. The givenness can be more or less adequate. In any case, categorial consciousness can build itself upon intuition so that not only the underlying simple acts but the entire consciousness has the character of a giving, “perceiving” consciousness. Where categorial reflection then takes hold of the categorial object given therein, the given becomes a “this” — a given object in the pregnant sense.

The categorial consciousness that builds itself upon intuition need not be a perceiving consciousness that gives the categorial objectuality. It can have the character of a re-presentifying consciousness; generally there are various modifications within intuition. Within the judgment-sphere, within the sphere of categorial being-consciousness, the propositionale is thus something objectual extractable from the judgment — in the truest and strictest sense extractable when the judgment is evident. In that case the propositionale is actual, and so too every nominale and other categoriale belonging to it.

### § 25. Review of the Previous Course of Considerations

Husserl reviews the entire course: from the outset, various concepts of meaning emerged. The first superficial consideration drew attention to the objectual reference of predicative acts. A nominal presentation refers to what is named, an adjectival presentation in the predicate to a property and in a way to the subject-object. The whole assertion refers to the subject and to a state of affairs. Then in a new way, the nominal presentation is said to have a meaning through which it refers to its named object. Meaning in distinction from objectuality could be phanologically understood — every predicative act has a certain predicative essence, something specific phanologically immanent in it. These were phanological meaning-concepts. But from the outset it was clear that phanological meaning-concepts could not be the whole story. Categorial objectualities emerged, distinguished from the object-about and possibly also from the asserted “situation of affairs” or “state of affairs,” designating a phenomenological (ontic) meaning-concept. The first series of considerations concerned the constitution of objects-about through nominal acts and nominalization. The second turned to the proper meaning-concepts, the difficult phenomenological (ontic) ones.

### § 26. The Correlation of Linguistic Expression, Phanological Essences, and Kategorialien

Husserl determined the Kategorialien as the objectual that is implicitly conscious in a full *katēgoria* and becomes an object-about through a new direction of gaze — “categorial reflection” — and a nominalization enacted therein. In the predication, in the act of verbally enacted assertion, the “what” of the assertion, the propositionale with all its articulations and forms, can be designated only by the entire expression of the assertion itself. In the actual asserting, this is “conscious” — it is what we mean. But a special direction of gaze and nominalization is required to make it an object in the proper sense. According to the linguistic expression — at least approximately, insofar as it faithfully mirrors the thought — the total meaning articulates into partial meanings. Differences of nominal meanings, adjectival meanings, predicate meanings, copula, etc. are then differences of the Kategorialien. To the total expression and its parts there also corresponds an articulation in the predicating act. And to these correspond the phanological essences, the articulations and formations of meanings in the phanological sense. But now it concerns the phenomenological (ontic): the Kategorialien are exact counterparts of the act-essences, of meanings in the phanological (phansic) sense. In this correlation, however, an essential difference is to be noted.

### § 27. The Essential Difference of the Correlative Meaning-Concepts

#### a) Every Assertion Has a Meaning in the Phanological (Phansic) Sense

Meaning, we customarily say, belongs to every assertion and every genuine assertion-part; and likewise each predicative act has its meaning. This holds of meaning in the phanological (phansic) sense. Asserting “gold is yellow,” we find — whether we judge symbolically or intuitively, and whatever the degree of intuitiveness — in reflection upon all these acts a common essence. We always say the same judgment; the judgment is a specific (phansic) unity over against the manifold of contentually different judgment-acts. All these acts have, as reflection and ideation teach, an immanent common essence, a (phansic) meaning-matter. The same holds for the corresponding judgment-modifications (acts of assertion-understanding without belief). When we compare judgments and mere judgment-thoughts — always of the content “gold is yellow” — we find in the comparison something evidently common, and this above all we will call the meaning. It is this “content,” but as something extracted from the acts through ideating abstraction. Every assertion, whether judged or merely understood, has a meaning; this is in this sense as self-evident as “every thing has a spatial shape.”

#### b) Not to Every Assertion Does There Belong a Kategoriale, Insofar as Truth in the Ontic Sense and Categorial Objectuality Are the Same

What about the correlative meaning-concept? Does a propositionale belong to every assertion, a kategoriale to every predicative act? The answer is evidently: No. The propositionale is given to us in the evidence of the judgment, or in the categorial reflection performed on the basis of judgment-evidence. We customarily say that truth is given to us in the evident judgment, that the givenness-consciousness of truth is evidence. If we speak thus, then obviously truth (in the ontic sense) and categorial objectuality are one and the same. The concept of the categorial hits upon the one, objectual concept of truth. Alongside this are act-side truth-concepts: a true judgment, truth as a predicate of judgments, etc. Equally legitimate is the phenomenological (ontic), objectual-side concept: then truth is the kategoriale given to us in the full evident judgment. Not every judgment can be made evident, grounded. Hence meaning in this sense does not belong to every assertion — while in the phanological sense it always has meaning.

On the other hand, in an improper sense every assertion can be ascribed reference to corresponding Kategorialien and thus meaning in this sense too. Just as we say every presentation refers to its presented object, but not every presentation corresponds in reality to an object.

### § 28. The Possibility of the Assumptive Turn Lying in the Essence of Every Categorial Act

Every categorial act admits the assumptive turn and grounds a modified judgment-manner that has its evidence-possibility and that, apart from the assumptive turn, fully occupies the forms of judgments that confer being-value upon the Kategorialien. To the essence of every presentation and every objectifying act belongs the possibility, while preserving its phansic meaning, of being assumptively turned and then functioning in assumptive judging. Even if we judge “an A is not,” we can nonetheless judge with evidence: “an A — therein lies something that is so-and-so determined.” “A tree — therein lies: a thing from the genus of organic beings, of plants, etc.” The judgment-manner is essentially altered: we do not posit the tree in the mode of being; we perform another mode. We posit “a tree” in an “assumptive” manner. In this way, every presentation has an object and every expression a meaning in the sense of a corresponding kategoriale.

### § 29. The Assumptive Kategoriale, the “Being-Meant” as Such, Is to Be Ascribed to Every Predicative Act

Husserl addresses the potential objection that under assumption one could relate any presentation to any arbitrary object. But every presentation presents a determinate objectuality belonging to it and no other. This means that to its essence, and more specifically to its meaning-related essence, a certain assumptive turning belongs. Every predicative act has its meaning-essence. In the “idea posited” mode, a lion is thus-and-so, and what that is is determined by the assumptive essence-analysis. Through the phanological meaning — through concept and proposition — what is to be judged is determined and not at all arbitrary.

The concept of meaning now shifts somewhat. Under meaning can be understood: first, the kategoriale itself; second, the meant kategoriale (which is really the earlier concept). The former: to certain predicative acts (those that can be made evident) there correspond givennesses, the Kategorialien — only true assertions have meaning in this sense. The latter: to the essence of every predicative act belongs its predicative essence, a phanological meaning-essence; and to this belongs the possibility of “same-sense” categorial reflections, assumptions, and assumptively performed explicating judgments — in which the relevant predicative acts themselves do not gain evidence, but in which the relevant same-sense assumptive modifications of the same gain evidence. Popularly speaking: meaning as meant, to-be-assumed, to-be-assumed categorial object is something belonging to every predicative act. Meaning in the sense of actual categorial object is something belonging only to certain predicative acts and is identical with the truth of the act or its meaning-related essence.

### § 30. Supplements

#### a) The Correlative Relation of Nominale and Object-About

Husserl remarks on the correlation between the two kinds of objectuality — Kategorialien and objects-about. However an object may constitute itself extra-categorially, as object-about it is only such within a categorial consciousness as nominally presented and in judgment-consciousness as nominally posited. The being of the object-about coincides in a certain way with the obtaining of the Kategorialien concerning it. Where a propositionale is given, eo ipso the object-about is given through the relevant nominale. The object can indeed appear perceptually, but then it is not an object-about — not this so-and-so determined object. As a thinking-object it is given only in the respective evidence-consciousness.

Husserl further notes that if a propositionale obtains — if truth corresponds to a proposition — this does not mean that all nominal presentations occurring in the proposition automatically have objects corresponding to them. There are modifications by which the presented object is not an object-about from the standpoint of the whole judgment — e.g., existential negation (“an A does not exist”) or hypothetical judgments. Only genuine partial positings of the judgment have objects-about attributed to them.

#### b) The Relation of Propositionale and State of Affairs / Situation of Affairs

Husserl considers whether Propositionalien relate to states of affairs and situations of affairs analogously to how Nominalien relate to objects-about. Two Nominalien refer to the same object when they are terms in an identity-truth. Similarly, two Propositionalien might be “equivalent” — themselves different but standing in the same relation to an identical objectual something, the same state of affairs.

Several cases are examined. (1) Multiple Propositionalien differing only by exchange of objectually equivalent Nominalien: “The emperor comes to Göttingen” and “Wilhelm II comes to Göttingen.” The same predicate is asserted of the same subject — the “state of affairs” is the same. (2) Cases of a priori equivalence like a > b and b < a, where the relation is reversed. Here the states of affairs are properly different but the underlying situation of affairs (*Sachlage*) is the same.

Husserl explores whether equivalent Propositionalien relate to states of affairs in the same way Nominalien relate to objects-about, and finds the situation more complex. In the sciences, the same “fact” or situation of affairs can be “formulated” very differently; the physicist who recognizes the equivalence says “the law is the same.” But strictly speaking, in the case of mere reversal of relations, the various truths are merely equivalent — not identical as categorial objectualities. One cannot straightforwardly say equivalent propositions relate to the same situation of affairs in the same manner as equivalent nominal presentations relate to objects.

## Chapter 6. The Determination of Mere Meaning (in Both the Phansic and the Ontic Sense)

### § 31. Categorial Object and Categorial Essence

The categorial object is given in “categorial perception” — in the evident judgment or the essentially equivalent nominal turn of the evident judgment. From the categorial object we distinguish the essence of the categorial object — the categorial essence. Only if the categorial object is from the outset an essence, a categorial essentia, does
it not to be distinguished between categorial object and its essence: the essence of an essence is itself identical with the essence.

For an individual object, it can be thought in various ways, this or that can be predicated of it, and propositional, nominal, and other categorial objects can constitute themselves. These are given in categorial perception, in the evident judgment. But opposed to categorial perception as a possibility stands categorial intuition that is not perception — just as opposed to simple sensory perceiving stands simple sensory phantasizing. It is thereby indifferent whether belief is connected with phantasizing or not. The objectual appears, is intuitively present, but only quasi-present. Exactly so at the level of discursive, intuition-based thinking: evidence gives the object as so-and-so determined, gives the categorial objects. Parallel to evidence is quasi-evidence — categorial intuition that is not really giving but quasi-giving in phantasy fashion.

Now every intuition is in a certain improper sense a giving act; from every intuition something objectual is to be extracted — from the perceiving and the merely phantasizing intuition one and the same, namely the intuitive essence. So from evidence and quasi-evidence alike an essence is to be extracted: the essence of the propositionale and every categorial object. This means a new gaze — a new seeing act — founds itself upon the one and other intuition, and we call this act ideation (here categorial ideation) or essence-seeing. In it we grasp something identical as a new objectual something, whether we perceive or phantasize.

### § 32. Meaning as Propositional Essence or Possibility

Every object has its essence. But the object is not the essence. The propositional essence or possibility yields yet another sense of meaning. The assertion “a quadrangle is round” has no meaning, one often hears said. This can mean that no truth corresponds to it, but it can also say that no possibility or propositional essence corresponds to it. If we could in phantasy present a propositionale of this essence, such an essence would be demonstrated and truly given. To pursue “meanings,” to see whether assertions mean something and what is enclosed in these meanings — that would mean to investigate the possibilities, to make the essences evident in intuition, and then further to judge intuitively about essences and to lay out what belongs to them as such.

While to every actuality-judgment a corresponding phantasy-judgment and essence-judgment can be assigned as a possibility belonging to the essence of the judgment, the converse does not hold. Not every phantasy has a possible perception corresponding to it. The essence is also called the ideal possibility. What is ideally possible is not also actual. Not every essentia has an existentia, but conversely every existentia has an essentia.

### § 33. Degrees of Perfection of Essence-Givenness

Husserl asks whether we have now reached the widest meaning-concept with the concept of nominal and categorial essence. Every intuitive thinking has its meaning accordingly, and symbolic-verbal thinking has meaning insofar as it covers intuitive thinking and brings it to “expression.” But what if it does not cover in this way? Even within the intuitive sphere, differences of more or less perfect intuitivity emerge. The propositional essence is given in propositional evidence, but the evidence may reach so far as to concern the categorial apprehensions and formations while perhaps not fully extending to the sensory foundations. The incompleteness lies in the admixture of non-intuitive components into the intuition. Essence-givenness can be more or less perfect. An essence-judgment can have its evidence, but the evidence can be more or less perfect. The same holds for verbal judgments, where symbolic acts interwoven with word-constituting acts are sometimes more, sometimes less saturated with intuition, and often entirely empty.

### § 34. Covering-Relations

#### a) First Sketch of These Relations

Husserl speaks of “covering” (*Deckung*). In the sphere of appearances, it emerges: (1) different acts can have the same appearance-content — a perception and a hallucination share the “perceptual appearance”; (2) covering-relations between appearances themselves — between impressional and phantasy-appearance, between the acts by virtue of their appearances; (3) the covering of appearances belonging to an appearance-connection — the covering of appearance-phases in the appearance-continuity.

#### b) Exposition of the Covering-Relations in the Lower and Higher Strata

For the lower stratum (simple appearances): various acts can share the same appearance-content; impressional and phantasy appearances cover one another; appearances in a perceptual context cover one another. For the higher stratum (categorial): judgment and corresponding “mere presentation” can be compared in covering; categorial phantasy-appearance and categorial impressional appearance cover one another, yielding the common essence — the essence of the “appearance” S is P; different categorial appearances referring to the same synthetic unity of simple appearances cover one another.

#### c) “Coverings” between Symbolic Categorial Acts. Obtaining the Mere Meaning in the Phansic and Ontic Sense

Husserl considers the coverings between symbolic and intuitive categorial acts or symbolic alone. The intuitive essence of all categorial appearances of the same categorial essence — the phanological intuitive meaning — has something in common with the empty essence, and this identical is the phanological meaning in the widest sense or the “mere” phanological meaning. Correlatively, from all acts of the same phanological meaning in the widest sense, the empty categorial essence is to be extracted — the mere phenomenological meaning or proposition in the phenomenological, objectively logical sense, and meaning in the ordinary sense.

### § 35. The Mere Ontic Meaning, the Proposition in the Objectively Logical Sense, Is the Empty Form of the Categorial

Husserl has arrived at a final meaning-concept: the widest phenomenological (ontic) meaning-concept, standing opposite the widest phanological (phansic) one. The first would coincide with what the *Logical Investigations* called the matter of the objectifying categorial acts as opposed to quality; the latter stood as its correlate. Taking an assertion “S is P”: it can be actually asserted in belief, or conjectured, or merely understood. The intuitiveness can vary greatly. But through all these modifications something common is present. We know it leads to deep and difficult problems to determine clearly what this commonality means, but through all the modifications something passes through that gives us a right to speak of commonality.

On the other hand, through phenomenological ideation (not phanological) something identical can be extracted from all these acts. Every judgment has its “content” — it judges something, namely S is P. This judgment-content is not the phanological meaning (the gaze is not directed at the judgment and its real components) but belongs to the sphere of intentional objectualities. Nor is the judgment-content identical with the categorial object (which does not exist if the judgment is false), nor with the categorial essence (which is only present when the judgment is intuitive or can authenticate itself through subsequent intuition as “possible”). Every judgment — false, absurd, lacking truth or possibility — has a content: every judgment has its meaning. This meaning we call the proposition (*Satz*). Every judging is holding-for-true; what is held for true is the proposition. The proposition is, as it were, the empty form of the categorial: the proposition-meaning comes to givenness on the basis of any categorial act of corresponding phanological meaning. When the proposition “has truth,” this means: to the empty proposition there corresponds a full proposition, a genuine kategoriale.

## Chapter 7. The Essential Distinctions of Judgments

The investigations conducted so far to separate and understand from their phenomenological origin a series of very important objectualities belonging to the categorial domain stand in close relation to very important distinctions of judgments: the distinction between existence-judgments, essence-judgments, and meaning-judgments. Of special interest is the clarification of the concepts of a priori and analytic judgment as opposed to a posteriori judgments.

### § 36. Existence-Judgments and Essence-Judgments

#### a) First Characterization of the Distinction

Existence-judgments (*Daseinsurteile*) — also called existential judgments in a distinguished sense, or factual judgments — are judgments that establish facts, that establish a being-there (*Dasein*). Their sphere is the sphere of experience, of empirical cognition. In them, the judging is a positing of individual being. The subject-objects are individual objects or complexes thereof; the states of affairs are individual states of affairs. The judgments concern what factually is the case — what exists in the spatiotemporal world or at least in some individual domain.

Essence-judgments (*Wesensurteile*), by contrast, do not posit individual being. They concern what belongs to essences as such. The judgment “red is a color” does not assert anything about any individual red thing but about the essence red. Essence-judgments have their evidence not in experience but in ideation — in the seeing of essences. Their truth is independent of whether any individual instances exist. The distinction between existence-judgments and essence-judgments is fundamental for the theory of science and for the demarcation of the a priori from the a posteriori.

#### b) Further Elaboration. The Givenness of Existence-State-of-Affairs and Essence-State-of-Affairs

Husserl elaborates: an existence-state-of-affairs is given in experience, in the perception and the connected determining and identifying cognition of individual objects. An essence-state-of-affairs is given in ideation, in the seeing of essences and their relations. The givenness is fundamentally different in kind. In the one case, being is posited and given through perception of individuals; in the other, essences are grasped through ideation and their essential relations seen — independently of any positing of individual existence.

### § 37. Modifications within the Sphere of Essence-Consciousness

Husserl discusses modifications within the sphere of essence-consciousness. Just as in the sphere of individual consciousness there are modifications — perception, memory, expectation, phantasy, etc. — so in the sphere of essence-consciousness there are corresponding modifications. One can see essences with full evidence, or one can merely think them, or one can assumptively posit them. The essence-judgment admits the same range of qualitative and intuitional modifications as the existence-judgment.

### § 38. Spheres of A Priori Judging

#### a) Ontological and Phanological Essence-Judgments

Within essence-judgments, a distinction is to be made between ontological and phanological ones. Ontological essence-judgments concern the essences of objects — what belongs to objects as such and to objects of determinate kinds. Phanological essence-judgments concern the essences of acts — what belongs to acts of consciousness as such and to acts of determinate kinds.

#### b) Purely Grammatical and Purely Logical Judgments

A further distinction within the a priori is between purely grammatical judgments and purely logical ones. Purely grammatical judgments concern the essential forms of meaning-combinations — what combinations of meanings yield a unified meaning at all, as opposed to senseless heaps of words. They ground the *Formenlehre der Bedeutungen* (morphology of meanings) — the pure grammar that Husserl had already outlined in the Fourth Logical Investigation. Purely logical judgments concern the essential laws of truth — what formal meaning-structures guarantee truth or exclude falsehood. They ground formal logic in the proper sense.

### § 39. Evidence of the Purely Grammatical and the Purely Logical Judgments. Explicit, Articulated Performance of the Categorial Articulations and Forms

Husserl discusses the evidence proper to purely grammatical and purely logical judgments. The evidence of purely grammatical judgments consists in the insight that certain meaning-combinations do or do not yield a unified meaning — that, for instance, a nominal meaning and a verbal meaning can combine into a propositional meaning, while two copulae cannot. This evidence requires explicit, articulated performance of the categorial articulations and forms in question. One must genuinely and fully carry out the categorial acts, not merely symbolically intend them, if the grammatical and logical relations are to come to evident givenness. The purely logical laws similarly require that the categorial formations be genuinely performed — that the acts not merely symbolically stand for but actually enact the relevant formal structures. Only in such explicit, fully articulated performance can the laws of logic display their self-evidence.

### § 14. Fixing the Terminology

For greater clarity, Husserl now fixes the terminology. By asserting judging or predicating he understands the self-enclosed assertion-consciousness that something is or is not, is constituted thus or not, etc. — excluding the acts in which word-sound consciousness, pointing-consciousness, or intimation in the broader sense takes place. It concerns purely the conviction of such and such being or non-being that comes to expression with the words. Opposed to actual predicating is the exactly corresponding thinking-without-believing: “predicative thinking-to-oneself” (mere presentation over against judgment). Where no ambiguity is to be feared, we can say “judgment” instead of “judging” and “predication” instead of “predicating.” He also speaks more generally of propositional acts that are being-positing or non-positing — namely predicating or predicatively merely thinking. Further, “predicative acts” includes all acts belonging to or capable of belonging to predication, whether full propositional acts or immediate part-acts capable of entering into them. Wherever the reference to objectuality is considered, the expression “presentations” is used for the relevant acts, especially where the difference between positing and non-positing remains outside the question. Predicative acts that are not full propositional acts can themselves be being-positing or non-positing: nominal acts are nominal positings or nominal “mere” presentations. All predicative acts are categorial, but categorial acts can also be non-predicative — relational apprehending and cognizing on the basis of mere perception, without words and word-meaning, encompasses certain categorial act-forms that are not predicative.

### § 15. The Identity-Predication Brings the Sense of Objectual Reference to First Expression

Following this terminology, Husserl favors nominal presentations understood as predicative acts — those that connect with nominal expressions and function as immediate parts of the predicative act. To understand what it means for a nominal presentation to refer to an objectuality, we are referred to the function of this presentation in predicative contexts. This path seems contrived at first glance — it seems so natural to say that intuitions interwoven with the words furnish the reference to the object. Even after recognizing that interweaving with intuitions is not essential to predicative object-consciousness, one might be inclined to seek objectual reference in the possible adaptation to intuition. But the reference to absurd presentations, for which corresponding intuition is necessarily denied, shows that objectual reference cannot be clarified by recourse to visualization. Moreover, the difficulties besetting us in the predicative sphere recur mutatis mutandis in the intuitive sphere. A reference to objectuality ascribed to intuitive acts is likewise not immediately self-evident; it too leads beyond the individual act to certain categorial connections of intuitions running exactly parallel to predicative ones. Having an intuition is not yet having an object; object here too is the identical of presentations.

Returning to the sphere of predicative acts, we find for nominal presentations that they present something — one and the same thing — in every predicative context. They can indeed be carried out in isolation, outside a predicative context, but then the presentation points back through an interwoven functional thought to some predicative context, and in any case it draws its originarily presenting character only from its predicative function.

A predicative form that brings the sense of objectual reference to first expression is the form of identity-predication. When we say “A is the same (the same object) as B,” two presentations are united into a full propositional act that means and says: “(The object) A is the same as the object B.” The identical object is meant in the first case, and it really is the same if the judgment is correct. But even if incorrect, it is still a meaning — or merely a presentation — of the identity of the object. The sense of objectual reference of presentation first comes to light here, insofar as the reflective talk of the identity of objectual reference of the presentations A and B receives its sense through the identity-assertion into which the presentations themselves enter.

### § 16. The Question of the Truth of Predicative Identification-Thoughts

Husserl attempts to penetrate deeper. One might think: what is the use of the possibility — grounded indeed in the essence of nominal presentations — of inserting them pairwise and quite arbitrarily into the form of predicative identification-thoughts, if in general no truth corresponds to such thoughts? What if there were a nominal presentation insertable into no valid propositional identity-act? Could one then still speak of this presentation presenting something, having a presented, named object? Just as every nominal presentation can be joined with any other in at least the form of a mere thought of identification, so too can each be made the subject-presentation in an arbitrary propositional thought, i.e., predicatively connected with any arbitrary predicate. But perhaps not a single such predicative thought corresponds to a correct judgment. And would the presentation then still present something?

Husserl argues that the statement that a nominal presentation presents a so-and-so-determined object necessarily has its validity if we only fill it with suitable content. When we unfold the sense of such a statement, we arrive at identifications and other predications into which the presentation enters as a member — predications that give rise to reflective talk in which we make presentations into objects of assertion. For example, what does it mean: the presentation “centaur” presents an object, namely an animate being, something between man and horse? This assertion about the presentation “centaur” refers us back to assertions like “A centaur is (by its essence) something between man and horse” — and this judgment we carry out without judging about presentations, without making acts or act-species into objects. Judgments of this kind belong to every presentation, and they are judgments that can be carried out with evidence. The judgment about centaurs does not posit any reality of centaurs but merely explicatively unfolds the essence of the centaur. But it is a judgment, and a true one. There are thus true judgments into which any arbitrary presentation can be incorporated, and each must thereby become the persistent member of various connecting identifications in which the identity of the object maintains itself as essential identity and the object itself unfolds its essence. Certain identifications and attributions thus belong, in true judgments, to the essence of every presentation.

### § 17. The Determination of Presenting Acts through Their Capacity to Enter into Identifying Synthesis

Husserl’s considerations in the preceding lectures concerned meaning-bestowing acts and the objectual reference ascribed to them. Meaning-bestowing acts are predicative acts — acts that are either full predications or that can be incorporated as immediate and proper components in predications. Only in their predicative function do they demonstrate their objectual reference. Analogous things hold for all acts called “presentations.” The proper objectifying functions are those in which something objectual becomes conscious as self-identical. We are thus led back to the acts of unity-consciousness and the intimately connected articulating identity-consciousness. All acts that can be distinguished therein as members or phases — acts that found the unity-consciousness or enter as members into an identifying synthesis — are called presenting acts on account of this capacity: on account of their capacity to fuse or combine with their like in selfsameness-consciousness and thus to make a self-same conscious. The continuous perceptual consciousness, for instance, is a unity-consciousness: the perceptual phases that enter into unity are presentations — perceptual presentations — because they can be so united and, by their essence, present an identical that displays itself intuitively in the flow of perceptual unity as the unitary, identical element. In the articulated synthesis where distinct perceptions or perceptions and memories combine and found a consciousness of the explicit “identical,” the individual perceptions are again, in a higher sense, presentations.

So too in the predicative sphere, where the acts are verbal, asserting. Within predication, the predicative ground-function is a unity-function, namely identification, and predication is fundamentally nothing other than identification and what is essentially referred back to identification.

### § 18. The Nominal Acts as the Properly Object-Giving Acts. The Possibility of Nominalization

Within predication, the nominal acts hold a distinguished position. They are presenting in a pregnant sense. They constitute in predicative consciousness the objects-about. Even when nominal acts do not stand at the subject-position and mark an object as *hypokeímenon*, they exercise the pregnant presenting function designated by the word “object-about.” The situation is different with attributive presentations — e.g., those expressed by adjectival predicates. If we say of a tree that it is green, the green is not objectual in the same way as the tree, although we say here too that the adjectival predicate presents something — the color green. But it is obviously one thing to present the green as in “The tree is green” and another to present it as in “the color green.” To the essence of the adjectival presentation belongs the possibility of nominalization, through which “the same green” constitutes itself as object-about.

Within the predicative sphere, we speak of presentation and reference to objectuality with regard to all part-acts that admit, in unity-consciousness, transformation into a corresponding nominal act — onto which a “this,” a “the same” can be linked, wherewith the nominal position is already carried out. This applies not only to part-acts of predications but also to whole predications. A whole sentence is ascribed an objectuality distinguished from the objects that are objectual in it through its part-presentations. Here again we are referred to the possibility of nominalization, which transforms the full predication in selfsameness-consciousness into a nominal presentation — “S is P — this is a very pleasant fact.”

In the pregnant sense, presentation is the nominal presentation; in it an object stands as object-about. But it draws the object, as it were, from the predicative act in its non-nominalized form — it moves “the same” into the objectuality-position. Through the unity-consciousness connecting the originally predicative act and its nominalization, a self-same stands there, and precisely in this lies the fact that we ascribe to the original act an objectuality that only through the nominal act has become an object-about.

### § 19. The Nominal Presentation Is a Ground-Function within the Full Assertion

From these considerations it follows that nominal acts hold a privileged position. They are the properly presenting acts — though they are properly presenting only by virtue of their predicative performance. Within predications and ever more interwoven predicative contexts, not everything is a nominal act. And yet we speak even outside the nominal of presenting and its objectuality, even for whole predications that are not nominal. But this happens because we can reach into a predicative part-act and extract an objectual something from it — through nominalization. Something is really objectual, objectual in the proper sense, when we have a certain nominal consciousness. And to say of a consciousness that something is objectual in it, though it is not a nominal consciousness, implies that this consciousness by its essence permits a nominalization that lets one look out from it what it does not itself see, and lets one extract and make present an object that is implicit in it but not in the proper sense present.

No thinking that would not be thinking about something — hence no asserting thinking without nominal presentations through which the assertion is assertion about this and that. On the other hand, no thinking is merely nominal presentation. Nominal presentation designates a ground-function within the full assertion; and likewise in all synthetic thinking. It has underlying acts constituting objects-about, but these are only part-acts bound in living function with other part-acts — attributing, subsuming, etc. On the other hand, a new positioning of the gaze is always possible that takes objects from the non-nominal part-acts and from the whole synthetically unified acts, nominalizing them and making what is conscious in them into an object-about in nominal fashion. All objective logical reflection is of this kind and must be distinguished from reflection on acts such as occurs in contexts of practical thinking-regulation.

### § 20. Recapitulation of the Preceding Reflections

Husserl recapitulates. We spoke of the reference to objects ascribed to predicative acts. We were thereby referred back to the unity of cognition, to unity-contexts of judging and ultimately of correct judging. The privileging of nominal presentations as the primarily presenting, object-opposing functions came to the fore. In the proper sense objectual, actually objectual, is the objectual of nominal presentations. Objectual is the identical to which nominal presentations refer in the context of judging — of which as the same these or those predicates are asserted. Object is what, as a self-same, ever newly determines itself in the context of cognition, enters into new selfsameness-relations, assumes various relative attributes, and so continually stands as an identical unity-point for the intertwining predications.

Every predicative act not functioning nominally can be nominalized — not by forming a nominal presentation that makes the act into an object, but one that “extracts” from it something objectual, making into an object-about what was in a certain way already conscious in it but not in the pregnant sense objectual. Through the unity-consciousness connecting the originally predicative act and its nominalization, a self-same stands there.

### § 21. True and Merely Presented Objectual Reference. Judging under Assumption

The objectual reference ascribed to nominal and predicative acts is either true or merely supposed. We say the presented object is in truth, or it is merely presented but in truth is not; or the object is presented but it is not yet decided whether it truly is or is not. This distinction leads us back to certain differences of judgment-unity. If the judgment-unity is a unity of correct judgments, the nominal presentations can be carried out in validity-consciousness, as positing. The presented is then not merely presented — it stands as existing, as valid.

On the other hand, a nominal presentation can occur in the context of judging with predicative function without this validity-consciousness. This includes all judging under assumption (*Assumtion*). One judges under the assumption of A, perhaps expressly under the formulated existential assumption “suppose A is.” In the hypothetical judgment one judges: if A were B. The state of affairs is grasped in nominalization but as assumed, not as valid.

Even if not every presentation refers to a true object, it remains that of every one it can be said unconditionally that it “presents an object” — insofar as it belongs to the essence of every presentation to be capable of being taken assumptively and of functioning in truth as a member of hypothetical judgments, so that genuine assertions arise that state what “belongs” to the “object” of the presentation — not as posited as existing but as assumed.

When we ask what the correctness of presentations or judgments means and how it demonstrates itself, we are referred back from the predicative sphere to the intuitions that give predicative acts their evidence. Intuitions can be complete or incomplete; only completeness of intuition yields true evidence for truth and possibility. Intuition is either of the character of “perception” — not only seeing but positing the object, being-giving — or of the character of modified intuition — not being-giving but merely presenting being, though in intuitive fashion. In the latter case it yields the possibility of the object, the objectual essence.

## Chapter 5. The Categorial Objects

### § 22. Straightforward — Categorial — Verbal Presenting. Categorial Form and Categorial Matter

Husserl begins with the problem of categorial objects. Every nominal presentation not only presents an object in naming — the object-about — but also has a meaning in various senses. Initially one might say: it presents an object by presenting it in this or that “manner” — as so-and-so determined, now standing in relation to these, now to those other objects. One can attend to this “manner” and mean the object not simpliciter but as so-and-so presented, categorially so-and-so formed. But this is not yet clear enough; it is not yet apparent whether this yields a new kind of objectuality and whether distinctions still need to be made.

An object of which we speak, which is a thought object for us, is naturally always thought in some manner. Something is for us an object, and we have some presentations from the group of those that by their essence refer to it. Every presentation entering into the unity of cognition — any discursive synthesis — has its categorial form. Initially the object may be presented in a straightforward presentation, in a continuous unity-consciousness, before all category. This straightforward presentation may be empty or intuitive. Nothing of thinking in the pregnant sense is yet present. Thinking in the pregnant sense comes about only when the presentation takes on categorial forms — for instance, when straightforward perceptions flowing into one another in unity-consciousness pass over into discursive intuition, where a this-consciousness makes what appears into an object-about and parts or characteristics are grasped. All this occurs even before verbal expressions, before the specifically word-related word-presentations. The discursive or categorial thinking introduces its plenitude of forms, a certain articulation; each categorial part-act has its determinate position and function and thereby its determinate form. What was in a certain way implicitly given in straightforward intuition undergoes its explication: now there stands a subject — and therein the object-about — and thereto related in the is-phenomenon the property attributed to it. Verbal thinking mirrors all these articulations and forms in word-presentations.

It is necessary to distinguish between categorial form and categorial matter. To the essence of thinking as *katēgoría* in the widest sense belong certain basic forms of thinking apprehension. These are the customarily so-called logical forms. These forms are of course only something abstractively isolable; they have a changing content. With the same form, predicative acts differ through their content, which is itself something abstract. Presentations like “something that is a,” “something that is β,” etc. have the same form but different content. One must carefully distinguish this difference of form and matter — the categorial in the pregnant sense — from what straightforward intuition already presents, for even there one can say that it presents the objectual in different ways. Here, however, we stand in the sphere of thinking, of the categorial in the pregnant sense.

### § 23. The Difference between Object-About and Categorial Object. Nominalization and Categorial Reflection

The question occupying us is: what should the categorial object be, in distinction from the object-about? How should a twofold objectuality be objectual in the categorial act? We know that wherever we speak of an objectuality, a nominal presentation must be constructible that makes it into an object-about. Where no nominal presentation is present from the start but rather another predicative act, the possible nominalization allows us to attribute objectuality to it.

The named object, the object-about, is the identical to which, in the determining judgment-context in which the nominal presentation predicatively functions, the manifold determinations refer. In thinking, we find at another positioning of the gaze something else: “the mill by the brook” — there is a this grasped as a mill, and the mill is the one by the brook. In the perceptual connections where this object straightforwardly presents itself, and then in the connections of discursive and conceptual acts, this object demonstrates itself as the subject of manifold determinations. We see, and categorially grasping we see again: this here, this is a mill, this is a brook, and this mill stands by the brook. Then the nominal apprehension: this mill by the brook.

The crucial move is what Husserl calls “categorial reflection” (*kategoriale Reflexion*). We can look into the act-formation from which the nominal presentation grows — look into the nominal presentation without making it into an object. We find there a givenness: not the object simpliciter (which is object in judgment-consciousness, insofar as we naively live in it), but rather “the so-and-so thought object as such.” We perform a new positioning of the gaze and make the “this is a” objectual rather than the “this” that was object in the proper sense within the judgment. Not the judgment but the state of affairs, and specifically exactly as it is asserted, becomes objectual. In this context, not only this or that object is given, but the categorial unity is given. The categorial acts are carried out in full, proper, “intuitive” fashion on the ground of perceptual intuition.

We designate these objectualities as “categorials” (*Kategorialien*) and speak of the “propositional” (*Propositionale*) as the categorial belonging to the full judgment. This propositional contains various components: the nominal, the identical, the relational, etc.

### § 24. The Categorial Reflection Is a Giving Act When the Underlying Judging Is Evident

If judging takes place “on the basis of intuition,” i.e., originarily — in the simplest cases on the basis of perception — and each of the categorial act-forms is really carried out and not replaced by empty pieces of judgment-intention, then it belongs to the essence of this intuitive, “evident” judging that it permits “categorial reflection,” and moreover that the categorial is “given.” That reflective seeing is a seeing, a giving and at once objectifying act, when the underlying judging takes place in a perceptual and generally “insightful” manner. The givenness can be more or less adequate; it depends on both levels — the lower and the upper. The lower level consists in straightforward perceptions, the upper in a series of categorial acts. Insofar as the lower gives the perceptual object (which sensory perceptions can only ever do incompletely), the higher acts have experiential ground. They themselves have full adequacy only insofar as they really conceptually grasp and form, in contrast to possible cases where empty acts enter.

The categorial consciousness built on intuition and itself to be addressed as intuition need not be a perceiving consciousness that gives the categorial objectuality. The consciousness can have the character of a re-presentifying consciousness; in general there are various modifications within intuition. Within the judgment-sphere, within the sphere of categorial being-consciousness, the propositional is an objectual something to be extracted from the judgment, and in the truest and strictest sense when the judgment is evident. In that case the propositional is real, and likewise every nominal and other categorial belonging to it.

### § 25. Retrospect on the Previous Course of Considerations

Husserl looks back. Our considerations on the essence of meaning led to the important concept of the categorial. From the outset, in asking about the meaning of expressions or predicative acts, various concepts of meaning arose. The first superficial consideration drew attention to the objectual reference of predicative acts. Then “meaning” in distinction from objectuality could be understood phanologically: every predicative act has a certain predicative essence, a specific phanologically immanent to it. The phanological concept of meaning. But from the outset we saw that it cannot rest with such phanological concepts. We came upon categorial objectualities that, distinguished from the object-about and possibly from the asserted “situation of affairs” or “state of affairs,” designate a phenomenological (ontic) concept of meaning. The tangle of meaning-concepts and interwoven objectualities led to difficulties: how one and the same act could be referred in different ways to objectual things.

A first series of considerations dealt with the constitution of objects-about in predicative acts through nominal acts and with nominalization. After these important investigations, we turned to the proper meaning-concepts — the difficult phenomenological (ontic) ones.

### § 26. The Correlation of Linguistic Expression, Phanological Essences, and Categorials

Husserl determined the categorials as the objectual that is implicitly conscious in a full *katēgoría* and becomes an object-about through a new positioning of the gaze — “categorial reflection” — and through a nominalization occurring within it. In predication, in the act of verbally carried-out asserting, there is on the one side the linguistic expression with its grammatical forms, on the other side the meaning-bestowing acts with their specific essences, which as such have their phanological determinations. What now corresponds to both on the objectual side? The full propositional as a categorial objectuality. We have here a threefold correlation: (1) the linguistic expression, (2) the phanological essence, (3) the categorial.

### § 27. The Essential Difference of the Correlative Meaning-Concepts

#### a) Every Assertion Has a Meaning in the Phanological (Phansical) Sense

Every assertion, says Husserl, has a meaning in the phanological sense. This follows from the fact that meaning in this sense is the specific essence of meaning-bestowal.

#### b) Not to Every Assertion Belongs a Categorial, Insofar as Truth in the Ontic Sense and Categorial Objectuality Are the Same

A categorial is given only when the judging is evident. If the judging is not evident — if it is false or without adequate intuitive grounding — then the categorial objectuality does not obtain, though the phanological meaning-essence is always present. Truth in the ontic sense and categorial objectuality are the same: what truly is the case is the categorial objectuality.

### § 28. The Possibility of the Assumptive Turn Lying in the Essence of Every Categorial Act

In the essence of every categorial act lies the possibility of the assumptive turn (*assumtive Wendung*). Every judgment, whether true or false, can be taken assumptively — i.e., one can think the same propositional content without the character of conviction, in the manner of a mere thought or assumption. The categorial objectuality is then not given in the full sense of truth, but the propositional content is the same.

### § 29. The Assumptive Categorial, the Meanedness As Such, Belongs to Every Predicative Act

The assumptive categorial, the meanedness as such (*die Gemeintheit als solche*), is to be attributed to every predicative act. Every predicative act has its propositional content that can be taken assumptively. The “meant as such” — the propositional content in its full categorial articulation — belongs to every judgment, true or false, evident or not.

### § 30. Supplements

#### a) The Correlative Relation of Nominal and Object-About

Husserl discusses the correlative relation between the nominal presentation and the object-about. The object-about constitutes itself through the nominal presentation in the context of predication. Through identification-syntheses, the identity of the object is established and maintained. The nominal presentation is the proper object-giving function, and the object-about is the correlate of this function.

#### b) The Relation of Propositional and State of Affairs or Situation of Affairs

The propositional (*Propositionale*) relates to the state of affairs (*Sachverhalt*) and the situation of affairs (*Sachlage*) as the categorial objectuality relates to the objectuality simpliciter. The propositional is the state of affairs as it is asserted — the state of affairs in its categorial apprehension. Different propositionals can correspond to the same situation of affairs, just as different nominal presentations can name the same object. The situation of affairs is the objectual common element; the propositional is the categorially formed mode in which it is meant.

## Chapter 6. The Determination of Mere Meaning (in the Phansical as Well as the Ontic Sense)

### § 31. Categorial Object and Categorial Essence

The categorial object is given in “categorial perception” — in the evident judgment or the evident nominal turn of the evident judgment and its givennesses. From the categorial object we distinguish the essence of the categorial object — the categorial essence. Only when the categorial object is from the outset an essence, a categorial essentia, does the categorial essence coincide with the categorial object. Every categorial object has its categorial essence — a species or universal that can be grasped through ideation.

### § 32. Meaning as Propositional Essence or Possibility

Husserl now develops the concept of meaning as propositional essence or possibility. The meaning of a proposition is the propositional essence — the essential content of what is judged, abstracted from the question of existence. This is the “proposition in itself” (*Satz an sich*) in Bolzano’s sense, interpreted phenomenologically. The meaning so understood is neither the act nor a piece of the act, but the categorial essence, the *eidos* that particularizes itself in the manifold of possible judgments of the same content. It is the same whether the judgment is true or false, evident or not, carried out in conviction or merely thought — the meaning remains one and the same identical essence.

### § 33. Levels of Perfection of Essential Givenness

The givenness of essences admits of degrees of perfection. A fully adequate givenness is one in which the categorial essence is given in complete intuitive fullness — all categorial forms are really carried out, all conceptual grasps are truly performed, all syntheses are genuinely executed. Less perfect are cases where empty components replace full ones, where categorial forms are merely symbolically intended but not intuitively realized.

### § 34. Covering-Relations

#### a) First Sketch of These Relations

Husserl introduces the concept of “covering” (*Deckung*). Different presentations or judgments can “cover” one another — they can enter into syntheses of identification that establish their reference to the same objectual content. Covering is the phenomenological correlate of sameness of meaning: when two presentations cover one another, they mean the same.

#### b) Exposition of the Covering-Relations in the Lower and Higher Layers

Covering-relations obtain both in the lower layer (straightforward intuition) and in the higher layer (categorial acts). In the lower layer, different perceptions of the same object cover one another in continuous perceptual unity. In the higher layer, different categorial apprehensions of the same state of affairs cover one another. The covering in the higher layer presupposes covering in the lower layer but goes beyond it, since it also involves the identity of categorial forms and conceptual content.

#### c) “Coverings” between Symbolic Categorial Acts. Gaining the Mere Meaning in the Phansical and Ontic Sense

Coverings also obtain between symbolic categorial acts — i.e., between purely verbal, non-intuitive acts. Two symbolic assertions of the same propositional content cover one another even without intuitive fulfillment. This yields the concept of mere meaning in both senses: in the phansical sense, it is the specific essence common to all acts of the same meaning-bestowal; in the ontic sense, it is the empty categorial form — the propositional content taken without intuitive fullness, the “mere meaning” as the empty form of the categorial.

### § 35. The Mere Ontic Meaning, the Proposition in the Objectively Logical Sense, Is the Empty Form of the Categorial

Husserl now determines the mere ontic meaning — the proposition in the objectively logical sense — as the empty form of the categorial. The proposition in the logical sense is not the truth (the filled categorial) but the possibility — the categorial form that can be filled or left empty. It is the identical content that remains whether the judgment is true or false, evident or not. In the filled case — where truth obtains — we have categorial reality. In the empty case, we have categorial possibility, the mere meaning. The proposition as meaning-content is contained in both the categorial reality and the categorial possibility of the same content.

## Chapter 7. The Essential Distinctions of Judgments

### § 36. Existential and Essential Judgments

#### a) First Characterization of the Distinction

Husserl introduces the fundamental distinction between existential judgments (*Daseinsurteile*) and essential judgments (*Wesensurteile*). Existential judgments — also called judgments of factual validity, existential judgments in a distinguished sense, judgments of fact — are judgments that establish facts, that establish a being-there (*Dasein*). They relate to individual existence, to the factual actuality of things, events, and states of affairs. Their truth is contingent — it depends on how things happen to stand in the actual world.

Essential judgments, by contrast, are judgments of essence, *a priori* judgments. They state what belongs to something by virtue of its essence, regardless of whether anything of the relevant kind actually exists. “A triangle has an angle-sum of two right angles” is an essential judgment — it holds by virtue of the essence of triangularity, not by virtue of the factual existence of any particular triangle. Essential judgments are necessary and universal; they hold under any possible circumstances in which the relevant essences are instantiated.

#### b) Further Elaboration. The Givenness of Existential State of Affairs and Essential State of Affairs

Husserl elaborates on the givenness of the two kinds of states of affairs. Existential states of affairs are given in experience — in perception and the cognition founded on perception. The truth of existential judgments is established through the confirmation of experience, through the evidence of factual perception. Essential states of affairs are given in essential insight — in *Wesensschau*, the intuition of essences. One apprehends the essence of triangle, of color, of spatial shape, etc., and on the ground of this essential apprehension one grasps the necessary relations belonging to the essence. The evidence of essential judgments is fundamentally different from that of existential judgments: it is the evidence of essential necessity, not of factual contingency.

### § 37. Modifications within the Sphere of Essential Consciousness

Within the sphere of essential consciousness, various modifications are possible. One can grasp an essence more or less adequately. The essential judgment can be carried out with varying degrees of intuitive fullness. The essence can be grasped in full intuitive clarity or merely emptily intended. These differences of fullness do not affect the identity of the essential content judged — the proposition remains the same — but they affect the epistemic value of the judgment, its degree of evidence.

### § 38. Spheres of A Priori Judging

#### a) Ontological and Phanological Essential Judgments

Husserl distinguishes between ontological and phanological essential judgments. Ontological essential judgments concern the essences of objects and objectualities — the essences of things, properties, relations, states of affairs, etc. Phanological essential judgments concern the essences of acts and act-characters — the essences of perception, judgment, meaning-bestowal, etc. Both are *a priori*, but they differ in their subject matter.

#### b) Purely Grammatical and Purely Logical Judgments

Within the sphere of essential judgments, Husserl further distinguishes purely grammatical judgments and purely logical judgments. Purely grammatical judgments concern the laws of meaning-combination — what combinations of meanings yield unified meanings and what combinations yield nonsense. “A round square” violates no grammatical law (it is a unified, though contradictory, meaning), while “a round or” violates grammatical law (it is meaningless). Purely logical judgments concern the laws of truth — what combinations of meanings are compatible with truth and what combinations lead necessarily to falsehood. “A round square” violates logical law (it is necessarily false), though it is grammatically well-formed.

### § 39. Evidence of Purely Grammatical and Purely Logical Judgments. Explicit, Articulated Execution of the Categorial Articulations and Forms

Husserl discusses the evidence of purely grammatical and purely logical judgments. These judgments have their own distinctive kind of evidence — the evidence of meaning-laws. For purely grammatical judgments, the evidence lies in the insight that certain meaning-combinations are impossible (yield no unified meaning). For purely logical judgments, the evidence lies in the insight into incompatibility — that certain predications exclude one another by virtue of the essences involved.

The fulfillment of purely logical judgments requires the explicit, articulated execution of the categorial articulations and forms. “Mere meaning” — taking in only the direction and determinacy of intention without intuitive fullness — suffices for purely logical insight, since the validity of logical laws depends only on the meaning-forms and not on the particular intuitive content filling them. This connects to the fundamental character of analytic truths, which — as Husserl emphasized already in the *Logical Investigations* — pass over into formal-logical laws when the terms are replaced by indeterminates. The analytic truths are the truths fulfilled purely on the basis of meaning.

## Supplementary Texts

### Beilage I: Course of the Lectures on the Theory of Meaning and the Sequence of Problems

This appendix, written by Husserl, provides a schematic outline of the lecture course and the sequence of problems touched upon or equipped with attempts at ordering. It covers the main distinctions introduced in the first two chapters: indicative signs and words, word-appearing and the word itself, the word-sign as perceived or phantasied natural-real object, the word as insensitive to such differences of appearance, the significance of expressions, and the distinctions between meaning-bestowing and meaning-fulfilling acts.

### Beilage II: Word-Sound Consciousness and Word-Consciousness

This brief appendix (relating to §3b) addresses the distinction between word-sound consciousness (*Wortlautbewußtsein*) and word-consciousness (*Wortbewußtsein*). Husserl elaborates on how the consciousness of the word-sound — the sensory appearance — is to be distinguished from the consciousness of the word as a meaningful sign. The word as word is more than its sensory substratum; it carries its meaning-function.

### Beilage III: Presentation as Phansical and as Ontic Meaning

Relating to §8, this appendix discusses the concept of presentation (*Vorstellung*) as meaning in both the phansical and ontic senses. Husserl further elaborates the two-sided concept of meaning: the phansical meaning as the specific essence of the meaning-bestowing act, and the ontic meaning as the categorial objectuality on the correlate side.

### Beilage IV: Various Concepts of Content, Specifically for Judgments

Relating to §8a and §35, this appendix discusses multiple concepts of “content” (*Inhalt*) as they apply to judgments. Husserl distinguishes: the content as the matter of the act (what is judged about, in what manner), the content as the propositional meaning (the ideal unity of what is stated), and the content as the judged state of affairs. These different senses of “content” must be carefully separated to avoid confusion.

### Beilage V: The Proposition as Idea of the Meaning and as Idea of the Meant As Such

Relating to §8a and §35, this appendix addresses the proposition (*Satz*) understood in two ways: as the idea of the meaning (*Meinung*) — i.e., the ideal essence of the act-side — and as the idea of the meant as such (*des Gemeinten als solchen*) — i.e., the ideal objectual correlate. The proposition in the first sense is the phansical meaning-species; in the second sense it is the ontic meaning, the categorial objectuality taken in its essential form.

### Beilage VI: Meaning as Inner Moment of the Judgment and Meaning as the Judged As Such

Relating to §8b, this appendix explores meaning as an inner moment of the judgment versus meaning as the “judged as such” (*Geurteiltes als solches*). The inner moment is the phansical meaning — something belonging to the experience-side. The judged as such is the ontic meaning — something on the objectual-correlate side. Husserl works to articulate the precise relation between these two.

### Beilage VII: The Mere Sense as the Meant As Such and the Object or State of Affairs to Which the Sense “Refers”

Relating to Chapter 3, this important appendix discusses the concept of “mere sense” (*bloßer Sinn*) as the “meant as such” (*das Gemeinte als solches*), distinguished from the object or state of affairs to which the sense “refers.” The meant as such is not the object simpliciter but the object taken in its mode of givenness, in its categorial apprehension. The “reference” of sense to object is to be understood through the identification-syntheses in which the same object is established across different senses.

### Beilage VIII: The Conditions of the Possibility of Valid Predications in General Are the Conditions of the Possibility of Objects in General

This brief appendix (relating to §16) states that the conditions of the possibility of valid predications in general are the conditions of the possibility of objects in general. This fundamental Kantian-sounding principle is given a phenomenological interpretation: objectuality constitutes itself in predication, and the laws governing valid predication are therefore simultaneously laws governing the possibility of objects.

### Beilage IX: The Relation of Predicative Act and Its Nominal Presentation

Relating to §19, this appendix discusses the relation between the predicative act as a whole and the nominal presentation that enters into it as a component. The nominal presentation draws its objectifying function from its role within predication. The relation is one of part to whole, but a distinctive kind: the nominal presentation is a ground-function within the predicative whole.

### Beilage X: Meaning and Categorial Object as the Truth Meant in the Assertion

Relating to §24 and §27b, this appendix identifies meaning and categorial object with the truth meant in the assertion. When an assertion is true, what is asserted is a truth — a categorial objectuality that truly obtains. The meaning of the true assertion is the truth itself, understood as the categorial objectuality.

### Beilage XI: Renewed Reflection on Meaning and Meant and Actual Object

Relating to §27b, this appendix undertakes a renewed reflection on the relation between meaning, the meant object, and the actual object. Husserl works through the difficulties of distinguishing the “meant as such” from the object that is meant, attending to cases where the meant object exists and cases where it does not.

### Beilage XII: State of Affairs — Situation of Affairs — Propositional

Relating to §30b, this extensive appendix develops the distinctions among state of affairs (*Sachverhalt*), situation of affairs (*Sachlage*), and propositional (*Propositionale*) in greater detail.

#### a) The Unity of the Predicative Appearance and Its Objectual Correlate

Husserl discusses the unity of the predicative appearance — the complete predicative act — and its objectual correlate. The objectual correlate of the predicative act is the state of affairs, but the state of affairs as asserted, i.e., in its categorial form.

#### b) The State of Affairs As Asserted as the Propositional, and the State of Affairs That Is Asserted

A crucial distinction is drawn between the state of affairs as it is asserted (the propositional) and the state of affairs that is asserted (the objectuality simpliciter). The propositional is the categorially formed mode of assertion; the state of affairs that is asserted is the same objectual situation that can be asserted in different ways.

#### c) Own-Presentation and Nominal Presentation — Object Simpliciter and Meaning

Husserl develops the distinction between own-presentation (*Eigenvorstellung*) and nominal presentation, and correlatively between the object simpliciter and meaning understood as nominal and propositional meaning.

### Beilage XIII: 1) Appearance, 2) Sense, 3) Meaning = Objectual Reference

This extensive appendix (relating to §34b) develops a threefold articulation: appearance, sense, and meaning understood as objectual reference.

#### a) Continuous Identity-Synthesis in Perception

Husserl discusses the continuous identity-synthesis in perception — how the manifold of perceptual phases constitutes the unity of the perceived object. The components of perceptual duration stand in relation to the identity of objectual reference: the object remains the same across the flow of perceptual appearances.

#### b) The Discrete Covering-Synthesis as Foundation of Identification in Re-cognition

Discrete covering-syntheses underlie identification in re-cognition (*Wiedererkennen*). This identification is made possible through the time-horizon as sense-reference to continuous synthesis. When we re-cognize an object, a discrete synthesis of identification connects the present presentation with a past one through the mediation of the temporal horizon.

#### c) The Identity of Meaning as Covering of the Same Meant, the Same Sense

The identity of meaning is the covering of the same meant, the same sense. Meaning and the horizon of appearances: the meaning is the identical sense that maintains itself across the manifold of appearances and their temporal horizons.

#### d) Extension of the Consideration to the Thinking-Sphere

Husserl extends the consideration to the thinking-sphere. The question of the identity of meaning is to be distinguished from the question of the demonstration of truth. The identity of meaning concerns the same propositional content; the demonstration of truth concerns the categorial fulfillment of that content.

### Beilage XIV: Ontic Meaning in the Sphere of Perceptions and Straightforward Intuitions

Relating to §35, this appendix discusses ontic meaning in the sphere of perceptions and straightforward intuitions — extending the concept of ontic meaning beyond the predicative-verbal sphere to the pre-predicative sphere of sensory experience.

### Beilage XV: The Possibility of Distinguishing Judgment-Classes within the Theory of Meaning

Relating to Chapter 7, this appendix discusses the possibility of distinguishing classes of judgments within the framework of the theory of meaning — existential, essential, and meaning-judgments.

### Beilage XVI: Doctrine of the Essences of Judgments

Relating to Chapter 7, this appendix sketches a doctrine of the essences (*Wesenslehre*) of judgments — i.e., an essential-analytic classification of judgment-types.

### Beilage XVII: The Judgment in Distinction from Judging

This important appendix distinguishes the judgment (*Urteil*) from judging (*Urteilen*). Judging is the act — the concrete psychic event of carrying out a judgment. The judgment is the ideal content — the identical propositional unity that remains the same across all instances of its actualization. This distinction parallels the general distinction between act and ideal content developed throughout the lecture.

### Beilage XVIII: The Ontic Judgment and the Judgment-Idea

This appendix discusses the ontic judgment and the judgment-idea. The ontic judgment is the categorially constituted truth — the judgment as it truly obtains. The judgment-idea is the essential form of the judgment taken in its possibility — the universal type of which every particular judgment of the same form is an instance.

### Beilage XIX: The Problem of the Ideality of Meanings

This final and most extensive appendix addresses the problem of the ideality of meanings.

#### a) Pure and Empirical Meanings. Their Difference Lies in the Relation of Actual Sense and Phantasy-Sense

Husserl distinguishes between pure and empirical meanings. Pure meanings are ideal species graspable through essential insight independently of all experience. Empirical meanings are bound to the particular — their content involves reference to particular objects, places, times. The difference lies in the relation between actual sense and phantasy-sense: pure meanings are indifferent to the distinction between actual and phantasied content, while empirical meanings essentially involve actual, unmodified positional acts.

#### b) The Empirical Meaning Is Not an Idea in the Sense of an Eidos

The empirical meaning is not an idea in the sense of an *eidos*. It cannot be extracted from a modified, phantasy-mode act in the way that pure meanings can. It can only be drawn from an unmodifiedly positing act — from an act that takes its object as actual. This distinguishes it fundamentally from pure meaning.

#### c) Meanings as Object of Apophantics. Pure Idea — Empirically Determined Idea

Meanings as objects of apophantics — the theory of propositions and their formal properties — are pure ideas. Logic as apophantics deals with propositions purely formally, abstracting from all empirical content. The purely logical concept of meaning is thus the concept of a pure idea. But there are also empirically determined ideas — meaning-types that, while retaining a core of formal-logical structure, incorporate empirical content.

#### d) Renewed Problematization: The Aspect of the Diversity of Experience-Horizon and Phantasy-Horizon

Husserl returns once more to the problematic: the distinction between experience-horizon and phantasy-horizon introduces a diversity into meaning that must be reckoned with. The same expression can function in connection with different experiential horizons, and the question arises whether and how the meaning remains the same across these diverse horizons. This final problematization points forward to themes that will become central in Husserl’s later genetic phenomenology and in the analyses of the lifeworld.
