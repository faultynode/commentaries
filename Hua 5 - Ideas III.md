# Ideas Pertaining to a Pure Phenomenology and to a Phenomenological Philosophy — Third Book: Phenomenology and the Foundations of the Sciences

## First Chapter

### The Different Regions of Reality (Material Thing, Body, Soul), the Correlative Fundamental Kinds of Apperception, and the Sciences Arising Therefrom

The opening paragraph of the entire work announces its central concern: establishing how the fundamental distinctions among types of reality — material thing, body [Leib], and soul — bear on the demarcation and inner meaning of the sciences. Husserl recalls that in the preceding phenomenological-kinetic analyses (from the second book of the *Ideas*), the all-governing distinction between mere material thing, lived body, and soul (or psychic I) has been secured, and its phenomenological sources have been studied. This articulation of experienced realities is given intuitively, prior to all thought and especially prior to all scientifically theorizing thought. Since experiential thinking can draw its ultimate justification only from experience — by orienting itself according to experience and binding itself to the inherent sense of the experienced — it follows with certainty that such fundamental distinctions among objectivities, arising as they do from fundamental features of constitutive apprehension, must be decisive for the separation of scientific domains and for the meaning of their respective problematics. Husserl announces his intention to pursue this connection.

### §1. Material Thing, Material Perception, Material Natural Science (Physics)

The first paragraph of §1 identifies material things as the first kind of reality. Husserl observes that although, according to the analyses already carried out, the constitution of material things is intimately interwoven with the constitution of other categories of reality, what gives material things their unity is a wholly distinctive type and connection of constitutive acts. The originarily giving act is material perception (thing-perception), or corporeal perception. This designates a fundamental kind of perception, in its essence completely self-enclosed over against every other kind. Husserl deliberately avoids calling it “outer perception,” since that label could and would have to apply also to the perception of the lived body, which constitutes a new, distinct fundamental kind. He defends the terminological transfer: just as perception of the outer is called “outer” although it is nothing outer in itself, so the perception of the material can legitimately be called “material perception.” Material perception is a special case of perception of the extensive, which also includes the perception of phantoms.

The second paragraph turns to the broader experiential context. In the coherent course of material experience, the nature constituting itself therein is experienced in its unified spatial-temporal-causal nexus. When this experience becomes theoretical experience grounding theoretical thought about nature, material natural science arises. The objectivity of this nature — nature in its first and foundational sense — rests on the mutual understanding of a plurality of experiencing subjects who have their bodies, bodies that appear to them as well as to those with whom they communicate. Yet this interweaving of material nature with embodiment and the psychic does not at all hinder nature’s self-sufficiency. The mode of theoretical experiencing and of theoretical thinking-intentions runs exclusively through material experiential apprehensions. The natural scientist is of course involved with body and soul in his cognition of nature, and so is the community of researchers to which every individual knows himself related. Yet however essential this is for the constitution of natural objectivity, it remains fundamentally twofold: to live the total apprehension of the material with all the constitutive components essentially belonging to it — including apprehensions of the bodily-psychic — in such a way that the theoretically experiencing gaze is fixated determinatively on material being itself, as opposed to being theoretically directed at bodies and souls, thereby pursuing physiological and psychological research.

The third paragraph develops the notion of different possible “attitudes” (Einstellungen) of the thematic gaze within the complex of founded apprehensions. Wherever apprehensions are founded in other apprehensions and higher-order apprehensions are formed, one must attend to the possibility — grounded in the very essence of complex apprehensions — of different orientations of the thematic gaze, which, as theoretical, determines the theoretical theme in the sense that the underlying apprehension prescribes. Since in the web of apprehensions constituting realities, the experience of materiality represents the lowest level that constitutes reality at all, the theoretically experiencing gaze encounters the material as something self-subsistent, unfounded, not presupposing and not having beneath it any other kind of being. Material nature stands there as something entirely self-enclosed and preserves its closed unity and what essentially belongs to it in this closure, not only in the mere context of theoretical experiencing, but also of theoretical experiential thinking — what we call natural science in the ordinary sense, or more precisely material natural science. How the stepwise constitution of material objectivity determines the various levels of natural cognition, and in particular how the difficult problems of clarifying descriptive versus explanatory science and the fundamentally different kinds of concept-formation and judgment-formation on each side are to be resolved — all this is a special domain of phenomenological-scientific-theoretical investigations, to be discussed elsewhere.

The fourth paragraph draws attention to a point that must constantly be kept in view with respect to all cognition of reality: the inseparability of the cognition of reality and the cognition of causality. All science of the real is causally explanatory if it genuinely and in the sense of objective validity wants to determine what the real is. Cognition of causal relations is not something secondary to the cognition of the real, as if the real were first something in and for itself and only occasionally, as something external to its being, entered into relations with other realities, acting on them and suffering from them. Rather, it is precisely the principle of reality as such that it has no such self-sufficient essence at all; it is what it is only in causality. It is something principally relative, demanding its correlative terms, and only in this connection of term and counter-term is each a substance of real properties. A substance in the sense of the well-known definitions of Descartes and Spinoza is therefore something principally different from an objective reality in the sense of Husserl’s delimitations.

The fifth paragraph addresses the way causality is given in experience. In the context of experience, causality is not straightforwardly given, just as the real itself that stands in causal relations is not straightforwardly given. In a certain sense one can always say: where something is experienced, something is given and given without further ado — for example, the tree that we see. And it is given in its circumstances. But regarding those circumstances, they lie in the entire co-intuited surroundings, and what in them is really a causally determining circumstance remains vague. The theoretically experiencing gaze easily grasps perceptually given features in the perceived, and insofar as something real is conscious, causality too is conscious — but utterly unclearly, needing to be brought out and conceptually determined only through theoretical experiential analysis and investigation. Likewise the real itself, the subject-term of the real relation, is something indeterminate: the real object is given only one-sidedly, the real state, although perceived, will in the continuing course of perception always be capable of richer disclosure, and the property manifesting itself in it will come forth ever more fully, and so on.

The sixth and final paragraph of §1 observes that scientific investigation, as one sees from the outset, always demands new penetration into real-causal connections. What methods are required to gain objectively valid reality-judgments, and what conditions of possibility for such judgments must be inscribed in the very essence of experience — to discuss this is a separate topic. The only aim here was to clarify why causal investigation plays such a governing role in the sciences of reality, and why so much will be said about causality in the further discussions.

### §2. Body, Bodily Apprehension, and Somatology

#### a) The Specific Determinations of the Body

The opening paragraph of §2a identifies a second fundamental kind of apprehension, one that constitutes its object as an object of the second level: bodily apprehension (Leibesauffassung). It is a new fundamental kind with respect to the fundamentally different way in which the uppermost stratum of bodily objectivity — the specifically bodily layer — constitutes itself as compared to everything that belongs to bodily matter. Correlated with this (naturally a priori) are the indicated essential differences between material bodily determinations and the specifically bodily ones. To the latter belong the real-unitary sense fields in their sensation-states, which vary according to their associated real circumstances. First among these are those fields that truly display the form of localization constitutive for this kind of realization in an immediately intuitive way — above all the tactile field as the primal field, insofar as it has the first, foundational localization, and the fields layered upon it, such as the warmth-cold field (Husserl notes that he says not “temperature field,” because temperature is a physical concept that has no place here). In further sequence, every sense field and every essentially closed group of sensations acquires a realizing attachment to the body, indicating different sides of its real sensitivity and constituting different strata of sensation-connections really belonging to it.

The second paragraph offers the visual field as an example. Husserl says one can apprehend one’s visual field as a continuously, though changeably, filled visual expanse — by abstracting from all the objectual apprehensions built upon it, through which one sees a thing-like objectual field and has in a certain appearance-cognition — and can recognize this enduring unity in experiential and thought-connections as belonging to the body, more specifically to this retina, with its inner order corresponding to the two-dimensional order of retinal sites as an expanse in the whole. One can then follow the connections, recognized through experience and thought, between the system of excitability of the retina — according to the coextensive order (where, as with all localization, it is not the actual spatial form that matters, but only the continuous insertion in the sense described by analysis situs) — and the system of visual sensations as excitation-effects, according to the order of the field itself.

The third paragraph extends this mediated relationship to all sensations. In this way, mediated by thought, one cannot see the visual field on the retina, but can apprehend it as analogously belonging to it, just as one apprehends the tactile field as belonging to the touch-sensitive bodily surface. Thus the universe of sensations (of sensory impressions) of each I acquires a relation to the body and to its parts, which are thereby characterized as “sense organs,” and becomes itself something bodily, though not material. All the extensions that possible experience can make in this respect are bound to the sense already given by the bodily apprehension, and this sense is fixed through perceived bodily existence with perceived localization. What is localizable is not everything whatsoever, but is essentially prescribed. Sensory pain and sensory pleasure can spread out, hence they are localizable, just as they are factually perceived in localization. Visual sensations are spread out in a field and are in principle localizable, although in the essential type of embodiment that can be attributed to the human body, the possibility of perceiving localized visual sensation is excluded, or, empirically speaking, is absent in us humans. But human body is essentially a particularization of body in general, and in this generality it is evident that in extension lies the fundamental condition of the possibility of localization, so that every sensory genus that offers itself mediately or immediately in the manner of extension could be perceptibly localized. Yet the bodily-attachable extends further: what does not even fulfill this condition can nevertheless belong to the body as something really dependent on bodily parts, through fixed correlation, provided that under constancy of other real circumstances, changes on the side of the dependent always correspond to material changes of the relevant bodily part. The apprehension as localization is then not possible, but it becomes a simile.

The fourth paragraph addresses the relation of the body to its material substratum. Various variations are possible. The body can contain material parts that can be excised without it remaining a body. But it is also possible that it remains a body upon excision of material parts and even that it does not lose its entire sensation fields. Equally the body can expand itself. Cutting a fingernail or hair, or its growing back, means the body loses or gains something. It also gains when one takes a staff or tool in hand, and likewise through clothing. A tool is an extension of the body — namely when it is “in use.” It is both an extension of the sensing body and of the body as organ of will. In the sensation fields already belonging to the body, sensation-changes of a content arise that would not arise without the extension. In the field of will, free movements and changes arise that otherwise would not arise. In principle nothing is thereby altered.

#### b) The Science of the Body: Somatology

The opening paragraph of §2b introduces the idea of somatology as the science directed at this field of being. Theoretical research can turn to the body, and bodily perception and experience — what Husserl proposes to call somatology — can assume the mode of theoretical experience and determine theoretical thinking. Since the specifically somatological is not a separate reality but a higher stratum of being built upon material reality, the theoretical experience and cognition of somatic being also requires material experience and corresponding material cognition. This latter belongs, logically speaking, to material natural science. If we call the science of embodiment “somatology,” then insofar as it traces the material properties of the body, it is material natural science. But insofar as it is specific somatology, it is something new, distinguished by a new fundamental form of experience. Looking more closely, this dual position applies to all zoological sciences — for example, the physiology of humans and animals. They are natural sciences in the narrower sense regarding the materiality of animal beings; they are somatology insofar as, in the physiology of the sense organs and the nervous system (which is better called the doctrine of the body’s sensations), they systematically carry out references to the sensation-spheres. Evidently the somatological experiential apprehension rules here, and without it nothing somatological is findable or indirectly reconstructible. The foundation is ultimately the direct somatic perception, which every experiential researcher can carry out only on his own body, and then the somatic interpretation that he performs in the interpretive apprehension of perceived foreign bodies as such, performing it in a way that gives this interpretation the character of an experience that can be confirmed, more closely determined, and eventually corrected by further similar empirical apprehensions and positing — in short, a verifying experience.

The second paragraph clarifies the extended sense of “perception” here. In a broader sense one can speak of perceptions in these interpretive acts, insofar as what characterizes perception is the consciousness of bodily self-presence, and in the perception of a foreign body we have, alongside the consciousness of the self-presence of the foreign body-thing, a certain secondary consciousness of bodily givenness with respect to the interpreted moments — though one that, more closely examined, turns out not to be genuine originary givenness but only givenness through a kind of presentification. Nevertheless it is not entirely without ground when we speak of “seeing” an animal, a human being, and of “seeing” their pain when they are pricked, their sensory pleasure when eating, and so on for all the specifically psychic. Such acts belong to the sphere of experience and not of mere reproduction of experience, as memories are. Husserl refers to the more detailed treatments of the essence of interpretation elsewhere.

The third paragraph draws a consequence about the unity of sensation-doctrine. According to this presentation, the entire doctrine of sensations treated by physiology and psychology — with all the well-known teachings about the different peculiarities of the sensory domains in their dependence on sense organs, sensory centers, and the nature of physiological sensory stimuli — forms a unity that, together with corresponding teachings about “feeling-sensations” and about sensations in the widest sense, belongs to somatology. What is researched is the embodiment in the sense of somatological experience: the real property-stratum of sensitivities belonging to the body as such, manifesting itself in the original sensations and generally in the sense fields as states of the soma. Husserl also mentions the reason why he has not named general biology and especially not botany. The obvious kinship between animal and plant, and the many parallel and closely related problems that this kinship brings in the execution of research directed at plant “life” and at animal materiality, brings a unity of natural sciences from this side (with respect to zoology as the natural science of the animal material body), but this kinship does not extend so far that a definite interpretation of the plant as a body (and ultimately as a body for something psychic in the full sense) could have been achieved, one that could have posed its definite somatological problems in the way that is possible first for higher animals and then, following the gradation of the animal, also for lower ones.

The fourth paragraph continues the reflection on plants. The general and entirely indeterminate empathy that analogy permits does not suffice for the researcher; he needs concrete experience of concrete sensitivities, referred to concrete organs, where the analogy of the organs with animal ones — to which experientially known sensitivities belong — must be great enough to ground a probability of interpretation. Where this is lacking, the treatment of botany as material natural science suffices, or rather another treatment is not possible. This would not, however, exclude the possibility that plants do have sensitivities, only that we would be unable to recognize them because every bridge of empathy and indirect analysis was missing. Husserl says he has only wanted to accommodate as much as possible to the prevailing physiological botany and biology in general, and leaves open whether interpretive experience might not play its fruitful role, as it doubtlessly does in zoology, although this too is often unrecognized.

### §3. The Delimitation of Somatology and Psychology

The first paragraph of §3 affirms that the separation carried out under the title of somatology is thoroughly natural: it demarcates a class of investigations as radically as is conceivable for any science, namely through a fundamental form of experience and experiential objectivity. Nevertheless it is understandable that an independent somatology was never developed, just as it is understandable that the idea of such a science (important as it is already for reasons of scientific theory) was never conceived. Its conception presupposes the pure isolation of sensation from the web of apprehension in which it is woven — hence unaccustomed phenomenological analyses and a turning of the gaze away from what is given in the full apprehensions and what determines our natural directions of regard. We perceive the body, but at once also the things perceived “by means of” the body in their respective modes of appearance, and at once we are also conscious of ourselves as humans and as I’s who perceive such things by means of the body. The body, apprehended as body, has its localization-stratum of tactile sensations, but we touch this thing here, we “sense” the touch of clothes, and so on. Hence the ambiguity of “sensing.” The body senses — that concerns the localized; through it we “sense” things — here “sensing” is perceiving spatial things, and it is we who have directed the mental gaze in perceiving at the thing, and this body is our body.

The second paragraph draws a consequence about phenomenological analysis. If we analyze the apprehension-connections phenomenologically, the apprehension-layering described in detail becomes evident. Whether or not it is reflectively recognized correctly, it governs theoretical experience and the problems to be posed on the basis of it, insofar as these are correctly posed and successfully carried out — as in all genuine theories, genuine sciences. And to these belong of course zoology and especially physiology on one hand and psychology on the other, all understood within proper limits. For on both sides, precisely in the sphere of the specifically somatological, there is no lack of great masses of badly posed problems and correspondingly worthless theories (such as the entire complex of problems and theories posed under the title “psychological origin of the representation of space, time, thing” — which is full of absurdity, especially regarding what in it ought to be reckoned to the somatological sphere).

The third paragraph addresses the intimate fusion of the apprehension-layer in which the body’s sensitivities (and thus the body itself) constitute themselves with those layers constitutive of the soul and the psychic I. This fusion is so intimate that the soul-apprehension must necessarily take the sensation-states of the body into itself. Sensations are, from the standpoint of pure consciousness, the indispensable material substrata for all fundamental kinds of noeses. When the consciousness we call thing-experience or body-experience contains in its concrete unity sensations as materials of apprehension (in the *Logical Investigations* Husserl used the misleading expression “representing contents”), and when every consciousness enters into the soul-apprehension and becomes a real state of the soul and of the psychic I in relation to real circumstances — then it is evident that the same sensations that function in the realizing apprehension of material perception as presentive contents for material features also receive, in the new realizing apprehension called bodily experience, localization as sensation-states, thereby letting specific bodily existence appear; and thirdly, under the title of perceptual states of the I (material perceiving and likewise bodily experiencing), they are components of the psychic and thus belong to the soul (namely soul-states) and correspondingly to the life of the I. All this can be seen — brought to clear givenness through pure essential analyses — and whoever has followed these demonstrations has seen it with us.

The fourth paragraph draws the consequence for psychology. It is therefore not accidental but understandable from essential grounds when psychology, understood as the science of the soul, has to do with all sensations as well. The question of how it has to do with them can be drawn only from the sense inherent in “psychological experience,” from the soul-real constituting itself in this new fundamental form of experience. We must interrogate this experience, observe as what the psychic is given when the intention of this type of experience takes effect and finds harmonious fulfillment, and this not factually but essentially. The same holds for the general question of what psychology deals with, what and in what sense belongs to it, and what principles of method the sense of this “what” prescribes.

The fifth paragraph introduces a polemical note. Others may think differently, believing one must go to the psychological institutes and consult the specialists to learn about the essence and method of psychology — just as mathematicians commonly believe only the professional mathematician can give account of the essence, goals, and methods of mathematics, and natural scientists believe the same about natural science. Husserl says he cannot argue with such a person, since that person has not yet understood what philosophy, as opposed to non-philosophical sciences, actually wants and must want. Whoever has understood this knows that methodical technique is not the interest and business of the philosopher but of the dogmatic researcher and of dogmatic science, and that the principial essence, the idea of every science of a categorical type, and the idea of its method, as the “sense” of every science, precedes it and can and must be grounded a priori from the very essence of the idea of its objectivity, which determines its dogma.

The sixth paragraph offers an analogy. To grasp the “essence” of number, to clarify the basic concept of arithmetic and to understand therefrom the principial sources of its method, no theory of integral equations and no reflection on such theories can teach us; we do not even need command of the multiplication table. To clarify, or scientifically determine, the essence of the soul and thereby the possible goals and methods (in principial generality) is not the business of the psychological technician, i.e. the psychologist, but of the philosopher. This holds for all categories of being that correlatively lead back to categorical fundamental forms of giving consciousness. Propositions like those claiming that all scientific method is one and the same, that philosophy must proceed methodically according to the model of exact science (especially mathematics and natural science), that philosophy obviously must rely on the special sciences and further process their results — such propositions have been repeated so often that they have become perfectly trivial. The kernel of truth they contain has not grown larger through repetition, but the damage done by the much greater portion of untruth in these skewed propositions has become immense. It threatens to consume German philosophy.

The seventh paragraph goes further. It is legitimate, Husserl says, for dogmatists not to listen to philosophers if, perfectly certain of their dogmatic progress, they simply want to be specialists and not philosophers. But if they want to be philosophers too and regard philosophy as a kind of continuation of dogmatic science, then they resemble people who imagine that with sufficient progress in physics and chemistry, humanity will eventually be able to cure not only physical but also moral syphilis through means à la Ehrlich-Hata.

The eighth paragraph returns to the question of sensations. The answer is clear: while in somatology, in accordance with its type of experience, sensations are manifestations of the body’s sensitivities, and the task of theoretical thinking in this science is to trace the causal connections belonging to these sensitivities, psychology — following the sense of its experience — has to trace the causal connections belonging to its experiential unity, the soul, and to give sensations the real-causal interest corresponding to their position within the psychic nexus. Full clarity is gained when we enter the general discussion. If the soul is the reality whose states go under the title “consciousness,” then this consciousness is given — whether through self-perception or through interpretive perception — as belonging to a body. The bodily objectivation lies at the ground and in such a way that the body receives the position of a reality that founds the soul.

The ninth paragraph spells out the layered structure. All in all, a human being is given as a reality that includes the material body-thing within itself, that through the stratum of sensation becomes a body, and through the soul-stratum interwoven with the sensation-stratum becomes a full human being. We have an intertwining of three realities, each later one in the series including the earlier within itself by merely contributing a new stratum. Sensation stands as something common at the border, so to speak, of the second and third levels. On the second level it is a manifestation of the body’s sensitivity. On the third level it is the material substratum for perceptive apprehensions — serving in the double apprehension-functions discussed above: as kinesthetic sensation in the function of the motivating, as presentive sensation in the function of the motivated, under given circumstances presenting something of the state-content of the material object (e.g. color, smoothness). All these apprehensions interweave with higher, specifically I-like consciousness. Whether or not an I-gaze passes through them, whether or not the I with its spontaneous acts reigns in them, they are in any case (like the spontaneous acts themselves) not merely occurrences of a pure consciousness. They themselves undergo their own apprehension — precisely the apprehension as psychic states. A human being or animal is not a mere body with whose sensation-states consciousness somehow connects; rather, the human being has a psychic character by virtue of which it takes up the sensations it senses through its embodiment into its consciousness in just this way, apprehends them in just this way, relates to what appears through them in just this way — theoretically experiencing, thinking, valuing, acting — so that the play of its reproductions runs in just such constellations, connects with the course of originary impressions (sensory and non-sensory) in just this way, and so on.

The tenth paragraph draws a comparison between the way sensation functions in somatic experience and in psychological experience. A sharp difference emerges. On both sides sensation is apprehended in fundamentally different ways, and so what comes to givenness differs: on one side, a sensitivity of the body, or a sensation-event as a comportment of the body; on the other side, in the experience of a psychic state, the sensation-event as something manifesting something bodily has no place — this somatic apprehension is not a component of the apprehension of the psychic state, specifically of the state of thing-perception in which the sensation functions as presentive, or of the receptive apprehension of an image-fictum, and so on. This is not altered by the circumstance that the soul-apprehension in general is founded in the bodily apprehension. Both apprehensions interweave through the double function of sensation, a function that is not merely factually double but fundamentally double, and both are interwoven but neither enters into the other. This holds for all sensations, including the sensory feelings founded in primary sensations, which on one side somatologically manifest the body’s feeling-sensitivity, while on the other side they enter into mental functions and bring nothing of somatic apprehensions into them.

The eleventh paragraph treats the consequences for causal research. The question of the how of sensation-research and especially of causal research on sensation is answered very differently for somatology and psychology. Sensation, considered psychically, is merely material for apprehensions (representations in a certain firmly delimited sense), whereby we must not forget the background-apprehensions through which the prominent foreground-apprehension necessarily makes something conscious that has a surrounding, that “stands out” from its surrounding. Psychological causality is causality that relates to the specifically psychic states. From the psychological standpoint, the material is simply there, it stands in function — that is the specifically psychic. A special causal engagement with it falls outside the psychological level. As soon as we ask about the causality of the material, we have changed our attitude and are pursuing somatology. There are no other causal questions to be directed at sensation than the somatological ones.

The twelfth paragraph addresses the sphere of specifically psychic causality. In the sense of the soul-apprehension, or the human-apprehension, the human being is dependent with respect to both somatic and psychic states on the material body-thing, not only because its sensations are so dependent, but also with respect to the specifically psychic. How far this extends is determined, as with every experiential apprehension, by the progress of actual experience, which more closely determines what the form of the apprehension-sense leaves open. Thus the course of sensory reproductions, and in further consequence the course of reproductions in general and the entire mode and rhythm of the apprehension-life and the intellectual and emotional life depending on it, proves to be dependent on the physical organization of the body.

The thirteenth paragraph notes an initial appearance of analogy that quickly breaks down. At first, the reality of the psychic seems to behave quite analogously to the reality of the bodily with respect to the kind of causal dependence. But very soon one sees essential differences. We have as the foundation of the structure of the founded reality we call an animal being the bodily matter, and to it belongs a self-enclosed causal nexus that fits into that of material nature. But material causality does not exhaust the matter. When the physical body assumes a particular material state under particular causal circumstances, a corresponding change in sensation occurs in the somatic sensation-stratum belonging to it as body. This in turn exercises no counter-effect back into the natural stratum. Sensations seem to follow certain material bodily states like a kind of shadow (as epiphenomena). The situation for the soul-stratum would be the same if it, too, like the somatic stratum of sensations, could be viewed as a univocal functional consequence of bodily states. Psychology, or anthropology and zoology, would then be fundamentally somatological sciences of a higher order. All spontaneity — such as the psychic spontaneity manifesting itself in free bodily movement — would be a mere “epiphenomenon,” and what we call willing in free movement would be a pure consequence of certain bodily processes, and the movement itself a process caused purely in the sphere of material causality.

The fourteenth paragraph reports the crucial finding. More precisely examined, however, we found in psychic reality a fundamentally different kind of dependence on body and matter: namely, the principial impossibility of unchanged persistence of the soul and with it the principial impossibility of a return to the same state. Already in this, an absurdity of psychophysical parallelism shows itself. If the soul were dependent on the physical body in the same way as sensibility, then it would have to be principially possible for the psyche of the old person to develop back to the psyche of the child — the identical child with identical states that had become the old person. But this is principially excluded by the very nature of the soul, its necessary developmental character.

The fifteenth paragraph adds a qualification regarding the somatic stratum. The one-sided and uniform dependence that the occurrences of the sense fields have on the materiality of the body does not negate the fact that through the somatic apprehension a new kind of objectivity with a new stratum constitutes itself. The new stratum is not eliminated but rather presupposed in the investigation of physio-somatic causal relations. The causal relations here hold between bodily physis and body — two realities, the one founded in the other — and as with causal relations among realities generally, the occurrence of states of one reality is the causally dependent effect of the corresponding states of the other under the appropriate circumstances. The reference to circumstances here means only material circumstances; the one-sidedness consists precisely in the fact that the founded reality brings no circumstances of its own, has no causalities of its own besides those belonging to the founding. The same would hold for the soul-apprehension even if the soul were merely a higher annex of the body in this way.

The sixteenth paragraph asserts the reality of psycho-physical dependencies running from the psychic into the physically bodily. How far they really reach is a matter of psycho-physiological experiential research. How far they can reach — and thus how far questions about “physiological correlates” and corresponding hypothetical constructions can be meaningful and guiding for actual research — is a matter of phenomenological essential research. It prescribes just as absolutely fixed boundaries for psychophysical investigations as geometry does for geodetic investigations. But more on this when considering the idea of a rational psychology. As far as the investigation has progressed, the idea of a psychology emerges as a science directed at psychic reality, one that is to be distinguished from somatology — both from physical somatology (which belongs to the general science of material nature) and from aesthesiological somatology — and at the same time is interwoven with it in exact correspondence with the founding of the realities.

The seventeenth paragraph discusses the status of psychology as a self-standing science. If the psyche is not a separate reality but only a reality-stratum above the soma, it cannot found a self-standing science. Physical natural science is self-standing, and relatively self-standing within it are disciplines like physical somatology; somatology is self-standing, but somatological aesthesiology is not self-standing; and again self-standing is anthropology (or fully understood zoology). This does not prevent a preeminent research interest from being devoted to the soul and the reality-questions and causality-questions belonging to it. But as with all reality-sciences, the proper object is the relevant kind of reality — the soul, or the human being with respect to his soul — and the soul is not a “bundle” of conscious experiences but the real unity that manifests itself in them. One can pass the soul over in silence, one can contemptuously call it a *façon de parler* — it remains the governing element in the apprehension and the determining element in the investigation. But it is better to speak correctly and not explain away what must always remain alive wherever correct thinking is called for.

The eighteenth paragraph notes the apparent incompleteness of the foregoing discussion insofar as it has not specially taken into account the pure and psychic I, and so has not more closely considered how it determines the task of psychology and the context of causal research. But it is immediately apparent that the investigation of the psychic apprehension-I is only one level of soul-research in general. How the I appears as pure I falls into the psychological sphere insofar as it investigates the arising of acts in the natural context. How the I develops as empirical I, transforms, gains new dispositions — this is only a particularization of the question how the soul develops, transforms, etc. Not everything psychic is specifically I-like. Associations form whether the I is involved or not. Whether and in what way special idiopsychic regularities belong to the I and its acts is a matter of special psychological research; in any case, the psychic I is co-determined by the whole soul-nexus, standing also under rules that reach beyond its narrower sphere and are generally valid for the psychic. We need not dwell longer here.

### §4. The “Communities” in Natural-Scientific Consideration

The first paragraph of §4 summarizes the methodological results. Starting from the material world, into which the body is integrated, and following the sequence of founded experiences, a series of corresponding experiential levels has yielded the original domains. The phenomenological clarification of the apprehensions and of the fundamental kinds of objectivities constituting themselves therein provides radical insight into the distinctive sense of corresponding sciences. The sequence could be extended further without gaining anything especially instructive. When psychic objects combine with one another, joining together in unions, societies of various levels, this yields no new objectivities from the standpoint of foundation through originary nature. No new soul arises as a soul of a higher level built upon the totality of bodies and their souls, no unified consciousness-nexus on the basis of which a new reality — that of a community-soul — would constitute itself.

The second paragraph explains what does occur. What is present from the natural-scientific standpoint is a number of individual human beings, each with a separate consciousness, a separate soul with a separate I. In the psychophysical reciprocal nexus made possible by the material mutual relations of the bodies, acts arise in individual souls that are intentionally directed at the psychically external. But what arises here are always only new states of the individual souls. It is no different from when a plurality of material things has a relatively closed causal nexus, thereby producing material systems that must perhaps be treated as material unities. A principially new kind of science does not arise thereby. A different question is whether we would say the same if the elements of these unitary formations were taken not as souls but as spiritual personalities. But at this point there are for us no spirits. We stand within natural science, defined by the totality of realities that are either themselves material nature or founded in material nature.

## Second Chapter

### The Relations between Psychology and Phenomenology

### §5. The Relation of Phenomenology to the Sciences

The opening paragraph of §5 announces a special interest in the relations between psychology and phenomenology. The entire analyses of this section were themselves phenomenological and could not, even where they connected with actual experience, be misinterpreted as experiential-scientific. Everywhere the singular experiential datum — for example, of some “apprehension,” “perception,” etc. — served only as an exemplar; the investigation always moved immediately into the essential attitude, researching eidetically what belongs to the essence, the possibilities enclosed in the essence of certain apprehensions to pass over into intuitive series and experiential series, to fulfill themselves harmoniously therein, to unfold their sense — i.e., the sense of what is meant in them, of the experienced as such — and thereby the sense of the relevant objectivities. The phenomenological analyses exemplified, on the one hand, in pieces of intuitive essential analysis the method and type of results sought; they served at the same time to draw the essence of the mutually founded reality-categories — matter, body, soul, and psychic I — from their ultimate sources and thereby to grasp the originary sense of the corresponding scientific domains.

The second paragraph adds that through these analyses (to be extended where necessary in the same spirit), all preconditions are fulfilled for determining the principial peculiarities of the methods of these sciences and for bringing to insightful understanding the extent to which, for example, physically-natural-scientific method and psychological method can run parallel, and the extent to which they must be fundamentally different. Norms that arise originarily here cannot be disregarded without confusing the course of science and misleading it into erroneous problem-formulations and experiential approaches. Not what calls itself “modern science” and not those who call themselves “specialists” make the method, but the essence of the objects and the correlative essence of possible experience of objects of the relevant category — that is, the a priori of phenomenological constitution — prescribes everything principial in the method. It characterizes the gifted specialist to grasp this intuitively (even if not to bring it to strict philosophical concepts and formulated norms) and to orient the special problems and methods accordingly. All the discoveries and inventions of specialists move within the framework of an absolutely unsurpassable a priori that one can draw not from their doctrines but only from phenomenological intuition. To grasp it scientifically is a special task of philosophy, not of the dogmatic sciences themselves.

The third paragraph observes that what normatively determines method in general is the theme of the general noetics that extends over all categories of objectivities and constitutive intuitions. We do not yet possess this noetics. It will be possible only after a sufficiently developed general phenomenological essential doctrine of cognition on the sides both of intuition and of specific thinking. But this much is clear without a developed noetics: the method of all sciences must be determined by the kind of originarily giving intuition essentially belonging to the object-category to which the science is related — that is, the fundamental kind of originary apprehension. That all natural cognition has its ultimate source in experience, concretely speaking, that all scientific grounding ultimately rests on acts of experience (the act originarily giving natural objectivity) — this is a commonplace. And if we accept it, as we must, it is clear that methodical norms emanating from experience and obviously grounded in its essence must be determinative for natural-scientific method. The same must hold for all sciences whatsoever; in all of them, grounding ultimately leads beyond the sphere of thinking to intuition and ultimately to originarily giving intuition, which can only fail to be experience when it concerns objectivities other than experiential objectivities (realities of the natural sphere). We have already established that to the different object-categories there must correspond essentially different constitutive apprehensions and hence different fundamental forms of originarily giving acts.

### §6. The Ontological Foundation of the Empirical Sciences

The first paragraph of §6 observes that the method in all sciences is also determined by the general essence of the objectivity, which presents itself intuitively in perfect presentification — that is, in perfect unfolding of the intentions lying in its apprehension — and this naturally in the essential attitude and in the direction not toward the apprehension but toward the constituting objectual element. The general essence, however, can be unfolded in thinking, and its unfolding leads necessarily to an ontology. Perfect method presupposes the systematic development of the ontology — the essential doctrine belonging to the relevant object-category. The total stock of cognitions it provides is an unconditional norm for everything that possible empirical cognition of the fact-sciences related to the categories can ever offer, and at the same time it enters into factual cognition. Every advance in the ontological direction, especially regarding the formulation of ontological basic cognitions or ontological disciplines that bring a not yet ontologically grasped side of the relevant object-category to ontological form, must benefit the empirical science. This has already been discussed and is drawn in here to ground the right, indeed the unconditional necessity, of a rational psychology.

The second paragraph affirms that such a discipline — one of enormous scope — must exist: not one constructed from above out of empty “concepts” (vague word-meanings) like the old metaphysical psychology, but one drawn from pure intuition as an essential doctrine. This first came to consciousness in the investigations on the phenomenology of cognition (in the *Logical Investigations*). It seems to have escaped all earlier researchers of cognition and of consciousness in general, despite all the ancient talk of an a priori of thinking and willing under the titles of logic and ethics. For what was offered and intended under these titles was anything but psychological essential doctrine in the relevant sense. In that work, phenomenology presented itself as a purely immanent description of what is given in inner intuition (loosely called “inner experience” in places) — a description that does not establish empirical facts but, in the attitude of “ideation,” throughout establishes essential connections. This was the basis of the attempted final refutation of psychologism in epistemology in the sixth *Investigation*. Accordingly, in the *Logical Investigations*, phenomenological essential doctrine and rational psychology coincided. That this is incorrect in various respects — that a rational psychology must be grasped as an ontology of a real constituting itself in the experiential nexus and then cannot coincide with the essence of an experiential nexus itself — we can easily see now that we have clarified the idea of reality in general and of soul-reality in particular and have given up the old (also still governing the author of the *Logical Investigations*) mistrust of soul- and I-reality. The remarkable relationship between phenomenology and psychological ontology, which allows the former to be incorporated into the latter, and in a certain way also the latter (like all ontological disciplines) into the former, will occupy us at length, and parallel relationships will be shown for the ontology of spirit.

### §7. Regional Concepts and “Genus”-Concepts

The first paragraph of §7 announces a topic of the greatest importance for the philosopher and phenomenologist: to bring to intuitive and perfect clarity what distinguishes the so-called objective regional concepts, and what the method is by which they can be derived a priori. This derivation is not meant in the sense of a “transcendental deduction” from some postulate or from some thought-system not itself given through intuition (as the system of judgment-forms is in the Kantian deduction of his categories), and yet it follows an apodictically evident “transcendental clue,” which leads us not to deduce but to find and grasp step by step through our own seeing. One must make clear what gives these concepts their unique significance and predestines them to be domain-concepts of ontologies, such that a priori there must be as many ontologies as regional concepts — whether these ontologies turn out to be rich or meager in content, expanding into large sciences or exhausting themselves in small groups of propositions.

The second paragraph extends the insight. It must be seen that all radical classification of sciences, and first of all of experiential sciences, must depend on this concept-formation “region,” and that there must be as many principially separated empirical sciences (or groups of disciplines) as ontologies. Without exhausting the matter, only enough will be said to exclude misunderstanding empiricist objections.

The third paragraph formulates the empiricist objection. Why should the concept “material thing” (which Husserl adduces as a regional one) be something essentially different from the concept “heavenly body”? Naturally it is a very general concept, perhaps in a certain way the most general, spanning whole groups of disciplines. But concepts arise from experience through generalization; it must remain open that we find grounds in experience for further generalization, and then the more general concept would play the same role as the thing-concept. And now even more so for the concept “animal” (another example of a regional concept): it arises no differently from “frog” or “reptile,” only it is more general. A further generalization leads from it to “living being,” and perhaps it goes yet further. All concepts stem from experience, general ones and special ones alike, and their usefulness must prove itself in the further progress of experience. We must always be ready to alter them accordingly.

The fourth paragraph provides the decisive rejoinder. Whatever the much-discussed, equivocal “stemming from experience” may mean — whether we have acquired the disposition to use general words in identical meaning in sleep or by miracle — word-meanings as logical essences can be valid only if, according to ideal possibility, the “logical thinking” that actualizes them in itself is adaptable to a “corresponding intuiting”; that is, if there is a corresponding essence, graspable through intuition as a corresponding noema, that finds its faithful “expression” in the logical concept. The logical essence constituting itself in pure thought and the intuitive noema stand in the definite essential relation of “adequate expression.” If this is the case, the concept is valid in the sense of the “possibility” of a corresponding object. The essential intuition can proceed on the ground of a singular imagination. It suffices to grasp the general essence, provided that it is far-reaching enough to truly bring the corresponding intuitive noematic essence to givenness, leaving no component of the thought-concept without a counterpart as a pure expression of a component of the intuitively given noema.

The fifth paragraph distinguishes validity of possibility from validity of being. A concept has validity of being only when not imagination but actual “experience” — that is, originarily giving and indisputably giving intuition — posits individual actuality as actuality that is meant in the noematic essence; or when, through “indirect grounding,” the positing of such actuality is rationally motivated on the basis of other experiences. Where concepts relate to the real, the verifying intuition and experience leaves principially much open; it leaves room for closer and different determinations. The intuitive noematic essences are correspondingly laden with indeterminacies, and in parallel, the logical essences and thought-concepts themselves.

The sixth paragraph develops this for the ongoing progress of knowledge. Given the endless possibilities, one must get to know the real object ever better, in the progress of experience determining more closely what remains open, drawing in ever new concepts that combine with the initial expressing concepts into more complete thought-expressions. But since real actuality is not a chaos but a regionally ordered whole, it does not require truly infinite numbers of concepts to get to know the thing. It turns out that from some real determinations infinitely many others follow according to recognizable rules, that there is classification according to which genus- and species-concepts can be formed, which group together limited groups of characteristic conceptual features to which countless others attach experientially and from which countless others are excluded. With a systematics of objects under these genera and species, a real division of all individuals of a most general, sufficiently separated domain of being is accomplished. Concepts of such capacity are evidently not to be drawn from noematic intuition alone; they all carry, besides their essential significance, an existential significance — or more correctly, alongside their pure meaning (their sense free from all assertoric positing) they carry a knowledge, a thesis bearing upon complexes of already scientifically fixed claims about real actuality, a precipitate of already won cognitive results regarding actual existence.

The seventh paragraph makes a general observation about all sciences. It holds for all sciences (including ideal sciences) that the formation of concepts — indeed already of “possible” concepts, drawn from clarity, concretely fixed by adaptation to intuition — serves the winning of true judgments, and that they also end by loading concepts with judgment-values, whereby the concepts themselves become judicative for the object-sphere of the science. Loaded with such judgment-values, the concepts subsequently enter all further combinations. This gives the concept of a concept a dangerous ambiguity. One must sharply distinguish the pure sense, free from all positing, from the sense laden with judgment-theses of the relevant expressions. It is clear that valuable judicative concepts, such as every reality-researcher seeks, can only be drawn from actual experience. So when the empiricist says all concepts stem from experience, he obviously has in mind from the outset the judicative concepts that constantly occupy him. Understandably he tends to evaluate concept-formations that move on the ground of mere imagination as “spinning out empty possibilities,” as “scholasticism.”

The eighth paragraph concedes the empiricist is right regarding judicative concepts but not in every way, and not even entirely with respect to those concepts themselves. They possess a pure essence fixable prior to all judicative content, which may range itself among essential connections that may harbor valuable cognition regarding the possibility of corresponding objects. And it is self-evident that these noematic essences constitute the sense belonging to the objectivity that is intuited or thought, and that every pure essential truth grounding in these essences prescribes an unconditionally valid norm for possible objectivities of such sense.

The ninth paragraph returns to noematic essences. Going back to these (whose mere thought-expression the consistent concepts form), they possess, purely as essences, their particularizations and connections, especially their subordination under more general essences and ultimately under highest genera that are absolutely closed in themselves, absolutely sharply delimited. All the distinctions of genus and species to be made here in pure intuition deliver something fundamentally different from the genera and species of the empirical reality-sciences, which gain their sense not through mere essences but through a judicative cognitive stock of experience.

The tenth paragraph focuses on the highest essential universalities of concern — such as “thing,” “animal being” — that is, basic concepts according to which the fundamental kinds of realities are separated, and ultimately a still higher essential universality such as the concept of reality itself, as fixed by essential definition (drawn purely from intuition). Starting from any given reality in actual experience — say a piece of gold — it is apprehended in a determinate sense, part of which certifies it as really given. Going over to pure sense by performing the eidetic attitude, we abstract from the existential positing of actual experience. The sense is only partially determinate; it is necessarily indeterminate insofar as it is the sense of a real, which as such would display ever new sides and properties in infinite and manifold experiential series — sides not prescribed by the initially fixed sense in fixed content but held open only as indeterminate yet determinable possibilities.

The eleventh paragraph undertakes the thought experiment of free variation. By suspending the demands of experience, we are free from all the fetters that physics and chemistry might impose. We move in free arbitrariness in the realm of “empty possibilities.” Holding fast to the identity of sense insofar as the presented objectivity should be able to appear as identical and internally consistent in every variation-series we carry out, we let the thing move freely in phantasy, deform its shape, change its qualitative and real properties at will, play ball with the known physical properties and property-laws, let property-changes run so that the laws reshape themselves, we even fancy new senses or new quantities for the old senses. In this way free phantasy produces the most unbelievable monstrosities of things, the maddest spectral things, mocking all physics and chemistry.

The twelfth paragraph draws the key insight from this variation. The totality of arbitrary productions that we win from one thing is identically the same as those obtainable from any other; everything is continuously transformable into everything, the totality of configurations is one and the same and fixed. And yet we see that even in this phantasy, averse to all natural-law binding, the system of products retains its rules that justify speaking of a closed system: they are products of a thing-shaping, thing-constituting, thing-property-executing, and again thing-constitution-destroying, phantasy. The thing serving as our starting point changes, remains for a while something appearing as a thing; and if we go too freely, disrespecting the essential relation of real properties to real circumstances, the thing disintegrates into manifolds of phantoms (sensory schemata), running in ways that manifolds constituting real things cannot and may not run. A thing is not a being in general but something identical in the nexus of causal dependencies. It is something that can only live in the atmosphere of causal lawfulness. This demands determinately regulated orders for the constituting sensory schemata. If freely roaming phantasy breaches these orders without restraint, not only is a single schema turned into a “mere phantom,” but the whole world becomes a course of mere phantoms — it is no longer a nature. But it is not for that reason wholly lawless. With unique genius Kant foresaw this, expressed in his distinction between transcendental aesthetics and analytics. For the mere phantom-world, the pure doctrine of time and pure geometry still hold; but it is a world without any physics.

The thirteenth paragraph begins the return from the phantom-world. Let us restrain our phantasy. Beginning again with a thing-experience — say the perception of a tree — we take the thing exactly as appearing in this perception, excluding all indirect knowledge, especially anything from physics and chemistry. A determinate objectual sense is thereby fixed that can be described. A tree appears, a fir tree, etc. The appearing, exactly in the given sense, appears truly only from certain sides and yet is meant as something more than what “truly” appears, but indeterminately. This indeterminacy points us into actual and further possible perceptions; on its basis we can ask how this object looks from other sides, how it determines itself through ever new perceptions. Every new experience raises new questions. However unknown the thing may be, one thing is a priori clear: a fixed framework is prescribed in advance for the course of possible experience, and indeed in advance by the sense of the initial perception. With it, not only an object in general but a thing-real — a substrate of (even if unknown) real properties, related to (however indeterminate) real circumstances — is posited.

The fourteenth paragraph explores free phantasy under the constraint of holding fast to the initial perception’s right. Freely we fancy a continuation of experiences that would harmoniously verify the perceived on all sides; the fixed perceptual sense then forces us to co-imagine real circumstances fitting as causal correlates of the manifesting properties. Following out these surrounding realities and shaping them further according to the once-adopted approaches, an entire world constructs itself — a world with its physical laws that need not at all be the same world we would have come to know through actual experience and experiential science. For in our phantasy-procedure, though bound by the first approach, we can take innumerable paths; each path binds us anew but leaves us infinitely many possibilities for further experiential steps. Depending on the mode of our imaginary determining, we can construct quite different worlds, all being worlds for the initial thing, each having its own and different lawful constitution, its different natural science, and in each the initial thing would be differently equipped — of a different nature.

The fifteenth paragraph draws the philosophical conclusion. Phantasy can still act freely enough — it can no longer posture as world-destroyer but only as world-builder, while infinitely many possibilities remain. But it is so bound by the mere presupposition that the initial perception should hold, that it should be harmoniously maintained as perception of its object as it posits it, for all its remaining indeterminacy, as an extensive real. As soon as we drop this presupposition and demand merely a maintaining unity (which even the phantom provides), reality falls apart and everything dissolves into a chaos of phantoms that, if we exhaust all possibilities, would among other things harbor the regulated phantom-connections in which all possible worlds and realities constitute themselves. But ultimately the idea of the phantom also contained a rule spanning the circle of possibilities. Accordingly, an a priori is truly inscribed in the course of all possible experience, obviously prescribed by the essence of thingly perception as a fundamental kind of perception or experience. With this, the idea of the thing has a unique distinction: it designates a categorial (or better, regional) framework for every sense belonging to and possible for an experience of such a fundamental kind — a framework to which, as to a necessary form, all closer determination of an indeterminately posited object is a priori bound.

The sixteenth paragraph draws the contrast between the regional concept and ordinary general concepts. The idea of the thing stands quite differently from the idea of any other general on the basis of experience. The idea of a mineral or a plant also prescribes a rule for the course of experience — but in a quite different sense. One must not confuse what a general concept prescribes and what the essence of the general perception as a fundamental kind of experience prescribes. The concept, more precisely the conceptual apprehension “as mineral,” prescribes in the manner of thinking. If the apprehension is to hold, it must verify itself, and as a conceptual apprehension whose sense includes being a conceptual apprehension of a thing, it must verify itself in experience. But the demands of experience precede those of conceptual thinking. If it is experienceable at all, it has its form: it is a thing. A conceptual apprehension and reality-positing as mineral can be false; experience can invalidate this or that moment belonging to the concept “stone.” But one thing it can never invalidate, as long as an experienceable object holds good at all: precisely what belongs to the object as object of such a regional experiential kind — the thingly.

The seventeenth paragraph summarizes the distinctive position of the thing-concept. We understand why a concept of the kind “extensive thing” must claim a distinguished position over against arbitrary other concepts. The thing is not a genus-concept of the same kind as mineral, equally tangential with it and similar genus-concepts, merely more general. As long as we ascend in species- and genus-formation and form genuine genera, we rise from the full material essence of the object to general essential features that could be common to several, unboundedly many objects. In this manner we win from the essence of a particular tone the essence “tone in general,” “acoustic in general,” “sensory quality in general.” But all their material content is in the sphere of realities something “contingent,” bound to a “necessary,” a necessary form — precisely that expressed by the concept “thing.” All material content can change and does change in thingly alteration; only one thing cannot change: the general thing-form. A heavenly body can change, its stock of material properties can variably shift, it eventually ceases to correspond to the idea “heavenly body,” and other genus-concepts replace it; but however it changes, thing remains thing, and even distribution or fragmentation changes nothing, since its very possibility is prescribed in the general form “thing.”

The eighteenth paragraph introduces the distinction between priority and posteriority in the world of essences. In the world of essences and essential concepts, a distinction between priority and posteriority is prefigured that justifies speaking, in a certain sense, of a priori and a posteriori concepts. This sense of a priori belongs to the concepts of realities and is a “transcendental” distinction, insofar as it and its difference from the a posteriori have their source in the fundamental property of realities: to “constitute” themselves as unities of manifolds.

The nineteenth paragraph states that the whole consideration can be understood as exemplary. What we brought to fullest insight for the idea of the thing as res extensa comes to the same insight in all analogous cases. In the essence of originarily giving consciousness in general, cardinal divisions according to fundamental kinds are grounded, and to seek them out systematically and describe them scientifically is one of the foremost tasks of phenomenology. To each such fundamental kind there evidently corresponds a regional concept circumscribing the sense-form of the respective fundamental kind of giving intuition, and correspondingly an object-region spanning all objects to which this sense is attributed. With respect to the fact that essential kinds of founding are also laid out in the essence of these originarily giving acts and that with them new fundamental kinds of giving intuition arise — kinds that are founded in the old — orders of lowest regional concepts and of concepts founded in them emerge, and corresponding foundational orders of object-regions (e.g. material thing, aesthesiological thing, human being or soul).

The twentieth paragraph identifies the a priori in the sense of region as the source-point of ontologies, whose necessity, distinguished position in the system of all sciences, and unique methodological function for the execution of factual sciences in the corresponding regional spheres now become truly understandable from the deepest, the primordial grounds of phenomenology. It is wholly clear that an essential science belonging to the regional a priori — e.g. thing in general, soul in general — must have a regionally different position and significance from all other essential cognitions that attach to “contingent” particularizations of the idea of thinghood, soul, etc. The series of experiential sciences of real actuality (as factual sciences) is thus confronted in a special way by the ontology of physical nature (as essential doctrine of natura formaliter spectata), and equally the ontology of animal or psychic nature.

The twenty-first paragraph affirms the self-evidence of these ontologies. No essence without essential truths; and that real essences — to whose form as reality manifold and (varying according to the type of reality) quite diverse interweaving already belongs — cannot be without a rich stock of essential cognitions is self-evident. With respect to the ontology of nature, we have sciences that subordinate themselves to it under the titles geometry and phoronomy, and likewise the a priori truths of pure time-doctrine, which are common property of all reality-sciences. What is still lacking, what has come to development in no tolerably systematic-scientific form, is the ontological sphere of specific materiality — precisely the core-piece of every “pure” regional natural science.

### §8. Rational Psychology and Phenomenology — Experimental Psychology

The first paragraph of §8 turns to the psychological sphere. That there must be a rational psychology, whether we have it or not, is evident. The rational existence of the science as idea precedes its possession. The necessity of a rational geometry was just as visible before its development as, for us who do not possess rational psychology, the necessity of this discipline itself. Yet this is not quite right anymore. Although a systematic unfolding of the idea of soul-reality is lacking, we already possess — in the form of phenomenology — a considerable piece of a rational psychology. Here we return to the main interest guiding our investigation.

The second paragraph considers what a rational psychology must contain. Setting aside all the essential truths belonging to the general idea of reality as such (which form a closed stock including the rational chronological truths and are common property of all rational sciences belonging to the reality-region), it is certain in advance that all phenomenological truths belong to rational psychology — first and foremost all those relating to the real (reell) essential moments of possible experiences, and then further all the immediately insightful cognitions belonging to the different levels of intentional correlates.

The third paragraph explains the need for closer consideration, given the near-universal misunderstanding of phenomenology caused by the prevailing naturalism among psychologists and natural scientists. Connected with this is the fundamentally mistaken view that phenomenology involves a restoration of the method of inner observation or of direct inner experience in general. Only this explains the superficial (indeed, not even superficial, since they miss the very sense of the matters) literary dismissals of the claim that phenomenology, by its very nature, must raise and must make: to be inaugurating a reform of psychology (as also of philosophy) that is fundamental and principially new in the literal sense.

The fourth paragraph outlines the content of the ontology. An ontology of psychic or ensouled realities (or, if we have no fear of traditional antipathies: rational zoology and anthropology) has to do first of all with the a priori of the distinctive real unity-type of real properties belonging to the idea of the animal real as such, and enclosed therein also to the idea of the soul. Talk of real properties leads to real states. These involve dispositional characteristics, capacities. Capacities are capacities for something, dispositions are dispositions for something. And for what — that is clear. We arrive at corresponding groups of “experiences” in the nexus of “soul”-life, and we know that no group of experiences falls outside. Every experience is a psychic state, as psychology generally speaks of psychic states in relation to the entire sphere of experiences.

The fifth paragraph introduces the phenomenological reduction. We know that within the framework of the phenomenological reduction there is a research — free from all co-positing of real existence, from all positing of factuality — into what belongs to every kind of “consciousness,” to everything called “psychic experience,” in terms of real (reell) moments, consciousness-correlates, all connections possible within the experiential nexus: an a priori or essential research. We deal with states. If we take the soul not as fact but as eidetic essence, the states too are taken as eidetic essences and have the eidetic form of states. We can have an interest in abstracting from this, in restricting our essential analysis to experiences in themselves, apart from what characterizes them as states, as manifestations of a real soul-unity with soul-properties. But everything we would research in the framework of such abstraction would naturally also belong within the framework of rational-psychological research. For it is evident that the very essence of every experience (as idea and not as fact) does not change and must not change through the realizing apprehension.

The sixth paragraph offers a recapitulation for the benefit of the natural-scientifically oriented psychologist. In factual experience, say, thing-perceptions are given. In inner perception, in reflection in Locke’s sense, they are originarily given; in “empathies” in the manner of “seeing” such experiences on the external habitus of another, or in memories of previously performed reflections or empathies, they are not originarily given but in the manner of presentifications. In any case: they are given as our experiences or as experiences of other human beings or animals — bodily-spiritual realities that have their real surroundings and belong to the one spatio-temporal world. The investigation of such psychic states — called perceptions — as states of actual real individuals of the actual world is the business of psychology, the inductive experiential science. How such experiences occur in humans and animals, under what real conditions and with what consequences, according to what general or special laws of nature — this can and must be ascertained by the methods of experiential sciences, through observation and experiment.

The seventh paragraph introduces the eidetic reduction. Perceptions can be researched not only as actually existing states within factual consciousness-unities belonging to factual psychophysical individuals in the factual world, but we can also perform an “eidetic reduction” — exclude all questions of real existence and carry out purely eidetic research. We then occupy ourselves with the eidos, the essence “perception,” and with what belongs to a “perception as such” — the eternally identical sense of possible perception in general. And likewise for every essential type of perceptions. We distinguish the “possible” perceptions in general according to fundamental types, ask for each what essentially belongs to it and what it necessarily demands according to its essence, what transformations and connections it makes possible purely through its essence — whether with phenomena of the same type or of other types. The same problems arise for memories, phantasies, expectations, dark representations, thinking-experiences of every kind, feeling-experiences, willing-experiences.

The eighth paragraph develops the transition to eidetic investigation. Like all being, not only actually experienced or factually experienceable but experienceable in principle (i.e. every objectivity of principially possible experience), they offer us their essence. Individual intuition turns — as is itself an essential possibility — into essential intuition, into the attitude of grasping in pure essential concepts and expressing in them the essential states of affairs on the basis of intuition. The essential doctrine of experiences must take experiences with the full content in which they present themselves in eidetic intuition. As eidetic, it rests (directed as it is at essences of psychic states) on psychological intuition — whether psychological experience or psychological fiction. The entire content of an experience standing in the gaze of reflective inner experience, as much as this gaze can grasp, or the entire content of what is given in the gaze of reflection in inner phantasy, passes in the essential attitude into the essence. It loses only the relation to the fact of experienced natural actuality; the existing transforms into an essentially-being, the individually unique into a “universal.” From the lowest essences, standing in the eidetic gaze as mere this-here and not graspable in strict concepts in the full richness of content, the consideration passes to higher universalities, to the sharp species-distinctions like perception and memory, perception and phantasy, thinking and willing, etc.

The ninth paragraph describes the tasks of analysis. Distinctions are to be made in concrete experiences through analysis, species-typical components are to be lifted out, fundamental kinds of non-self-sufficient moments are to be distinguished, and every reasonable goal of a systematic experience-analysis is to be pursued with a readily appearing multitude of particular, perfectly determinate, and obviously solvable tasks. Since to the essence of the experiences called “intentional” — which in an assortment of configurations will dominate the interest — the “relation to something” and manifold correlates inseparably belong, these too cannot be passed over and left unresearched. The mighty themes of noesis and noema are necessarily to be treated together from psychological interest and within the psychological-eidetic apprehension. What the psychologist is presented with, in his psychological intuition, is precisely the psychic, soul-real — the respective experiences as psychic states. In this realizing apprehension they enter with everything they demand, hence with the relation to space and time, into the eidos. All experiential thesis falls away, as does the actuality-thesis of all nature, as in all essential intuition. The pure essence contains no thesis other than an essential thesis.

The tenth paragraph draws the parallel to geometry. The experienced is exactly the same as what the geometer has: the geometer turns ordinary empirical intuition of spatial things into the eidetic — whether figures on the blackboard or models from the model-cabinet. His interest concerns the spatial shape — not the experienced or quasi-experienced shape in phantasy, but the “pure” spatial shape, the shape-essence to be grasped in the essential attitude on the ground of empirical apprehensions. Insofar as shape is a fundamentally essential moment of the material thing as res extensa, the geometer, as essential researcher of possible thing-shapes, is eo ipso also a rational physicist. Exactly so in the case of psychology. And exactly so everywhere a region of realities separates itself originally and necessarily provides the foundation for two kinds of sciences: experiential sciences and eidetic sciences.

The eleventh paragraph notes the relationship between experiential and eidetic science. The experiential science traces existence, the eidetic traces essence — and the same essence that constitutes the “content” of the existing and of possible existing in general. Everywhere the eidetic science precedes the experiential science. What the region and the essential content of the intuitively graspable reality-configurations (and hence also configurations of possible states and circumstances) prescribe a priori cannot be dispensed with by the experiential science. Essential truths hold, in unconditional universality and necessity, for everything possible and for everything that proves itself actual in actual experience. Theoretical experiential research does not presuppose the eidetic; the theoretical interest can be directed at the experienced, can observe general regularities in experiencing, establish and order them. There was surveying before geometry, astronomy before mathematical mechanics. There was also a developed psychology before eidetic psychology; there was and there still is, in the form of modern experimental psychology.

The twelfth paragraph highlights the significance of eidetic science for empirical science. If the totality of eidetic truths belonging to a regional reality-sphere is large enough to constitute the field of its own great science, then the constitution of this science must signify a decisive advance for the corresponding experiential science. A science — that means an infinity of systematically connected truths discoverable in systematic unity, truths that do not lie on the surface but are discovered only as fruits of laborious research. To recognize a rational discipline as possible and to set it in methodical motion — one that establishes or puts into sure prospect an infinity of truths for one of the great regions of experience, truths holding in unconditional necessity for everything experienceable in this region — that means raising the corresponding regional experiential science to a new level. In this way physical natural science was raised to a new level in the 17th century through the recognition that the long-established geometry was a fundamental piece of a mathesis of nature spanning not only thing-shape but the whole material thing, and that its construction must be methodically foundational for a new, incomparably more capable natural science illuminated by the light of rational mathematics. The belief that experience and induction (which had been practiced long before Galileo and Kepler) made modern exact science means not understanding the sense and history of this science.

The thirteenth paragraph applies this to psychology. For anyone who has appropriated even a piece of real phenomenology in its sense and has grasped with understanding some connected groups of definite problems (such as those touched on in the present treatise), it must be clear that phenomenology — or the essential doctrine of experiences to be incorporated into the idea of a rational psychology — opens an infinite field of truths that, as bearing a priori on psychic states, infinitely enrich psychological cognition, and in a similar sense as mechanical, phoronomic, and generally mathematical cognition a priori enriches empirical natural science. Whoever believes that what is achieved in phenomenology is already achieved by psychology through inner experience speaks exactly as wisely as whoever thinks that what geometry achieves for the physicist is already achieved by physical observation and experiment. To be sure, the situation, despite a general analogy, is not the same, and so the simile must not be pressed. Rational psychology is not a mathematics, and specifically phenomenology of experiences is not a mathematics of experiences. Common to both is that they are essential doctrines attached to the regional a priori. But not every essential doctrine has the type of a mathematics. Rather, that is a quite determinate scientific-theoretical type whose form is to be systematically exhibited — a task for a different ontology, the formal mathesis universalis (united with formal logic).

The fourteenth paragraph explains the difference in theoretical type. Which scientific-theoretical form (theory-form, as Husserl said in the *Logical Investigations*) a regional eidetics has depends on the regional a priori. If the idea of space — a certain three-dimensional “Euclidean manifold” — belongs to such an a priori, this determines a mathematics. But the experiential stream offers in its essence nothing like a space, no ordering-system of coexistence analogous to space; it is not a field of a mathematics. With this fundamentally different scientific-theoretical type much is connected. First, the number of immediately insightful essential truths of geometry and of all natural-ontological disciplines is very small (provided we orient ourselves after physics and not after the so-called descriptive natural sciences). The great mathematical sciences that supply nature-science with the a priori of its sphere of being arise in pure deduction from a few axiomatic foundations. Quite otherwise in rational phenomenology. The field of immediate insights is an endless one, and indirect derivation plays a role essentially only by way of other sciences and their psychological significance.

The fifteenth paragraph discusses why phenomenology is initially difficult to recognize as a new discipline. With the kind of essential insights demanded here, the beginner is at first inclined to let descriptive psychology and phenomenology blend indistinguishably. Husserl provisionally does not separate essential doctrine of consciousness-states from transcendental phenomenology (as he himself had not yet separated them in the *Logical Investigations*). Phenomenology would immediately be acknowledged as an essentially new discipline and would command at least respectful attention if it built towers of formulas upon one another, deduced in mathematical fashion, and impressed with the apparatus of indirect methods, tables, drawings, instruments, and experimental arrangements. Since it draws purely from intuition, what is new or especially scientific about it? The psychologists say they practice intuition themselves. If the phenomenologist, instead of entering the higher problem-levels of experimental psychology, delves into inner experiential analysis — well, then he performs quite useful preliminary work that fits perfectly into “modern psychology.”

The sixteenth paragraph rebuts these objections. Those who speak this way have simply not taken the trouble to study carefully what lies before them and have not heeded the famous Chwolson eleventh commandment, which one can also formulate: never write a critique before you have understood the criticized in its plain sense. It is not a pleasant impression to observe the critic denying where the author has asserted nothing, and an even less pleasant one when he kindly agrees where the author has likewise asserted nothing. In every reality-sphere there is description, and description establishes existence, real existence. Every natural description, as carried out in the descriptive natural sciences, is of this kind. One describes: the lion is yellow. We can imagine blue-green lions, lions of all colors — but experience shows no such lions, so they appear in no natural history. Of course the experiential science we call psychology also describes objects falling into its sphere — types of characters, dispositions, etc. Not fictitious but experienced ones: they occur. This is how the actual human being of the actual world is. Fictitious character-types, dispositions, etc. are precisely fictitious.

The seventeenth paragraph contrasts this with eidetic phenomenology. In its domain there is principially no description in this sense at all. In its domain, principially, all real existence, the entire spatio-temporal actuality, is excluded. Its determinations concern not realities but essences; its truths say what holds for such essences, or for everything falling under such essences as such, in unconditional necessity and universality. They speak of experiences exactly as geometrical truths speak of bodies, arithmetical truths of numbers, phoronomic truths of movements: just as these do not speak of the earth or even of the factual universe and the bodies, movements, numbers occurring in it, so phenomenology does not speak of psychic beings and psychic states in this universe or in any sphere of actual existence. When the phenomenologist says “there are” experiences, psychic states like perceptions, memories, etc., his “there are” means exactly as much as the mathematical “there are” — e.g. there is a series of cardinal numbers, there are relative primes, there is no regular decahedron. Founded in both cases not by experience but by essential intuition.

The eighteenth paragraph marks the fundamental distinction between essential truths and experiential truths. Experience is a title for existence-disclosing, originarily grasping acts. What essential intuition brings to originary grasping are not individual items of existence but essences of lower or higher universality; corresponding individuals need not exist, and if they are to exist, only actual experience can establish it. Essential truths are absolutely binding, unsurpassable, unconfirmable and unrefutable by experience. Experiential truths, assertions that in their sense posit existence and thus require existence-experience in their grounding, are contingent truths that, as grounded by experience, can be modified and overturned by experience: they hold only presumptively, subject to further experiential confirmation. Even if the factually bound description of one’s own and others’ psychic states could suffice for the psychologist in some narrow domain, it would still be a tremendous expansion of cognition that the factual in its descriptive content has not only factual but essential-necessary validity. And so it means very much that we see it is not only physical nature but, in far greater scope, psychic nature — and in particular the stream of psychic states of animal subjects — that is bound to a priori laws; all factual cognition, all factual laws are regulations standing out from a mighty background of a priori and absolutely necessary essential laws, binding only what the stock of essential laws has left open in the individual-real existence.

The nineteenth paragraph offers a historical observation in support. This is indubitable truth for whoever truly gives himself to the sense of the present pieces of actual phenomenological research. To find one’s way into the wholly unaccustomed kind of eidetic seeing and researching is admittedly not easy, and whoever is as completely satisfied with the art and method of contemporary experimental psychology as most representatives of this discipline seem to be may naturally be inclined to act like the Abbé Galiani, who famously refused to look through a telescope: he too was perfectly certain of his astronomy and perfectly satisfied with its kind. The future will probably teach here too that evident givennesses cannot be done away with by not looking at them, and someday psychologists will consider the “instrument” of phenomenological essential doctrine no less important — indeed at first probably much more important — than the mechanical instruments. The simile of the instrument must not be pressed. The phenomenological method enters as little into competition with the experimental-psychological as mathematical methods in physics do with the experimental-physical. Experimental psychology should not be abandoned but rather, through phenomenological foundation, made incomparably more productive — through it, transformed in the genuine sense into an exact, rationally explanatory science.

The twentieth paragraph adds a qualification regarding the limits of experiment. The recognition of phenomenology will also contribute to correcting the overestimation of the possible achievements of experiment and to bringing insight that, for essential reasons, the situation for psychology is not fully analogous to that in physical natural science, and that a correspondingly incomparably greater significance of intuitive essential cognition belongs to the former. And this will hold especially for that quite different psychology, not yet appreciated, whose quite differently founded kind has come to the attention of only individual researchers: psychology as a human science (Geisteswissenschaft). Whoever says such things today is very unwelcome and exposes himself to vehement repudiations. One speaks of “attack” against experimental psychology, one acts as if such statements belittled this science and its researchers. Husserl adds a defense: he has never uttered a word contrary to the self-evident respect to which the new experimental psychology is entitled through the serious and, within certain limits, very fruitful work and the significance of its representatives. A principial critique going to the roots of psychological method is not contrary to this. Were the great surgeons like Volkmann or Billroth any less great because surgery and the whole of medical therapeutics are founded by natural-scientific theory only in a small measure?

The twenty-first paragraph addresses the charge of demeaning psychology. Husserl has also said, and thereby touches sensibilities he had to touch for the sake of the plight of contemporary academic philosophy, that the mixing of natural-scientific psychology and philosophy has given rise to a philosophy that is not only false but shallow — one that considers itself philosophy and yet does not even see a single principial philosophical problem. Every real philosopher of our time knows this situation. The older representatives of psychology, who through their education still had an inner contact with philosophical problems and also participated in real philosophical work, underestimate the danger. The younger generation of psychologists, crushed by the enormously swollen experimental-psychological literature and the difficulties of constructing and mastering the technical apparatus, necessarily lacks this contact. It falls into an unclear and superficial naturalism and proves its qualification for philosophical professorships by literary productions that sin against the idea of a scientific philosophy. Husserl takes back not a word of this judgment, but it does not hit psychology or psychologists as such, only the psychologists who take philosophy too lightly.

The twenty-second paragraph rebuts the inference that phenomenology supports gaining phenomenological results from experimental psychology. The critical refutation of the idea (arising only from misunderstanding phenomenology as an empirical analysis of one’s own psychic states) that one could win phenomenological results from natural-scientific-experimental psychology does not at all imply that experimental arrangements cannot gain a good phenomenological function. Speaking of experimental psychology, Husserl sharply emphasized its nature as experiential science. The word “experiment” should have the same sense as in “experimental physics.” But the exclusion of experience for cognitive grounding does not mean excluding experience as a basis for the intuitive conception of essence. The natural scientist needs experience because he seeks factual truths; the essential researcher does not need experience because he seeks essential truths: it grounds none of his truths. But he needs intuition, clear apprehensions of individual moments of the essence to be intuited; he operates with exemplary intuitions. In principle, imaginative phantasies can serve him as well as perceptions, and it lies in the nature of things that in incomparably the widest scope his essential thinking is guided by phantasy. Only phantasy gives him — as it does every essential researcher — the ability, in the freedom of configuration, to traverse freely and on all sides the infinite manifolds of possibilities, here of experiential possibilities. On the other hand, phantasy has well-known disadvantages: it does not hold up, it quickly loses its fullness, it sinks into the half-clear and dark. Naturally the phenomenologist will draw, wherever he can, from the original source of clarity, from fully living “impression.” And why then should not instrumental aids and experimental arrangements of the same kind that normally serve experientia also help to gain the best kind of intuitive material? This is not everywhere possible in the freest scope, but it is so to a great extent, especially in the field of sensibility — not only sensation-sensibility but also sensory intuitions and valuations and volitions. And of course beyond that as well. In this sense every phenomenologist constantly experiments — naturally without useless protocols, fixation of all experimental conditions, description of all arrangements, etc. For the experiment is supposed to supply not experience of the real but merely an exemplary intuition as a basis for essential intuition. The excellent instrumental means of the psychological experiment can also function usefully in this regard, in a completely altered method corresponding to the altered aim.

### §9. The Significance of Phenomenological Description for the Domain of Experience

The first paragraph of §9 claims that the assertion that psychology must reach a new level through phenomenological investigation of consciousness and the entire sphere of experiences with their essential correlates is not a priori constructed but arises from the recognition of the enormous difficulties of phenomenological analysis of experiences — in their experiential moments and strata, in the interweaving of their connections, their modes of flow, their I-relations and noematic relations — combined with the self-evident insight that all this enters into the empirical apprehension as psychological states and must be known if psychology is to have the ability to state in strict concepts what the experiential stream is as the continuously changing total state of the soul and what the psychic fact within such a stream actually is, whose causal-real connections it wants to bring to cognition. Whoever is foreign to phenomenology has not the remotest idea of the difficulties, entanglements, and manifold essential necessities that prevail here.

The second paragraph observes that the true method is not to bind oneself to individually given experiences in actual experience for description, but to carry out the entire work of essential distinction and conceptual essential grasping in the pure essential connection, in rational phenomenology rather than in empirical psychology, and then to carry out in the experiential science the mere application of the phenomenological results. Naturally, with the essential description goes at the same time the recognition of rational essential connections, which likewise must find their constant and necessary application in the experiential-scientific sphere, that of psychology in the ordinary sense.

The third paragraph summarizes. Phenomenology is therefore nothing less than mere descriptive psychology, and it is not even empirically descriptive essential doctrine — i.e. research of experience-essences in the nexus not of experience-given experiential actualities but of ideal experiential possibilities: however much this alone is already something totally different from what has always been understood as descriptive psychology. Essential description in itself does not yet yield an essential law. But essential description (namely, grasping of essences in intuition and conceptual fixation of them) is immediately followed by the cognition of essential connections, and in each already lies a hidden a priori lawfulness. That the experiential stream is not a mere fact but a particularization of an idea to which an abundance of a priori connections belong, forming the fixed framework of all empirical possibilities — this is what the psychologist must make clear to himself.

### §10. The Relation of Phenomenology to the Writings of Bolzano, Lotze, and Brentano

The first paragraph of §10 addresses a confusion. The misunderstanding of the essence of phenomenology is connected with the recent tendency to designate Lotze and Bolzano as founders of phenomenology — with reference to the impulses Husserl received from them, of which he remains grateful. But Bolzano’s great logic comes into question here as little as can be: he had not the slightest inkling of phenomenology in the sense Husserl’s writings represent. Of the thought of an essential research in intuition, of an a priori arising thereby, of a foundation of philosophy and psychology on essential cognition, Bolzano is as far as Mill. Husserl’s path to phenomenology was essentially determined by the mathesis universalis (of which Bolzano also saw nothing), and for the conception of this idea, Bolzano’s sketch of a limited piece — the doctrine of propositions in themselves and truths in themselves — was of inestimable value. But had Husserl not already had the pure idea in the mathematical sphere and for the sphere of logical mathematics, he would have seen the sense of Bolzano’s theory as little as all those who used and cited the *Wissenschaftslehre*. The problems that attached to this substrate forced the transition from psychology to phenomenology. But already the problems were completely foreign to Bolzano.

The second paragraph addresses Lotze. With all his brilliant individual remarks, Lotze lacked the sense for radical principial research. It was never his way to go back to the ultimate roots and, putting aside all theoretical preconceptions, laboriously to feel his way up through the branchings of problems to the clear, radically grounded truth. He constantly had his system before his eyes, constantly lived in the interests of the reconciliation of understanding and feeling. His genial interpretation of the Platonic theory of ideas will remain his greatest, unforgettable merit. The consequence he drew so little that Husserl must still call his epistemology a contradictory hybrid. His “phenomenology” reduces to the indication of some a priori relations in the sphere of sensory contents; when one looks more closely, he lacks the genuine concept of essence to be grasped by essential intuition as an absolute measure of truth. What could be a complete essential doctrine of consciousness, a constitution of objectivities — of this he never had an inkling, and thus no inkling of what “we here call phenomenology.”

The third paragraph treats Brentano. Some take phenomenology as a continuation of Brentano’s psychology. However highly Husserl esteems that genial work, it must be said that Brentano has remained far from a phenomenology in Husserl’s sense, to this day. He has, however, won epoch-making merits for the very enabling of phenomenology. He offered the modern age — drawn from consciousness itself in immanent description — the idea of intentionality; although he often oversteps the lines of pure description, at least many of his concept-formations have their source in real intuition. They could and had to educate toward intuition in general and thus also, after the turn to essential intuition, in the new direction; but the essence of intentional analysis he did not see.

### §11. Differences in the Relations of Physics and Psychology to Their Ontological Foundations. The Significance of Description in Both Sciences

The first paragraph of §11 identifies a source of the psychologist’s confusion: the inclination to model psychology on physical natural science, especially physics. Physics rests on rational disciplines, but of a quite different fundamental character from phenomenology. Phenomenology is an infinite field of essential analyses and essential descriptions, but not a field of deductions; rational natural science — e.g. pure geometry or phoronomy — is an infinite field of deductions, but not a field of essential analyses and descriptions. Geometry begins with the fixation of a few concepts drawn from intuition. Obviously the conceptual essences are drawn from the intuition of spatial structures in a way requiring no great complications (“reductions,” “analyses”). Immediately essential insights arise, expressed as axioms in conceptual thinking: a few axioms, and everything else is formal deduction according to the principles of formal mathesis.

The second paragraph observes that in empirical natural science we find a great deal of description — entire “descriptive natural sciences” — and for the descriptive concepts, coming from phenomenology, one immediately looks for the essential domains from which the descriptive concepts would have to draw their norming essences through intuition. There must then be a rational discipline that would be a fundamental field for essential descriptions and would have to count as the foundation of natural science. But here we find the astonishing situation that description in the natural sphere claims no objective validity in the full sense, that it does not even claim or strive for the conceptual strictness we posit as a life-question for psychology if it is to be a strict science, and that description partly plays a totally different role in external than in psychic nature. This also shows that external description of the descriptive sciences is no theoretical foundation for the explanation of the “abstract,” the nomologically explanatory sciences. Mineralogy, geology, and all so-called descriptive sciences are nothing less than foundations of physics.

The third paragraph explains why. Physics “explains” what they “describe,” but it does not build itself on the descriptions. The procedure of physics — the science of objective nature in its pure objectivity — is remarkable regarding the concrete intuitive basis it, like every science, requires. It operates and handles the intuitively given things, names them, uses descriptive concepts. But there is nothing to observe of a laborious working-out of concepts from sensory intuition, of a painstaking scientific work of clarifying the empirical concepts pre-given by everyday life through essential analysis. There must be no need for this, given such a highly developed science. The psychologist, consciously or unconsciously imitating the physical procedure, also proceeds briskly — taking the human being and its soul-life in the natural nexus just as the physicist takes material things — going straight into causalities, experimenting, varying circumstances, tracing real dependencies, satisfying himself initially with the concepts he already has.

The fourth paragraph notes that within psychology itself, the need for something more was felt. Hence the theoretical esteem for “descriptive”-psychological research and the repeatedly and vividly emphasized necessity of such, following Brentano, going so far as to demand an own psychological discipline under the title “descriptive psychology” as an underlying level of the “genetic,” the real-causally explanatory psychology. Significantly, what is sought under this title is not a description analogous to the descriptive-natural-scientific — not something like a description of different types of habitual dispositions, character-types, race-types, etc. — but the descriptive analysis of the “givens of inner experience.” And one did not notice that in it, insofar as it brought genuinely good results, experience as such — the binding to actuality through actual doxic theses — was completely irrelevant and all investigation was to be carried out in essential attitude, if it had not already been unnoticeably so carried out.

The fifth paragraph then provides the key differentiation. The full clarification of the situation on the side of physical natural sciences — an understanding drawn from the essence of the sense of natural-scientific description and explanation, the answering of the question about the a priori of description — these are tasks whose solutions are important in themselves and especially significant within the problem-circle of the phenomenological constitution of natural objectivity, thus also for a phenomenology and theory of natural cognition. What has been said here should, however, suffice to awaken the awareness of the differences in the situations on the natural-scientific and psychological sides.

The sixth paragraph enriches this awareness through a pointed contrast. The main essential stock of the descriptive concepts of descriptive natural sciences stands in a fundamentally different relationship to the objects of physical nature than does the entire essential content of the descriptive concepts of “descriptive psychology” to the objects of psychic nature. On the side of descriptive natural science, one notices that its descriptive concepts stem mainly from that level of object-constitution at which realities are already fully constituted for intuition — but realities that, from the standpoint of the final idea of natural objectivity, are mere “appearances.” All properties of these appearances that have not yet stripped off the relation to the contingent subject or contingent sensibility belong to the class of “secondary qualities,” while the “primary” ones define themselves correspondingly as “objective” properties. This distinction is crucial. Opposite the real as appearance stands the real as thing-in-itself, as thing of physics.

The seventh paragraph elaborates on the physical objectification. The first is the thing consistently experienced by the individual subject of normal sensibility — colored, shining, sounding, etc. Each subject of the same normal sensibility constitutes a corresponding appearance-thing, mutually identified in communication. But even if all experiencers had truly the same “normal sensory organization” and all experienced under corresponding “normal” circumstances, so that the appearances of the different subjects agreed exactly, this agreement would be contingent. Physics eliminates the relation to the “normal organization.” It says: normality is something contingent, completely relative, and accordingly the objectivity constituting itself from such agreement is also relative and contingent. What is once called normal can at another time be called abnormal and vice versa. Physics rejects every such normality as a measure of objective being. Rather, it takes the thing actually (intuitively) experienced by the subject as appearance, and even the intersubjectively identified thing — identified by subjects communicating and finding they perceive the same — it takes not as the objective thing in the stock of identified predicates of intersubjective intuition, but itself only as intersubjective “appearance” of the true thing.

The eighth paragraph draws the essential consequence for physics. From the standpoint of objective natural science, the descriptive represents only a preliminary level of cognition — sufficient for aesthetic, practical, and other interests, but not for the theoretical interest in nature as it is “objectively” “in itself.” A closer look at the method of physics makes it understandable why, although it naturally starts from “appearances” (since only from such can the objective be determined), and therefore no less naturally talks of them and describes them, it has no interest in systematically fixing appearances in the style of descriptive natural sciences in relation to a human normality, but only in naming them sufficiently for the purposes of objective-causal analysis with respect to the general appearance-properties in question. Fine differentiations do not concern the physicist, since all exact determination belongs to the objective sphere, and the appearance is not itself the object of interest but only as a manifestation of objective properties.

The ninth paragraph presents the sharply different situation in the psychic sphere. The soul is not a substance of appearances; the parallel to appearance in the above-designated sense is missing, as is the whole extraordinarily complicated system of constitutive unities each of which is “appearance” in relation to the unity of the higher level and is in turn a unity appearing in appearances of a lower level (sensory schemata, visual things of various levels and strata, adumbrations). The psychic unity constitutes itself directly in its states, and the soul-states are the “consciousness-experiences” that are adequately given themselves, without the intermediary of appearance, in the plane of immanent temporality (in which all our analyses are conducted). The “relativity” to apprehending subjects drops away. The possibility of “deception” remains, but not that of “viewing from different standpoints.”

The tenth paragraph draws the cardinal methodological consequence. These experiences, as belonging to the true being of the soul itself, are therefore the theme of psychology itself and not merely media through which the theme is first to be gained. The intuitive thing, the thing of my direct experience, manifests itself in the sensory schema somewhat as the soul manifests itself in its consciousness-states. If the intuitive thing were the objectively actual thing (that of physics), then the physicist too would have to describe the respective sensory schema as the momentary and changing state of the thingly property, and the thingly properties themselves would contain the secondary qualities, which would then find their expression in descriptive concepts. Because the intuitive thing is mere “appearance” of the thing of physics — which is not itself intuitively to be had, hence never truly experienced — the experienced and its experiential properties are not the physical theme but only a means to reach the theme. In psychology, however, there are no “appearances” of the soul, but experience of it itself — experience as there can be of any real, namely by the fact that its states are experienced and belong, as states of the real itself, to the scientific theme, requiring scientific description.

The eleventh paragraph formulates the consequence. If we understand by description the conceptual expression of the perceived itself, of what is experienced in the proper sense, then psychological description determines the psychic itself, and specifically the psychological description of experiences determines the psychic itself with respect to its states, to which we are evidently referred back in all other psychological descriptions. By contrast, natural-scientific description determines only an appearance, not the thing itself that is to be determined. The infinite field of experiences thus becomes for psychology an infinite field of determining description, without whose strict scientific shaping it cannot claim to be a strict science. The strict shaping of description demands phenomenological analysis; the concepts are scientific only when their pure essences are grasped and, within their essential connections, strictly delimited and distinguished.

The twelfth paragraph concludes §11. The true method is that not in experience but in the pure essential connection, not in empirical psychology but in rational phenomenology, the entire work of essential distinction and conceptual essential grasping is carried out, and then in the experiential science the mere application of the phenomenological results follows. Phenomenology is therefore nothing less than mere descriptive psychology, and it is not even empirically descriptive essential doctrine; essential description gives in itself no essential law, but essential description is immediately followed by cognition of essential connections, and in each lies a hidden a priori lawfulness.

### §12. Further Clarification of the Relation of Rational Psychology and Phenomenology

The first paragraph of §12 opens with a dramatic observation. We live in a time of great upheavals. Rational ontology and rational psychology — and how long until also rational cosmology and theology — the much-maligned and seemingly permanently retired disciplines of past epochs seem to be reviving. But it would be bad if the dead were to be resuscitated — they who were alive only as materially inadequate convictions and had to pass away because what they set forth as truth had no place in the realm of truth, of eternal life. Only the general idea of such rational disciplines lives again, but with completely new content, in our analyses. We may rest assured that a rational psychology as demanded here will stand sub specie aeterni. To such a rational psychology belongs, besides the essential doctrine of experiences, much else — for example, the a priori stock of the psychic I, the doctrine of the pure I, etc.

The second paragraph raises a paradox the reader has likely already noticed: phenomenology seems actually to include the whole of rational psychology, so that the part swallows the whole. Going from the empirical world, the empirical human being, the empirical soul to the soul-idea, grasping the idea of the total soul-states, isolating them abstractively (as we isolate space and spatial shape when we do geometry), and establishing a pure essential doctrine of experiences — wherever essence rules, we must not stop so long as essential necessities prevail. All necessary belongings-together must be followed until the circle is closed. But what a wonder! The experiential stream harbors, according to ideal possibility (that is, necessary and apodictically necessary possibility), the cogito with I and cogitatum; and it harbors all sources for possible world-constitutions and soul-constitutions. Following the a priori, we encounter the essential doctrine of possible natural cognition in general and, with respect to correlates, all the transcendencies we have come to know as different levels of thing-constitution. And we encounter empirical I’s, bodies. The essential analyses related to them necessarily fit into the experiential analyses; the essential research of experiences demands that of the noemata, that of the lawful regulations laid out as ideal possibilities in them, that of the manifold “unities” of “manifolds,” each of which indicates an essential-lawful regulation of consciousness in general. But consciousness in general is nothing other than experience in general and experiential nexus, or is enclosed therein. So truly the part swallows the whole, and rational phenomenology ultimately embraces not only the rest of rational psychology but all rational ontologies — at least with respect to their axioms, insofar as these express immediate essential connections of possible consciousness.

The third paragraph presses further. Do not even the deductively derivable propositions of ontology express possible consciousness-connections? Is not the entire geometry, rational phoronomy, rational natural science in general, and so every rational discipline, a piece of phenomenology? Starting from empirical psychology, in the progression to the idea of rational psychology, phenomenology arises as a mere essential doctrine of experiences as states of a soul thought in regional generality. But the soul itself, with all that belongs to it as reality in terms of real properties, real circumstances, and real comportments, is subject to rational consideration. That essential doctrine is thus a mere part. Rational psychology itself presents itself as one alongside other rational disciplines and in connection with them. So all rational disciplines seem to be swallowed by phenomenology.

The fourth paragraph emphasizes the importance of achieving full clarity on the true situation. For the psychologist who treats phenomenology merely as an auxiliary discipline and has as little independent interest in it as the physicist has in geometry, these questions matter little. For the experiential researcher, the essential thing is simply to have the rational discipline belonging to his sphere and, where he has it imperfectly, to acquire it through genuine execution of the eidetic attitude and research within its framework. What matters is his doing, not his reflective “philosophical” ideas about it — just as it does no harm if natural scientists, having done geometry in the eidetic attitude, subsequently declare it to be nothing other than empirical science. Quite different when the prevailing interests are scientific-theoretical, metaphysical, philosophical in any good sense, and especially when the psychologist himself wants to be a philosopher. If he cannot rid himself of the prejudices intertwining so easily with the naive and experiential-scientific attitude, he cannot be a real philosopher; he then practices a flattened philosophy, a monstrosity of natural science and philosophy.

The fifth paragraph addresses the parallel questions arising with regard to the human sciences and their ontology. Wherever one begins, all essential truths — immediate or mediate — and thus all rational disciplines we already know or will come to know seem to be swallowed by phenomenology. Since we are so strongly inclined to suppose psychology wherever the word “consciousness” or “experience” appears, even rational psychology seems to swallow everything. Is consciousness something other than the psychic, and is psychology something other than science of the psychic? Could it be anything more than scholastic subtlety to distinguish between science of consciousness and psychology?

The sixth paragraph raises a further consequence: just as rational psychology seems to swallow all rational disciplines, so empirical psychology seems to swallow all empirical ones. Does not phenomenology, in its ideal consideration, dissolve the essence of all objectivities into unities of consciousness-manifolds? Does not the application to the empirically given mean that all factual existence of every kind reduces to consciousness-connections? We thus have the finest “psychomonism.” Psychology encompasses all sciences.

The seventh paragraph, leaving the psychomonistic question for later, turns to the key resolution. It is of the greatest importance to free oneself from the prejudice that experience, consciousness is in itself something psychic, eo ipso a matter for psychology, whether empirical or rational eidetic. One should not fight over words. Husserl will continue to understand by “psychic” the soul-like in the sense clarified, which must be strictly observed. Then an experience is psychic as a state of a soul, or of a human or animal I — a reality founded in physical nature. Whoever cannot free himself from this particular apprehension, who cannot carry out the phenomenological reductions and grasp the pure, absolutely posited experience, the pure consciousness as idea — for him, entry into transcendental phenomenology and into philosophy in general is barred. Material thing, body, soul — these are quite determinate and specific transcendencies, constituting themselves in pure consciousness for the pure I in their own way and fully understandably, and only through reduction to this is the “absolute” to be won, the absolute relatum for all realities, whose being is throughout relative being. Pure consciousness has the absolute priority; in relation to it all being is the a posteriori, and this relation between a priori and a posteriori already belongs in the essential sphere. Accordingly, it is a fundamental necessity, of cardinal importance for philosophy, to rise to the recognition that between the eidetics of consciousness-states (a piece of rational ontology of the soul) and the eidetics of transcendentally purified consciousness (or experiential-being) a distinction must be made, and that the latter — genuine and pure phenomenology — is no more rational psychology than it is rational natural science. Only one thing justifies calling the eidetics of psychic consciousness-states phenomenology: the already mentioned circumstance that the pure experience with its whole essence enters into the psychic state and undergoes only an apprehension that does not alter it but peculiarly grasps it. This apprehension turns the a priori into an a posteriori and itself presupposes the a priori. These are connections that, once understood, have nothing wondrous about them. The wonder of all wonders is pure I and pure consciousness — and this very wonder vanishes as soon as the light of phenomenology falls on it and subjects it to essential analysis. The wonder vanishes in transforming itself into an entire science with a fullness of difficult scientific problems.

## Third Chapter

### The Relation of Phenomenology and Ontology

### §13. The Field of Phenomenological Research

The first paragraph of §13 turns to the general investigation of the relationship between phenomenology and ontology. We place ourselves on the ground of transcendental phenomenology. In transcendental experiencing, all “transcendent being” — understood in the normal sense as true being — is excluded, “bracketed.” What alone should remain is “consciousness itself” in its own essence, and in place of transcendent being, the “being-meant” of what is transcendent and thus all kinds of correlates, the meanings, the noemata. This refers especially to the sphere of intentional experiences. We retain perception and the perceived as such (insofar as, after excluding the actual being of the perceived, we can evidently say that perception is in its essence perception of this and that objectual content — it means it, is consciousness of it); we retain memory and the remembered as such, thinking and the thought as such — in short, noesis and noema, and on the latter side several fundamentally essential distinctions. The excluding epochē can have two directions: a transcendent (everything that is not itself experience or experiential correlate) may be posited and then the positing bracketed; or reflection may turn upon the experience itself and the experiencing I, finding psychic states and psychic subject and soul — and there too the reduction is carried out. All this also in the eidetic attitude, and then all ontologies fall under the reduction.

### §14. The Inclusion of the Ontologies in Phenomenology

The first paragraph of §14 observes that nevertheless pure phenomenology seemed to harbor all ontologies — the pure just as the psychologically turned version. More precisely: the roots of all ontologies are the fundamental concepts and axioms of the same. These seem to belong within phenomenology; they let themselves be reinterpreted into certain essential connections of pure experiences. If so, the consequence seems unavoidable that the same must hold for the derived propositions and thus for the entire disciplines, since these only establish in indirect cognitive connection with axioms what belongs to one and the same essential domain, the one defined by fundamental concepts and axioms. Having given special attention to the axioms of the a priori disciplines, we can answer: it is imperative to carry through the distinction between science of transcendental consciousness in general and the intuitive essential doctrine of this consciousness. Regarding the former, the framework extends very widely. Everything — the transcendental interpretations of all ontologies, the carrying out of every ontological theorem as an index for quite determinate connections of transcendental consciousness — belongs here. Further, every empirical truth, every proposition of the experiential sciences, and every really existing thing becomes an index for transcendental connections; thus a research must be possible that makes the entire realm of factual consciousness — the entire stock of absolute monads with their factual experiential content — the subject of scientific consideration.

### §15. The Significance of Ontological Determinations for Phenomenology and the Difference of Attitude in Both Sciences

The first paragraph of §15 addresses the main theme of the chapter: the positing of essences and essential connections expressed in ontological fundamental concepts and principles. According to our findings, the fundamental forms (material and formal) of constitution of objectivities in consciousness correspond to ontic fundamental concepts functioning as fundamental concepts in ontologies. They thus seem from the outset to belong to phenomenology as well. Indeed, have we not regarded it as a fundamental task of phenomenology to investigate the reality “material thing” — corresponding to the fundamental type of experience called material experience — according to its modes of givenness and to clarify in what phenomenological connections this reality-type constitutes itself as a unity? The same obviously holds for the idea of geometrical bodies, of the body, of psychic unity, of the animal being — in short, for all regional categories of possible realities. And mutatis mutandis for formal logic as a science of thought-meanings in formal universality and of the corresponding objects in formal universality. All such concepts form from the outset guiding concepts for entire phenomenological fields of investigation. The most primitive axioms also belong here.

The second paragraph addresses how “the same” concepts and propositions function in ontological and phenomenological research. In the eidetics of space, material nature, spirit, etc., we pursue “ontology” as dogmatic science. We judge about spatial structures, souls and psychic properties, human beings as such. In phenomenology, we are the science of “origins,” of the “mother” of all cognition, the mother-soil of all philosophical method. If philosophers always said or were guided by the unspoken conviction that natural cognition of practical life and of dogmatic sciences does not suffice, that it is laden with unclarities, that the fundamental concepts of all (dogmatic) sciences need “clarification” and a leading-back to their origins — then everywhere what is felt as a deficiency finds its final fulfillment in phenomenology, and the relevant conceptual clarification is only a step toward phenomenological clarification and further essential investigation within the framework of problems of phenomenological constitution.

The third paragraph explains the difference more precisely. In ontology we posit as truly existing — in eidetic truth, not in the experiential world — spatial structures as such, and this means positing spatial structures as such. A geometrical judgment holds only if the idea, the essence “space and spatial shape” is, or, extensionally speaking, if a spatial shape is possible. In the course of ontological research, eidetic individual items are posited — corresponding to a positing of essences as objects — and together these make up the domain of the ontology delimited by the validly posited regional idea. Propositions of eidetic generality then find application in the empirical sphere. We know then in advance (“a priori”) that nothing can occur in the existential sphere that is essentially excluded by the essences particularizing themselves in it, and that everything that does occur must occur as they require as a necessary consequence enclosed in them.

The fourth paragraph brings full clarity about the goals and manner of ontological judgment. Ontologies make being-judgments (eidetic existential judgments) — positive and negative, thetic and hypothetical — about essences and essential individual items and the spheres designated by the regions. Every valid ontological judgment presupposes eidetic individualities (equivalent to a positing of corresponding essences as objects).

The fifth paragraph contrasts the phenomenological side. Phenomenology’s domain is not spatial structures, things, souls, etc. as such in eidetic generality, but transcendental consciousness with all its transcendental occurrences to be researched in immediate intuition and in eidetic generality. Thus within its domain falls the intuition of spatial structures, the thinking and cognitive recognition of them, the experiencing of things, the experiential-scientific consciousness, etc. So “spatial structure,” “thing,” and whatever other ontological concepts and essences come up — but they come up in a quite different way, already expressed in the fact that they do not designate the domain. In phenomenology of thing-consciousness, the question is not how things in general are, what belongs to them as such in truth; but how consciousness of things is constituted, what kinds of thing-consciousness are to be distinguished, how a thing as such presents and manifests itself in consciousness-terms. But also how consciousness can of itself be cognition of the existence and non-existence, possibility and impossibility of a thing — what that is in its essence: a “verification” of thingly existence, a grounding of a natural-scientific proposition, etc. A “thing” as correlate is not a thing — hence the quotation marks. The theme is thus totally different, even if essential connections run back and forth.

### §16. Noema and Essence

The first paragraph of §16 warns against confusing noema (correlate) and essence. Even the noema of a clear thing-intuition or of a continuous harmonious intuitive nexus directed at one and the same thing is not and does not contain the essence of the thing. The grasping of the one is not the grasping of the other, although here an essential change of attitude and direction of grasping is possible through which the grasping of the noema can pass over into the grasping of the corresponding ontic essence. In the latter case we have a different kind of intuition than in the former. Speaking of “essential intuition” (Erschauung) only for the intuitive grasping of essences, we need a different terminus for the intuition of the noema.

The second paragraph clarifies the difference between positing a noema as existing and positing the corresponding objectivity. This is easily shown with thought-meanings, the specifically logical ones. The objectivity appears in the noema as a unity-moment that different noemata can have as “identical” — but in quotation marks, just as “identical” itself was rightly put in quotation marks. In logical meanings, the thought as such (logical meaning in the noematic sense) can be “absurd” — yet it, which still “exists” within the being-category “logical meaning” and more generally “noema,” has its actual being, like the thought-meaning “round square.” The essence “round square” does not exist; but to judge this, the presupposition is that “round square” is a meaning existing in this unity. It is clear that in the intuition-sphere it cannot be otherwise than in the thought-sphere, except that here an important essential relation between intuitive noema and essence of the intuited holds: where the former can be grasped, the latter can be too, and vice versa — even when the intuition is imperfect.

The third paragraph draws the consequence. Despite the phenomenologically ascertainable essential relations between intuitive grasping of noema and intuitive grasping of ontic essences in the case of any intuition of the onta, it is nonetheless an essential change of judgment-posture when we, instead of judging phenomenologically about the noema, judge ontologically about the essences and essential individualities as such. On the other hand, in the stage of phenomenological constitution (e.g. of the physical thing), one does not judge about things as such, but the preconditions are fulfilled to grasp the essence “thing” harmoniously and in the greatest conceivable perfection and to reject disharmonious essential posits, and thereby eo ipso to achieve the most perfect evidence of the axioms in their pure and alone valid sense and to reject false axiomatic posits as absurd. As soon as we perform the required change of judgment-direction, we have overstepped the domain of phenomenology and entered that of ontology.

### §17. The Significance of Ontological Concepts for Psychology

The first paragraph of §17 notes that what has been set forth for the opposition of phenomenology and ontology must naturally transfer to the relation of psychology and ontology. Every phenomenological description passes through psychological apprehension into a psychological one regarding the experiences of an experiencing soul, just as conversely every description of psychic experiences passes, through the ideally possible reduction (eidetic and purifying from realization), into a purely phenomenological one. If within psychological research the not infrequent dispute is to be settled whether pretended descriptions of perceptions of things, of space, of movement, etc. are correct — and whether the designation as perceptions precisely of things, of space, of movement is valid (which says something about the essence of the psychic experiences) — the interest is evidently not ontological. In psychology, things are not the judgment-themes as they are in physical natural science, where essential insights about things in general are naturally to be drawn upon in judging the particular experienced things.

The second paragraph adds that ontological axioms can indeed serve as criteria for the correctness of noematic descriptions — but the approach is then no longer purely phenomenological but a mixed phenomenological-ontological one. Since a great interest attaches to performing the reductions cleanly and maintaining them in research, Husserl chooses to discuss the situation not in the framework of phenomenology but of psychology, noting merely that once full security in the command of phenomenological method is achieved and the sense of every going-beyond and returning is clarified, there also emerge methodologically permissible transgressions that from a higher standpoint justify themselves within a suitably extended phenomenological immanence.

## Fourth Chapter

### The Method of Clarification

The opening paragraph observes that the investigation just carried out served to clarify the relationship between phenomenology and ontology, but we also recognize its significance for the clarification of phenomenological (and likewise psychological) method in a certain direction. It lies in the nature of phenomenology and the unique achievements falling to it for our total cognition that it must constantly be reflectively related back to itself, that it must bring to fullest clarity from phenomenological sources the method it practices. There is no science behind it to which it could shift any work; every unclarity remaining gives a favorable breeding-ground for confusions and smuggling-in, for corruption of phenomenological results, and for misinterpretations of the entire phenomenological method. The radical science demands the most radical strictness, which in turn demands the most perfect transparency in the method of clarification.

### §18. The Need for Clarification of the Dogmatic Sciences

The first paragraph of §18 states that all sciences are subject, with respect to their stock of propositions and concepts, to the self-evident demand of grounding. The statements should hold, and they can hold only if the concepts hold in their way. It is an ideal of science to win, for its respective domain, a valid system of propositions (and thus of concepts) that, in the material of thinking — namely word-meanings — univocally determine everything in the domain: all that is, in terms of characteristics, relations, connections. The demand is that this achievement, as far as it counts as accomplished in however narrow a circle, be able to verify itself through grounding at any time. The conviction of the truth of a theory and its practically useful application does not presuppose the ability of grounding, i.e. the ability to see it through from the ground up. But remarkably, neither does the genesis of theories require insight.

The second paragraph develops this observation. A remarkable teleology prevails in the development of human culture in general and of scientific culture in particular: valuable products can arise without insight or through a mixture of insight and instinct. That they are valuable is of course shown by a subsequent grounding that verifies the validity-claims within the framework of perfect insight. All science, even the most highly developed exact sciences, are cultural values of this kind. However laughable it would be to claim that insightful grounding played no part or no great part in their genesis and development, all sciences are far from a sufficient, perfectly insightful grounding. And this already with respect to the conception of the concepts building up their predications.

The third paragraph traces the problem historically. Only partly drawn from the very imperfect naive empiricism of the everyday person by the first creators of the sciences, and partly clarified only one-sidedly and incompletely in new formations, concepts enter into the work of science with a stock of confusion, without generally gaining in depth of clarity in the progress of this work. Indeed, the reverse process takes place. The higher science grows, the richer its “methodology” becomes, the more the main work shifts into the symbolic thought-sphere; the concepts originally oriented by intuition are used merely symbolically, as counters, with which one operates without going back to intuition. The art of inventing ever new symbolic procedures becomes ever more perfectly practiced; what was relatively insightful on a lower level is symbolized again on a higher level and robbed of its insightfulness (as an unnecessary thought-burden). Against this one-sided devotion of humanity to developing sciences as thought-techniques, intuitionism reacts with full right. It is a matter of putting an end to the intolerable plight of reason, which in the midst of all riches of theoretical possession sees its proper goal — understanding the world, insight into truth — receding ever further. But intuitionism must not degenerate into mysticism; it must take on the sober tasks arising from the described situation.

### §19. Clarification of the Conceptual Material

The first paragraph of §19 identifies the first work to be undertaken: the conceptual material with which science operates, and first of all the primitive concepts. Through concepts, science refers to the objects of its domain. Despite the decried deficiencies, its procedure is of course not entirely foreign to intuition. Concepts are repeatedly referred to what is intuited, and conversely, under the guidance of intuition, concepts are formed (drawings, models, observations, experiments). Yet the concepts lack that clarity necessary for their cognitive validity.

The second paragraph distinguishes three kinds of concepts in every science directed at individual being: (a) logico-formal concepts, which are the common property of all sciences whatsoever — concepts like object, characteristic, state of affairs, relation, number, etc., and the concepts expressing meaning-forms, meaning-categories like “concept,” “proposition in general”; (b) regional concepts, expressing the region itself (e.g. “thing”) and the modifications of the region like “thing-property,” “thingly relation,” etc. — these too have in a certain way formal character, running through all sciences of a region; (c) the material particularizations of the regional concepts themselves, with which all materially determinate statements carry their references to the matters according to their material stock. Mere modes of number belong to arithmetic, mere modes of the idea “meaning” to the logic of meanings, mere modes of spatiality to geometry — these are all formal modes. Of an entirely different character are concepts like color, tone, kinds of sensory feelings and drives, etc. They bring the material content into all determinations.

The third paragraph states that for the clarification of every science, the clarification of concepts of all these groups is necessary, and the sequence of groups marks a rank-order. First the logico-formal concepts should be clarified — a common concern of all sciences. Then the regional-formal concepts, and finally the particular concepts that are the specific property of particular sciences. If all possible essential doctrines, all eidetic disciplines, had historically come to development as the natural sciences have (though with similar deficiencies), we would have to say that the clarification of ontology must precede that of the empirical sciences. With regard to conceptual clarification, the entire work demanded for all sciences would already be accomplished by the conceptual clarification of the ontological disciplines. For every concept has its conceptual essence, which ideally falls under an essential genus that could function as a domain for an essential doctrine. In the totality of ontologies in the widest sense, all essences would be included; the clarification of primitive concepts through recourse to primitive essences would already be accomplished. But in truth very few ontologies are constituted, because their effective development as sciences demands from the outset a perfection of intuition that is only exceptionally and only for essences of certain classes relatively easy to achieve.

The fourth paragraph illustrates the process of clarification with an example. Suppose it is a matter of bringing the concept of the material body to clarity — what “thing” “really means,” what it “looks like.” We proceed from examples that are unquestionable applications of the word “thing” — stones, houses, etc. — but are not satisfied with merely gathering them by name, i.e. “thinking” them through mere word-meanings; rather we pass over to intuition, to perception of such individual items or to vivid phantasy. We look to see what in the intuitively given is highlighted, covered, conceptually meant by the word-concept and what is not, what in it is the “so-called” properly, i.e. which essential moments of the intuitively given are the ones on account of which the matter is so “called.” In the main this is the Socratic procedure. Of course it is not a matter of fixing a usage, but of bringing a noematic essence to relief in the intuitively given and fixing it as what is meant through the mere word-meaning. Thereby the word-meaning, the concept, proves itself valid, for now an essence corresponds to the concept.

### §20. Elucidation and Clarification

The first paragraph of §20 makes a further distinction. If the concept is complex, elucidation (Verdeutlichung) and clarification (Klärung) must be kept apart. The elucidation of the concept — of what is meant by the word as such — is a procedure taking place entirely within the mere thinking-sphere. Before the slightest step toward clarification is made, while no or a quite unsuitable and indirect intuition is at one with the word, one can consider what lies in the meaning — e.g. in “decahedron”: a body, a polyhedron, regular, with ten congruent faces. Successively what belongs to the thinking-sphere can be extracted and combined: “a geometrical body which is completely bounded by ten congruent plane surfaces.” There can be various logically equivalent expressions. We thus have to distinguish the unelucidated, unanalyzed concept and the analytically elucidated concept, and the “analytic judgments” in the sense Kant initially had in mind.

The second paragraph explains what happens in clarification proper. In clarification we transcend the sphere of mere word-meanings and meaning-thinking; we bring meanings to coincidence with the noematic of intuition, the noematic object of the former with that of the latter. The coincidence must be so complete that to every partial concept extracted through elucidation there corresponds an explicated moment of the intuitive noema. Evidently the explication of what is meant in intuition is different from the explication of what is meant in thinking. Very often the adaptation to a corresponding intuition leads to a conflict: different meaning-explicates demand intuitive correlates that “do not agree” in the unity of a single intuition — they do not come to unity in the normal sense but to a connection of two intuitions in the form of conflict with respect to the relevant moments.

The third paragraph notes the relation between word and noemata. One must attend not only to the relation between meaning and the intuited as such (thought-noema and intuitive noema) but also to the relation of the word itself, with its word-sound and the word-tendency adhering to it, and the noemata. The word “means” something — its meaning demands a fitting, fulfilling essence. But the word with the tendency adhering to its sound demands a meaning and is one with it as word. The word can be equivocal and as word can demand various meanings — sometimes unrelated, sometimes closely related, sometimes broader, sometimes narrower. Through this much confusion and error enters science. Clarification also has the function of giving the old words a newly constituted sense.

The fourth paragraph addresses the main work of clarification on the side of intuition. The goal of clarification can be stated as follows: it wants, so to speak, to create the pre-given concept anew, to feed it from the original source of conceptual validity — intuition — and to give it within intuition the partial concepts belonging to its originary essence. The clarification must follow exactly the levels of constitution of the relevant exemplary intuition-object. A thing is not given, a thing-concept is not brought to real clarity, when a thing is simply seen. A phantom is also seen, and a simple seeing yields no more than what corresponds to the phantom, namely the sensory schema. If it is to be clarified what “thing” means, or what “real thing-property” means with its essential relation to real circumstances, then the clarifying intuition must follow the manifolds of sensory schemata in relation to the manifolds of the schematized circumstances and must bring to fulfillment the intuitive components that give the sensory schemata the value of thing-intuitions.

The fifth paragraph draws the final general lesson. We see from here in perfect clarity the general task and the most comprehensive (though lying at infinity) ideal: in systematic completeness to encompass in intuition and in perfected clarity the world of ideas, the world of possible essential kinds of possible objectivities in general, and on the ground of the noemata lying in intuition to win the overall possible conceptual essences and to assign to them the purely expressing word-meanings and words themselves — which would constitute a totality of perfectly clarified concepts or terms. And from there one arrives again at the infinite ideal of a system of all ontologies and eidetic disciplines in general. With the explication of essences in self-givenness and the consideration of essential connections, necessities, possibilities, and impossibilities of combination, of the manifold essentially laid-out relations, which, expressed in faithful concepts, become fundamental truths of ontology — all such research has close relation to phenomenology. It is itself an eidetics. An all-encompassing system of eidetic disciplines encompasses it too. But all clarifying and axiomatically clear ontological insight that is not directly phenomenological becomes so through a mere turning of the gaze, just as within the whole of phenomenological insights some must arise that through a mere turning of the gaze become ontological. One can say that all eidetic axioms, in the context of phenomenology, can be found through mere execution of the change of gaze and corresponding positing — so that phenomenology contains the mother-soil from which all ontological insights originate. But it is principially significant that it owes nothing to the other ontologies and can owe them nothing, just as little as to all other dogmatic sciences, and that it is nothing less than a kind of continuation of the ontologies. What can and must be said, however, is that only through the thoroughly reflective work of phenomenology — which systematically analyzes what is necessarily performed on the other side, which fixes all the motives lying in the phenomena and asks after their motivates — can the ontologically founded research unfold its full force and receive its full security. Only the phenomenologist will be qualified to perform the deepest clarifications regarding the essences building up in systematically constitutive strata and thus to prepare the grounding of the ontologies that we so sorely lack.

## Supplements

### Supplement I: Draft of the Second Book from 1912

#### §1. The Constitution of the Soul (Empathy)

The first paragraph of Supplement I addresses the difficult question of the “constitution” of the soul and the psychic I united with it. We see animals and human beings as ensouled beings, which happens by certain things being apprehended as bodies — i.e. by having courses of consciousness and the constitutive unities belonging to them interpreted into them. The body must be apprehended as a real unity (though not yet as an “objective” material thing through intersubjective constitution, which itself presupposes a reference to a plurality of human beings), in order to function as a bearer of the psychic. In the framework of the pure I, this means the body is given as a unity of possible schematic manifolds, recognizable and identifiable in distinct experiential series. In empathy or interpretation (Eindeutung), the body is understood as a body — first simply as a bearer of the psychic, and not only of sensations but also of “acts,” of intentional experiences. Therewith a pure subject belongs to the content of the interpretation as the subject of the interpreted cogito. This pure I has its surrounding world (Umwelt), its Here and Now, in relation to which its thinghood is oriented, and this Here and Now stands to a thing appearing to the pure I in a distinguished way — namely its body — similarly to how my Here and Now stands to my body.

The second paragraph discusses the constitution of objective things through empathy. If I could “push away” my body, which factually always has my orientation-center in it and thus always accompanies my pure I, it would offer all the appearance-series that other things offer; it is in itself a thing like any other, only that it cannot be pushed away and therefore can appear only in restricted groups of appearances. Similarly, the pure I interpreted into the foreign thing “body of another” has its non-removable thing “own body,” and this, in the sense of empathy, is the very same thing that appears as the bearer of empathy or interpretation. Therewith it is also given that the surrounding world of the foreign pure I is the same as that of my pure I; the things of the surrounding world are unities of a higher level, constituting themselves by way of interpretation. Where two communicate, the appearance-manifold constituting a real thing for one subject and the appearance-manifold constituting “the same” thing for the other are different; through interpretation, these two manifolds are coordinated so that the “objective” thing constitutes itself as “the same.” Every new pure I set through interpretation multiplies the number of closed groups of appearance-manifolds, and so the objective thing is a unity of an unrestricted manifold of groups of appearance-manifolds, and empathy is one of the fundamental forms of experience already for external thinghood.

The third paragraph carries the objectification further to the body itself. Just as the material body is objectified through the manifold of its appearances to different subjects, so the soul (Sk) constitutes itself in corresponding manifolds. Already constituted are the connections of body and soul for each subject. What are the S-manifolds — the appearances of the objective real soul? They are themselves already constituted unities, not through schematization (as with the body) but through a different process of constitutive manifestation. When I, as the experiencing pure I, apprehend an appearing thing as a body, the lowest apprehension-level is the interpretation of a flow of consciousness, which is not yet anything real, just as the schema is not yet a thing-real. This pure consciousness has an originary connection with the body as it appears to me, requiring special description. Initially, the appearing body of the other is characterized as a body: as a bearer of sensory sensations, sensory feelings and drives, as a bearer of all the “materials” further interwoven with act-characters as noetic forms, so that a whole flow of consciousness comes about, including acts of the specific form of cogito and a pure I.

The fourth paragraph extends the analysis to objectivity through intersubjective constitution. In my experience, the foreign consciousness appears in regulated dependence on circumstances within my world and thereby receives a realizing apprehension as a soul-real. But we do not yet have the objectivity of the soul or of the real connection of soul and body — the full human being. Rather, this is the primary intuitive experiential unity “foreign human being for me,” a mere “appearance” (a readily misunderstood expression). Any other I has from “the same” soul its own version, and this bespeaks: through possible mutual understanding, this identification is possible, and only through it does the objective soul, objectively united with the objective body, and thereby the objective human being, constitute itself. We understand thereby the phenomenological construction of the soul- and human-apprehension according to noesis and noema, insofar as constitution in general is in question. The soul does not appear in the sense of adumbrating; the soul does become presentive to every pure consciousness in a mere noematic “representation”-appearance. Apprehended as objective being, it is the unity manifesting itself in all these “representations” of the soul and verifying itself in mutual understanding.

The fifth paragraph draws two consequences about the experience of one’s own soul. Does each person then have a more direct experience of his own soul than of another’s? Every pure I has of its consciousness — its pure consciousness — immediate, i.e. not empathy-based, experience. But aside from the fact that one’s own bodily apprehension is in many respects more imperfect than the foreign one (however much it has the advantage through the originary grasping of bodily sensations), and that one’s own soul-grasping in the subjective stage would remain quite rudimentary without the grasping of the foreign — the objectivity of one’s own soul presupposes mutual understanding. One’s own soul, insofar as it is to have objective existence in the objective world, is a constituted unity of a manifold of “appearances” or “representations,” which are indeed already “realities” from the standpoint of the single I but not objective realities, not “substances.”

#### §2. The Realizing Apprehension of the Pure I

The first paragraph of §2 goes a step further by considering the pure I not as the experiencing one, but as the one interpreted into the appearing and objectified body. It is the subject of the interpreted cogitations, subject of the surrounding world given to it through these cogitations — which is not only a surrounding thing-world but also a world of values and a practical world. The pure I is an identical unity, but not a unity that represents or manifests itself in any sense; rather, it is a cogitating unity, and how this is to be understood can only and can adequately be drawn from every cogito. This I is therefore something totally different from all the unities we have come to know — including the psychic unity — although it too can undergo intersubjective identification and objectification.

The second paragraph describes the realizing apprehension. Every cogito, together with the entire stream of consciousness to which it belongs, undergoes a realizing apprehension insofar as this stream is apprehensible as dependent on the body and, through it, on the real-constituted and posited material world. Looking now at the pure subject of the cogitations, and considering that it is an identically unitary element in their manifold change, we see that it must necessarily undergo a corresponding realizing apprehension with them. The I that thinks is now not simply one that thinks now this way, now that way, but a real I that — belonging to this body, animating it and dependent on it — comports itself in corresponding ways with respect to a change of real circumstances. The pure I has now entered into an apprehension through which an “empirical” I is constituted — an I that can no longer be adequately given as what it is from every cogito, but must be gotten to know, and whose acquaintance is never complete, because it is experience that must provide this acquaintance (experience of the apprehension-type constitutive for this I), and like all experience (as related to the real) it leads us into endless possibilities of ever new circumstances.

The third paragraph addresses the objective world-inclusion of the empirical I. Through the realizing apprehension that the pure I undergoes, it receives (as soul and real soul-subject) objective spatiality and temporality. There would be no sense in speaking of such for the pure I in and of itself. But the empirical I is from the outset constituted as a member of an objective world. It belongs to it through its founding in the body. With the constitution of the material world, objective space and objective time are constituted. The manner of objectification of sensations in the form of “localization” then yields, with the realization of the “sense-body” — the body endowed with sensibilities — also the firm incorporation of sensations into the spatial and temporal world, following the principle that the localized is objectively simultaneous with the extensive element providing the locale. Through the interweaving of sensations and the specifically psychic, the soul-real too gains its objective incorporation. Immanent time becomes a manifestation of objective time; the immanent “simultaneous” becomes a manifestation of the objective “simultaneous.” Thus the world of realities becomes a single world, spanned first by the objective time-form as reality-form in general.

#### §3. First Indication of the Necessary Distinction between Natural-Scientific and Human-Scientific Attitude

This section (whose beginning was incorporated into the second book and is therefore omitted here) develops the essential possibility of apprehending the pure I as empirical. An analogizing example is given: a tone, considered purely in itself, has in its duration a unity — it is the identical, perhaps unchanged in quality, fluctuating in intensity and timbre, tone. But if I apprehend it as coming from the violin, changing when the violin is bowed more strongly, weakening when bowed more weakly, changing as the violin approaches or recedes — then the tone-unity is apprehended in dependence on “real” circumstances and is itself a real thing (in our concept of reality), having its real properties manifesting themselves in its changing “states.” The identical of the tone in and for itself becomes, through relation to circumstances, a dependent identical and is realized. The same holds for the schema, and analogously for the pure I — but with a fundamental difference. The pure I is a unity of a totally different sense than the unity of a continuity of phases. The pure I does not properly extend itself and does not endure; it is indivisible and unextendable, one for the cogito and identical in every other. But as the identical subject of all these acts, it does undergo realization, similar to the tone-unity, and in its way enters into the realized.

The paragraph then distinguishes the real soul from the real soul-subject. The body is the bearer of soul-life as the flow of real psychic states, and in this flow the real I-subject holds sway as an own, though not self-standing, real unity lying in the soul-ground. While the soul as subground cannot be lacking, the soul can lack the real subject — it is then a soul of a lower level, a subjectless soul. Subjectless insofar as no real psychic I-subject belongs to it. But the human being has a psychic subject, although there may be periods where real circumstances produce no states of the “I think” type. Then the real I sleeps, meaning: in the world there exist real but unrealized possibilities for “awakening.” With reference to this, the human being, even when not performing an “I think,” is apprehended as disposed to an “I think”; and insofar as empirical grounds exist for apprehending the course of possible acts in relation to circumstances so that a higher unity, an I-like individuality, manifests itself, the person has character even while sleeping.

#### §4. The Connection of Soul and Body

The first paragraph of §4 considers body and soul in their real relation on the ground of nature, into which material things, bodies, and souls are all integrated. Body and soul show themselves connected. But the connection is contingent from only one side. Psychic reality is founded in bodily matter; bodily matter is not conversely founded in the soul. More generally: the material world within the total objective world we call nature is a self-enclosed and separate world needing no aid from other realities. The existence of spiritual realities, however, is bound to the existence of a material nature — and this not from contingent but from principial grounds. The res extensa, when we interrogate its essence, contains nothing of spirituality and nothing demanding a connection with real spirituality beyond itself; but conversely, real spirituality can essentially be only in attachment to materiality, as real spirit of a body. “Body” here means not merely a material thing that — in some arbitrary way — stands with a second reality called soul in a connection of functional dependence.

##### a) The Body as Localization-Field of Sensations

The paragraph explains that a “body” as such is distinguished in a particular way: not merely because certain sorts of real effects it experiences in material nature bring about consciousness-consequences in my consciousness, but because a considerable part of the great content-class belonging to the consciousness-sphere under the title “consciousness-material” is so intimately one with the material body that in intuitive givenness not merely a connection but a unity shows itself. What we call body thus already has a stratum belonging to the “psychic” that from the outset stands there as an apprehensive stratum belonging to the whole of the body itself. We must abstract from it to obtain the merely material body. The body is, we say, often a bearer of sensations and always newly “excitable.” It bears sensations — manifold sensations like tactile sensations, kinesthetic sensations; temperature, smell, taste sensations are “localized” on and in it, forming a stratum of being lying on and in it. All other sensations that are not localized in this way gain indirectly and not contingently apprehensions through which they receive a relation to the body and its various “sense organs” — a relation that presupposes otherwise localized sensations. The distinction the body has as a localization-field of certain sensation-groups is the precondition for further distinctions.

The paragraph then provides a closer description of localization. A certain group of “sensations” (under which title we can comprise all consciousness-contents that have the character and function of “materials” from the standpoint of pure consciousness) are given in immediate experiential intuition as lying in or on the body, spread over it, being in it — in short, as localized. Not all sensations possess this peculiarity and can assume it at all. Color and tone sensations (phenomenologically so closely related to the localized sensations) lack this localization. “Seeing with the eye” is essentially different from “touching with the finger,” insofar as the eye is not a localization-field for visual sensations the way the finger is for tactile sensations. The eye is indeed a localization-field, but only for contact sensations.

##### b) The Body as Organ of Will

This section notes that the examples have been drawn from a closed group of sensations playing the role of constituting “materials” in the phenomenological constitution of the material thing-world. But other sorts of consciousness-materials are also represented by localized sensations. Before discussing these, a special distinction of kinesthetic sensations must be noted: they can have a double character in their continuous courses. Either the course is “spontaneous,” a “free” course, or it is characterized not as “done” but as “done to,” as a passive course in which spontaneity has no part. The body is thus the organ of voluntary movements, and it is so in as many ways as there are series of kinesthetic sensations with their associated appearing limb-movements. The subject then becomes active in the material world beyond the body by means of the free movements of its limbs. Only bodies have a stratum of localized sensations, including kinesthetic ones. They are the only objects immediately freely movable by a willing subject. Mere material things are only mechanically movable and otherwise only indirectly spontaneously movable by means of a body.

##### f) The Constitutive Role of Movement-Sensations in Comparison with Other Sensations, and the Relation between Body- and Thing-Constitution

The first paragraph of this section seeks to understand the constitution of bodily givenness in its essential interweaving with the givenness of the material thing. The kinesthetic sensation-courses have a special distinction predestining them for an exceptional role in thing-constitution. They can appear in consciousness with a double character: either as “spontaneous,” “free” courses, or as “done to,” “passive.” In the bodily apprehension, this grounds the distinction between passive movements (merely mechanical movements of the body as physical thing) and free bodily movements characterized as “I perform a movement of my hand,” “I raise my foot,” etc. Originally constitutive are merely the free courses of kinesthetic sensations in their motivation-relation to the series of motivated thing-apprehensions with the orders of materials implied in them, presenting color, roughness, etc.

The second paragraph compares tactile and visual sensations regarding their significance for bodily constitution. They belong to quite different constitutive orders and play quite different roles. It can be shown that a merely eye-endowed subject (an evidently makeable idea) could have no appearing body, but a merely tactually endowed subject could — under conditions fulfilled in us humans. The former would have its thing-appearances in the play of kinesthetic motivations but could not apprehend them bodily. One would not say the merely seeing subject “sees” its body, for this seen body would lack the specific distinction from an arbitrary thing. Even the free movement of this “body” going hand in hand with the freedom of kinesthetic courses would not make it a body and would not bring about localization. What is missing is the possibility of localizing visual contents on the visual body — the possibility of seeing the eye as touching the seen thing, so to speak, visually.

The third paragraph extends the analysis. Primary localization belongs only to tactile sensations and to the parallel temperature sensations, taste sensations, and (very imperfectly) smell sensations. They function simultaneously as thing-constituting and body-constituting — constituting the body both as thing and as localization-field, as bearer of sensations. All other sensations contribute to thing-constitution through interweaving of apprehensions, but cannot primarily contribute to body-constitution. Only kinesthetic sensations — despite functioning everywhere as motivating and thus not in the motivate — can beside the motivating function take on a secondary function of localization, through constant interweaving with primarily localized sensations, especially tactile ones. Their localization is rather indeterminate, but it is not without significance — it makes the unity between body and freely movable thing more intimate.

The fourth paragraph notes that in further consequence, all sensations constitutive for spatial-thingly features in the manner of adumbration that are incapable of localization (even secondary) gain a determinate relation to the body. Optical sensations, for example, gain a relation to the eye in the head — the head constituted through touch as a movable member of the body. The eye “directs” itself in seeing (in the free act of eye-movement) at the seen and accommodatingly adjusts itself. In all perceiving, the body with its sense organs is involved, and all experienced things have in experience a relation to the bodily. The body is not only involved regarding all experiences; interwoven with the psychic subject as it is, it is the subjective object to which all other objects are “opposite,” surrounding objects. Further, the body is involved not only regarding all experiences of thing-reality but also regarding the initially intuitively grasped whole value- and will-world. New kinds of objectivities constitute themselves under the assistance of such sensibilities, and these too are in their new being-stratum related to embodiment. The body has the distinguished position of being the center of all action and the center of all “working” in the originary sense in relation to other objects of the world.

The fifth paragraph summarizes a key result. Restricting ourselves to the mere givens of theoretical experience and excluding all values and practical objectivities, an important result of our consideration is that nature, the body, and (in its interweaving with the body) the soul constitute themselves in mutual reference to one another, all at once together.

#### §5. Solipsistic and Intersubjective Experience

The first paragraph of §5 notes that the studies of the relations between material thing and body and their constitution held to the lower experiential level — experience for a single experiencing subject — only to a certain degree. As soon as we speak of a single subject, we have already apprehended ourselves objectively, as a single over against an actual and possible plurality, as an object experienceable and identifiable by many. In this objectivity we apprehend everything experienced — things, bodies, souls, humans, animals — all integrated into the one objective spatio-temporal world. The components pertaining to this objectivity are in all natural experience; we have abstracted from them, and they can be easily abstracted, leaving the entire content of actual experience insofar as it is simply intuitive experience in which the perceived certifies itself in purely self-present givenness. The components beyond this point to possible “interpretation” of certain things as bodies and as bodies to which a pure I belongs — not the inwardly intuited one, but only a presentified one, with “another” flow of consciousness than the immanently intuited one, to which also perceptions and experiences of the very same things belong.

The second paragraph outlines the constitution of objectivity through intersubjective experience. On the lower stage, a constituted real unity for one pure I becomes, through empathy and mutual understanding, a member of a manifold in which the same objective unity constitutes itself. What was already a real thing for one pure I that had not yet performed empathy now becomes mere “appearance”; the appearance-manifolds are now the real unities corresponding to the various I’s, and through empathy they receive the apprehension “the same objective D.” The objective thing is something that only appears in these and is only through such appearances — hence demonstrable only through empathy as objective reality. This objectification also affects the body insofar as it is a material thing, and in further consequence the soul.

The third paragraph raises the higher-level question. Given the multiplying of subjects through empathy, the question arises how, relative to this relativism of the “objectively existing,” an objectively true can be worked out — how such a thing is possible at all. We cannot remain at the level of mere intuition once intersubjective communication reveals discrepancies unresolvable by any subjective experiential expansion. The objectification regarding all givens of “subjective” or “immediate” experience is the same level. It requires not only empathy but mutual understanding and, based upon this, a thinking that unifies the intersubjectively different experiences — which are nonetheless, through mutual understanding, apprehensible as experiences of the same — and makes the identical intelligible as presenting itself differently to different subjects. Making it intelligible for thought means explaining it. What does this explaining accomplish? Everywhere, as a matter of experience, we have a unity of manifolds. Experiencing is a seeing, an intuitive grasping of a unity on the basis of individual items of the manifold. But thinking explicates — it pursues the causal relations. For material things, this means doing physics and mathematizing.

#### §6. Significance of the Problem of Constitution; Phenomenology and Ontology

The first paragraph of §6 notes that such problems animate Kant’s critique of reason, though not yet in fully ripened form; they presuppose a phenomenology and an insight into the constitution of the lower experiential levels — a task Kant neither grasped nor, where he brushed against it, suspected in its magnitude. Hence he lacked the correct formulation of the problem. The problem of constitution in general, in its levels, the necessity of carrying out the associated systematic descriptions at all levels, of exhibiting the essential connections, of considering the various a priori possibilities — all this is foreign to Kant, however much his critique of reason inclines toward and occasionally approaches this problematic.

The second paragraph makes a key methodological distinction. The ontological mode of consideration is, so to speak, katastematic. It takes the unities in their identity and for the sake of their identity as something fixed. The phenomenological-constitutive consideration takes the unity in flux — namely as unity of a constituting flux — pursuing the movements, the courses, in which such unity and every component, side, real property of such unity is the identity-correlate. This consideration is in a way kinetic or “genetic” — a “genesis” belonging to a totally different, “transcendental” world from the natural and natural-scientific genesis. Locke once speaks of a “history” of consciousness, and evidently his intention was directed at such genesis — which he, leader of empiricism, immediately confused with psychological genesis and thereby thoroughly falsified. Every cognitive unity, especially every real one, has its “history,” or, correlatively, consciousness of this real has its “history” — its immanent teleology in the form of a regulated system of essentially belonging modes of manifestation and verification. What the thing itself is emerges in evidence on all sides of its essence only in its history, which brings the unities and their moments to relief by setting the constituting manifolds in motion.

The third paragraph draws the practical consequence. What concerns ontology: it is quite conceivable that someone performs so perfect an insight that he can, for example, purely and completely unfold the essence of spirit or nature and fix the axiomatic principles belonging thereto. But de facto, what succeeds for us so well in mathematics does not succeed equally in the real ontologies. Here phenomenology first educates us to complete seeing, and although what it seeks is not an essential doctrine of realities but of the constitution of realities and of the pure I and I-consciousness in general, it is only in conjunction with it that the full essential grasping of the real itself and thereby the founding of ontology according to categorial concepts and principles succeeds.

### Supplement II (to p. 11): The Personal I and the Body

This brief supplement observes that as soon as the I is touched upon, it governs our apprehensions. It is not only alive as pure subject of the manifold experiencing, thinking, valuing, practical attitudes, but is a favored object of our attitudes and, as such, a personal I that in its doing and suffering practices “self-preservation,” that has personal properties, character-dispositions, and manifold dispositions, subject to praise and blame, needing to protect itself against the dangers of the thing- and human-world or to win them over as tools. The body with its organs “reacts” to external things; as a consequence the I has certain perceptions of the things and takes a position toward them. On one side stand the things, among them the body (also just a thing), but a thing to which a soul-life is attached — initially perceptions and sensations (which remain unseparated) — which the soul, given its causal relation to the body-thing, peculiarly localizes in certain sense-fields upon the body.

### Supplement III (to p. 17): Identity of the Thing and Identity of the Soul

This supplement contrasts the identity of the material thing with that of the soul. The material thing may change however it will; to its essence (in the sense of the apprehension of matter) belongs the possibility that the circle of thingly states can return upon itself. The material real must remain identical so that, upon return to the same circumstances, it has the same states — however infinitely improbable this may factually be. The same holds for the body-real insofar as sensitivity is given in such a way that if the body is the same in its materiality and material states, it would also have to be the same as body, and upon return to the identical bodily state, the sensation-stratum would also be the same. Quite otherwise the soul: to its essence, under the title of formation of dispositions, belongs one-sided changeability. It can principially not return to the same state.

### Supplement IV (to §§7–9): Type — Empirical Classification

The first paragraph of Supplement IV affirms the possibility of a pure grasping of types of phenomenologically concrete configurations. Taking the perception-appearances of one and the same human being, of one and the same house, etc., we find within the continuity of appearances belonging to the same object, in many ways, individual appearances and appearance-series of typical similarity. Moving to greater generality — the type of the Caucasian human, the Negro, the Mongol, etc. — a general typics also corresponds regarding the appearance-manifolds. And if we start from a still more general intuitive type of a human being in general, an animal in general, etc., we see that we have inexhaustible infinities of pure types of experienceable realities, and even more so of types of perception-appearances. A systematic, exhaustive classification of all types of perception-appearances, let alone of all types of possible cogitationes, is unthinkable.

The second paragraph asks what remains for phenomenology. The answer: even if we cannot determine the individual types of all possible general types, we can easily gain a height of generality allowing fixation in exact and always re-identifiable concepts — for example, the type “thing-perception,” “outer” perception, thing-intuition in general (not just perception but also retention, recollection, anticipatory intuition, mere phantasy). The phenomenological interest does not and cannot go to conceptual fixation of all possible typical particularizations lying under these fixed generalities. Phenomenology cannot master the typical particularizations down to the undifferentiable concretions as geometry masters all possible pure spatial shapes through its axiomatics and fundamental concepts. An essential difference of phenomenological essential determinations from geometrical ones emerges: geometrical species-formations are not type-differentiations. The type is an intuitive unity, but geometry does not give a systematics of these sensory, ideally turned types or laws for such types; it has its own kind of concept-formation and its own genera of ideas — geometrical purity excludes the typical of sensuously intuitive givens.

The third paragraph identifies what can initially be fixed in phenomenology: the sharply demarcated class-ideas of concrete cogitationes, and the essential analyses that fix the ideas of real (reell) components and correlates belonging to these class-ideas. It turns out that the abstract material — the non-self-sufficient components and formal character-traits from which all cogitationes are built — goes back to certain essential fundamental genera. This thoroughgoing elementary analysis — the exhibition of elements, characters, and forms lying in very different dimensions, both the real and those belonging to the correlate — is an enormous and extraordinarily difficult task. Since everything proceeds in the attitude of reduction, every determination implicitly contains an essential law for possible singular individualities.

The fourth paragraph addresses the relationship to the physical sphere. In the sphere of psychological realities, the situation is fundamentally different from that in physical realities. To the essence of spirit, of spiritual states and characteristics, mathematical determinability does not belong. Psychic acts and states like phantasies, memories, judgments, wishes, hopes, volitions are not measurable, not “exactly” determinable as approximations to ideal mathematical structures. There is indeed something like continuity, and in many directions; but psychic phenomena are not decomposable into components each of which could ideally be incorporated into a mathematical manifold, and their complexion harbors no mathematizable substance in the sense of matter. The experiential stream is no mathematical manifold. We can indeed think exact ideas into it — as when we speak of time-phases of perception-appearances or of a phenomenon declining from clarity into darkness. But there is here principially a lack of possibilities for determining what is given through exact concepts in a fixed method of approximation. The gradual gradations are gradations of a rich qualitative content in manifold genera and species that could never be mathematically ordered.

The fifth paragraph explains the deeper reason. In natural science, the immediately experienced nature with its immediately experienced properties is only an incomperfectly given, always only appearing nature that always has before it the nature still to be worked out. In progressing from experience to experience, through level upon level of appearances, mathematically ideal concepts guide us — concepts related to the pure concepts extractable from the appearance-content itself, but not identical with them. The concept-formations of mathematical optics root in experiencing with its experiential colors but are not immanent to them. As for the consciousness-phenomena we want to make into objects as they are in themselves, they are naturally not something representing itself through something else. The concepts for their determination can only be abstracted from them themselves, in view of their givenness and adequate givenness. Where we find the flowing, we must form the idea of the flow; where we find the vague, the idea of vagueness; where we can find only typical differences, we must form type-concepts. We do not therefore sink into a flow. We have barely surveyable multitudes of strictly graspable differences, and even where lower concretions are only fluidly and typically distinguishable, we find everywhere at higher generality firm, though not mathematical, differences — concepts not to be reached through fluid transitions. Thus perception in general, more specifically perception of the physical, is something absolutely fixed and describable in its generality. Perception and assertion, perceiving and willing, willing and enjoying — these are phenomenological class-differences as firmly and absolutely separated as the imaginative differences between color and tone in the sphere of outer experience.

## Afterword

### Preliminary Remark

The Afterword begins with a preliminary remark explaining that the following pages give the text of the explanatory exposition Husserl appended as a foreword to the (then imminent) English edition of his *Ideas*. These remarks should be useful also to the German reader, since Husserl addresses the general misunderstandings that have obscured the true sense of his transcendental phenomenology. Trapped in the thought-habits of the philosophical tradition, readers overlook the radically new in phenomenology’s method and field of work. There is no consideration here of the specifically German situation — the life-philosophy striving for dominance, the new anthropology, the philosophy of “existence.” Hence no consideration of the charges of “intellectualism” or “rationalism” levied from those quarters. In the concept of philosophy Husserl restores the most originary idea of philosophy — the one that, since Plato’s first firm formulation, underlies our European philosophy and science and designates an imperishable task. Philosophy is, for Husserl, in its idea, the universal and in the radical sense “strict” science: science from ultimate grounding, or equivalently, from ultimate self-responsibility. All the objections — of intellectualism, of sticking in abstract one-sidedness, of principially never reaching the originally concrete, practically active subjectivity and the problems of “existence,” likewise the metaphysical problems — rest on misunderstandings, and ultimately on the failure to understand the principially new in the “phenomenological reduction” and thus the ascent from mundane subjectivity (the human being) to “transcendental subjectivity.”

### Section 1

The first section recapitulates what the *Ideas* attempt: the founding of a new science on the ground of an absolutely autonomous field of experience — that of “transcendental subjectivity.” Transcendental subjectivity is not a product of speculative construction but a realm of direct experience, though one that for essential reasons had remained inaccessible until now. Transcendental experience becomes possible only through a radical change of the attitude in which natural, worldly experience runs its course — a change called “phenomenological reduction.” The transcendental phenomenology of this volume is not founded as an empirical science of the experience-facts of this field; factual data serve only as examples, much as empirical examples serve the mathematician. The concern is an “a priori” (eidetically oriented) science that takes the factual experiential field of transcendental subjectivity merely as pure possibility, placing it alongside freely varied pure intuitive possibilities, and bringing out the unbreakable essential structure of transcendental subjectivity as its “a priori.” The eidetic phenomenology is restricted in this book to the realm of “description” — immediately insightful essential structures — since this realm already forms a systematically closed infinity of essential features. Excluded remains the problematic of the temporalizing of the immanent time-sphere. Reserved for the second volume were the I-problems, the problems of personality, and the transcendental problem of “empathy.”

### Section 2

Section 2 warns against a common misunderstanding: when it is said that all radical-scientific philosophy rests on the foundation of phenomenology and is in a broader sense “phenomenological philosophy,” this does not mean that philosophy is only an a priori science. The task of this volume — a science of the eidetic essence of transcendental subjectivity — does not imply that a science of the factual transcendental subjectivity is already accomplished. The science of pure possibilities must precede the science of factual actualities and serve as its concrete logic. So it will be for transcendental philosophy too, although the dignity of the achievement of the system of the transcendental a priori is far higher.

### Section 3

Section 3 identifies the main source of difficulty: the distinction between transcendental phenomenology and “descriptive” or “phenomenological” psychology. The attitude-change called phenomenological reduction is performed by the philosophizing person from the natural attitude in which one experiences oneself as a human person living among others in the world. In the purely inner turn, following exclusively “inner experience” (self-experience and “empathy”), a purely descriptive cognition of psychic life as it is in itself is gained — a “phenomenological psychology” on the ground of inner intuition. In the right method, not merely meager typifying and classificatory descriptions but a great self-standing science results — and especially when one first aims not at a science of factual data but at an essential science, asking about the invariant essentially own structures of a soul, a community of psychic life: its a priori.

The phenomenological reduction transforms the psychological subjectivity: it loses what gives it validity as something real in the naively experienced world — the sense of being the soul of a body existing in the given spatio-temporal nature. All experience-based judgments about this world, and all positive sciences resting on natural worldly experience, are excluded — including psychology. On the other hand, the gaze becomes free for the universal phenomenon: “consciousness-world purely as such” — purely as conscious in the manifold flowing consciousness-life, and in particular as appearing “originally” in manifold “harmonizing” experiences, characterized in consciousness as “actually existing.” This universal phenomenon — “world existing for me” (and “for us”) — the phenomenologist makes the field of his new theoretical interest.

### Section 4

Section 4 addresses the ways to the beginning of philosophy. The necessary starting-point is the natural-naive attitude that has the world of experience as its “self-evident” ground of being. Husserl chose the path that proceeds egologically as a self-reflection staying within purely inner-psychological intuition, leading finally to the awareness that, in consistently exclusive inner experience, one has a self-enclosed, internally coherent own-being. To this own-being belongs all actual and possible experience through which the objective world is there, with all its experiential verifications. I, as this absolutely positable own-being — the open-ended field of pure phenomenological givens and their inseparable unity — am the “transcendental I”; the absolute positing means I no longer have the world given in advance as simply existing, but now exclusively given is my I purely as the being-in-itself that is world-experiencing, world-verifying.

### Section 5

Section 5 discusses the transcendental-phenomenological idealism arising from this reflection — in the sharpest contrast to all psychologistic idealism. The presentation in the relevant chapter of *Ideas I* suffers from incompleteness, as Husserl concedes: it lacks the explicit treatment of the problem of transcendental solipsism, or rather of transcendental intersubjectivity. The supplements were to be brought by the second volume. The objection of solipsism should never have been raised against phenomenological idealism by anyone who understood the presentation more deeply; it could only have been raised as an objection to the incompleteness of the presentation. Husserl declares he has nothing to retake regarding the transcendental-phenomenological idealism: he still considers every form of the usual philosophical realism principially absurd, and no less so every idealism to which realism opposes itself.

The phenomenological idealism does not deny the actual existence of the real world — as if it thought it were an illusion. Its sole task is to clarify the sense of this world — exactly the sense in which it counts for everyone as actually existing and with actual right. The result of the phenomenological sense-clarification of the mode of being of the real world is that only the transcendental subjectivity has the being-sense of absolute being, that only it is “irrelative” (relative only to itself), while the real world has an essential relativity to transcendental subjectivity, since it can have its sense as existing only as an intentional sense-formation of transcendental subjectivity. The natural life and its natural world-having is limited only in that, continuing in its “naturalness,” it has no motives for passing into the transcendental attitude. All this gains its full sense only when the phenomenological disclosure of the transcendental ego is carried so far that the experience of co-subjects contained in it has won its reduction to transcendental experience — when it shows that “transcendental subjectivity” means not only I as transcendental I-myself but also the co-subjects verifying themselves in my transcendental life as transcendental, in the co-verifying transcendental we-community. The transcendental intersubjectivity is that in which the real world, as objective, as existing “for everyone,” constitutes itself.

### Section 6

Section 6 addresses those who set aside the phenomenological reduction as a philosophically irrelevant peculiarity, thereby leaving only an a priori psychology, which they then identify with Brentano’s psychology of intentionality. Husserl insists on the essential difference. Even Brentano’s “psychognosy” — his purely descriptive inner-experience-based psychology — though “phenomenological” in the broad sense of that term, belongs to the whole tradition from Locke through Mill and is characterized by the naturalist conception of the pure soul as a complex or heap of temporally coexisting and successive data running under their own or psychophysical rules. Descriptive psychology would then have to classify the basic kinds of these “sense-data” and the elementary forms of their complexion; explanatory psychology would then seek the rules of causal formation and transformation, similarly to natural science.

Husserl explains the essentially new insight of transcendental phenomenology (and thereby also for descriptive psychology): a concrete description of the consciousness-sphere as a closed sphere of intentionality (only so is it concretely given) has a quite different sense from nature-descriptions. Concrete description of consciousness-experiences necessarily requires also the description of the objects conscious (intended) in the respective experiences “as such” — as they inseparably belong to the experience itself as its objective meaning. Further, intentional synthesis comes into consideration: one and the same intentional object as such is, in purely inner-psychological regard, an ideal index for a manifold of firmly correlated consciousness-modes whose typics essentially belongs together with the typical kind of the intentional object. One must not merely say every consciousness is consciousness-of and then distinguish types; one must interrogate the different categories of “objects” — purely as such of possible consciousness — and trace back the essential forms of possible “manifolds” through whose synthesis the consciousness of identity of a respective object of the relevant category comes about. The universal task of disclosing the phenomenological “constitution” of objects — here projected back onto the natural-psychological attitude — emerges.

### Section 7

The final section reaffirms that this work, as a philosophical one, has a limited task. It seeks to be a first attempt — grown from decades of reflection directed exclusively at this goal — to set in motion the radical beginning of a philosophy that, in Kant’s words, “will be able to present itself as a science.” The author had early and to this day to renounce the ideal of working out for himself a systematically closed logic, ethics, metaphysics justified from absolutely compelling insight. The reason: the conviction was and remains indubitable that philosophy cannot naively begin straight ahead, cannot settle as positive sciences do on the pregiven, unquestioned ground of worldly experience. That they do so is what creates all their foundational problems and paradoxes. A philosophy with problematic foundations, with paradoxes resting on the unclarity of fundamental concepts, is no philosophy — it contradicts its very sense. Only in radical reflections on the sense and possibility of its undertaking can philosophy take root. Having had to lower the ideal of his philosophical striving practically to that of a genuine beginner, the author has in old age at least arrived at the complete certainty of being able to call himself a genuine beginner. He sees the infinitely open land of true philosophy, the “Promised Land,” spread out before him, which he himself will no longer experience as already cultivated. May one laugh at this confidence, but let one see for oneself whether it does not have some ground, in the fragments presented here as beginning phenomenology. He would gladly hope that those who come after will take up these beginnings, steadily carry them further, and also improve their great imperfections — imperfections unavoidable in scientific beginnings.
