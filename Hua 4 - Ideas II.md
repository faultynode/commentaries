# Commentary on Edmund Husserl, *Ideen zu einer reinen Phänomenologie und phänomenologischen Philosophie, Zweites Buch: Phänomenologische Untersuchungen zur Konstitution* (Husserliana IV)

## Editor’s Introduction

The editor, Marly Biemel, opens by situating the *Ideen II* within the broader arc of Husserl’s intellectual development. After the publication of the *Logical Investigations* in 1901, Husserl entered a period of doubt, intensive work, and sparse publication. During this time he underwent one of the great transformations of his thinking, marked by the discovery of the phenomenological reduction. He first lectured on this new method in 1907 under the title *Die Idee der Phänomenologie*. It was only in 1913, at the urging of colleagues and students, that he resolved to publish the systematic work that would serve as the actual program of phenomenology: *Ideas Pertaining to a Pure Phenomenology and Phenomenological Philosophy*.

Biemel then outlines the plan of the original design. The work was to comprise three books. The first book, the *General Introduction to Phenomenology*, was devoted to fundamental methodological considerations and the analysis of pure consciousness made possible by the phenomenological reduction, and appeared in 1913. The second book was announced as treating certain especially significant problem groups whose systematic formulation and typical solution would be prerequisite for clarifying the difficult relationships of phenomenology to the physical natural sciences, to psychology, and to the human sciences, as well as to all aprioristic sciences. It was thus to contain two parts: analyses and science-theoretical considerations, in order to deepen substantially the understanding of phenomenology won in the first book and to gain an incomparably richer knowledge of its vast problem domains. The third book was to be devoted to the idea of philosophy, showing that genuine philosophy, whose idea is to realize the idea of absolute knowledge, is rooted in pure phenomenology, and that the systematically rigorous grounding and execution of this first of all philosophies is the indispensable precondition for any metaphysics and any other philosophy that will be able to present itself as science.

### Later Changes to This Plan

Biemel explains that the text of the present edition comprises: the second book, titled “Phenomenological Analyses of Constitution” in three sections, and the third book, “Phenomenology and the Foundations of the Sciences.” The third book thus corresponds, according to the original announcement, to the second part of what had been planned as the second book. The volume that was to be dedicated to the idea of philosophy is missing. How did this shift in emphasis come about?

The editor reports that the Husserl Archive possesses a manuscript for the second book written in 1912, immediately after the first, which corresponds entirely to the announced plan. No manuscript for the third book exists from this period, yet Husserl still held to the original plan as late as 1922. Why did Husserl not publish the second book immediately after the first?

While the part temporarily designated “science-theoretical” remained unchanged from later revisions and was retained in the 1912 version, the analyses were repeatedly reworked by Husserl from 1912 to about 1928. The question of the constitution of objectualities in consciousness became during these fifteen years a central problem of Husserlian philosophy and gained a singular significance for phenomenology. It was no longer a matter of drafting “phenomenological sketches”; rather, the actual task of phenomenology now became the clarification of the constitution of every reality. Already in 1913, under the title “Nature and Spirit,” Husserl worked out a large manuscript on the constitution problem, and his seminar exercises in the following years were devoted above all to this theme. What was initially meant only as indication and transition grew into an independent task. This shift in emphasis is responsible for the fact that Husserl not only did not leave the second book in its first draft, but expanded the constitutional analyses so extensively that the treatment of the relation of phenomenology to the sciences could no longer find a place in the second book and was therefore given as a third book. The so-called “science-theoretical part” thus takes the place of that “first philosophy” announced in 1912 but never written within the framework of the *Ideen*.

Biemel then provides a detailed account of the various reworkings of the second book. The very compact presentation of the 1912 manuscript treated the constitution of sense-intuitive nature and, in direct continuation, the constitution of body and soul. From a recapitulation of the main results of these analyses, Husserl then moved on to the sciences corresponding to these objectualities, which in turn were grounded by the aprioristic sciences. The latter received their actual illumination from phenomenology.

In 1915 Husserl produced a new working-out of the second book, incorporating material both from the 1912 manuscript and from his manuscripts for the lectures of 1913 and 1915. Edith Stein, who had been among Husserl’s students since 1913, produced (probably around 1916) a Kurrent-script copy of this stenographic manuscript, for which she also drew on the first draft of 1912 (for the third book, exclusively the latter). This first Stein copy of the second book comprised 294 folio pages for the second book and 238 pages for the “science-theoretical part.” The first Stein copy is not preserved as a whole. It contained §§1–17 of the present text, from §18 only the introduction, the first half of point (a), and point (f) in its entirety, then corresponded again to §§19–33, with only §§25 and 33 being less extensive. At §33 the text of the 1915 version breaks off.

Around 1918 Edith Stein produced a second working-out of the second book, also in Kurrent script on folio sheets, but more than twice as extensive as the first (over 700 pages). It already corresponded in its general outline to the present text, aside from the supplements. By drawing on numerous other manuscripts, she expanded §§18, 25, and 33 and assembled the text from page 143 onward entirely anew. From there on, no coherent manuscript was available for the second section. The manuscripts from 1912 and 1915 were combined with numerous other manuscripts, especially from the war years, with dates extending to January 1917. It is to be assumed that Husserl gave Edith Stein a selection of manuscripts whose use he left to her discretion. Even within the first 33 paragraphs, which are relatively unified manuscript-wise, parts of the 1912 draft were incorporated in a criss-cross fashion that is surprising at first glance. For the second half of the section, the most diverse manuscripts alternate with one another. This procedure, corresponding to the taking-up, referring-back, and anticipating of the train of thought, receives its justification from Husserl’s own 1912 remark that “it is an important result of our consideration that ‘nature’ and ‘body,’ in their interweaving, and with this again the ‘soul,’ constitute themselves in mutual reference to one another, all together.” The unity of the constitution of thing, body, and soul, their properly understood “simultaneity,” excludes every psychologistic theory of strata; it is more strongly emphasized in the first draft than in the later versions.

For the third section (“The Constitution of the Spiritual World”), there is again a large main Husserl manuscript, though Edith Stein once more drew on other analyses for expansion. Although written already in 1913, this manuscript was taken into account only in the second Stein working-out; indeed, its sheets were in part inserted uncopied into the Stein version.

Until 1924 work on *Ideen II* seems to have rested. Only in the third section of the second Stein version did Husserl enter annotations, supplements, and changes. In 1924/25 Professor Ludwig Landgrebe, who had become Husserl’s private assistant a year earlier, produced a typewritten copy of *Ideen II and III* as they existed in Edith Stein’s handwriting. He had to take into account Husserl’s proposed changes in the third section as well as a number of supplementary sheets, and he also compared the text with the originals to eliminate copying errors. He made no independent expansions of the main text, but he did draw in numerous manuscripts and manuscript parts as supplements (Beilagen): both parallel passages and texts that are close in content to the *Ideen* but go beyond their immediate problematic and offer outlooks on the surrounding complex of questions.

For the text of the present edition, the Landgrebe working-out had to serve as the basis, since it is the latest and most complete. A renewed copying was made necessary by numerous annotations, supplements, and appendices that Husserl inserted into this version between 1924 and 1928. These insertions had already been transcribed from the shorthand by Professor S. Strasser. Biemel then outlines the editorial principles governing how Husserl’s later annotations were handled: corrections and supplements were incorporated directly into the text where possible; outlooks on new problem domains and critical remarks were given in footnotes; attached sheets that could not be directly incorporated were given as supplements; and tersely noted critical remarks expressing dissatisfaction without offering a new presentation were relegated to the text-critical appendix.

The editor notes that the division of the text into paragraphs was taken from the Landgrebe version, deriving largely from the Stein working-outs. The chapter divisions of the second book, however, were only tentatively entered by Husserl into the Landgrebe version: in the first section Husserl provides the breaks and nearly complete headings, in the second section the breaks and keyword hints for the titles, and in the third section only vague indications. These chapter headings therefore come from the editor. The titles of the books also come from the editor.

Biemel closes with acknowledgments and a note about UNESCO’s support for the editorial project.

## Second Book: Phenomenological Investigations Concerning Constitution

## First Section: The Constitution of Material Nature

## First Chapter: The Idea of Nature in General

### §1. Preliminary Delimitation of the Concepts of Nature and Experience (Exclusion of Meaning-Predicates)

Husserl opens the investigations by orienting them toward nature as the object of natural science. Nature, he says provisionally, is the entire spatiotemporal universe, the total domain of possible experience — which is why the expressions “natural science” and “experiential science” are commonly used interchangeably. But the universe encompasses everything “worldly,” yet not in the full sense all individual objects whatsoever. The question is therefore: how does nature, and natural perception or natural experience, specifically determine itself?

He recalls the initial determination that nature is the field of transcendent realities, specifically spatiotemporal realities. But the concept of the real spatiotemporal objectuality does not suffice, as will soon become apparent. Not all predicates that are truthfully attributable to spatiotemporal realities, and that we actually do attribute to them, belong to the essence of that natural object that is the correlate of the idea of natural science. The investigation should concern nature in the sense of this correlation. It is by no means the way of this science to restrict itself arbitrarily in the selection of its objects or of the predicates pertaining to its objects. Rather, an essential idea of nature lies at the foundation, even if undefined. Correlatively: the consciousness that functions as natural-scientific experience and hence also as natural-scientific experiential thinking has its own essential phenomenological unity, and this consciousness has in nature its essential correlate. A governing apperception determines in advance what counts as a natural-scientific object and what does not, hence what is nature in the natural-scientific sense and what is not. This is what must be brought to clarity.

In this regard, it is evident from the outset that all the predicates we attribute to things under the headings of agreeableness, beauty, utility, practical appropriateness, perfection — values, goods, purposive objects, tools, good-for-something, and so on — remain entirely out of consideration. They do not concern the natural scientist; they do not belong to nature in his sense.

### §2. The Natural-Scientific Attitude as Theoretical Attitude

Husserl announces that the foregoing point will become comprehensible when the type of attitude of the natural-scientifically intuiting and thinking subject is examined more closely. Through its phenomenological description we will recognize that what this subject calls nature is precisely the intentional correlate of the experience carried out in this attitude. He proposes the following thesis: the thematic attitude of natural experience and experiential research of the natural scientist is the doxic-theoretical attitude. In contrast to it there are other attitudes, namely the valuing attitude (in the broadest sense, the attitude of evaluating as beautiful and good) and the practical attitude. The talk of attitudes obviously refers to the respective subject, and accordingly we speak of the theoretical or also the cognizing subject, the valuing subject, and the practical subject.

Nature exists for the theoretical subject; it belongs to its correlate-sphere. But this does not simply mean that nature is already completely determined as the correlate of a possible theoretical, cognizing subject. Nature is the object of possible cognition, but it does not exhaust the total domain of such objects. Nature as mere nature contains no values, no artworks, and so on — which are nonetheless objects of possible cognition and science. For now, however, we consider the general aspects.

### §3. Analysis of the Theoretical Attitude, of Theoretical Interest

Husserl asks what the theoretical attitude means. It is not simply determined by the conscious experiences we call doxic (objectivating), that is, acts of presentation, judging, and thinking (here always meaning non-neutralized acts), since doxic experiences also occur in the valuing and practical attitudes. Rather, what is characteristic lies in the manner in which such experiences are carried out in the function of cognition. This happens not merely in general such that a glance of the subject passes through them to the represented, the perceived, the remembered, the thought; rather, the subject lives in these acts in a phenomenologically distinguished manner.

Husserl draws a crucial distinction: it is one thing to see, that is, to experience at all, to have something in the perceptual field — and quite another to carry out seeing in the specific sense of attending, to “live” in seeing in a preeminent way, to be active as an ego specifically “believing,” judging, to carry out an act of judging as a cogito, to be directed with an active glance upon the objective, to be specifically “meaning”-directed. It is one thing simply to be conscious that the sky is blue, and another to live in the carrying out of the judgment that the sky is now blue, attending to it, grasping it, specifically meaning it. Doxic experiences in this attitude, in this mode of carrying out — where I think, carry out an act in the specific sense, posit the subject and then posit the predicate, and so on — are what we call theoretical acts. In them, an object is not merely present for the ego in general; rather, the ego is directed toward it in an attending manner (and then in a thinking, actively positing manner), and is thus grasping-directed — it is “theoretically” objectivating in the actual sense.

### §4. Theoretical Acts and “Pre-giving” Intentional Experiences

Husserl now considers the situation when the subject is a theoretical subject in this sense, which it is not perpetually. It is then “objectivating” in the specific sense: grasping and positing an objectuality of whatever kind as being (in the validity-mode of belief) and further determining it in explicative syntheses, possibly in predicative-judicative fashion. But the relevant objectuality is already constituted in consciousness before these theoretical acts, through certain intentional experiences — and not through all experiences that can be designated as related to this objectuality. In other words, “related to it” does not mean that the glance of specific meaning that prevails in all theoretical acts passes through all of them; rather, it passes only through those that are sense-giving or determining for the theoretically grasped object as such. The remaining experiences, for example feeling-experiences of this or that particular kind, are lived through; as intentional experiences they are also constitutive — they constitute new object-strata for the relevant object. But the subject is not theoretically directed toward these strata; they do not constitute the respective theoretically meant and judicatively determined object as such (or help determine this object in a theoretical function). Only through a turning of the theoretical glance or a change in theoretical interest do they pass from the stage of pre-theoretical constitution to that of theoretical constitution; the new sense-strata enter the framework of theoretical sense, and a new or newly and more properly meant object becomes the object of grasping and theoretical determination in new theoretical acts. The entire consciousness-intention is thereby essentially altered, and even the acts responsible for the other sense-bestowals undergo a phenomenological modification.

That this is a necessary state of affairs is shown by the fact that even the theoretical acts through which the pure subject relates to a given object delimited by a constitutive sense (for example, a natural object) — however they may occur as subjectifying, attributing, collecting, relativizing, and other acts — also exercise a constitutive performance: “categorical” objectualities constitute themselves (in a quite determinate sense: thought-objectualities), which in turn become theoretical objects only when the theoretical subject directs itself toward these new objectualities (above all, states of affairs, collections, etc.) in a meaning-setting manner, carrying out new acts that grasp them in their being and theoretically determine them — subject-acts, predicate-acts, and so on of a higher level.

With reference to these higher-level acts — always introduced by turns of the glance of specific meaning, which would be a special kind of “reflection” — the categorical objectualities constituted through the preceding theoretical acts are pre-givennesses. (A state of affairs that holds analogously also in other cases where feeling-acts function as pre-constitution.) When the turn of the glance occurs, the pre-giving acts, in our case the categorical acts, have already elapsed in their original mode of execution; they are no longer actual steps of spontaneous meaning and theoretical determining, of subject-positing, predicate-positing, stepwise collecting, and so on. They are alive only in another, essentially modified form of “still” having and holding-in-grip what has been constituted (as already happens in the further development of categorical acts with respect to those preceding them in the chain), together with the casting of a meaning-ray back upon their synthetic “results.”

Husserl emphasizes that these difficult relations must be well understood. It belongs to the peculiar nature of the theoretical attitude and its theoretical acts that in them the objects in a certain way already lie before us that are only then first made theoretical. Pre-theoretically, objects are already constituted, only they are not theoretically appropriated, are not meant objects in the distinguished sense, let alone objects of acts that theoretically determine them.

As can be seen from the foregoing, the “pre-given” objects can themselves originally “stem from” theoretical acts, and thus already be theoretical objects relative to those acts. This can occur in various ways: first, in the manner that such theoretical objects have just now originally constituted themselves in “genuinely” (that is, spontaneously) carried-out theoretical acts, and that, following upon this, a grasping-meaning glance of the theoretical subject is directed upon what has been constituted. This is made possible by the fact that the individual spontaneous act-steps, after their execution, are retentionally preserved in consciousness, in the modified form of passive states, and that finally at the end of the entire thought process there stands a unified state of consciousness that, analogously to a simple presentation, can function as a pre-giving consciousness and take up a new theoretical direction of the glance upon its unitarily conscious object.

But obviously other cases are also possible. For example, in the form of a recollective occurrence a state of affairs constituted earlier in spontaneous and articulated thinking can “resurface.” It does so in the medium of a reproductive modification of the resulting state of the earlier thinking, and this now functions as the pre-giving consciousness for the acts of the new theoretical attitude. The same holds for theoretical “occurrences” in which new, not merely recollectively re-presented, states of affairs surface as certainties, possibilities, probabilities, and function as a “stimulus” for the thinking relating to them.

The pre-givennesses of acts of a theoretical attitude (that is, the categorical acts carried out in original thinking-spontaneity) cannot always trace back again to theoretical acts from which they stem. We arrive in every case at pre-given objectualities that do not stem from theoretical acts, that therefore constitute themselves in intentional experiences which contribute nothing of logico-categorical formations to them.

What is said about pre-givennesses of theoretical acts holds likewise for other spontaneous acts and their pre-givennesses; the discussion requires a corresponding supplement. Running parallel to the theoretical attitude as possibilities are the axiological and practical attitudes. In this regard, analogous results can be established. Valuing acts (taken in the broadest sense, as every kind of acts of liking and disliking, of every kind of position-taking of the feeling-sphere and of every kind of syntheses proper to and carried out in the unity of a feeling-consciousness) can relate to pre-given objectualities, and their intentionality proves itself at the same time as constitutive for objectualities of a higher level — analogues of the categorical objectualities of the logical sphere. We are dealing with a class of objectualities that constitute themselves as spontaneous products, as polythetic formations of polythetically unified (joined into the unity of a constituting act) producing acts. They are not only founded objectualities in general and in this sense objectualities of a higher level, but precisely as spontaneous products, objectualities that originally constitute themselves and that only as such come to possible original givenness.

Husserl illustrates this with an example. Earlier he contrasted the mere seeing-consciousness of the blue sky with the theoretical carrying-out of this act. We no longer carry out the seeing in this distinguished way when, seeing the radiantly blue sky, we live in delight over it. If we do that, we are not in the theoretical or cognitive attitude but in the feeling-attitude. Conversely, pleasure can perfectly well be present while we are theoretically directed — as when, as physicists, we are observationally directed toward the radiantly blue sky — but now we do not live in the pleasure. This is an essential phenomenological modification of the pleasure, or of the seeing and judging, depending on whether we pass from one attitude to the other. This peculiar change of attitude belongs as an ideal possibility to all acts, and to all of them there corresponds the corresponding phenomenological modification. That is, all acts that are not from the outset theoretical can be transformed into theoretical acts through a change of attitude.

We can consider a picture “enjoyingly.” Then we live in the carrying-out of aesthetic pleasure, in the pleasure-attitude, which is a “enjoying” one. We can then judge the picture with the eyes of the art critic or art historian as “beautiful.” Then we live in the carrying-out of the theoretical, the judging attitude, no longer in the valuing, pleasure-giving attitude. If we understand “valuing” or “holding as valuable” as feeling-behavior, and indeed as such in which we live, then it is no theoretical act. If we understand it, as equivocally often happens, as a judicative holding-as-valuable, possibly predicating about the value, then a theoretical and not a feeling-behavior is thereby expressed. In the latter case, in the appraisal as value as it has arisen from the attitude of purely enjoying devotion, the artwork is objective in an entirely different way: it is something intuited, but not only sensibly intuited (we do not live in the carrying-out of perceiving) but axiologically intuited. In the active devotion of aesthetic “being-pleased-in-occupation-with-it,” of aesthetic joy understood as an act, the object is an object of enjoying. On the other hand, in aesthetic judging and appraising, it is no longer object in merely enjoying devotion but object in the special doxo-thetic sense: the intuited is given in the property-character (the character that constitutes a so-being) of aesthetic delightfulness. This is a new “theoretical” objectivity, and indeed a peculiar one of a higher level.

Living in merely sensible intuiting, of the lowest level, carrying it out theoretically, we have a mere thing theoretically grasped in the simplest way. Passing over into aesthetic value-grasping and value-judging, we have more than a mere thing: we have the thing with the so-being character (or the explicit predicate) of value; we have a valuable thing. This value-object, which in its objective sense includes the so-being character of worth, is the correlate of theoretical value-grasping. It is thus an object of a higher level.

We note that the generally original value-judgment — generally speaking, that every consciousness that originally constitutes a value-object as such — necessarily has a component that belongs to the feeling-sphere. The most original value-constitution takes place in feeling as that pre-theoretical (in a broad sense of the word) enjoying devotion of the feeling ego-subject, for which Husserl says he has used the expression “Wertnehmung” (value-perception, or value-apprehension) in lectures for decades. The expression designates an analogue of perception (Wahrnehmung) belonging to the feeling-sphere; perception in the doxic sphere signifies the original (self-grasping) being-there of the ego at the object itself. Thus in the feeling-sphere: that feeling in which the ego lives in consciousness, feeling itself to be present at the object “itself” — and that is precisely what is meant by the talk of “enjoying.” Just as there is, so to speak, a presenting from afar, an empty presenting-meaning without being-there-oneself, so there is an empty object-related feeling; and just as the former fulfills itself in intuitive presenting, so the empty feeling is fulfilled through enjoying. In both cases we have parallel striving intentions: presentational (cognitive, tending toward cognition) striving and valuational, tending toward expectation, toward enjoyment. The similarity is what the parallel of expressions “Wahrnehmen” (perceiving) and “Wertnehmen” (value-perceiving) was meant to express. “Value-feeling” remains the more general expression for value-consciousness, and as feeling it lies in every mode of such consciousness, including the non-original mode.

It should also be noted that even in a value-apprehending (and, doxically turned, value-intuiting) consciousness, the intuition can be “inadequate” — namely anticipating, and thus equipped with emptily anticipating horizons of feeling, similarly to an outer perception. In a single glance I grasp the beauty of an old Gothic building, which I fully grasp only in a running-through value-apprehension, yielding with the corresponding doxic turn a fuller value-intuition. The fleeting glance can ultimately be entirely emptily anticipating, pre-grasping the beauty on the basis of indications without anything actually being grasped. And this feeling-anticipation already suffices for a doxic turn and predication. The same holds everywhere, including in the will-sphere. There is a difference between actual willing, living in the will-attitude, and being theoretically directed, positing and judging the willed as practically demanded, and the like.

We can live in the willing decision-making or in the executing doing: then certain presentational acts are presupposed, possibly thinking-acts of various levels, and valuing acts. But all of these are acts not carried out in the distinguished sense. The actual carrying-out lies in the willing and doing. The attitude changes and becomes theoretical when we look theoretically-graspingly at the resolution and the deed, possibly judging on the basis of this theoretically intuiting or presenting behavior.

What is at issue here is in fact a matter of general essential peculiarities belonging to all acts with a founded structure. The experiencing subject can first of all live in the carrying-out of an act, and equivalent to this is the expression: in the distinguished sense the ego is directed toward what is objectively given, is devoted to the objective. What is objective is thereby conscious as characterized in various ways according to the fundamental kind of the act: judgment-object, value-object, will-object. But a priori there belongs to this situation the “possibility” of a change of attitude of the subject, by virtue of which, whenever it was not from the outset in a theoretical attitude, it can pass over into a theoretical attitude, in which the objective becomes a theoretical object, that is, the object of an actually carried-out belief-positing in which the ego lives and grasps what is objective, takes it as existing and posits it.

### §5. Spontaneity and Passivity; Actuality and Inactuality of Consciousness

This capacity, this “I can” of the subject, can at any time be made thematic and intuited in its own manner. And so what was “pre-theoretically” conscious and objective becomes, in the subsequently “disclosing” reflective theoretical grasping, “properly” conscious in its objectuality. It is thus to be noted that in the manifold interweavings of theoretical and other acts, essential phenomenological differences emerge that are more easily seen than clearly designated.

With reference to these differences especially, Husserl employs the talk of theoretical, axiological, and practical attitudes, intending thereby also to indicate that “having intentional experiences in the consciousness-nexus” and “carrying out acts as spontaneities oneself” do not yet say as much as being directed toward their objects — specifically, being theoretically directed or directed toward values or toward deeds in general, toward the practical in any sense, however broad. We are directed in this way only where we live in the relevant acts in a preeminent sense, or where we are preeminently directed toward their objects.

Here two kinds of distinctions intersect. First, the distinction between the spontaneously (and, in the case of many-leveled acts, in articulated steps) carried-out act, as opposed to the consciousness in which the same objectuality that was to be constituted through the former is “passively” conscious in a confused state. Every spontaneous act necessarily passes after its carrying-out into a confused state; spontaneity, or what is properly to be called activity, passes into passivity — though a passivity that, as already noted, points back to the originally spontaneous and articulated carrying-out. This pointing-back characterizes itself as such through the evidently belonging “I can” or capacity to “reactivate” this state, that is, to transfer it into the consciously grasped “repetition” of that production from which it formerly emerged and in which it “once again” emerges as the same state, letting the same result, the same final sense, come forth in the same validity.

But likewise, as we saw, such a state can occur in consciousness without having emerged in this way from a just-elapsed spontaneity as secondary passivity. Remaining now in the sphere of spontaneous act-executions, after the above clarification, different kinds of overlapping spontaneities can appear in differing phenomenological dignity: one as the so-to-speak dominant one, that in which we preeminently live; the other as the serving or as the remaining to the side, in the background — that in which we do not preeminently live (acts that are characterized, regardless of their other genre-intentional peculiarity, as acts of “interest”).

Husserl illustrates: we receive joyful news and live in the joy. In a theoretical act we carry out the thinking-acts in which the news constitutes itself for us, but this act serves only as a foundation for the feeling-act in which we preeminently live. In the joy we are “meaning” (feeling-meaning) in the manner of feeling-“interest,” turned toward the joy-object as such; the act of joyful turning has the higher dignity here; it is the main act. But it can also be reversed, or a change of attitude from the joy-attitude to the theoretical attitude can occur: we then live in theoretical consciousness (we are “theoretically interested”), the theoretical act provides the “main thing,” and yet we still rejoice, but the joy remains in the background.

So in all theoretical research: in it we are theoretically directed, even though at the same time spontaneous and lively pleasure-turnings may be carried out, as for example in physical-optical research a lively feeling for the beauty of the occurring appearances. There may also be in the background a resolution to show the beautiful appearance to a friend, while we nevertheless are not in the practical attitude but continuously maintain the “theme” of the theoretical attitude (in short: the theoretical theme). Conversely, it may be that we are in the practical attitude and remain in it, maintaining the “practical theme,” while some appearance close to our other theoretical interests arouses our interest on the side. But it does not therefore become the theoretical theme; it remains a serving element for the context of praxis — unless we exchange the practical attitude for the theoretical, abandoning the practical theme in order to seize the theoretical one.

In such thematic interweavings, new objectualities are always constituting themselves, possibly with ever higher constitutive strata, stemming variously from theoretical, valuing, practical acts, and having, depending on the attitude, differing thematic significance. In particular, they can always again become theoretical themes through transition into the theoretical attitude; they then become objective in a special sense: they are grasped, become subjects of predicates that theoretically determine them, and so on. Correspondingly, in the extrathematic sphere, in the sphere of passivity, manifold objectualities confront us that, through the intentionality in which they are conscious (however “confused”), point back to such connections.

### §6. Difference of the Transition into the Theoretical Attitude from the Transition into Reflection

Husserl warns that one must carefully distinguish the transition into the theoretical attitude discussed here from the transition that every act in principle permits: the transition into an immanent perception directed toward the act itself, or into an immanent retention if the act has fleetingly passed. That too is a theoretical attitude; the perception, the retention, is a general objectivation, and in so-called immanent reflection upon the act we live executively in this objectivation, and so we are theoretically directed. But what Husserl has in mind here is a different, far more remarkable, and in principle universal theoretical attitude belonging to all acts.

In aesthetic pleasure something is conscious to us as aesthetically pleasing, as beautiful. Let the starting point be that we live in aesthetic pleasure, thus devotedly giving ourselves over to the appearing object. We can reflect upon the pleasure, as when we state: I take pleasure in it. The judgment is indeed a judgment about my act of pleasure. But it is something entirely different to direct the glance upon the object and its beauty. The beauty I intuit at the object, not indeed like its color or shape in simple sensory perception, but at the object itself I find the beautiful. “Beautiful” says nothing less than a reflection-predicate, as when I say that it is something pleasing to me. The “pleasing,” the “delightful,” the “sad,” and all coordinate object-predicates are in their objective sense not relation-predicates referring to acts. They arise through the change of attitude described: the relevant acts are presupposed. I still have pleasure, still feel joy and sadness and the like. But instead of simply being joyful or sad, that is, carrying out these feeling-acts, I bring them through a change of attitude into another mode; they are still experiences, but I do not live in them in the distinguished sense. I look toward the object and find in it, in my altered, now theoretical attitude, the correlates of these feeling-acts — an objective stratum superimposed upon the stratum of the sensory predicates: the stratum of the “delightful,” the objectively “sad,” the “beautiful” and “ugly,” and so on. In the theoretical attitude of reflection, by contrast, I can find not objective but only consciousness-relative predicates.

It is clear that all talk of objects, their predicates, properties, relations, the states of affairs pertaining to them — for instance, laws — points back to theoretical acts in which objects are given, perceived or otherwise intuited, theoretically explicated, thought, and so on, or can be so. When we assign objectualities to all intentional experiences, including feeling-experiences — objectualities toward which they take a position in the manner of feeling, objects under the title value-objects, practical objects, and so on — this is done with reference to the fact that to the essence of every act there belong in principle possibilities of various theoretical directions of the glance, in which such objects as lying, so to speak, implicitly in the feeling-behavior are graspable — including the objects peculiarly belonging to each fundamental kind of act, such as values belonging to valuing.

### §7. Objectivating and Non-objectivating Acts and Their Correlates

Husserl here ties into the foregoing a further distinction. Every fundamental kind of act is characterized by its own fundamental kind of “act-quality.” Thus the objectivating acts are characterized by the quality of doxa, of “belief” in its various modifications; the fundamental kind of acts we call in a broad sense valuing acts, by the quality of valuing, and so on. Theoretical acts are the genuinely or explicitly objectivating ones; for the genuine having-an-object, having-a-thing-before-one, the peculiarly grasping, positing attitude of the theoretical subject is required. Every non-objectivating act allows objectualities to be drawn from it through a turn, a change of attitude; in this lies that every such act, by its essence, is implicitly also objectivating — it is essentially not only built in a higher stratum upon objectivating acts, but is itself objectivating in what it newly contributes. Thus a living-into this objectivation becomes possible, through which not only the object of the underlying objectivation but also what is newly objectivated through the new feeling-stratum comes to theoretical givenness.

If the pleasure is founded upon a simply objectivating perceiving, then I can grasp theoretically not only the perceived but also what is newly objectivated through the pleasure; I can, for example, grasp the beauty as a theoretical predicate of the perceived, as was set out above. Now two possibilities are obviously available: (1) either an act is from the outset only objectivating (if that is at all possible), or if it also has a differently qualified stratum, interwoven in its essence with a new objectivation, we leave it out of play, do not live in it — then we grasp merely things and merely logical characters of things. The object-characters corresponding to the new acts or new qualities are either not present from the outset (if that is at all possible) or remain outside of action, outside of consideration. There is then no beautiful and ugly, no delightful and undelightful, no useful, good, no utensil, no cups, spoons, forks, and so on. All such words contain in their sense predicates that stem from non-objectivating acts. (2) Or we move along also in the sphere of the new and founded qualities. We draw into the domain of theoretical interest, into the framework of the theoretical attitude, also the predicates correlative to these acts; then we have not only mere things but precisely values, goods, and so forth.

### §8. The Sense-Objects as Constitutive Primordial Objects

Husserl observes that in all these forms of constitution of objects we are led back to objects that no longer point back to pre-given objects of the sort that have originally sprung from some theoretical, valuing, or practical spontaneities. In other words: if we follow the intentional structure of any given objects and the retrospective indications that lie in consciousness in the form of secondary receptivities, if we produce the spontaneities that bring the relevant objectualities to fully proper original givenness, we arrive — possibly in a series of steps — at founding objectualities or noemata that contain nothing more of such retrospective indications, that are grasped or graspable in the simplest theses, and that do not point back to any antecedent theses requiring reactivation that contribute to the constitutive stock of the object. The objects that are phenomenologically characterized in this peculiarity — the so-to-speak primordial objects to which all possible objects point back according to their phenomenological constitution — are the sense-objects.

However, the characterization given is not yet complete; the relationships are more difficult than they initially appear. Connected with this is the fact that the concept of the “sense-thing” is not unambiguous, just as, correlatively, the concept of presentation in the pregnant sense — that of sensory presentation (sensory perception, sensory memory, etc.) — is not unambiguous.

### §9. Categorical and Aesthetic (“Sensory”) Synthesis

Husserl now takes as his starting point the distinction between categorical (formal, in a certain sense analytical) synthesis and aesthetic (sensory) synthesis. We know that however-constituted objects (objects of entirely arbitrary regions, kinds, and species) can be substrates for certain categorical syntheses, can enter as constitutive elements into “categorical” formations of objects of a higher level. Among the latter belong collectiva, disjunctiva, states of affairs of every kind, such as relations between some A and some B, or property-states-of-affairs such as that A is a, and the like. We find such formations in the doxic sphere, where doxic theses are built upon one another — subject-positing as foundations for predicate-positing, and so on — but also in the feeling- and will-sphere: will-positing carried out on the basis of will-positing (end-means), and the like. We encounter unities of feeling- and will-behavior as well as formations that essentially belong within them, whereby states of affairs come to explicit if not intuitive givenness, and thus logical formations that are all essentially states of affairs or possible parts, moments, of states of affairs.

Now objects can not only be constituted categorically through multiple theses, with these theses categorically united in their constitutive performance; multiple theses can also contribute to the constitution of objects in another manner. Original constitution of one object always takes place through one thetic consciousness, and what functions as “matter” for the unified thesis, supplying the objectual “content” or objectual sense, can for its part point back to multiple theses. But the unity of the object need not everywhere presuppose a categorical synthesis, thus including one in its sense.

Thus every simple perception of a thing (an originally giving consciousness of the present existence of a thing) leads us intentionally back; it demands individual considerations, individual run-throughs, transitions in perceptual series that are indeed spanned by the unity of a continuous thesis, but obviously in such a way that the manifold individual theses are by no means unified in the form of a categorical synthesis. What gives these individual theses their unity is a synthesis of an entirely different kind: we will call it the aesthetic synthesis.

Seeking to delimit the two in their peculiarity from one another, we find as a first distinguishing mark that categorical synthesis, as synthesis, is a spontaneous act, whereas sensory synthesis is not. The connecting is in one case itself a spontaneous doing, an activity proper; in the other case it is not. The objective sense of a pure sense-object (pure thing) is a synthesis of elements that have not themselves come about through aesthetic synthesis: these are the ultimate sensory features.

For a further characterization of aesthetic synthesis, Husserl adds that the individual grasping of a thing or of its essentially proper parts and sides contains partial meanings in the form of “secondary passivities” that, as such, are sense-determining and motivate the further course of perception: thus in the apprehension of the shape of a thing from one side, continuous courses of other side-apprehensions of this same shape are intentionally included. These hints do not, however, suffice for an exhaustive description of aesthetic synthesis; that would require its own large investigation. Here he only emphasizes that the function of aesthetic synthesis is to be traced through various strata. When we consider a thing, we necessarily consider it always in some respect, that is, we are directed toward a specially grasped “feature” as a particular moment of the purely aesthetic sense: in the example cited, the shape. We can further restrict ourselves to the purely visual grasping and find within this domain the synthetically unified partial meanings.

It is not necessary, then, that these partial meanings always have the form of “secondary passivity,” that is, that they point back to acts that highlighted something already apprehended for itself. Thus in the apprehension of a unified surface, there lie potentially acts that would bring individual partial surfaces to experience, even though these were not previously given as separate. The same can be shown for every “sensory sphere.” Another function of aesthetic synthesis is to unite the objectualities that have constituted themselves in the various individual sensory spheres with one another: for example, the visual thing-stratum with the tactile one. Finally, there are the syntheses that establish the relation between the moments of the “thing-appearance,” through which the apprehending ray passes, and the correlative “perceptual circumstances” (for example, the eye position in seeing, the arm, hand, and finger position in touching), which in the natural attitude directed toward the perceptual object do not come to grasping or proper co-meaning.

In all of this the thing continually presents itself as something that is such and such, even though no concepts, no judgments in the predicative sense, mediate. We always attend to certain “features,” and while we consider the thing from the standpoint of one feature, it simultaneously stands before us intentionally as equipped with other features; partly these are determinate features already lying ungrasped in the perceptual field — we need only send a grasping glance toward them to fulfill the intentions, or else to transform them into determinately or indeterminately grasping, even if intuitively non-giving, intentions, which in the latter regard naturally holds for the unseen sides of the thing. Partly they are indeterminate. In this case, horizons and possibly determinately directed meaning-rays are reactivated that contributed to the apprehension-sense in the form of non-activated “confusions.” Yet — as already noted — the analysis need not be a reactivation. To be sure, one can say that no analysis can highlight what was not already in some way implicitly implied in implicit synthesis, that we can only bring out parts where, under alteration of the apprehension, we had meant parts in, even if in the form of confused co-meanings. Thus our thing-apprehension continually shifts; it takes up apprehension-moments within the unitary style prescribed by thing-consciousness; subsequent explication then transforms the confused co-apprehension into a possibly thematic thesis, a theoretical grasping, which will mostly be paired with closer determination and, in unity with the kinaesthetic courses, with closer intuitional fulfillment. But insofar as such apprehension-changes are possible in advance given the essence of thing-apprehension (yet not empty but motivated possibilities), there are “implicit” partial apprehensions that were not really represented in the original apprehension.

### §10. Things, Spatial Phantoms, and Sensation Data

The objects that have so far served as representatives of sense-objects were real things, as given above all prior to any thinking (prior to any operation of synthetic-categorical acts) in “sensory perception.” They are not spontaneous products (products in the proper sense, which presupposes genuine activity, doing) and yet “synthetic” unities from components (which, like them, need not necessarily be synthetically connected). The unity of the visual sense-thing does not necessarily require the connection with the unity of the tactile sense-thing. And not only that: already in the constitution of a sensory-spatial entity as such — even if it is a pure visual spatial phantom (a pure colorfully filled shape, without relation to tactile or other data of other senses, and also without any reference to moments of “materiality” and thus to any real-causal determinations) — we have a formation of a hidden and analytically demonstrable constitutive synthesis; for it is an “appearance” that points back to kinaesthetic “circumstances” to which it belongs.

We are analytically led ever further back and arrive finally at sense-objects in another sense, which underlie all spatial objects and thus also all thing-objects of material reality (understood constitutively) and which lead us back to certain ultimate syntheses — but to syntheses that lie before all thesis. Taking as the most convenient example a sounding violin tone: it can be apprehended as a real violin tone, thus as a spatially-real occurrence. It is then the same whether I move away from it or approach it, whether the adjacent room in which it sounds remains open or is closed. Under abstraction from material reality, one can still retain a tonal spatial phantom, appearing in a determinate orientation, proceeding from a spatial location, sounding through space, and so on. Finally, even the spatial apprehension can be put out of play, so that instead of a spatially sounding tone, the tone is taken as a mere “sensation datum.” In place of what, in approaching and receding, was conscious as the unchanged tone out there in space, there appears, in turning the glance to the sensation datum, something continuously changing.

It is to be seen that such a tone-datum could be constituted without any spatial apprehension having been carried out at all — one which in our example is only abstractively set aside, or rather (to correct the misleading expression) which is put out of play but in its altered mode still remains an experience, indeed a pre-giving experience for the spatial tone. But the spatial tone, we say, is not a necessary pre-givenness. A tone would be conceivable that lacked every spatial apprehension. Here, with the pure sensation datum, we encounter a pre-givenness that lies even before the constitution of the object as an object.

We can describe this through a contrast of two possible cases: the first possibility is that in the background of consciousness a tone sounds that is already apprehended as an object but not grasped; the ego is perhaps turned to something else. In the case of the second possibility, the talk of the sounding tone means a sensation-state that, while functioning as a stimulus for the ego, does not possess the character of an object-consciousness to which a sounding tone is objectively conscious. A genetic remark can serve for clarification: a consciousness-subject that had never “perceived” a tone, that is, grasped it as an object for itself, could not have any object “tone” impose itself upon it as an object. The once-performed grasping (the original object-consciousness) can lead to object-apprehensions without a meaning-turn-toward, whether in the form of memory of similar tones or in the form of a background-consciousness of a newly sounding tone. Naturally, not every turning-toward a tone can genetically point back to a turning-toward a constituted object “tone”; there must be a tone-sensing that is not an objectual apprehending or grasping; there must be an original constitution of the object “tone” that lies before as a pre-giving consciousness — one that is properly not pre-giving but already objectually apprehending. Setting aside the genetic considerations (which need not therefore be empirical-psychological), two phenomenologically possible cases distinguish themselves: the case of a merely objectual apprehension, which is an objectivating consciousness but a modified one compared to the consciousness distinguished as turning-toward and grasping; and the case of a sensation-state that is not yet objectual apprehension. Mere apprehension thus presents itself as an intentional derivation from grasping, somewhat analogous to how reproductive memory is a derivation from perception.

An object constitutes itself originally through spontaneity. The lowest spontaneity is that of grasping. But grasping can be a kind of reactivation — namely, reactivation of a modified grasping that brings something already conscious-as-objectual into the glance of the grasping ego. Or it can be an original act that constitutes the object in the most original manner. We see, then, that all spatial-thingly objectivation ultimately leads back to sensation. With all objectualities we are led back from the categorical to the sensory objectualities. As such, on the one hand, we are to designate sensory objectualities that in a certain sense are αἰσθητὰ πρῶτα, namely containing only representatives of one sensory sphere, and indeed in such a way that they contain no implicit special apprehensions and so do not intentionally point back to hidden theses that would need to be made proper through reactivation. An example is the tone already apprehended as spatial, insofar as it is correct, as we do indeed believe, that in the case of such objectualities no intentional back-references to perceptual circumstances lie present that would need to be fulfilled through proper meanings.

From such objects we are finally led to the sensation data constituted in the most primitive manner, which constitute themselves as unities in the original time-consciousness. All primitive objects, whether sensation-objects or unities already constituted in a thing-like manner in one sensory sphere (though not real objects in the full sense), are originally given as objects through mere single-rayed “reception.” In the broader sense, thing-objects constituted through the participation of several sensory spheres are also receptive, but for proper givenness they require, according to what has been set out, articulated processes, chains of receptions. We could also say that the former objects would be merely accepted, while the latter are received and accepted at the same time. Received, insofar as they contain intentional components that point back to non-actual acceptances as implied constitutive components.

### §11. Nature as Sphere of Mere Things

Husserl now returns to the idea of nature as the correlate of modern natural science, whose radical phenomenological delimitation was the aim of the preceding investigation. It is clear that “nature” in this sense is a sphere of “mere things,” a sphere of objectualities that, through a demarcation a priori prescribed in the essence of constituting consciousness, separates itself from all other objectual spheres to be treated theoretically. We could, and could have earlier, easily say: natural science knows no value-predicates and no practical predicates. Concepts like valuable, beautiful, lovely, charming, perfect, good, useful, deed, work, and so forth, as well as concepts like state, church, law, religion, and other concepts — that is, the objectualities to whose constitution valuing and practical acts have essentially contributed — have no place in it; they are not nature-concepts. But it must be understood from within, from phenomenological sources, that in this abstraction from predicates of the value-sphere and practical sphere we are not dealing with an arbitrary, accidental abstraction, which as such would yield no radically self-enclosed idea of a scientific domain and hence also not the idea of an a priori self-enclosed science.

We gain such an a priori closed idea of nature as the world of mere things when we become purely theoretical subjects, subjects of a purely theoretical interest, and set out to satisfy purely this interest — in the sense described earlier. We thus carry out a kind of “reduction.” All our feeling-intentions and all apperceptions stemming from the intentionality of feeling are as it were bracketed, by virtue of which spatiotemporal objectualities, before all thinking, in immediate “intuitiveness,” continually appear to us with certain value-characters, practical characters — characters that go beyond the stratum of mere thinghood. In this “pure” or purified theoretical attitude we thus no longer experience houses, tables, streets, artworks; we experience only material things, and of such value-laden things only their stratum of spatiotemporal materiality, and likewise for human beings and human societies only the stratum of psychical “nature” bound to spatiotemporal “bodies.”

Yet a certain restriction must be made: it would not be correct to say that the correlate of mere nature is a purely “objectivating ego-subject” that carries out no valuations whatsoever. It is indeed a subject that, vis-à-vis its object (the reality constituted in appearances), is indifferent, namely does not value such being for its own sake and thus has no practical interest in its changes, in shaping them, and so on. But this subject does value the knowledge of appearing being, the determination of this being through logical judgments, through theory, through science. It values the “so it is,” the “how is it?” And it also values practically, it has an interest in changes, it produces them practically in the experiment — but not for their own sake, but rather to make visible connections that could further the knowledge of appearing being.

The correlate of nature is thus not a subject that altogether does not strive, will, or value. That is inconceivable. What is abstracted from in nature-cognition is only all other values as opposed to the knowledge-values: I want nothing further than to experience nature more richly in “theoretical experience” and to cognize, on the basis of experience, in theoretical knowledge, what the appearing, nature, is.

All pure theory, all purely scientific attitude, springs from the theoretical interest in an originally-to-be-constituted objectuality or genus of objects. In the case of natural science, this objectuality originally to be constituted is nature, the real unity of all natural objectualities. “Natural objectuality” here designates a genus of objects whose coexisting exemplars essentially necessarily close themselves into a really connected unity, with the additional characterization that for these objects a valuing consciousness has contributed nothing as “constitutive,” that is, to their sense-content. And precisely because the valuations that the nature-experiencing, natural-science-pursuing subject carries out as such are not constitutive for the objects with which it deals, it could rightly be said that in its domain there are no value-objects and the like.

One thing, however, must be noted. The valuing and willing acts of the natural-scientific subject do not, it is true, contribute constitutively to the sense of natural objects. But these acts are present; they belong to the subject’s concrete consciousness. They co-determine the full intentional situation, even if they are not what gives nature its natural-scientific sense.

## Second Chapter: The Ontic Sense-Strata of the Intuitive Thing as Such

### §12. Material and Animal Nature

Husserl directs our attention to the totality of “real” things, the entire thing-world, the “universe,” or nature, which in its forms of space and time encompasses all factual realities and, out of essential grounds, equally all a priori possible realities. Already at first glance a distinction grounded in essence stands out: the distinction between nature in the narrower, lowest, and primary sense — namely material nature — and nature in the second, expanded sense, that is, ensouled, in the genuine sense “living,” animal nature. Everything that we designate in the ordinary sense (that is, in the naturalistic attitude) as existing, including sensations, representations, feelings, psychic acts and states of every kind, belongs in this attitude to living nature; they are “real” acts or states, ontologically characterized precisely by being animal or human activities or states, and as such ordered into the spatiotemporal world. They accordingly are subject to the determinations belonging to “all individual objectuality whatsoever.”

All thingly being is temporally extended; it has its duration and fits itself with its duration in a fixed manner into objective time. It has, with its duration, a fixed place in the one world-time, which is a universal form of existence for all thinghood. Everything else the thing “is,” according to every other essential determination belonging to it, it is in its duration, with the closer determination of a “when.” We therefore suitably distinguish between temporal determination (the duration of the thing) and real feature, which as such fills the duration, spreads over it. Hence, a priori, every feature of a thing is necessarily, throughout its duration, contentually continuously changing or not changing, with discrete jumps admissible in the former case. The thing “alters” when the temporal filling of its duration is continuously or abruptly changing; the thing remains unaltered when this is not the case.

Furthermore, all thingly being has its position in world-space, which is relative to all other thingly being and in principle changeable. It is something movable in space, by virtue of the corporeal extension that essentially belongs to it and is exclusively its own, which can continuously change its position in space. These propositions can be understood so generally that they hold in fact and a priori for all thingly being whatsoever.

Yet with respect to corporeal extension, material thinghood and thinghood in the sense of animal nature are separated. Not without reason does Descartes designate extensio as the essential attribute of the material thing, which for that reason is also called simply corporeal, in contrast to psychical or spiritual being, which in its spirituality as such has no extensio, rather essentially excludes it. Indeed, it must above all be brought to insight that extensio, rightly understood, divides nature in the first sense from nature in the second sense, although the fully comprehensive essential attribute of material being is not mere extension but materiality, insofar as the latter in itself requires both spatial and temporal extension. What matters, however, is the recognition of the distinguished manner in which everything else that belongs to a material thing is a priori (that is, always essentially) referred to its extension. Spiritual nature, understood as animal nature, is a complex consisting of a lower stratum of material nature with the essential mark of extensio and an inseparable upper stratum that is of a fundamentally different essence and above all excludes extension.

### §13. The Significance of Extension for the Structure of “Things” in General and of Material Things in Particular

Husserl now aims to clarify the peculiar way in which everything a thing otherwise is and essentially is is referred to the extension that necessarily belongs to it, and furthermore how the psychical determinations belonging to animal realities gain their necessary spatial determination in an entirely different manner — through the foundedness of the psychical in the material. By spatial or rather corporeal extension of a thing we understand the spatial corporeality belonging to its concrete essential constitution, taken in full determinateness as it belongs to this constitution. Accordingly, not only does every change of magnitude with preservation of similar spatial shape count as a change of extension, every change of shape with preservation of magnitude, every deformation in whatever sense — but also every change of position is a change of the extension.

Extension is thus not a mere piece of space, even though at every time-point of the thing’s duration it coincides with such a piece. By its essence, neither space itself nor any of its pieces can move; space itself can never have a hole — that is, a place empty of spatiality that might subsequently fill itself through influx. Space is absolutely “rigid”; its parts are not, in our determinate sense, “extensions,” not “bodies,” not even rigid bodies in the sense of physics.

Husserl maintains that this changeable determination — spatial extension or corporeality — has an entirely unique position among the constitutive properties of a material thing. To the essence of extension belongs the ideal possibility of fragmentation. It is evident that every fragmentation of the extension fragments the thing itself, decomposing it into parts each of which again has the full thingly character of material thinghood. Conversely, every division of the thing into things, every thing-fragmentation as such, also fragments the thingly extension. The thing is not extended merely in the sense that among other determinations it possesses a determination called corporeal extension; rather, according to everything whatsoever that it is contentually, and indeed is in itself (according to its full time-filling essence, according to its features), it extends itself, is something that fills its spatial corporeality.

There is a principled division between the corporeal extension-determinations of the thing — magnitude, form, figure, and the like (ideally speaking: the geometrical determinations) — and its real qualities, or rather their state-modes under the respective circumstances, in the respective time-phases. Every corporeal quality of a thing “fills the spatial body”; in it the thing spreads out; in each quality the thing fills its corporeality (extension), and the same at the same time-point for all real qualities. Each thing is distinct: each may have its different spatial extension and may have it qualitatively filled in very different ways. But the general type is always and necessarily the same.

Extension is thus, even though — indeed precisely because — it is “real property” in an entirely different way, the essential characteristic of materiality: it expresses a form of existence characteristic of material or physical being (a form of essence for all real determinations in which thingly existence explicates itself). But this holds only for the merely physical thing, not for the full thing in general. Temporality belongs to the essence of any thingly being whatsoever. Human beings and animals have their position in space and move in it like mere physical things. Naturally one says: they do so “by virtue of” their corporeal bodies. But it would be absurd to say that only human bodies move, not human beings; that human bodies walk on the street, ride in carriages, live in the country or in the city, but not human beings.

### §14. The Significance of Extension for the Structure of Animalia

The objects of nature in the second, expanded sense are, taken in full concretion, animal realities, characterized as ensouled bodies. They are founded realities that presuppose within themselves, as a lower stratum, material realities — so-called material bodies. These now have, and this is the new point, alongside the specifically material determinations, new systems of properties: the psychical ones. It remains an open question whether under this title two things should actually be distinguished: a sensory (aesthetic) stratum and a properly psychical one. In experience the relevant new properties are given as belonging to the relevant body; for their sake it is called a body, a “body for a soul” or for a spirit. On the other hand, these properties are precisely not material properties, and that means: it belongs to their essence that they have no extension, that they are not given in the manner of all the properties filling the bodily extension.

It is not a matter of chance but of essence whether properties extend themselves or not — hence whether the objects that have these properties are material or not. Human beings and animals have material bodies, and insofar they have spatiality and materiality. But according to the specifically human and animal, that is, the psychical, they are not material, and thus taken as concrete wholes in the proper sense they are not material realities. Material things are fragmentable, parallel to the extension belonging to their essence. Human beings and animals are not fragmentable. Human beings and animals are spatially localized; their psychical aspect too has spatial ordering at least by virtue of its essential foundedness in the bodily. We will even say that some of what is reckoned under the broad and initially unclarified title of the psychical has something like a spreading (though not a spreading in space). But in principle nothing on this side is extended in the proper sense, in the specific sense of the described extension.

### §15. The Essence of Materiality (Substance)

Before pursuing further the differences between localization and extension — and thus the mode of connection between the material body and that which completes animality, the psychical — Husserl proposes first to examine the connecting members more closely.

The physical or material thing is res extensa. The sense of its “essential attribute,” extensio, has been discussed. What now constitutes the concept of this res? What does extended reality mean? What does reality in general mean? One also speaks of extended substance. What does this substantiality mean, in the greatest possible generality?

The material thing falls under the logical category of individual simpliciter (“absolute” object). To this are referred the logical (or formal-ontological) modifications: individual property (here, thingly quality), state, process, relation, complexion, and so on. In every domain of being we find analogous modifications; the goal of phenomenological clarity requires going back to the individual as the primordial objectuality. All logical modifications receive their sense-determination from it.

#### a) The Phenomenological Analysis of Thing-Givenness as the Path to Determining the Essence “Material Thing”

If we want to grasp and conceptually determine the essence “thing,” we must not hold to vague talk and inherited philosophical prejudices, but draw from clear givenness itself. We must go back exemplarily to the consciousness in which things are originally and so completely given that nothing can be lacking for the grasping of the universal essential form that prescribes the a priori rule for such objects. Bringing things exemplarily to givenness means not merely perceiving them or clearly phantasying oneself into a perceiving. It does not suffice to see this table and cast a perceiving glance upon it, or to take together several perceptions of the table and perceptions of other things. Rather, it is necessary, perceiving, experiencing, to “follow” what is perceptually meant — whether actually experiencing or in phantasy. The point is to make present (or freely feign) continuously connected perceptual series in which the perceived object is one and the same and in the progression of perceptions shows ever more completely what lies in it, what belongs to its essence.

In the noema of perceiving — that is, in the perceived, taken phenomenologically exactly as it is an intentional object therein — a determinate directive to all further experiences of the relevant object is contained. In perceiving, this table is now given, but given in each case in a determinate manner. The perceiving has its perceptual sense, its meant-as-such, and in this sense lie directives, lie unfulfilled forward-indications and back-indications, which we need only follow. Only if one questions the thing-noema, the thing-meaning, bringing it to unfolding givenness in all directions, and lets it give its answer in the carrying-out of its directives, does one actually gain the essential components of thinghood and the necessary essential interweavings without which something meant as a thing cannot be thought at all.

#### b) Movability and Changeability as Constituents of the Material Thing; the Thing-Schema

It is easy to convince ourselves that the possibilities of movement and rest, of qualitative change and non-change, are grounded in principle in the essence of the material thing as such. A thing can, for example, be factually unmoved and unchanged; but that it should be in principle immovable and unchangeable is absurd. On the other hand, it can be absolutely unchanged: in intuition we can grasp the idea of a thing unchanged in every respect at suitable examples (even if only as an ideal limiting case).

Starting from this idea and holding to the thing for itself, disregarding the thing-context, we notice that we then have no means at all to distinguish the essence of the thing from the essence of a mere phantom — that the surplus on the side of the thing would not come to genuinely exhibited givenness. A mere phantom is present, for example, when we learn in the stereoscope to bring suitable groupings to corporeal fusion. We then see a spatial body for which meaningful questions can be posed regarding its shape, its color, its smoothness or roughness and similarly ordered determinations. But on the other hand, the appearing entity can be so given that the question whether it is heavy or light, elastic, magnetic, and so forth has no sense — more precisely, no foothold in the perceptual sense. We simply do not see a material thing. The entire group of material determinations is absent from the sense-content of the apperception. They are not merely left indeterminate and open, but rather essential groups of features are not represented in the apperception at all — namely those of specific materiality.

In this manner we also see a rainbow, the blue sky, the sun, and so on. We see from this that a filled spatial body (qualified body), through the qualitative fullness extending over it, is not yet so much as a thing — a thing in the ordinary sense of a material real. Likewise it is clear that every sensory thing, as a basic piece of its essence (always, irremovably), requires such a filled spatial body in its givenness. As filled spatial extension it is always necessarily given, but as more. We say: to the essence of a thing belongs a sensory schema, and by this we understand this basic framework, this corporeal (“spatial”) shape with the fullness extended over it. The thing appearing as at rest and qualitatively unchanged “shows” us no more than its schema — or rather the apparency — while it is at the same time apprehended as material. In this regard it does not “show” itself, does not come properly into view, into original givenness. In what is “properly” given, nothing would change if the entire stratum of materiality were stricken from the apperception. This is in fact conceivable. In original experience — perception — “body” is inconceivable without sensory qualification, but the phantom is originally given and thus also conceivable without the components of materiality, while materiality for its part is non-self-sufficient (one-sided detachability).

If we take into account the various changes — the extensive ones (change of place, deformation) and the qualitative ones — we notice the same thing again: what comes to actual perception in perception of thingly changes in the proper content of the appearing thing are only continuous courses of sensory schemata. But again it is clear that nothing is given here that could not also be given as a pure “phantom.”

It should be expressly emphasized that the concept of the schema (of the phantom) is by no means restricted to one sense-sphere. A perceived thing also has its tactile schema, which emerges in tactile apprehension; in the full schema there are precisely as many strata to be distinguished as we can find types of sensory data that extend over the identically appearing spatial extension of the thing.

#### c) Demonstration of the Materiality of the Thing Through Its Dependence on Circumstances

Now it is time to say what was lacking, and to take up the presupposition we made. We have hitherto taken the thing in isolation. But the thing is what it is in relation to “circumstances.” If we contrast phantom-change and thing-change, it is clear that the two are not the same and do not differ merely by a contentual stock that under the title “materiality” is added in one case and is missing in the other. Obviously, thing-changes can occur with the sensory schema unchanged, and conversely, with a changed schema the thing can remain unchanged — for example, when the same unchanged thing is perceived under changing daylight or changing colored illumination.

The actual reality, which here is called materiality, lies not in the merely sensory schema, not in what could belong to the perceived if nothing of the sort as relation to “circumstances” existed or had any sense for it; rather, it lies precisely in this relation and the mode of apprehension corresponding to it. The thing looks different again and again under changing illumination — in relation to another luminous body — and not arbitrarily but determinately. Functional connections obviously obtain here, which relate the schematic modifications on one side to those on other sides.

In the sense of the apprehension of a thing as a thing (and not of the mere phantom), it lies that such schemata, running through determinate series of modifications and changing or not changing in a determinate manner, are experienced as manifestations of one and the same. But we experience them as that insofar as they run their course as “dependents” of belonging “real circumstances.” Thus in our example we experience the same thing with respect to its optical properties, which maintain their unity and determinateness through the change of illumination by corresponding light sources. The unity goes through the schemata insofar as they are colorfully filled. What constitutes itself here is the “objective” color — the one the thing has whether it hangs in sunshine or dull daylight or in the darkness of the wardrobe, and so for all illumination conditions, to which quite determinate schemata functionally belong.

As long as circumstances remain unchanged, the schema too remains unchanged. Continuous change of circumstances has continuous change of the schema as a consequence, and likewise, with continuous non-change of the appearances functioning as circumstances, continuous non-change of the dependent schema.

#### d) The Schema as Real Determination of the Material Thing

The respective schema, by virtue of this realizing apprehension (namely as constitutive for the real thing as substrate of real properties), gains the character of a real determination of a special sense. Over against the real, unitary property — in our example, the unchanged objective color — stands the momentary, circumstance-corresponding, law-governedly changing real state. It coincides with the schema yet is not merely a schema (the thing is not a mere phantom). To the altered apprehension corresponds an altered correlate. In the thing-apprehension the schema is not perceived as merely sensory-filled extension, but is perceived as “certification” (original attestation) of a real property and thereby as a state of the real substance at the relevant time-point. The property itself comes to genuinely fulfilling, that is, original givenness only when the functional series come to original unfolding, in which the dependencies on the belonging circumstances come to original givenness — in other words: the causal dependencies.

The causalities are in this case not merely supposed but “seen,” “perceived” causalities. The direction of the glance in the meaning-grasping of the real property and in the meaning-grasping of the causal dependence of its respective states on the belonging circumstances (which then themselves come to objectual grasping) are different, although the glance in both cases passes in a certain way through the schema or rather through the corresponding stratum of its fullness. And again the direction of the glance is altered — possibly within the same state of the subject-matter pre-given for these various graspings — when directed upon the thing itself as the identical substrate of this or that attesting property, or of the respective states referred to this or that circumstance. As many directions of unity are prescribed in the causal apprehension of the schema — namely directions for possible perceptual series in functional relation to series of perceivable circumstances — so manifold is the thing-real, the unitary material “substance”; according to the apprehension-sense itself it is determinable in terms of properties, and it actually has these properties (real, substantial properties) when the fulfilling experience originally exhibits them in thing-states (modes of behavior) dependent on circumstances.

#### e) Closer Determination, Re-determination, and Cancellation of Thing-Experience

It belongs further to the general essence of thing-apprehension that in the progress of experiences that ever more richly attest the respective thing, ever richer directions of determination emerge and in them ever new empty places of determinability can be established. A priori, what the respective thing itself is proves itself only in the progressus of continuing attesting experiences. As principled possibilities there always stand side by side: (1) the possibility of thoroughly concordant and only more closely determining experiences; (2) the possibility of partly concordant, partly discordant experiences — that is, experiences that determine the same thing anew and differently; (3) finally, the possibility of irreconcilable discordances, with which the non-being of what was experienced in temporary concordance, or of the thing determined “differently” in particular respects, proves itself. But if the thing is, it is as the identical real of its real properties, which are, so to speak, the mere rays of its unitary being. As this identical it is, in every still-so-imperfect experience that leaves ever so much open, posited in a motivated manner, and the justifying force of the motivation grows with the richness of the attestations arising in the progress of experience.

Constantly it is, in that it behaves thus and so under the circumstances belonging to it: reality or, what here is the same, substantiality and causality belong inseparably together. Real properties are eo ipso causal. To know a thing means therefore: to know experientially how it behaves under pressure and impact, in bending and breaking, in heating and cooling — that is, how it comports itself in the context of its causalities, into what states it gets, how through them it remains the same.

To pursue these connections and, on the basis of progressing experience, to determine the real properties in scientific thinking — this is the task of physics (in an extended sense of the word), which, guided by the nearest unities in the hierarchical sequence of experiences and experiential attestations, progresses to ever higher unities.

### §16. Constitution of Thing-Properties in Manifolds of Dependency-Relations

Through all changes of state there runs, in reference to the real circumstances (actual and possible), a unity, so that from the side of the apprehension every change of state — whether it belongs to the same property as unchanged, or to continuously or discretely changed properties — stands or can stand as uniquely determined in the given real context. The thing as unity of its properties can thus “itself” change or remain unchanged in the alternation of all kinds of states or modes of behavior. Every real property is, as real, changeable. Thus the unities of attestation in their temporal continuity are, in their dependence on the unities of circumstances, apprehended as phases of a unity that maintains itself through them in duration.

Iron melts and changes its state of aggregation, which for its part obviously has the character of a real property in our sense. The weight of a material body changes continuously as it is carried toward the earth’s poles. More precisely considered, the formation of unity as that of an identity in the flux of temporal change is nothing specifically peculiar to the thing. To the essence of every unity, including every non-substantial unity as a mere unity of duration, there belong a priori possibilities that the duration is filled with an identical or (apart from individual jumps) continuously changing temporal content.

It must be noted that the constitution of real properties can also take place in higher levels, so that level-formations are possible whereby in unities of attestation there in turn attest higher unities, possibly determined from them with the help of thinking grounded in experience. This holds generally.

We had above the principle of the coordinating of schematic givennesses that is formally determining in the apperception of the real: same circumstances — same consequences. This principle requires a certain modification of the sense initially given to it: the circumstances were schematic givennesses, as were the consequences. This holds when we are dealing with properties of the lowest level and the real is experienced as a lasting unity of one and the same unchanged property.

But in the essence of every thing (as we grasp it in the essence of experience, in ideally possible experience) lies the ideal possibility of its — the real’s — change. The spring is elastic. Under the same schematic circumstances we experience how the same schematic change-series appear: the same vibration-modes with like-directed, like-strong impacts, and so on. Now the impact can be so strong that the “elasticity limit” is exceeded; the spring breaks and falls apart into several separated pieces. Or the spring is heated to red-hot, it loses its elasticity, or the elasticity changes insofar as it still remains. These are property-changes.

Thus we have not only unity in the changes of properties and in the individual changing property — as we generally have unity in change-series — but through the property-changes there runs a new unity: so constituted is the thing that under quite determinate changes of real circumstances it changes its real properties in a determinate manner. The change-series of real properties, in their dependence on the changes of real circumstances, in turn attest a unity.

Everywhere, dependencies and property-constitutions of the first level are separated from those of a higher level, and the formation of unity at the higher level is again a formation of real properties, because in the context of experience the property-changes of the lower level are not unmotivated but in turn prove themselves to be dependencies on real circumstances. The intuitive thing is not apprehended precisely in the full rigor of this idea of reality, although this idea, as one can recognize, is already laid out in the very formation of unity given without further ado with its apprehension. It was first the new natural science that grasped this idea — to be drawn out of the experiential apprehension — of a strict identity in absolutely determinate and unambiguous dependencies of causality, and that developed the demands lying in it.

Husserl adds a substantive note on the topic of division: the apprehension of a thing as a thing-system, its construction from molecules, and so on. How is the idea of a real whole of real parts, which themselves in turn function like things, to be grasped a priori? What a priori possibilities obtain here? How does it stand with the a priori possibility of “continuous” space-filling, specifically real space-filling? These are problems left for further investigation. He also notes that the present analyses confine themselves to a particular type of material thinghood: the solid body, which constitutes itself as a thing with a rigid spatial shape that transports itself in movement. This restriction is not arbitrary; it can be shown that we have here the basic piece of the constitution of material nature. Fluid and gaseous bodies constitute themselves for us as material things but present themselves as space-fillings that harbor or can harbor material things of the first and originally constituted kind — solid bodies — as movable within them with greater or lesser resistance.

### §17. Materiality and Substantiality

In the foregoing considerations, Husserl has intentionally let the general aspect of thinghood as such — “reality” — stand out more strongly against what belongs to materiality as such as the specific feature of extensive reality. That general aspect, which would best be designated as reality, is called substance. (Unfortunately all these philosophical terms are burdened with equivocations and lack any deeper clarification.) In contrast, Husserl has here drawn from intuition a firm essential stock whose fundamental significance is evident.

To separate these genuine “realities” from the widely spread broadest sense of the word, which comprises any individual (or temporal) being, he says substantial reality; henceforth, wherever he speaks simply of substantiality, substance, or thing, this is what he means. Extensive substance counts only as a particularization. The role of extension (corporeality) has already been discussed. Accordingly, determinations like position, figure, and so on, as belonging to the extension, are not substantial properties; they belong rather already to the schema. This does not prevent shape and position of a thing from being dependent on non-change and change of circumstances and experienceable in this causal dependence. In reference to this, the specific extension-determinations become attestations of proper real thing-properties, upon which in turn the properties attesting themselves in the fullness of the schema are functionally dependent. In this manner we experience in the thing firmness and fluidity, elasticity, and so on. These are thus substantial properties like others; they belong to the material thing, which extends itself in space with them as with all substantial properties, has its spatial shape and position, which for their part are not material properties.

## Third Chapter: The Aistheta in Reference to the Aesthetic Body

### §18. The Subjectively Conditioned Factors of Thing-Constitution and the Constitution of the Objective Material Thing

Husserl announces that the entire preceding analysis moved within a definite narrow frame that must be firmly delineated. The real unity that constituted itself for us step by step, with all its levels, has not yet reached the full concreteness of the material thing as we encounter it in natural experience.

The real unity that constituted itself stepwise has not yet reached the last stratum at which the truly objective material thing is constituted. What has been described is the thing as it constitutes itself in the continuously unitary manifold of sensory intuitions of one experiencing ego — in the manifold of “sense-things” of various levels: manifolds of schematic unities, of real states, and of real unities of different levels. This is the thing for the individual, ideally isolated subject — but only in such a way that this subject remains, as it were, self-forgetful, and this self-forgetfulness also remains unnoticed by the analyst.

#### a) Dependencies of the Intuitive Characteristics of the Material Thing on the Experiencing Subject-Body

For the production of a full givenness of a material thing — that in which it demonstrates its actual reality — this self-forgetfulness is not well suited. We need only consider how a thing demonstrates itself as such, according to its essence, and we recognize that such an apprehension must from the outset contain components that refer back to the subject, specifically as a human (or better: animal) subject in a fixed sense.

It turns out that the characteristics of material things as aistheta, as they intuitively stand before me, are dependent on my characteristics as the experiencing subject, referred to my body and my “normal sensibility.” The body is first of all the means of all perception; it is the perception-organ; it is necessarily involved in all perception. The eye is directed toward the seen in seeing and runs over corners, surfaces, and so on. The hand glides over objects in touching. Moving myself, I bring my ear closer in order to hear. Perceptual apprehension presupposes sensation-contents that play their necessary role for the constitution of the schemata and thus of the appearances of real things themselves.

The possibility of experience requires the spontaneity of the courses of the darstellende (presenting) sensation-acts, which are accompanied by and motivated as dependent on kinaesthetic sensation-series. With the localization of the latter in the relevant movable body-member, it is given that in all perception and perceptual demonstration (experience) the body, as freely movable sense-organ, as freely movable whole of sense-organs, is involved. From this original ground everything that is real-thingly in the ego’s surrounding world has its relation to the body. The distinction that the body becomes the bearer of the null-point of orientation — the Here and Now from which the pure ego surveys space and the entire sensory world — is obviously connected with this. Every appearing thing has eo ipso an orientation-relation to the body, and not only the thing that actually appears but every thing that is to be capable of appearing.

Beyond its distinction as orientation-center, the body gains significance for the construction of the spatial world by virtue of the constitutive role of sensations. In all constitution of spatial thinghood, two kinds of sensations with quite different constitutive functions are involved and necessarily involved if presentations of spatial things are to be possible. First, sensations that, through the apprehensions they receive, constitute corresponding features of the thing as such in an adumbrating manner. Thus sensation-colors with their sensation-spreads, in whose apprehension the corporeal colorings with the corporeal extension of these colorings appear. In the tactile sphere, the roughness-sensations, with whose apprehension thingly roughness appears, likewise the warmth-sensation versus the corporeal warmth, and so on.

Second, the “sensations” that do not receive such apprehensions but are on the other hand necessarily involved in all such apprehensions of other sensations, insofar as they motivate the latter in a certain way — while themselves receiving an entirely different kind of apprehension that thus belongs correlatively to every constituting apprehension. In all constitution and at all levels we necessarily have mutually related “circumstances” and what “belongs” to all circumstances: everywhere we find the “if-then” or “because-so.” The sensations that receive extensional apprehensions (as thingly extended features) are, in their actual and possible courses, motivated and apperceptively referred to motivating series, to systems of kinaesthetic sensations, which freely run their course in their familiar orderly connection. Perception is everywhere a unity of performance that essentially grows out of the interplay of two correlatively related functions. At the same time it emerges that to every perception there belong functions of spontaneity. The courses of kinaesthetic sensations are here free courses, and this freedom in the course-consciousness is an essential piece of the constitution of spatiality.

#### b) The Significance of Normal Perceptual Conditions for the Constitution of the Intuitive Thing and Anomalies

The perceptual courses by virtue of which one and the same external world stands before me do not always show the same style; rather, differences become noticeable. First, the same unchanged objects look different depending on the changing circumstances. The same unchanged shape has a changing look depending on its position relative to my body: it appears in changing aspects that present “it itself” more or less “favorably.” Setting this aside and considering the real properties, the same shape-identical object has different appearance-colors (filling of the shape) depending on its position relative to a luminous body, and with different luminous bodies again different colors, but in a regulated and more closely determinable manner. Certain conditions then stand out as the “normal” ones: seeing in sunlight and bright sky, without the influence of other bodies determining the appearance-color. The “optimum” reached here counts as the color itself, in contrast to, say, the evening glow that “outshines” all proper colors.

Husserl explains that to normal experience, in which the world originally constitutes itself as the world “as it is,” there belong further normal experiential conditions: for example, seeing through air — which counts as immediate seeing without mediating things — touching through immediate contact, and so on. If I insert a foreign medium between my eye and the seen things, all things undergo a change of appearance. It then means: the same thing, but seen through different media. The thing is not dependent on such changes; it is the same. But the “mode of appearance” of the thing (in this case of the phantom) is dependent on whether this or that medium mediates between eye and thing.

Equally, if instead of the interposition of a medium between organ and thing we consider an anomalous change of an organ itself: if I touch with a blister on my finger, if my hand is scraped, all tactile properties of things are given differently. If I see with squinting eyes, touch with crossed fingers, I have two “seen-things,” two “touched-things,” but say that only one actual thing is present.

Further important considerations concern other groups of anomalies. If I eat santonin, the whole world “apparently” changes, it “changes” its color. The “change” is “apparent.” Husserl shows that for the constitution of “change of things according to their color,” more is required than mere alteration of full schemata with respect to their color: change of things is from the outset constituted as causal change in reference to causal circumstances. These causal circumstances are thingly. The relativism of spatial things in relation to other things determines the sense of thing-change. But to this the psychophysical conditionalities by no means belong.

However, the body also enters into the causal connections: if it is apprehended as a thing in space, then it is apprehended not as a mere schema but as a point of intersection of real causalities in the real (exclusively spatial-thingly) context.

As for eating santonin, this too, setting aside all “psychical accompanying facts,” is a material process, and this could very well enter into a real relation with an optical change of the rest of the material world if the constitution of the experiential world required it. But so long as and whenever I experience the change of all visible colors as an optical change of things, I must assume a causal relation between some causative thinghood. Once experiential motives oppose this, a transformation of the apprehension must necessarily occur, whereby the seen “change” loses the sense of change and gains the character of “semblance.” A semblance-change is a schematic alteration that under normal conditions is apprehended as change (in relation to causality-constituting experiences) but is now given in a way that cancels the causal apprehension.

#### c) The Significance of Psychophysical Conditionality at the Various Constitutive Levels

The real world originally constitutes itself in levels such that, as a lower stratum, the manifold of sense-things (full schemata) builds itself up in the unity of the spatial form. The sense-things constitute themselves in the subjective manner of “orientation,” and for us (whether this is a necessity is a special problem) in such a way that a distinguished sense-thing, “the body,” is given as the constant bearer of the center of orientation. The realization completes itself in the manner that sense-things become states of real things; the system of real characteristics constitutes itself — a system of regulated mutual references of sense-things under the title of causality.

The constitution of the lower stratum is what bestows upon all things in experience — namely, insofar as they are sense-things in their momentary states — the most primordial psychophysical conditionality. The sense-things are what they are as unities “in” a manifold of perceptions and kinaesthetic constellations of subjectivity, constantly conscious as motivants for the belonging aspects as motivata. Only in this connection are the aspects aspects of sense-things.

To this there essentially belongs a possible change of attitude that makes the sense-thing conditionally dependent on bodiliness, on my looking with open eyes, my eye movements, on my gliding touch with subjectively moved hands, and so on. This entire system of conditionality is the foundation of the higher apperceptive stratum and becomes the psychophysical conditionality between my body and its causal interweavings in extra-bodily nature on one side and subjective sensation-courses, aspect-courses, and so on, on the other.

In the system of normal — “orthoaesthetic” — appearances, which close themselves into the unity of a concordant experience, breaches occasionally occur. All things appear suddenly changed, including the body. Husserl analyzes how, when one sense fails or an organ enters anomalous conditions while the others function normally, we have a concordantly maintained world-apprehension through the remaining senses, and the anomalous member can be identified as such. The anomalies, in their form of psychophysically conditioned consequences of the organ’s alteration, expand the original system of psychophysical conditionalities. There is the one normally constituted world as the true world, as the “norm” of truth, and there are manifold semblances — deviations of the mode of givenness that find their “explanation” through the experience of psychophysical conditionality. What anomalies contribute is only the rule of my subjectivity: that things are experienceable things for subjects and that with body-thingly causalities, conditional rules of sensation-series are connected.

#### d) The Physical Thing

The objectification carried out in the relativisms of experience posits the thing as the identical substrate of identical properties. The thing looks different depending on whether I press my eye (double images) or not, whether I eat santonin or not, and so on. But it is the same in consciousness, and the change of coloring does not count as a change of the property that the color indicates. The thing is what it is in the thing-context and “in relation to” the experiencing subject, but it is yet the same in all state-changes and appearance-changes that it undergoes as a consequence of changing circumstances, and as the same thing it has a stock of “enduring” properties.

In the thing-constitution that takes place for the individual subject in relation to a relative constancy of bodiliness, there is first distinguished as a lower stratum: (1) the thing itself (as it itself is) with its constitutive features, as they themselves are, over against the various and variously more or less complete modes of givenness — the features belonging to the thing “itself” are the respective “optimal” ones. This holds for all features, geometric as well as sensory qualities. (2) Once the “sense-thing” is constituted and the real-causal thing of the level of sensory and proper experience is founded in it, a new constitution of a higher level occurs in view of the relativity of this “thing” with respect to the likewise constituted bodiliness. This relativity requires the constitution of a physical thing manifesting itself in the intuitive thing. In this relativity, the geometrical determinations and the specifically “sensory qualities” play an entirely different role: the geometrical determinations belong to the physical object itself, to physical nature in itself; not so the sensory qualities, which belong entirely to the sphere of appearing nature.

#### e) Possibility of the Constitution of an “Objective Nature” at the Solipsistic Level

Husserl notes that we have followed the constitution of material nature through various strata and have seen that already for the “solipsistic” subject — the subject in isolation — motives exist for the distinction between an “appearing” thing, whose qualitative stock is relative to my subjectivity, and the “objective” thing, which remains what it is even when changes occur in my subjectivity and dependently in the “appearances” of the thing. Under the title “true” or “objective” thing, a twofold meaning is to be understood: (1) the thing as it presents itself to me under “normal” conditions, over against which all other thing-like unities constituted under “anomalous” conditions sink to “mere semblance”; (2) the identical stock of qualities that can be worked out in abstraction from all relativity and fixed logico-mathematically: the physical thing.

But the question is whether the motives for the necessary distinction between subjectively conditioned and objective thing that offer themselves in solipsistic experience are sufficient, or necessarily present. If a subject always had only normal perceptions and never experienced any alteration of any of its organs, or if it experienced an alteration for which no possibility of correction existed (loss of the entire tactile field, psychical diseases changing the entire perceptual type), then the hitherto assumed motives for the distinction of “semblance” and “reality” would fall away, and the level of “objective nature” could not be reached by such a subject. But this danger is removed as soon as we lift the abstraction and take into account the conditions under which the factual constitution stands: namely that the experiencing subject is in truth not solipsistic, but one among many.

#### f) Transition from Solipsistic to Intersubjective Experience

Husserl now considers the possibility of a solipsistic world more closely. He imagines his experiential domain with everything remaining the same except that all bodies that he could apprehend as bodies of other psychical subjects are absent. If this apperceptive domain is absent, it does not co-determine his thing-apprehensions. Otherwise he has the same sensation-manifolds, the “same” real things appearing with the same properties. Seemingly nothing essential has changed; seemingly only a piece of the experiential world is missing — the animal world and the group of causalities in the world-context that it brings with it.

But then Husserl imagines that suddenly, at a point in time within the time co-constituted with this solipsistic world, bodies appear in his experiential domain, comprehensible and understood as human bodies. Now, for the first time, there are other human beings with whom he can communicate. He communicates with them about the things that in the new time-period are there in common. And it turns out — very remarkably — that extensive complexes of thing-assertions that he made on the basis of earlier experiences (all experiences that agreed splendidly) are not confirmed by his present companions; their consistent experience stands in consequent conflict with his. How does it stand with the demonstrated reality of the first time-period? The answer is clear: as soon as he communicates his earlier experiences and they become aware of the consistent conflict with their intersubjectively constituted world, he becomes an interesting pathological object for them, and his so well-demonstrated reality is called a hallucination of someone who was mentally ill up to that point. No matter how far one pushes the perfection of the demonstration of the solipsistic world, nothing changes in this state of affairs as an a priori one.

Strictly considered, the solus-ipse does not deserve his name. The abstraction we performed does not yield the isolated human being or the isolated human personality. This abstraction did not consist in arranging a mass murder of the human beings and animals of our surrounding world, sparing only one’s own human subject. The then remaining subject would still be a human subject, still the intersubjective object, still apprehending and positing itself as such. Rather, the constructed subject knows nothing of a humanity-surrounding, knows nothing of a reality or even a real possibility of “others” in the sense of bodies comprehensible in terms of the humanity-apprehension. One notices that the body-apprehension plays a special role for intersubjectivity, in which all objects are “objectively” apprehended.

The thing that constitutes itself in regulated manifolds of concordant experiences for the individual subject thereby receives the character of a merely subjective “appearance” of the thing of “objective reality.” Each of the intersubjectively communicating subjects has its own perceptions and perception-appearances of the same thing, its appearance-unity, which itself is only in a higher sense an appearance, with appearance-predicates that may not without further ado count as predicates of the appearing “true thing.”

#### g) Closer Characterization of the Physical Thing

Husserl describes the “physical nature” as follows: the thing in itself consists of continuously or discretely filled space in states of movement — states called energy-forms. What fills the space is subject to certain groups of differential equations, corresponding to certain basic physical laws. But there are no sensory qualities there. And this means there are no qualities at all. For quality of space-filling is sensory quality. But how is filled space without quality even conceivable?

To attribute reality in itself to appearing things with their sensory qualities is not feasible, as the natural scientists quite rightly say. For depending on the kind and condition of the sense-organs, the sensory qualities change; they are dependent on the sense-organs and dependent in general on the body and the entire constitution of the experiencing subject. It also turns out that the true physical facts corresponding to qualitative differences like red and green, warm and cold, present themselves without qualitative transition as mere quantitative differences of one and the same — as temperature, ether-vibrations, and so on.

Should we say that God sees things as they are in themselves, and we through sense-organs that are a kind of distorting glasses? Husserl argues that the result is that we must understand the sense of the distinction between secondary and primary qualities well, and must understand the non-objectivity of the former only to mean that they are in no way withdrawn from the relativism of appearances. Nature is an intersubjective reality, and reality not only for me and my accidental fellow human beings, but for us and for all who are to enter into commerce with us and be able to communicate with us about things and people.

The thing is a rule of possible appearances — a reality as a unity of a manifold of regulatedly belonging appearances. The objective determination determines the thing through what must belong to it if it is to appear to me or to anyone in commerce with me and is to count as the same for everyone belonging to the communicative community. What is common is space-time determination and a lawfulness that, with its concepts referred to the “physical thing,” is a unitary rule of all the appearances of the intersubjective community that constitute and must constitute the same thing in the rational communication.

The objectively real is not in my or anyone’s “space” as “phenomenon” (the “phenomenal space”) but in objective space, which is a formal unity of identification belonging to the changing qualities. Pure space does not arise from my appearing space through abstraction but through an objectivation that takes every sensibly appearing spatial shape characterized by sensory qualities as “appearance,” placing it in appearance-manifolds that belong not to an individual consciousness but to a social consciousness.

#### h) Possibility of the Constitution of an “Objective Nature” at the Intersubjective Level of Experience

Husserl now considers, analogously to the solipsistic case, what conditions must be fulfilled for the intersubjective level so that the constitution of an “objective” nature can and must come about. He began from the conditions as they factually obtain: that individual differences stand out from a basic stock of shared experiences and lead to the distinction between determinations belonging to the thing “itself” and merely subjectively conditioned ones. A priori, other conditions can also be constructed. We can imagine a world of human beings in which there were no illness, no illusions, hallucinations, and the like; and in which all communicating human beings apprehend the world completely alike (aside from the always necessary difference of orientation). Would things with secondary qualities then count simply as the last objectivity? Or would one recognize that this state of affairs is accidental and not necessary?

To this he says: naturally we must distinguish the constitution of the sensory world and the constitution of the “true” world — the world for the scientific subject, active in spontaneous, “free” thinking and research. If we live passively, animal-like “in the world” and in commerce with our fellows, all as “normal” as we are, a common experiential world constitutes itself. But we are free intelligent beings. Even if no anomalies confront us, we can make voluntary interventions in our own and others’ bodies, and now “anomalies” appear. Thinking, we pursue the causal connections and fashion the “physical worldview.”

We see that, on one hand, for the solipsistic level there already exists the possibility of pressing forward to the constitution of the “objective” (physical) thing. On the other hand, for the intersubjective level there does not exist an unconditional necessity of getting that far. But a principled difference exists between these two possible paths to the construction of an “objective nature.” The solipsistic subject could indeed have an objective nature over against itself, but it could not apprehend itself as a member of nature, could not apprehend itself as a psychophysical subject, as an animal, in the way that happens at the intersubjective level of experience. This can naturally become evident only when one follows the constitution of animal nature — to which the investigation now turns.

## Second Section: The Constitution of Animal Nature

## Introduction

### §19. Transition to the Consideration of the Soul as Natural Object

Husserl now turns to the investigation of the essence of the soul — the human or animal soul as it is the object of natural-scientific research in its connection with the material body. As strict phenomenological method demands, we hold to what original experience teaches. All vague experiential knowledge, all confused and unclarified interpretations of the psychically given that have sprung from experience are set aside, together with all convictions prevailing in psychological science. What we seek lies not in the pursuit of theoretical, mediated thinking but at its beginning: we seek its most originary presuppositions. The sense of “soul” that perfect intuition of the psychical prescribes cannot be overturned by any theory. It prescribes an absolutely binding rule to all theoretical research; any deviation from it yields nonsense. Legitimate theory can achieve nothing other than determining predicatively, through mediated thinking, what originally giving intuition (in our case, experience) has in a simple manner posited as being and as being of a determinate “content” or sense. What the “origin-analysis” has drawn from the originary intuition as the originary sense of the object cannot be cancelled by any theory. It is the presupposed norm to which all possible theoretical cognition is rationally bound. The point is to draw the genuine concept of the psychical “from experience” — not in the sense of empirically proceeding, but of considering in essential intuition the essence of what is experienced as such and then, in the unfolding of the intentions essentially lying in such experiencing, intuitively grasping and descriptively expressing the sense of the experienced.

### §20. The Sense of Ordinary Talk of the “Psychical”

The psychical is given to us in connection with the material. Among material things there are (or are a priori possible from the standpoint of essential consideration) those that are soulless, “merely” material, and others that have the rank of “bodies” and as such show a connection with a new stratum of being, called the psychical. What falls under this title? The first thing experience brings to givenness here is a beginningless and endless stream of “experiences,” of which manifold types are well known to us from inner perception, “introspection,” in which each of us grasps “his own” experiences in their originarity — and which he can also grasp intuitively (though no longer originarily and in the “actual”) in inner memories, inner free phantasies, inner image-presentations. Such experiences are furthermore given as present actualities in the form of interpretive grasp of alien psychic life, in more or less clear intuitiveness.

As the figurative expression “stream of experience” (or stream of consciousness) already indicates, experiences — sensations, perceptions, memories, feelings, affects, and so on — are not given to us in experience as intrinsically connectionless annexes of material bodies, as if they were unified with one another only through the common phenomenal attachment to these bodies. Rather, they are one through their own essence, connected and intertwined with one another, flowing into one another in strata, and possible only in this unity of a stream. Nothing can be torn from this stream, nothing can be, so to speak, cut off as a thing for itself.

This unitary stream harbors in a certain way still further unities within itself — unities that are likewise intuitively graspable with a suitable direction of the glance and that must no less be taken into consideration if we want to bring to clarity the phenomenologically originary field of the psychical. To this field are referred the ego-concepts, to be taken in various senses, as well as the proper soul-concept, which by no means coincides with the concept of experience and stream of experience. For us the first question concerns the unity of the pure (transcendental) ego, then the unity of the real psychic ego — the empirical subject belonging to the soul, where the soul is constituted as a reality connected with or interwoven in the bodily reality. A special study will be demanded by the body and the question whether its essential determinations are only those of a special material thing, or whether it does not bear a new, extra-material constitutive stratum that is not yet to be designated as psychical in the pregnant sense. Under the title “empirical ego” requiring clarification, we find furthermore the unity “ego-human-being.”

### §21. The Concept of “Ego-Human-Being”

Starting from the richest ego-concept — the everyday one — Husserl notes that each human being grasps in “self-perception” precisely himself, and likewise in the experiential acquaintance with another precisely this other human being. Everyone speaks in the ego-discourse of his acts and states in the form “I perceive, I judge, I feel and will”; likewise in the form “I am so constituted” of his personal properties, his inborn or acquired character-dispositions, abilities, and more or less enduring dispositions. And equally regarding others: someone is of strong character, virtuous, cheerful or melancholy, has a choleric temperament, is in love, and so on. At the same time, it is said: he dances, does gymnastics, eats, writes letters; he has psychophysical abilities; he is a good dancer, a mediocre gymnast, and so on. In the normal ego-discourse, the “whole” human being with body and soul is encompassed under the ego. It can therefore very well be said: I am not my body, but I have my body; I am not a soul, but I have a soul.

If the unity of the human being comprises the two components not as two merely externally bound realities but as most intimately interwoven and in a certain way mutually interpenetrating components, it becomes comprehensible that states and properties of each component count as those of the whole, of the “ego-human-being” itself.

On the other hand, it is easy to see that the psychical has a preeminence and is what essentially determines the ego-concept. If the soul falls away, we have dead matter, a merely material thing that no longer has anything of the human ego about it. The body, on the other hand, cannot fall away. Even a ghost necessarily has its ghost-body. Husserl explores the a priori (though entirely empty) possibility of actual ghosts: a psychical being is conceivable without a material body, thus as a ghost rather than a natural animal being, but by no means without a body altogether. If a psychical being is to have objective existence, the conditions of the possibility of intersubjective givenness must be fulfilled. Such intersubjective experienceability is conceivable only through “empathy,” which for its part presupposes an intersubjectively experienceable body that is understandable as the body of the relevant psychical being.

The preeminence of the psychical or spiritual over the body thereby emerges: the spirit must, in order to be objectively experienceable, be the ensoulment of an objective body (only not a priori of a material body), whereas conversely the objective experienceability of a spatial phantom or material thing demands no ensoulment. The body is not only a thing in general but an expression of the spirit, and it is at the same time an organ of the spirit.

## First Chapter: The Pure Ego

### §22. The Pure Ego as Ego-Pole

Husserl asks us to imagine a self-perception performed in which we abstract from the body. We then find ourselves as the spiritual ego related to the stream of experiences — “spiritual” meaning here only generally that this is the ego that does not have its seat in bodiliness. I “think” (cogito), that is, I perceive, present in some mode, judge, feel, will, and find myself as the one and the same in the change of these experiences, as “subject” of acts and states. This subject has absolute individuation as the ego of the respective cogitatio, which is itself something absolutely individual. Here we can draw various lines, first gaining the ego as the pure ego — the one already discussed extensively in the First Book. We restrict ourselves to intentional experiences that “we” have “carried out” and through which we, or more clearly: I, who in each case “think,” have directed the ego-ray toward the objectual content of the act. We fix the ego that rules in the “I think” itself, and purely as what it rules there — the ego given in absolute indubitability as the “sum cogitans.”

In every act-carrying-out lies a ray of directedness that I cannot describe other than as taking its point of departure in the “ego,” which thereby evidently remains undivided and numerically identical while it lives in these manifold acts, spontaneously active in them, and in ever-new rays passing through them toward the objectual content of their sense. The pure ego is, depending on the kind of act carried out, related to objects in very different modes. In desiring I find myself attracted by the desired object, striving toward it without reaching it in mere desire. In loving I feel myself inclined toward the beloved, attracted, possibly wholly devoted, absorbed in the beloved. In hating I am directed toward the hated yet repelled.

The pure ego lives not only in individual acts as carrying-out, active, suffering; freely and yet objectively attracted, it moves from act to act, experiences stimuli from the objects constituted in the “background” without immediately heeding them, lets them intensify, knock at the gate of consciousness, yields, and turns from one object to another, building freely this or that multi-leveled act-unity.

It is important to note that the pure ego is not only a carrying-out ego in the acts in the specific sense of cogito. As soon as the respective cogito sinks into inactuality, the pure ego also sinks in a certain way into inactuality. It withdraws from the relevant act. But it does not become something separated from all experiencing. Rather, the difference of actuality and inactuality designates a different essential structure of the intentional experiences and therewith an inseparable difference of the “how” in ego-experiencing. The ego can never vanish; it is always there in its acts, but differently depending on whether they are or become actual acts. The pure ego does not arise or perish. What belongs to the pure ego is only the essential peculiarity that it has its entrance and its exit, that it begins and ceases to function actually, to rule. This is the same as saying that acts in the specific sense of the cogito become events in the stream of consciousness, since the essence of such acts consists in being “carried out” by the pure ego, in being intentional experience.

### §23. Graspability of the Pure Ego (the Ego-Pole)

It belongs to the essence of every cogito generally that a new cogito of the kind we call “ego-reflection” is in principle possible, which grasps, on the basis of the earlier (thereby phenomenologically modified) cogito, the pure subject of the same. Thus it belongs to the essence of the pure ego to be able to grasp itself as what it is and how it functions, and to make itself into an object. The pure ego occupies a wholly isolated position vis-à-vis everything real and everything else that can be called “being.” Everything objective in the broadest sense is thinkable only as the correlate of possible consciousness, more precisely of a possible “I think,” and thus as relatable to a pure ego. This holds also for the pure ego itself. To the essence of the pure ego belongs the possibility of an original self-grasping, a “self-perception,” and of corresponding self-grasping modifications — self-memory, self-phantasy, and the like.

It is to be noted that everything constituted as a unity of a duration, changing thus and so in it, is itself already a consciousness-constituted unity. The deepest stratum — immanent time and all its ordered experiential unities — has been intentionally left out of consideration in this treatise. The identical pure ego belongs to this sphere. It is as identical the ego of this immanent time. Every cogito with all its components arises and perishes in the flux of experiences. But the pure subject does not arise and does not perish, although in its way it “appears” and “disappears” again. It enters into action and exits action again.

### §24. “Changeability” of the Pure Ego

In what sense the pure ego changes in the change of its acts can be seen with evidence. It is changeable in its activities — in its activities and passivities, in its being attracted and repelled. But these changes do not change it itself. In itself it is rather unchangeable. It is not an identical something that has first to attest and prove itself in manifold states of enduring properties determined by changing circumstances. It is therefore not to be confused with the ego as the real person, with the real subject of the real human being: it has no original and acquired character-dispositions, no abilities, dispositions, and so on. To know what I myself am as a human personality, I must enter into the infinity of experience. But to know that the pure ego is and what it is, no accumulation of self-experiences can teach me better than the single experience of a single simple cogito. The ego does not appear, does not merely present itself one-sidedly, does not merely attest itself according to individual determinations; rather, it is given in absolute selfhood and in its non-adumbratable unity, adequately graspable in the reflective turn of the glance back upon it as center of function. As pure ego it harbors no hidden inner riches; it is absolutely simple, absolutely manifest; all richness lies in the cogito and in the adequately graspable manner of its function.

### §25. Polarity of Acts: Ego and Object

Insofar as every cogito demands a cogitatum, and this stands in relation to the pure ego in the act-carrying-out, we find in every act a remarkable polarity: on the one side the ego-pole, on the other the object as counter-pole. Each an identity, but of a radically different kind and provenance. The ego is the identical subject of function in all acts of the same stream of consciousness, the center of emission and reception of all consciousness-life, all affections and actions, all attending, grasping, relating, connecting, all theoretical, valuing, practical position-taking, all being-pleased and being-saddened, hoping and fearing, doing and suffering. All these diverse specifications of intentional relatedness to objects have their necessary terminus a quo, the ego-point, from which they radiate. Often, if not always, we find here double radiations: from the center through the acts toward their objects, and conversely rays running back from the object toward the center.

Turning now to the entirely different unity of the object (an object that is not itself a subject), it too refers us back to manifold acts to which it gives unity in a determinate sense — but in a wholly different sense than the act-reference to the centralizing subject does.

### §26. Waking and Dull Consciousness

The pure ego enters and exits: it belongs to the essence of consciousness in the unity of the flux that the pure ego can light up not everywhere but only in individual acts. For it irremovably belongs to the essence of consciousness that every act has its dark horizon, that every act-carrying-out sinks into darkness when the ego turns to new lines of cogitation. In the essence of consciousness it does not necessarily lie that an actual cogito must be carried out in it. Our “waking consciousness” can be interrupted by a sleeping, entirely dull consciousness, without a distinction between actual field of vision and dark background. Husserl invokes Leibniz: the monad passes from the stage of evolution to that of involution and in higher acts becomes a self-conscious “spirit.” The pure ego that becomes actual is nothing inserted or added from outside, nothing that first comes into being in the moment of its actual appearance only to vanish again into nothing. “The pure ego must be able to accompany all my representations.” This Kantian proposition has a good sense if we understand “representations” here as all dark consciousness.

### §27. “Ego-Human-Being” as Component of the Surrounding World of the Pure Ego

By virtue of the polarity belonging to the essence of the cogito, the waking ego is intentionally related to the objectual content of its carried-out cogitations. Their objects are its objects. Potentially this holds also for the objects of the uncarried-out noeses, those sleeping in the background, which make up the field of the ego’s freedom. I, as the human being, am a component of the real surrounding world of the pure ego, which, as the center of all intentionality, also carries out the intentionality through which precisely I, the human being and personality, constitute myself. The pure ego is numerically single with respect to “its” stream of consciousness. If it posits a human being and in him a human personality, it implicitly posits a pure ego belonging to this human being with its own stream of consciousness. The empathized pure ego is in principle an “other”; if I posit several human beings, I also posit several principally separated pure egos and belonging streams of consciousness.

### §28. The Real Ego as Transcendent Object Constituted — the Pure Ego as Immanently Given

The real egos, like realities in general, are merely intentional unities. While the pure egos are to be drawn from the originary givenness of every cogito in which they function — originally and in absolute selfhood, in the sphere of immanent phenomenological time, neither capable of nor requiring constitution through “manifolds” — with the real egos and all realities things stand in reverse. They are constituted unities not only with reference to one pure ego and one stream of consciousness with its appearance-manifolds, but with reference to an intersubjective consciousness: an open manifold of monadically separated pure egos and their streams of consciousness, unified through mutual empathy into a connection that constitutes intersubjective objectualities.

### §29. Constitution of Unities Within the Immanent Sphere. Enduring Opinions as Deposits in the Pure Ego

All consciousness-data, consciousness-levels, and noetic forms that can be “accompanied” by the identical ego of an actual and possible “I think” belong to one monad. Within a monadic absolute stream of consciousness, certain formations of unity appear that are quite different from the intentional unity of the real ego and its properties. Among these are such unities as the enduring “opinions” (Meinungen) of one and the same subject. They can in a certain sense be called “habitual,” but what is meant is not a habitual habit as though the empirical subject gained real dispositions. The habitus here belongs not to the empirical but to the pure ego.

The identity of the pure ego lies not only in the fact that I can grasp myself as the identical ego of the cogito, but also in this: that I maintain in my position-takings a necessary consistency. Every “new” position-taking institutes a lasting “opinion” or theme (an experiential theme, a judgment-theme, a joy-theme, a will-theme), so that from then on, whenever I grasp myself as the same I was before, I also hold fast to my themes, taking them over as actual themes just as I previously posited them. This means: themes are originally posited, simply or in response to motives; on the basis of the same motives I, the position-taking ego, cannot behave otherwise. I can become “unfaithful” to my position-taking only by becoming another, insofar as I am subject to different motivations. In truth I am not unfaithful to myself; I am always the same, but in the changing stream of experiences in which ever-new motives constitute themselves.

Every “opinion” is thus an institution that remains in the subject’s possession so long as no motivations arise that demand a “change” of position-taking, a surrender of the old opinion. Every opinion of one and the same ego necessarily persists in the chain of recollections so long as it is not struck through on the basis of motives.

Husserl analyzes the constitutive structure of these enduring unities in detail: convictions, grudges, resolutions, enthusiasms, loves, hatreds, and so on. The various enduring experiences, belonging to duration-stretches that are separated within phenomenological time, have a relation to one another and constitute a durably persisting item — the conviction, the grudge, that arose at such and such a time from such and such motives and from then on is an enduring possession of the ego, even in the intervening stretches of phenomenological duration in which it was not experientially constituted. He draws the noesis-noema distinction to clarify further: the unity of a conviction is not the unity of the judging-experience but the unity of “the” judgment that persists for the judging subject, which it grasps in repeated and arbitrarily repeatable acts as the one and same conviction, as one it constantly has. These unities have their duration; they can cease and possibly be newly instituted. All these unities are unities with reference to a pure ego, to whose stream of consciousness they belong, constituting themselves as its “possession.”

## Second Chapter: Psychic Reality

### §30. The Real Psychic Subject

From the pure or transcendental ego Husserl now distinguishes the real psychic subject, or the soul — the identical psychic being that, really connected with the respective human or animal body, makes up the substantial-real double being called human or animal, animal in the wider sense. With the emphasis on the substantial reality of the soul it is said that the soul is, in a sense analogous to the material body-thing, a substantial-real unity — in contrast to the pure ego, which according to the foregoing is not such a unity.

The psychic ego or soul is an entirely different unity, also principally referred to a monadic consciousness-connection, from the pure ego. What essentially lies in it we recognize by starting from the psychological ego-idea and clarifying the sense of talk about human or animal subjects by returning to the demonstrating experience. The psychic ego, the human one for example, taken apart from bodiliness, is not the monadic flux that experientially belongs to this body, and also not something occurring as a real moment in this flux, but rather a unity essentially referred to it yet in a certain sense transcendent to it. The subject is now a substrate for properties (personal properties in a certain broadest sense) analogously to how a material thing is substrate for thing-real properties.

The soul is related to the consciousness-experiences in such a way that it has them, lives through them, and lives in them; but these experiences are not its properties but its mere modes of behavior, its mere “psychic states.” The analogy of the psychic unity with the unity of the material thing extends so far that we can say: between material properties that attest themselves in the changing physical behavior of the thing and psychic properties that attest themselves in corresponding experiences as psychic modes of behavior, there is, formally speaking, full analogy. Psychic properties are thus “unities of attestation.”

Among the psychic properties stands every personal property: the intellectual character of the human being and all intellectual dispositions belonging to it, the feeling-character, the practical character, every spiritual ability and skill, mathematical giftedness, logical acuity, magnanimity, friendliness, self-denial, and so on. Also the senses and their characteristic dispositions are psychic properties. Every psychic property has a relation to determinate groups of actual and possible experiences that stand to it similarly to how every material property stands to the actual and possible schematic “appearances” in which it attests itself.

The soul itself is nothing more than the unity of its properties; in its states it “behaves” thus and so, in its properties it “is,” and each of its properties is a mere ray of its being. We can also express this: the soul is the unity of the “spiritual capacities” built upon the lower sensory ones, and it is nothing more. If the old psychology was a doctrine of capacities, then it was what it alone could and had to be as a science of the soul. If it erred, that lay not in the supposedly “mistaken faculty psychology” but in its methodological failure — its omission of the systematic study of psychic states, especially of “consciousness-states,” which, as the material of attestation of everything psychic, would have required the most thorough study.

The main point supporting the analogy must be expressly noted. Modes of behavior, as real ones in the material sphere, point back to “real circumstances,” and only in the interplay of modes of behavior and circumstances of behavior does the substantial-real property attest itself in the framework of originally giving experience. Exactly so with the soul: the soul (or the psychic subject) behaves in a regulated manner under the belonging circumstances. This lies enclosed in the experiential apprehensions and is therefore phenomenologically to be drawn from them.

Looking back at the pure ego by comparison with the psychic one, it must be noted that although it too is said to “behave” thus and so in its acts, the concept of modes of behavior and states applicable to it is totally different from that holding in the sphere of reality, where all modes of behavior are, according to the constituting apprehension, causally referred to “circumstances.” This is a radical difference of sense, since causality and substantiality are not external annexes but point back to fundamental kinds of apperception.

### §31. Formal-General Concept of Reality

The analogies established between matter and soul, which could certainly be pursued further, are grounded in a commonality of ontological form. Through this, a formal-general and obviously highly important concept of reality — namely substantial reality — determines itself from originary sources. In formal generality, the concepts real substance (concretely understood as thing in a broadest sense), real property, real state (real behavior), and real causality are essentially connected concepts. Realities are what they are only with reference to other actual and possible realities in the interweaving of substantial “causality.”

### §32. Fundamental Differences of Material and Psychic Reality

Husserl now asks in what respect reality in the indicated sense can be spoken of equally for material and psychic nature. As for causality, it is an idea constitutive for material nature, the idea of the physical thing: all “inner” features of the thing as a persisting being are themselves persisting, and every such feature expresses a persisting law-governed behavior in the causal context of change.

For the reality of the soul, things stand differently. It too is a persisting being over against changing circumstances, but it requires closer examination what sort these “circumstances” are and what kind of “persisting” this is. It is the bearer of a psychic life with its subjective possession, extending as a unity through time (the same time in which the body endures), and it “acts into” the physical and receives effects from there. Thanks to these regulated modes of behavior, psychophysical properties are attributed to it. But these are not persisting properties of the same kind as the thingly ones.

The thing constitutes itself as a unity of schemata, specifically as a unity of causal necessity in the context of dependencies presenting themselves in manifolds of schemata. The soul, by contrast, does not schematize itself. For the material, the manifold that functions as state is of the type “schema” (sensibly filled corporeal extension). The schema itself is already a unity of attestation — unity in the manifolds of adumbrations. All this implies that the attestation through adumbrations is something principally different from the attestation of real properties through states.

If we orient the concepts “nature” and “reality” by the essence of the material thing, the soul must be said not to have substantiality in that sense. The soul has no “in itself” like “nature”: neither a mathematical nature like the thing of physics nor a nature like the thing of intuition (since it is not a schematized unity). Regarding causality: if we call causality the functional or lawful dependency-relation that is the correlate of the constitution of persisting properties of a persisting real of the type “nature,” then in the case of the soul there is no causality to speak of at all. Not every lawfully regulated functionality in the sphere of facts is causality.

The material thing can, as a principled possibility, be entirely unchanged — unchanged in its properties and even in its states. But the psychic “thing” can in principle not remain unchanged; it cannot persist in an unchanged psychic state. Psychic life is by essential necessity a flux; it thus lacks any analogue of a spatial form as the form of possibly unchanged existing realities. With the necessity of change of states, there is given for the soul the necessity of change of psychic properties through new formation of dispositions: every experience leaves dispositions and creates something new for the psychic reality. The soul itself is thus a constantly self-changing reality.

While the material thing is principally fragmentable — parallel to its extension — the soul has no places, no parts. It is an absolutely unfragmentable unity, in the genuine and strict sense of one soul. Not excluded, however, is that the psychic ego might change individual dispositions, entire groups and connections, the entire “character” in a rapid transition and thus “become a completely different one.”

### §33. Closer Determination of the Concept of Reality

Material things are exclusively conditioned from outside and are not conditioned by their own past; they are historyless realities. This follows from the fact that to the essence of material reality belongs the possibility of material identity through all changes, in such a way that no addition or fragmentation of materiality takes place. So constituted is material reality that upon a cyclical return to identically the same external circumstances, it would necessarily have identically the same total state. By contrast, it belongs to the essence of psychic reality that it in principle cannot return to the same total state: psychic realities have a history. Two bordering cycles of external circumstances would act in the same manner on the same soul, but in the soul itself the psychic courses of states could not be the same, because the earlier state functionally determines the later.

Thus realities seem to divide principally into mere nature-realities, supra-natural realities (natureless, having no nature-sides or nature-determinations at all), and mixed realities that, like the soul, have a nature-side and an idiopsychic side. Correspondingly, on one side we have a stock of persisting properties determinable logico-mathematically as to their “in-itself,” and on the other side “properties” of an entirely different kind — unities that by their essence are in a constantly flowing transformation and development and in principle admit of no mathematization.

If we orient the concepts “nature” and “reality” by the essence of the material thing, we must say that they do not belong to the psychical as such. But through its connection with the corporeal, the psychical has an attachment to nature and “existence” in a second sense: existence in space, existence in space-time. And so it has a quasi-nature and a quasi-causality. The attachment to the body provides this “quasi-nature” with integration into the context of nature in the narrower sense. The result of the consideration leads us back to the starting point: what we have to contrast with material nature as a second kind of reality is not the “soul” but the concrete unity of body and soul, the human (or animal) subject.

### §34. Necessity of the Distinction Between Naturalistic and Personalistic Attitude

Before entering into the closer treatment of the constitution of this reality, Husserl inserts a consideration to dispel an obvious concern. In relation to the psychic ego taken as constituted through physiopsychic and idiopsychic dependencies, difficulties arise. What we have given in immediate experiential apprehension together with the human body as human subject is the human person, who has spiritual individuality, intellectual and practical abilities and skills, character, and disposition.

This ego is certainly apprehended as dependent on its body and thereby on the rest of physical nature, and also as dependent on its own past. But something striking emerges: in my theoretical, emotional, and practical behavior — in my experiencing, thinking, my position-takings of liking, rejoicing, hoping, wishing, desiring, willing — I feel myself conditioned by things; but this evidently does not mean psychophysically conditioned. The person’s reality consists precisely in having real properties (personal properties) that possess regulated relations to this surrounding world.

There is a puzzling situation. What the naturalistic thinker would say — that the individuality dissolves into a connection of bodies in physical nature with a system of psychic correlates — may not suffice. Husserl insists that we must here acknowledge two modes of apprehension as essentially different: the psychological apprehension and experience versus the human-scientific (personal) apprehension and experience. The ego “psychically” apprehended is the psychic ego; the ego apprehended in a human-scientific manner is the personal ego or the spiritual individual.

## Third Chapter: The Constitution of Psychic Reality Through the Body

### §35. Transition to the Constitutive Consideration of “The Human Being as Nature”

The theme of the following considerations is the constitution of the natural reality “human being” (or animal being) — the human being as it presents itself in naturalistic consideration: as material body upon which new strata of being, the bodily-psychic ones, are built. It is possible that much must be drawn into this constitutive consideration that later investigations will show belongs to the personal or spiritual ego. The definitive separation between “the human being as nature” and “the human being as spirit” can be created only when both objectualities have been subjected to constitutive consideration.

### §36. Constitution of the Body as Bearer of Localized Sensations (Empfindnisse)

Husserl now turns to the constitution of bodiliness itself. We can choose the special case where the spatially experienced body that is perceived by means of “the body” is the body-body itself. For the body too is externally perceived, though within certain limits that prevent its being regarded without further ado as a thing like others.

When I touch my left hand with my right, I have touch-appearances — I perceive a soft, smooth, shaped hand. The indicating movement-sensations and the representing touch-sensations, which are objectivated as features of the thing “left hand,” belong to the right hand. But touching the left hand, I also find in it series of touch-sensations: these are “localized” in it, yet they are not property-constituting (as roughness and smoothness of the hand, this physical thing, are). If I speak of the physical thing “left hand,” I abstract from these sensations — a lead ball has nothing of the sort, nor does any “merely” physical thing. If I include them, the physical thing is not enriched; rather, it becomes a body, it senses.

Touch-sensations belong to every appearing objective spatial place of the touched hand when it is touched at that place. Likewise, the touching hand, which itself appears as a thing, has its touch-sensations at the spatial-corporeal place where it touches. When one body-part touches another, we have this doubly in both body-parts, because each is for the other a touching, affecting external thing, and each is at the same time body.

The body thus constitutes itself originally in a double way: on one hand it is a physical thing, matter, having its extension with its real properties — coloring, smoothness, hardness, warmth, and so on. On the other hand, I find on it and sense “on” it and “in” it: warmth on the back of the hand, cold in the feet, touch-sensations at the fingertips. These occurrences that we call Empfindnisse (sensings) are absent from “merely” material things.

The localized sensations are not properties of the body as a physical thing, but they are properties of the thing-body, namely effect-properties. They occur when the body is touched, pressed, pricked, and they occur where it is so affected and at the time when it is so affected. The same sensation of pressure from the hand lying on the table is apprehended at one time as perception of the table surface and, with “another direction of attention,” in the actualization of another apprehension-stratum, as finger-pressure-sensation. Similarly with cold of the thing-surface and cold-sensation in the finger.

### §37. Differences Between the Visual and Tactile Domain

Husserl identifies a striking difference between the visual and tactile spheres. In the tactile domain we have the tactually constituting external object and a second object — the body, likewise tactually constituting itself — and we have finger touching finger. Here lies that double apprehension: the same touch-sensation, apprehended as feature of the “external” object and apprehended as sensation of the body-object. And where one body-part simultaneously becomes external object for another, we have double-sensations (each has its sensations) and double apprehension.

We do not have anything similar with the purely visually constituting object. The eye does not appear visually in the relevant way, and it is not the case that the same colors are localized as sensations at the visually appearing eye which are attributed to the seen external thing as features. There is no spread-out eye-ness such that eye could pass along eye and the phenomenon of double-sensation could arise. I do not see myself as I touch myself. What I call seen body is not seen seeing, as my body as touched body is touched touching. A visual appearance of an object that sees — in which the light-sensation is intuited as being in it — is absent. The role of visual sensations in the correlative constitution of body and external things is thus different from that of touch-sensations. The eye is also a localization field, but only for touch-sensations and, as every “freely movable” organ, for muscle-sensations.

Localization of Empfindnisse is in fact something principally different from extension of all material thing-determinations. They do spread out in space, cover spatial surfaces in their way, and so on. But this spreading-out is something essentially different from extension in the sense of all the determinations that characterize the res extensa. The Empfindnis that spreads over and into the palm is not a real thing-characteristic (always speaking within the framework of intuitions and their givennesses) as the roughness of the hand, its color, and so on are. The latter constitute themselves through sensory schema and adumbration-manifolds. For the Empfindnis, it makes no sense to speak of such things.

A merely eye-possessing subject could have no appearing body at all; it would have its thing-appearances in the play of kinaesthetic motivations (which it could not apprehend bodily), it would see real things. The body can constitute itself as such originally only in tactuality and in everything that localizes itself with touch-sensations, such as warmth, cold, pain, and the like.

### §38. The Body as Will-Organ and Bearer of Free Movement

The distinction of the body as localization field is the presupposition for its further distinctions over against all material things: in particular, that it — already taken as body (as the thing that has its stratum of localized sensations) — is a will-organ, the only object that is immediately spontaneously movable for the will of my pure ego, and the means for producing a mediate spontaneous movement of other things. Mere material things are only mechanically movable and only mediately spontaneously movable; only bodies are immediately spontaneously (“freely”) movable, through the free ego belonging to them and its will. These free acts are what allow — as we saw earlier — an object-world to constitute itself for this ego in manifold perceptual series.

### §39. Significance of the Body for the Constitution of Higher Objectualities

Beyond this, the body is also involved in all other “consciousness-functions,” and this has various sources. Not only the sensory sensations that have constitutive function for the constitution of sense-things have immediately intuitive localization and body-relatedness grounded therein, but also sensations of entirely other groups: the “sensory” feelings, pleasure and pain sensations, the well-being streaming through and filling the whole body, the general malaise of “bodily indisposition,” and the like. Here belong groups of sensations that play an analogous role as stuff for the intentional experiences of the feeling-sphere, or for the constitution of values as their intentional correlates, as the primary sensations do for the intentional experiences of the experience-sphere, or for the constitution of spatial-thingly objects. Further belong here various hard-to-analyze sensations forming the material foundations of the desiring and willing life: sensations of energetic tension and relaxation, of inner inhibition, paralysis, liberation, and so on.

All these sensation-groups have as Empfindnisse an immediate bodily localization: they belong for every human being immediately intuitively to the body as his own body. With this stratum the intentional functions are connected: the stuffs receive spiritual formation, just as above the primary sensations receive apprehension, enter into perceptions, upon which perceptual judgments build, and so on. In this way the entire consciousness of a human being is in a certain way connected with its body through its hyletic foundation. But the intentional experiences themselves are no longer directly and properly localized; they no longer form a stratum at the body. Perception as touching apprehension of shape does not sit in the touching finger; thinking is not really intuitively localized in the head the way tension-Empfindnisse are.

### §40. More on the Localization of Empfindnisse and the Non-thingly Properties of the Body

Husserl asks how the constitution of the body through its Empfindnis-stratum is to be understood and what creates unity here. If an object moves mechanically touching over my skin surface, I have a determinately ordered sequence of Empfindnisse; if it always moves in the same way, the result is always the same. This is all “self-evident” — it lies in the apprehension: under such circumstances this body-body behaves in such a way that it is not merely excitable at all, but excitable in a determinate way under determinate circumstances. The extensional locality corresponds to a place-moment in the sensation, and the stimulus-strengths and stimulus-kinds to determinate moments that make the sensation concrete.

The sensibility of the body thus constitutes itself entirely as a “conditional” or psychophysical property. The body is apprehended not merely with the thing-apprehension but with the co-apprehension of sensation-fields, given as belonging in the manner of localization to the appearing body. The body stands there in concrete perception as a novel apprehension-unity, a physico-aesthesiological unity. It is constituted as its own objectuality fitting the formal-general concept of reality as a thing that preserves its identical properties over against changing external circumstances — but where the dependency-relations in which it stands to external nature are different from those of material things among themselves.

### §41. Constitution of the Body as Material Thing in Contrast to Other Material Things

Husserl has shown how, correlatively to the material world, a subject of bodily-psychical capacities constitutes itself, with the body appearing simultaneously as body and as material thing — but as a thing of a special kind.

#### a) The Body as Orientation-Center

Every ego has its thingly perceptual domain and necessarily perceives things in a certain orientation. Things appear from this or that side, and in this mode of appearance the reference to a Here and its basic directions is irremovably enclosed. All spatial being appears necessarily as nearer or farther, as above or below, as right or left. The body has for its ego the unique distinction that it bears the null-point of all these orientations within itself. One of its spatial points is constantly characterized in the mode of the ultimate central Here — a Here that has no other outside it in relation to which it would be a “There.” All things of the surrounding world have their orientation to the body. Thanks to its capacity for free movability, the subject can bring the system of its appearances and thereby the orientations into flux, but the form of the orientation-order with a center necessarily remains preserved.

#### b) Peculiarity of the Appearance-Manifolds of the Body

While I have the freedom to change my position relative to all other things and thereby to vary the appearance-manifolds arbitrarily, I do not have the possibility of removing myself from my body or it from me. Correspondingly, the appearance-manifolds of the body are restricted in a determinate way: certain body-parts I can see only in a peculiar perspectival foreshortening, and others (such as the head) are altogether invisible to me. The same body that serves me as the means of all perception stands in my way in the perception of itself and is a remarkably incompletely constituted thing.

#### c) The Body as Member of the Causal Context

If we nonetheless apprehend the body as a real thing, this is because we find it integrated into the causal context of material nature. Alongside the free kinaesthetic courses there appear others characterized not as “done” but as “done to,” as passive courses in which spontaneity has no part. In this case we simultaneously have an experience of the mechanical process of bodily movement and a givenness of this process in the “psychical” character of undergoing. Movements of my body are apprehended as mechanical processes like those of external things, the body itself as a thing that acts upon others and upon which others act.

### §42. Characterization of the Solipsistically Constituted Body

Summarizing: (1) Seen from “inside” — in “inner attitude” — the body appears as a freely movable organ (or system of such organs) by means of which the subject experiences the external world; further, as bearer of sensations and, thanks to the interweaving these enter with the rest of psychic life, as forming a concrete unity with the soul. (2) Seen from outside — in “outer attitude” — it stands there as a reality of its own kind: as a material thing of special modes of appearance, “switched in” between the rest of the material world and the “subjective” sphere; as center around which the remaining spatial world groups itself; as standing in causal relations to the real external world, but at the same time as “conversion point” where causal relations are converted into conditional ones between external world and bodily-psychical subject.

But in solipsistic experience we do not reach the givenness of ourselves as a spatial thing like all others, nor the natural object “human being” (animal being). To get there we must go beyond our own subject and turn to the animalia that encounter us in the external world.

## Fourth Chapter: The Constitution of Psychic Reality in Empathy

### §43. Givenness of Other Animalia

In experience, in the sphere of original constitution, what is originally given is the multiplicity of things in space and time in manifold appearances, and among them animals, including human beings (“rational” living beings): not as connections of separately given items, but as double-unities, unities distinguishing two strata within themselves — unities of things and subjects with their psychic life. With the human-apperception is eo ipso also given the possibility of mutual relations, of communication between human and human, and the identity of nature for all human beings and animals.

### §44. Ur-presence and Appresence

Husserl introduces a fundamental distinction between Urpräsenz (original presence) and Appräsenz (appresence). An experience in which a subject has the perceived object given in original presence means that the object stands there “actually,” “in the original,” and is not merely “co-presented.” Appresence points back to original presence. The objects of perception then divide into: (1) those that can be originally present not only for one subject but, when originally present for one, can ideally be originally given for every other subject — the totality of possibly originally present objects forming a domain of common original presence is nature in the first and most originary sense, the spatio-temporal-material nature; (2) the subjective over against the objective — the individually unique, temporal, total stock of what is originally present that can be given as originally present to only one subject. Therein belongs every subject itself with all its acts, states, noematic correlates, and also with the bodiliness and properties that constitute themselves for it in inner attitude.

### §45. Animalia as Originally Present Body-Bodies with Appresent Interiority

The originally experienceable, the being presentable in original presence, is not all being, not even all experienceable being. Realities that cannot be given in original presence for several subjects are the animalia: they contain subjectivities. They are peculiar objectualities whose original givenness presupposes original presences while they themselves cannot be given in original presence. The externally given bodies I experience in original presence as I do other things; the interiority of the psychical, through appresence.

I find in my physical surrounding world bodies — material things of the type of the material thing “my body” constituted in solipsistic experience — and I apprehend them as bodies, that is, I empathize into each an ego-subject with everything belonging to it. Thereupon, the system of appresentations that has its original template in the solipsistic subject in original connections of regular coexistence grows, through continued experience of other humans already constituted through empathy, into a system of ordered indications. To the appearance of the other human being there belongs, beyond what was mentioned, also the psychical act-interiority.

### §46. Significance of Empathy for the Constitution of the Reality “Ego-Human-Being”

Under the title “other human being” we have a body in unity with sense-fields and soul-fields, with a subject of acts. This belonging obtains of course also for myself. Originally given to me in my own case were pieces of these belongingnesses; the rest is then empirical extension, transference in experiential thinking. But in the attitude of “self-experience,” it would not occur to me to seriously stuff all my psychical life into my body, to “introject” it. Only with empathy and with the constant direction of experiential consideration upon the psychic life appresented with the other body and constantly taken objectively together with it does the closed unity “human being” constitute itself, and this I then transfer to myself.

As for the experience of others: every human being stands there bodily in the spatial context, among things, and to every body belongs its entire and determinately empathized psychic life, so that when the body moves and is at ever new places, its soul also “moves along” as it were: it is constantly one with the body. But the soul is nowhere, and its connection with the body is grounded only through functional connections: the body is “organ” of the subject. The “being-somewhere” of the human being has its good sense, but “being regularly coordinated and thereby localized” and “being in space itself” are two different things. That body and soul form a peculiar experiential unity and that the psychical obtains its place in space and time by virtue of this unity — therein consists the legitimate “naturalization” of consciousness.

### §47. Empathy and Nature-Constitution

Empathy leads to the constitution of the intersubjective objectivity of the thing and thereby also of the human being, in that now the physical body is a natural-scientific object. The natural-scientific object is the x of “mathematical” qualities, toward whose substructing the causal analysis and the thinking grounded upon it compel. The seen, actually experienced things with their actually experienced causalities proved themselves, in their intuitive qualities, to be subjectively conditioned. So the human being now has, as natural-scientific object, a peculiar structure: the physical body is mathematical (“theoretical”) substructions pointing back to the “merely subjective” appearances; the higher stratum is the subjectivity localized in the substructed mathematical spatial body — the soul with its consciousness-experiences.

The analysis of nature and naturalistic consideration shows that it requires supplementation, that it harbors presuppositions within itself and thus points beyond itself to another domain of being and research: the field of subjectivity that is no longer nature.

## Third Section: The Constitution of the Spiritual World

### §48. Introduction

The following investigations are devoted to clarifying a connected group of metaphysical and science-theoretical distinctions that all have their source in the difficult distinction between soul and spirit, which is thus the fundamental one in this whole group of distinctions. Dependent on it are the contrasts between nature and spiritual world, between natural sciences and human sciences (Geisteswissenschaften), between natural-scientific psychology on one side and personality-theory (ego-theory, egology) and social theory (community-theory) on the other. Our entire worldview is fundamentally determined by the clarification of these distinctions.

Within phenomenology, great research-domains correspond to them. For decades, reactions have made themselves felt against the naturalistic interpretation of the human sciences — self-evident to the natural-scientific age — as merely descriptive natural sciences. Dilthey stands in first place here.

## First Chapter: Contrast Between the Naturalistic and Personalistic World

### §49. The Personalistic Attitude in Contrast to the Naturalistic

Husserl ties his considerations to the findings of the previous sections, which were carried out in reference to the naturalistic attitude. He now announces a new attitude that in a certain sense is very natural but is not natural-istic: what is experienced in it is not nature in the sense of all natural sciences, but rather a counter-image of nature. The difficulty lies not in performing the attitudes — we constantly glide effortlessly from one attitude to the other, from the naturalistic into the personalistic, in the relevant sciences from the natural-scientific into the human-scientific — but in the reflection and in the phenomenological understanding of the apprehension-changes and experience-changes and of the correlates constituted through them. Only within the framework of phenomenology can these differences be maintained without confusion, in absolutely secure separation.

#### a) Introjection of the Soul as Presupposition Also for the Naturalistic Attitude

Starting from the soul: it is, natural-scientifically, nothing for itself; it is a mere stratum of real occurrences at bodies. In material nature, certain distinguished things occur — distinguished through a stratum of real characteristics that are not specifically physical, not “material,” “extensive”: the properties of excitability or sensibility. The new properties constitute themselves in the form of “localization” and are, by their sense, dependent on physical bodiliness. Likewise the higher stratum, the specifically psychical, is experienced in a similar way — also as a stratum at the body-thing, as it is in a certain way also “localized.” The soul animates the body and the ensouled body is a natural object within the unity of the spatiotemporal world.

Husserl illustrates with the example of watching a cat play: I see it as a physical organism but also as a sensing and ensouled body, as a cat. The sensation-stratum is not there as something beside the physical thing; there is a body that has physical and aesthesiological characteristics in unity. Likewise the body is experienced as the body of a soul. The soul animates the body, and the ensouled body is a natural object within the spatiotemporal world. One could use the admittedly misleading expression “introjection” to describe this situation: the surplus of reality over the merely physical thing is nothing separable on its own, nothing beside but rather at the physical thing; it moves “with” it and gains its spatial determination through being at the spatial.

#### b) Localization of the Psychical

Every stratum is a constituted unity. The “material” of the constitution — the sensation-material — can be considered in its essence when we strip away the realizing apprehension. We can then find unities in the manifold that are no longer nature-unities. With such a change of attitude, all ordering into the objective world, into world-space and world-time, is lost. The soul is in the body and where the body currently is. As soon as consciousness loses the apperceptive apprehension as psychical state, as stratum at the body — as soon as it is purely posited as itself in phenomenological reduction — its empirical ordering into objective space falls away.

#### c) Temporalization of the Psychical (Immanent Time and Space-Time)

The same holds for time. Pure consciousness has its own time-field, a field of “phenomenological” time, not to be confused with “objective” time constituting itself along with nature. Through the psychical apprehension, consciousness-experiences obtain the sense of psychophysical states, and therewith their ordering into objective time. The temporalization of consciousness-time is a particularly deep-going one, since the latter, so to speak, coincides perfectly with absolute time.

#### d) Methodical Reflection

In these considerations Husserl has made use of the right of phenomenological reduction. Its liberating power lies in freeing us from the sense-limits of the natural attitude and every relative attitude. The natural human being and especially the natural scientist does not notice these limits, does not notice that all his results are tagged with a determinate index indicating their merely relative sense. The educational value of the phenomenological reduction lies also in making us receptive to changes of attitude coordinate with the natural (or, as we now more clearly say, the natural-istic) attitude, which likewise constitute only relative and limited being- and sense-correlates.

#### e) Naturalistic and Natural Attitude

Husserl now enters the investigation proper. What “human being and human soul as nature” means is held firmly fixed. We then find no difficulty in the fact that the human soul has, among other psychic states, states of the ego-type, occurrences of the type cogito. They are, like the psychical in general, “introjected” into the physically appearing body in the naturalistic experience. That “in” this human being an “I think” appears is a natural fact, grounded in the body and bodily occurrences, determined by the substantial-causal connection of nature.

Among psychic states belong also the acts in which the human being has a consciousness of himself, his fellow human beings, and the surrounding real actuality — acts in which he pursues physical natural science, psychology, history, or also acts as a practical human being, uses the things of his surrounding world, reshapes them, values them aesthetically, ethically, and in terms of utility, or enters into communicative relations with fellow human beings.

An abundance of remarkable relations between the subject and its “surrounding world” belong here, all grounded in the fact that the human being “knows” of himself, his fellows, and of a surrounding world common to all. This surrounding world contains not only mere things but use-objects, artworks, literary products, means of religious and legal actions, and so on. And it contains not only individual persons: persons are members of communities, of personal unities of a higher order.

As soon as we vividly make present any of these personal relations and live ourselves into the personal bearers of these relations, we notice that we are in an essentially different attitude from the naturalistic one previously exercised. In the latter, the “objective” physical total-nature was there for us, with bodies distributed in it, founding sensibilities and psychic lives. Quite otherwise is the personalistic attitude, in which we always are when we live with one another, speak to one another, shake hands in greeting, are related to one another in love and aversion, in thought and deed, in speech and response.

This is an entirely natural and not an artificial attitude. In the natural ego-life we do not always, indeed far from predominantly, view the world naturalistically. Upon closer consideration it will even turn out that the naturalistic attitude subordinates itself to the personalistic and gains a certain independence only through a kind of self-forgetfulness of the personal ego — thereby at the same time illegitimately absolutizing its world, nature.

### §50. The Person as Center of a Surrounding World

As a person I am (and every other person is) what I am as subject of a surrounding world. The concepts ego and surrounding world are inseparably referred to one another. Each person has its surrounding world, while at the same time several communicating persons have a common surrounding world. The surrounding world is the world perceived, remembered, thought, surmised, or inferred by the person in her acts — the world of which this personal ego is conscious, which is there for it, to which it relates in one way or another: thematically experiencing and theorizing regarding the things appearing to it, or feeling, valuing, acting, technically shaping, and so on.

Not simply and in general is the physical reality the actual surrounding world of any person, but only insofar as the person “knows” of it, insofar as it has grasped it through apperception and positing or has it consciously co-given in its existence-horizon — clearly or unclearly, determinately or indeterminately. If the person knows nothing of the discoveries of physics, the world does not belong with the sense-content of physics to its actual surrounding world. The surrounding world is no world “in itself” but a world “for me” — the surrounding world of its ego-subject, experienced by it, posited in its intentional experiences with a respective sense-content.

First the world is, at its core, a sensibly appearing world characterized as “at hand,” given in simple experiential intuitions. Upon this experiential world the ego finds itself related in new acts — valuing acts, acts of liking and disliking. In them the object is conscious as valuable, pleasant, beautiful, and so on, and in various modes. One sees here what a fundamentally new sense the relation of the human being as personal subject to its surrounding-world objectualities has, as opposed to the natural relation between the naturally understood human being (as psychophysical reality) and other realities. Instead of the causal relation between things and human beings as natural realities, there steps the motivation-relation between persons and things, and these things are not the in-themselves-being things of nature but experienced, thought, or otherwise positingly meant things as such — intentional objectualities of personal consciousness.

### §51. The Person in the Personal Association

The subject finds in its surrounding world not only things but also other subjects; it sees them as persons, active in their surrounding world, determined by their objects. In this attitude it does not occur to it to “introject” the spirit into the body. Persons belonging to the social association are given to one another as “fellows,” not as objects but as counter-subjects who “with” one another live, associate, and are related to one another in acts of love and counter-love, hate and counter-hate, trust and counter-trust.

The specifically social, communicative acts — acts in which the ego turns to others, and these others are also conscious as those to whom it turns, and who further understand this turning and possibly direct themselves accordingly — are what establish among persons who already “know” of one another a higher consciousness-unity, drawing the surrounding thing-world into it as the common surrounding world of the position-taking persons. Also the physical world in this apperceptive inclusion has social character: it is a world that has spiritual significance.

Husserl develops a rich account of the communicative surrounding world, the social subjectivity, and the spiritual world. The idea of the world as a spiritual world constitutes itself in the form of the total concept of social subjects in actual or potential communication — together with the belonging total concept of social objectualities. For every subject that is a member of a social total-association, one and the same spiritual world constitutes itself — a connected multiplicity of subjects, of individual spirits and spiritual communities, related to a thing-world that is not spirit but reality for spirit, and yet as reality for spirit constantly also bespirited, spiritually significant.

### §52. Subjective Appearance-Manifolds and Objective Things

To the second class of objects belongs the whole infinity of appearances of things — things of the intersubjectively constituted physical nature. This nature appears to everyone, but to each in principle in a different way. Each has his thing-appearances; at best, with a change of subjective circumstances, one can have the same appearances that another had before; but never can different subjects at the same time-point of intersubjective (“objective”) time have the same appearances. All the sensory schemata, adumbrations, sensory stuffs of which we spoke in thing-constitution are specifically “subjective.”

In the stream of subjective experiences, the subject attests itself as a real personality; the experiences are at the same time its states. By contrast, in the sensory schemata, in the subjective thing-appearances, it is not the subject that attests itself but its surrounding-world things.

Looking back at what was established earlier about the subject and the object-world given to it: the thingly surrounding world of the person, the intuitively present or intuitable surrounding world, coincides with the world as it appeared to the solipsistic subject — things appearing in manifolds of adumbrations, grouping themselves in space around a central Here. Other subjects who stand opposite me can have the same things given in the same determinations, but their actual appearance-manifolds are in principle not the same. Each has his “Here,” and for the same phenomenal Now it is a different one from mine.

Husserl then addresses the question of whether talk of “intersubjective space” and “intersubjective time” has its justification here. Not only as nature but also as spirit — not apprehending myself and others as nature but setting them as such — I find myself and others in the spatial and temporal world. I am now, was before, and will be later. But the person’s being-somewhere is essentially different from introjection: the spirit has a place not as things have a place but in the sense that it stands in a constant functional relation to a body, which for its part has its place in the surrounding world.

### §53. The Relation of Nature-Consideration and Spirit-Consideration to One Another

Husserl now acknowledges a tension between the nature that stood at the beginning of the investigations and the nature that grew up in the communal context. He began from the naturalistic (natural-scientific) attitude, in which nature — as physical, bodily, psychical nature — comes to givenness and theoretical cognition. But this naturalistically considered world is not the world. Rather, what is pregiven is the world as everyday world, and within it arises the theoretical interest in the world and the sciences relating to it. We seem to get caught in a vicious circle: if at the beginning we posited nature simply, persons were subordinate natural objects; but if we followed the essence of personality, nature presented itself as something constituting itself in the intersubjective association of persons, thus presupposing them. Change of attitude means nothing other than thematic transition from one direction of apprehension to another, to which correlatively different objectualities correspond. The question is whether genuinely two worlds are at issue — “nature” on one side, the spiritual world on the other — separated by cardinal differences of being.

## Second Chapter: Motivation as Fundamental Law of the Spiritual World

### §54. The Ego in the Inspectio Sui

In the personalistic attitude I take myself simply as I ordinarily take myself when I say “I” in any “I think.” There is no question of my meaning or finding myself and my cogito as something at the body, as founded in it and localized as an annex. Rather, conversely: the body is my body and it is mine first as my counterpart, my object. What I find as the originally and specifically subjective is the ego in the proper sense — the ego of “freedom,” the attending, considering, comparing, distinguishing, judging, valuing, attracted, repelled, inclined, disinclined, wishing and willing ego: the in every sense “active,” position-taking ego. But this is only one side. Opposite the active stands the passive ego, and the ego is always, where it is active, at the same time passive. What is subjective in the original sense is also the “passive” ego — the ego of “tendencies,” experiencing stimuli from things and appearances, being attracted and merely yielding to the pull. Subjective are also the “states” of the ego — states of mourning, of cheerfulness, passive desiring, resignation as a state.

We find as the originally and specifically subjective: (1) a subjective being as being and behavior of the ego — the subject and its acts, states, activities and passivities; (2) a subjective being as being for the subject — the possession of the ego, consisting of the sensation-material and the totality of objects that constitute themselves for the subject in the course of its genesis.

### §55. The Spiritual Ego in Its Behavior Toward Its Surrounding World

This ego of intentionality is, in the cogito, related to its surrounding world and especially to its real surrounding world. This relation is immediately not a real relation but an intentional relation to something real. The real relation drops away if the thing does not exist; the intentional relation persists. That every time the object exists a real relation “parallels” the intentional one — vibrations spreading through space striking my sense-organs — is a psychophysical fact, but it does not lie in the intentional relation itself.

If we consider the relations of the subject to its surrounding world as the world posited by it, we find a wealth of relations between the posited objects and the “spiritual” subject — subject-object relations. Among these are relations of subjective-objective “causality,” a causality that is not real causality but has a wholly peculiar sense: that of motivation-causality. Experienced objects of the surrounding world exercise a greater or lesser “stimulus,” “awaken” an interest, and by virtue of this interest a tendency of turning-toward, which freely flows out in the turning-toward or flows out only after counter-tendencies are weakened and overcome. All this plays itself out between ego and intentional object.

### §56. Motivation as Fundamental Lawfulness of Spiritual Life

We see that by the spiritual or personal ego is to be understood the subject of intentionality, and that motivation is the lawfulness of spiritual life.

#### a) Reason-Motivation

First, the way perceptions and the like motivate judgments, how judgments justify and correct themselves through experiences, how in conflict with experience the cancelling negation motivates itself, or how judging is motivated by other judging in inference — but also in an entirely different way, how judgments are motivated by affects and affects by judgments, how feelings, desirings, and willings are motivated. In short: the motivation of position-takings by position-takings. Pure reason-motivations are those in the framework of evidence, which, when they prevail in purity, establish constitutive consciousness-unities of a higher level with correlates of the region “genuine being” in the broadest sense.

#### b) Association as Motivation

Here also belongs the entire realm of associations and habits — relations between earlier and later consciousness within one ego-consciousness. This is not motivation of position-takings by position-takings (active theses by active theses), but of experiences of any kind: those that are “precipitates” of earlier reason-acts, or those that are entirely reason-less — sensibility, what obtrudes itself, what is pregiven, the mechanism in the sphere of passivity. The individual therein is motivated in the dark background, has its “psychical grounds,” which one can seek through “psychoanalysis.”

#### c–f) Further Dimensions of Motivation

Husserl analyzes further the interweaving of associative motivations and reason-motivations, the relations between nature-causality and motivation, and the relationships between subjects and things under the viewpoints of causality and motivation. He shows that body and spirit form a comprehensive unity, and that the body is not merely a physical thing but an “ensouled” object carrying spiritual significance.

### §57. Pure Ego and Personal Ego as Object of Reflexive Self-Apperception

Taking the personal ego as found in the inspectio (without regard to its unity with the expressing body given to us in empathy), it seems at first not to differ from the pure ego. But when in living, unreflected acts of reflecting I move in fields of reflection (the objectivated subjectivities), I experience how I “behave” under various subjective circumstances, what experiential peculiarity I possess as a motivation-subject — in other words, what kind of personal subject I am. We thus distinguish from the pure ego-reflection the reflective thematic experience of the personal ego with reference to its “personal properties” or “character-properties.”

### §58. Constitution of the Personal Ego Before Reflection

Reflecting, I always already find myself as a personal ego. But originally it constitutes itself in the genesis pervading the experiential flux. The great question is whether the personal ego constitutes itself on the basis of ego-reflections, or whether it already constitutes itself in the pre-reflective sphere through associative connections. The ego is originally not constituted from experience in the sense of associative apperception but from life: it is what it is, not for the ego, but is itself the ego.

### §59. The Ego as Subject of Capacities

The ego as unity is a system of “I can.” We must distinguish between the physical “I can” (bodily and bodily-mediated) and the spiritual “I can.” The spiritual ego can be apprehended as an organism of capacities, their development in a normal typical style, with the stages of childhood, youth, maturity, and age. Capacity is no empty ability but a positive potentiality that in each case comes to actualization, constantly in readiness to pass over into activity. The personal ego constitutes itself in original genesis not only as a personality determined by drives but also as a higher, autonomous, freely active ego, led by reason-motivations.

### §60. Person as Subject of Reason-Acts, as “Free Ego”

Husserl develops four sub-themes: (a) the “I can” as practical possibility, as neutrality-modification of practical acts, and as original capacity-consciousness; (b) the “I can” motivated in knowledge of one’s own person — self-apperception and self-understanding; (c) external influences and freedom of the person; and (d) the generally typical and the individually typical in the understanding of persons.

### §61. The Spiritual Ego and Its Foundation

The foundations upon which the understandably motivated spiritual life of the other builds up present themselves as “modifications” of my own. Here we come again to the old distinction between reason and sensibility. The sensory sphere, the sphere of the most broadly conceived foundation, contains associations, perseverations, determining tendencies, and so on. This “makes” the constitution of nature, but it also continues after nature is there for the spirits: through all spiritual life runs the “blind” efficacy of associations, drives, feelings as stimuli. Spirit is not an abstract ego of position-taking acts but the full personality — the ego-human-being who takes positions, thinks, values, acts, accomplishes works. To it belongs a foundation of experiences and a foundation of nature (“my nature”), which attests itself in the mechanism of experiences. This nature is the lower psychical.

## Third Chapter: The Ontological Priority of the Spiritual World Over the Naturalistic

### §62. Interpenetration of Personalistic and Naturalistic Attitude

At the point where the two attitudes — naturalistic and personalistic — and correlatively the two kinds of reality — nature and spirit — come into relation with one another, Husserl shows that the spirit in its spiritual acts is dependent on the soul, insofar as the experiential stream lets the acts well up from itself; the spiritual ego depends on the soul and the soul on the body. But the spirit does not therefore stand in a relation of causality to nature. It has a foundation that is conditionally dependent; it has as spirit a soul, a complex of nature-like dispositions that are conditioned by physical nature. The spirit in its freedom moves the body and thereby accomplishes an effect in the spiritual world. Works, however, are as things at the same time objects in the natural world, just as the body is simultaneously an object of the spiritual world and a thing in nature.

### §63. Psychophysical Parallelism and Interaction

Husserl examines the problems of psychophysical parallelism and interaction, showing that neither the parallelistic nor the interactionist framework adequately captures the relationship between nature and spirit. The spirit’s dependency on nature is real but not exhaustive of its being; the spiritual life has its own lawfulness — that of motivation — that is irreducible to natural causality. The constitutive analyses show that the naturalistic and personalistic attitudes cut through the absolute consciousness in different ways, each yielding its own lawful order.

### §64. Relativity of Nature, Absoluteness of Spirit

From the foregoing considerations follows the limit of possible naturalization: the spirit can be apprehended as dependent on nature and itself naturalized, but only up to a certain degree. A univocal spirit-determination through merely nature-like dependencies, a reduction to something like physical nature — something analogous in its entire mode of determination to the goal of natural-scientific univocal determination — is inconceivable. Subjects cannot consist entirely in being nature, for then what gives nature its sense would be absent. Nature is a field of thoroughgoing relativities and can be so because these are always relative to something absolute that thus bears all relativities: the spirit.

If we strike all spirits from the world, there is no nature anymore. But if we strike nature — the “true,” objective-intersubjective existence — something still remains: the spirit as individual spirit; only the possibility of sociality is lost, the possibility of a comprehension that presupposes a certain intersubjectivity of the body. Even so, we have an ego with its consciousness-life, and even therein it has its individuality, its way of judging, valuing, letting itself be motivated in its position-takings.

Individuality in the spiritual sense is something entirely different from natural individuality. The thing has its individual essence as what is here and now. But this “what” itself is a “universal”: every thing is an exemplar of a universality. The spirit, by contrast, is an experiencing, position-taking, motivated being. Every spirit has its manner of motivation; unlike the thing, it has in itself its motivation. It is not individuality first by being at a determinate place in the world. Absolute individuation already belongs to the pure ego of the respective cogitatio, which is itself something absolutely individual. Spirits are not unities of appearances but unities of absolute consciousness-connections, more precisely ego-unities. And appearances are correlates of consciousness-connections that have their absolute being. All individuation of appearances depends on the absolute individuation of spirits; all nature-existence depends on the existence of absolute spirits.

## Supplements (Beilagen)

The volume contains fourteen supplements (Beilagen I–XIV) drawn from various manuscript sources, dating from 1912 to approximately 1928. These supplements amplify, deepen, and in some cases provide alternative treatments of themes from the main text. They include: an attempt at a stepwise description of constitution (Beilage I); the ego as pole and the ego of habitualities (Beilage II); localization of ringing in the ear (Beilage III); a draft introduction to the “Constitution of the Spiritual World” (Beilage IV); the pre-givennesses of spirit in spiritual life (Beilage V); the inspectio sui — “I do” and “I have” (Beilage VI); the ego and its counterpart (Beilage VII); on the unity of “body” and “spirit” (Beilage VIII); spiritual products (Beilage IX); the personal ego and surrounding world, the levels of constitution of objective reality, pure ego and personal ego (Beilage X); the inductively-naturally apprehended human being and the free personality (Beilage XI); supplements to the third section of the second book, divided into two major parts — (I) the person, the spirit and its psychical foundation, and (II) subjectivity as soul and as spirit in natural-scientific and human-scientific attitude (Beilage XII); “personal subjectivity” as theoretical theme (Beilage XIII); and the human-scientific attitude with natural science placed within it, covering the different kinds of intuitive causality, abstract-scientific research, natural science within human science, and the concept of objectivity (Beilage XIV).

These supplements extend and deepen the analyses of the main text, particularly regarding the constitution of the person, the distinction between naturalistic and personalistic (human-scientific) attitudes, the relationship between soul and spirit, the role of the body in spiritual life, and the ontological priority of spirit over nature. They represent materials spanning the full period of Husserl’s work on these problems from 1912 to 1928, and provide crucial additional perspectives on the central themes of the *Ideen II*.

Husserl now expands the analysis of motivation in multiple directions. He examines how in the unity of a thing-consciousness we have “conditionalities” — a “because” and “so” to be explicated in various directions: if I turn my eyes thus, if I instigate the optical experiential series in a certain way, then I must see this and that. On the noematic side, we have static and dynamic belongingnesses, and the static ones pass over into dynamic ones with the change of experiences. But there is a main difficulty: it is not said that in the unity of my experiential stream every experience is necessarily conditioned by the preceding and co-experienced experiences. Husserl identifies a form of motivation enclosed in the form of inner time-consciousness itself: the subjective form of the Now, the Earlier, and so on, which is something absolutely fixed. In this sense, one can say that the thoroughgoing unity of the consciousness-stream is itself a unity of motivation.

#### d) Motivation on the Noetic and Noematic Side

Grasping the structure of thing-constituting consciousness, Husserl shows that all nature with space, time, causality, and so on is fully dissolved into a web of immanent motivations. We can consider the noetic experiences according to their relations of motivation, the belongingness-connection, whereby progression from positing to positing occurs in this peculiar character of “as a consequence.” Or we can consider the thetic correlates, the themes, in their noematic belongingness, where again on this side the correlative “as a consequence” appears.

#### e) Empathy into Other Persons as Understanding Their Motivations

In empathy, the “mine”-apprehension is naturally also introjected. It is another ego, one I initially do not know but know according to the general ego-being. I know what “a personality,” a human being, is in general, and it is the business of the empathy-experience to instruct me progressively about his character, his knowledge and ability, and so on. Common to both the self-regard and the regard of others is the givenness of the fellow human being through comprehension, but this functions differently in each case: once what is comprehended is nature, the other time it is spirit.

#### f) Nature-Causality and Motivation

The “because-so” of motivation has an entirely different sense from causation in the sense of nature. No causal research, however far-reaching, can improve the understanding we have when we have understood the motivation of a person. The unity of motivation is a connection founded in the relevant acts themselves. When we ask about the “because,” about the ground of a personal behavior, we want nothing other than to come to know this connection. When the human-scientific researcher speaks of “rules” or “laws” under which modes of behavior or formations of cultural configurations stand, the “causalities” in question are nothing less than nature-causalities. The historian’s question is: what did the members of the social group present, think, value, will in their communal life? How did these people mutually “determine” one another and let themselves be determined by the surrounding thing-world?

The true nature of the physicist is a methodically necessary substructions of thinking and is only to be constituted as such; only as “mathematical” does it have its truth. By contrast, it makes no sense to underlay motivation — which is to be grasped originally-intuitively — with any unanschaulich substructions of thinking.

What I do not “know,” what does not stand before me in my experiencing, presenting, thinking, acting as presented, perceived, remembered, thought — does not “determine” me spiritually. And what is not in my experiences, even if unnoticed or implicitly intentionally enclosed, does not motivate me, not even in an unconscious way.

#### g) Relations Between Subjects and Things Under the Viewpoints of Causality and Motivation

Husserl asks: when we consider subjects as subjects of intersubjective motivations and find them determining others and determined by them, is this not causation? He distinguishes: (1) relations of the real subject to real objects (things, persons) that are genuinely real, that is, real-causal effects, where objects and persons are posited as natural, real, dependent in their real existence and so-being on one another; (2) relations between subjects, which are not posited as causal-real objects, and intuited, judged things “as such” — that is, between subjects and thing-noemata, which relations necessarily have their reverse side in relations between certain acts of the subjects.

In the comprehensive consciousness — for example, when we associate with other persons, when they address us and we address them, receive commands from them, fulfill them, and so on — we do not find the naturalistic unity between body and soul. The things surrounding us are our intuitive counterpart; they have their colors, smells, and so on. Everything here is intuitive thinghood. And as for the persons who stand opposite us in society, their bodies are naturally given intuitively, and in unity with them their personality. But we do not find two externally intertwined things — bodies and persons. We find unitary human beings who associate with us, and the bodies are included in the human unity. In their intuitive content — in the typical of bodiliness in general, in many particular variations of facial expression, gesture, spoken “word,” tone of voice — the spiritual life of the persons expresses itself: their thinking, feeling, desiring, their doing and non-doing. We are altogether not in the attitude of grasping nature-causality. In empathy, consciousness is set in relation with consciousness, my will and the other’s will in a determinate consciousness-milieu, and in a somewhat modified way — as compared with individual consciousness — one act motivates the other here.

#### h) Body and Spirit as Comprehensive Unity; “Bespirited” Objects

The thoroughly intuitive unity that presents itself where we grasp one another as persons — for example, speaking to persons or listening to their speaking, working together with them, watching their doing — is the unity of “expression” and “expressed,” which belongs to the essence of all comprehensive unities. The unity body-spirit is not the only one of this kind. When I read the “pages and lines” of this book, physical things stand there, the book is a body, the pages are pages of paper sheets, the lines are blackenings and physical imprints. But do I grasp this when I “see” the book, when I “read” it? Obviously I am differently attuned. I see the thingly, insofar as it appears to me, but I “live comprehendingly in the sense.” And in doing so, the spiritual unity of the sentence and the sentence-connections stands before me, and these in turn have their character — for example, the determinate stylistic peculiarity that distinguishes this book as a literary product from another of the same genre.

One might say: with the physically appearing as a first objectuality there is connected a second — the sense that “ensouIs” the physical. But Husserl asks: am I directed toward a second objectuality only externally connected with the first? Is not rather what I am directed toward a thoroughly fused unity that does not at all stand beside the physical? Husserl goes on to develop this point by analyzing how “bespirited” objects — artworks, tools, written texts, cultural artifacts — present themselves as unities of expression and expressed in which the physical serves as bearer and medium of spiritual meaning without the two strata being separable as externally juxtaposed things.

### §57 (expanded). Pure Ego and Personal Ego as Object of Reflexive Self-Apperception

Taking the personal ego as found in the inspectio (without regard to its unity with the expressing body given to us in empathy), it seems at first not to differ from the pure ego. But when in living, unreflected acts of reflecting I move in fields of reflection, I experience how I behave under various subjective circumstances — what experiential peculiarity I possess as a motivation-subject, what kind of personal subject I am. Self-perception is a reflection (self-reflection of the pure ego) and presupposes by its essence an unreflected consciousness. The unreflected ego-life, in relation to all kinds of pre-givennesses and to a thing-world, value-world, and so on, takes the distinguished form of self-reflection or self-perception. Through such reflections I come to know my unreflected ego-life; they bring structures of such life into the focal point of noticing.

We thus distinguish from the pure ego-reflection the reflective thematic experience on the basis of the developed experiential apperception, whose intentional object is this empirical ego — the ego of empirical intentionality — as self-experience of the personal ego with reference to the experiential connections in which this personal ego, according to its “personal properties” or “character-properties,” demonstrates itself.

The personal ego is the human-ego. I experience the behavior of others under their surrounding-world circumstances, and from the repeated reflection on their similar behavior under similar circumstances arises an inductive apperception. Insofar as I apperceive myself as a human being in the human context and often enough find occasion to observe my own behavior and its regularity, I come to know myself as a personal “reality.” The personal reflection I thereby exercise is thus intentionally a very mediated one.

### §58 (expanded). Constitution of the Personal Ego Before Reflection

Reflecting, I always already find myself as a personal ego. But originally it constitutes itself in the genesis pervading the experiential flux. The great question is whether the personal ego constitutes itself on the basis of ego-reflections — that is, quite originally on the basis of pure self-perception and self-experience — or whether merely by virtue of lawfulnesses like those standing under the title “association” apperceptions in general, and especially those of the personal ego behaving in a regulated manner with reference to subjective circumstances, can develop so that reflections on cogitations play no privileged role; or whether precisely they have a special and quite essential constitutive function. The ego is originally not constituted from experience in the sense of associative apperception but from life: it is what it is, not for the ego but is itself the ego. The ego can be more and other than the ego as apperceptive unity. It can have hidden abilities (dispositions) that have not yet emerged, not yet been apperceptively objectivated, just as a thing has properties not yet incorporated into the thing-apperception.

### §59 (expanded). The Ego as Subject of Capacities

The ego as unity is a system of “I can.” The physical “I can” (bodily and bodily mediated) must be distinguished from the spiritual. I have power over my body; I am the one who moves this hand and can move it. I can play the piano. But it does not always work — I have unlearned it, I am out of practice. I exercise my body. I can also become nerve-sick and lose mastery over my limbs — “I cannot.” In this respect I have become another.

“I am bodily-practically normal” means the enduring normal lower stratum, that I can “naturally-freely” move my organs as perception-organs and practical organs of the sensory life. I am spiritually normal in presenting when I can freely carry out my spatial experiences, phantasy-formations, freely run through my memories; in a typical, natural scope, not unlimited. I have a normal memory, a normal phantasy, likewise a normal thinking-activity. On the other hand, I have my peculiarity, my way of moving, of doing, my individual valuations, my way of preferring, my temptations, my powers of overcoming certain groups of temptations. The spiritual ego can be apprehended as an organism of capacities, their development in a normal typical style. Capacity is no empty ability but a positive potentiality that in each case comes to actualization, constantly in readiness to pass over into activity. Motivation is for consciousness something open, understandable; the “motivated” decision is as such clear through the kind and strength of the motives. Ultimately everything points back understandably to primordial capacities of the subject and then to acquired capacities sprung from earlier life-actuality.

### §60 (expanded). Person as Subject of Reason-Acts, as “Free Ego”

Husserl develops four sub-themes under this heading. (a) The “I can” as practical possibility, as neutrality-modification of practical acts, and as original capacity-consciousness: the subject has the consciousness of “I can” in a threefold sense — as the mere practical possibility entertained in phantasy, as the neutralized form of a practical act, and as the living awareness of one’s own power to act. (b) The “I can” as motivated in knowledge of one’s own person — self-apperception and self-understanding: my knowledge of what I can do, of my character, my typical modes of behavior, grows through the history of my self-experience and self-reflection; my “I can” is not merely abstract capacity but concretely shaped by the self-knowledge I have accumulated. (c) External influences and freedom of the person: external influences of other persons and of cultural formations impinge on the person, but the person relates to them freely — whether yielding, resisting, or transforming what it receives. Freedom here does not mean absence of influence but the capacity of the person to take a position vis-à-vis the influences it undergoes. (d) The generally typical and the individually typical in understanding persons: every person shares with others a general type of human existence — the phases of development, basic modes of behavior — but each also has an irreducibly individual type. Understanding a person means grasping both the general-typical and the individual-typical in their concrete interweaving.

### §61 (expanded). The Spiritual Ego and Its Foundation

The foundations upon which the understandably motivated spiritual life of the other builds up present themselves as “modifications” of my own. If I want to understand the development of a human being, I must describe from stage to stage what the surrounding world was in which he grew up, and how the surrounding things and people, as they appeared to him, motivated him. I thereby come upon a factual stock that is in itself unintelligible: this child has an original joy in tones, that one does not; one is inclined to quick anger, another to patience. Nature-causal factors also enter in.

Taking the personal ego in its developmental context, we find two strata that may possibly separate: a double “subjectivity.” The higher is the specifically spiritual — the stratum of the intellectus agens, the free ego as ego of free acts, including all genuine reason-acts. Then belongs to it also the unfree ego — unfreedom understood in the sense that holds for an actual ego: I let myself be drawn down by sensibility. This specifically spiritual ego finds itself dependent on a dark foundation of character-dispositions, original and hidden dispositions, and on the other hand dependent on nature.

We arrive here again at the old distinction between reason and sensibility. The sensory sphere has its own rules — its understanding-rules of concordance and discordance — and it is a stratum of hidden reason, at least as far as the constitution of nature reaches. In the sensory sphere we have associations, perseverations, determining tendencies, and so on. Through all spiritual life runs the “blind” efficacy of associations, drives, feelings as stimuli, tendencies arising in darkness — all determining the further course of consciousness according to “blind” rules.

Spirit is not an abstract ego of position-taking acts but the full personality: the ego-human-being who takes positions, thinks, values, acts, accomplishes works. To it belongs a foundation of experiences and a foundation of nature (“my nature”), which attests itself in the mechanism of experiences. The only truly unified person is the ego when it has a certain thoroughgoing unitary style in its manner of judging, willing, deciding, aesthetically valuing — but also in the manner of its “ideas” and involuntary phantasy, its apperception in perception, and the peculiarity of its “memory-activity.”

The experiential connection belonging to the human being is not merely a bundle of experiences or a mere consciousness-“stream” in which experiences float along; rather, all experiencing is experiencing of an ego that does not itself stream as its experiences do. Constantly there is a foundation that it has as pregiven; the ego’s ego-ruling becomes eo ipso experiencing. This foundation of pregivenness points back to the other, psychically called foundation of firmly regulated habitus.

### §62 (expanded). Interpenetration of Personalistic and Naturalistic Attitude

At the point where the two attitudes come into relation, Husserl shows that the course of the experiential stream stands under the law whose index is the reality “soul” in its dependence on the nature-body. The spirit in its spiritual acts is dependent on the soul insofar as the experiential stream lets the acts well up from itself; so the spiritual ego depends on the soul and the soul on the body. But the spirit does not therefore stand in a relation of causality to nature. It has a foundation that is conditionally dependent; as spirit it has a soul — a complex of nature-like dispositions conditioned by and dependent on physical nature.

The spirit in its freedom moves the body and thereby accomplishes an effect in the spiritual world. Works are as things simultaneously objects in the natural world, just as the body is simultaneously an object of the spiritual world (already by being a sense-bearer for comprehension) and a thing in nature. It is not only appearance for me but “ensouled” for me — it is in consciousness the organ of my original free movements.

### §63 (expanded). Psychophysical Parallelism and Interaction

Husserl subjects both the parallelistic and the interactionist conceptions of the body-mind relation to critical scrutiny from the standpoint of constitutive phenomenology. Neither framework captures the true relationship between nature and spirit. Parallelism treats the psychical and the physical as two complete and self-enclosed causal series running side by side, but this fails to account for the constitutive dependency of nature upon consciousness — the fact that nature is constituted as nature only for and through experiencing subjects. Interactionism posits real causal traffic between the mental and the physical, but this obscures the fundamental distinction between real causality (which holds between natural things) and motivation (which holds between the spirit and its surrounding world). What the phenomenological analysis reveals is that the spirit’s dependency on nature is genuine but of an entirely different kind from natural causality: it is conditional dependency, whereby the psychophysical conditions make possible the occurrence of experiences and thus the life of the spirit, without determining the content and direction of spiritual acts. The constitutive analyses show that the naturalistic and personalistic attitudes cut through the absolute consciousness in different ways, each yielding its own lawful order, and that neither can be reduced to the other.

### §64 (expanded). Relativity of Nature, Absoluteness of Spirit

From the foregoing considerations follows the limit of possible naturalization. The spirit can be apprehended as dependent on nature and itself naturalized, but only up to a certain degree. A univocal spirit-determination through merely nature-like dependencies — a reduction to something like physical nature, something analogous in its entire mode of determination to the goal of natural-scientific univocal determination — is inconceivable. Subjects cannot consist entirely in being nature, for then what gives nature its sense would be absent. Nature is a field of thoroughgoing relativities and can be so because these are always relative to something absolute that thus bears all relativities: the spirit.

If we strike all spirits from the world, there is no nature anymore. But if we strike nature — the “true,” objective-intersubjective existence — something still remains: the spirit as individual spirit. We have then the individual spirit not as a person in the narrower, social sense, related to a material and thereby also to a personal world. But we still have an ego with its consciousness-life, and even therein it has its individuality.

Individuality in the spiritual sense is something entirely different from natural individuality. The thing has its individual essence as what is here and now, but this “what” itself is a universal: every thing is an exemplar of a universality. The spirit, by contrast, is an experiencing, position-taking, motivated being. Each spirit has its manner of motivation; unlike the thing, it has in itself its motivation. It is not individuality first by being at a determinate place in the world. Absolute individuation already belongs to the pure ego of the respective cogitatio. Spirits are not unities of appearances but unities of absolute consciousness-connections — ego-unities. All individuation of appearances depends on the absolute individuation of spirits; all nature-existence depends on the existence of absolute spirits.

Insofar as soul is naturalized spirit and to the spirit belongs specific individuality, the soul as natural reality is not merely an exemplar of a universal. On the other hand, everything whereby the soul determines itself as natural reality is exemplary and universal. The individuality lies not in what is here nature. Nature is the X and in principle nothing other than X, determining itself through universal determinations. But the spirit is no X; it is what is given in itself in spiritual experience.
