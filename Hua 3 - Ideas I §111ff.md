# Commentary on Edmund Husserl, *Ideen zu einer reinen Phänomenologie und phänomenologischen Philosophie*, Erstes Buch (Husserliana III/1), §§111–153

-----

## § 111. Neutrality Modification and Phantasy

Husserl opens this section by warning against a dangerous equivocation lurking in the expression “merely-thinking-to-oneself” (*sich-bloß-denken*), namely the confusion between the **neutrality modification** and **phantasy**. What makes the confusion so difficult to untangle is that phantasy is itself a species of neutrality modification, and yet it must be sharply distinguished from the general neutrality modification that applies to all types of positing.

More precisely, Husserl defines **phantasizing** as the neutrality modification of “positing” re-presentation (*Vergegenwärtigung*), that is, of memory in the broadest conceivable sense. He notes that ordinary usage conflates “re-presentation” (reproduction) and “phantasy.” His technical usage reserves “re-presentation” as a general term that is neutral with respect to whether the associated positing is genuine or neutralized. Re-presentations thus divide into two groups: **memories** of every kind, and their **neutrality modifications**, i.e. phantasies.

Husserl cautions that this division cannot ultimately count as a genuine classification, a point to be demonstrated later. He then makes a crucial structural observation: every lived experience whatsoever, as a presently existing experience, essentially admits of the possibility of reflection in which it is necessarily characterized as certainly and presently existing. Accordingly, to every experience there corresponds a series of ideally possible memory modifications, and to each memory modification its neutrality modification—a phantasy. This holds for every experience regardless of the direction of the pure ego’s regard.

To illustrate, Husserl asks us to consider a phantasy world to which we are attentively turned. It belongs to the essence of the phantasizing consciousness that not only this world but also the perceiving that “gives” it is itself phantasized. The crucial point is that the modification that would convert any experience—including the phantasizing experience itself—into mere phantasy or neutralized memory must not be confused with the neutrality modification that we can counterpose to every “positing” experience. Memory is one specific kind of positing experience; normal perception is another; the perceptual or reproductive consciousness of possibility, probability, dubiousness, doubt, negation, affirmation, etc. are still others.

Husserl then provides a concrete example. The neutrality modification of normal perception, which posits in unmodified certainty, is the **neutral image-object consciousness** that we find as a component in the normal contemplation of a pictorially presented abbildliche world. He invites us to consider Dürer’s copper engraving “Knight, Death and the Devil.” We distinguish: (1) the normal perception whose correlate is the physical thing “copper engraving sheet”; (2) the perceptive consciousness in which colorless figurines—“knight on horseback,” “Death,” “the Devil”—appear in the black lines; and (3) in aesthetic contemplation, the “depicted” realities—the knight of flesh and blood, etc.

The consciousness of the “image” (the little gray figurines through which, by virtue of founded noeses, something else is “depicted” through resemblance) is an example of the neutrality modification of perception. The depicting **image-object** stands before us neither as existing nor as not existing nor in any other positional modality; rather, it is conscious as existing, but as quasi-existing in the neutrality modification of being. Similarly, the **depicted** itself, when we comport ourselves purely aesthetically, is taken as “mere image” without receiving the stamp of being or non-being. This is not a privation but precisely a modification—that of **neutralization**. It need not be an operation subsequent to a prior positing, though occasionally it can be.

## § 112. Iterability of the Phantasy Modification, Non-Iterability of the Neutrality Modification

Husserl highlights a decisive differentiating point between phantasy (as neutralizing re-presentation) and the neutrality modification in general. The **phantasy modification**, being a re-presentation, is **iterable**: there are phantasies of arbitrary levels—phantasies “within” phantasies. By contrast, the **repetition of the “operation” of neutralization is essentially excluded**.

Husserl anticipates that his claim about iterated reproductive (and also depicting) modifications will meet widespread objection, and attributes this to a lack of practice in genuine phenomenological analysis. As long as one treats experiences as “contents” or psychic “elements”—effectively as little things (*Sächelchen*)—and believes that the difference between “sensation contents” and corresponding “phantasy contents” lies merely in factual characteristics like “intensity” or “fullness,” no progress can be made.

One must learn to see that the distinction is a **consciousness-difference**: the phantasm is not merely a pale sensation-datum but is essentially a phantasy *of* the corresponding sensation-datum. This “of” cannot arise from any degree of thinning out of intensity or content-fullness. Anyone practiced in reflections on consciousness will see the levels of consciousness present in phantasies within phantasies, or memories within memories or phantasies. They will also see what lies in the essential nature of these level-formations: every phantasy of a higher level can be freely converted into a direct phantasy of what is mediately phantasied in it, while the free possibility of transition from phantasy to the corresponding perception does not obtain. Here there is a gulf for spontaneity that the pure ego can cross only in the essentially new form of realizing action and creation (including voluntary hallucination).

## § 113. Actual and Potential Positings

Husserl advances the discussion of neutrality modification and positing by introducing the distinction between **actual** and **potential positing**, using **“positional consciousness”** as the general title. The difference between actuality and potentiality of positing stands in close relation to the previously discussed differences of attentional actuality and inactuality, but does not coincide with them. Through consideration of the neutrality modification, a doubling enters into the general distinction between attentional actuality and inactuality, or more precisely, an ambiguity in the concept of “actuality” that requires clarification.

The neutrality modification presented itself in the contrast between genuine believing, conjecturing, etc. and the peculiarly modified consciousness of merely “thinking oneself into” a believing, conjecturing, etc. Husserl already indicated the essentially different behavior of non-neutral and neutral consciousness with respect to the potentiality of positings. From every “actual” consciousness, various positings potentially contained in it can be drawn out, and these are then *actual* positings: in every actually thetially intended content, real predicabilities lie. But a neutral consciousness “contains” no “actual” predicabilities. The unfolding through attentional actualities yields only neutral acts and modified predicates.

Husserl notes that the original time-consciousness itself functions like a perceptual consciousness and has its counterpart in a corresponding phantasy-consciousness. But this all-encompassing time-consciousness is obviously not a continuous immanent perceiving in the pregnant sense of actually positing perceiving. It is not a continuous inner reflecting in which experiences would become objectified in the specific sense of being posited and actually grasped as existing.

Among experiences there are distinguished ones called immanent reflections, specifically immanent perceptions, which are directed at their objects in an actually being-grasping, being-positing manner. Alongside these there are also transcendently directed perceptions of the same being-positing character—the so-called outer perceptions. “Perception” in the normal word-sense means not only that a thing appears to the ego in bodily presence, but that the ego becomes aware of the appearing thing, grasps it, posits it as actually existing. This actuality of the positing of existence is, as previously discussed, neutralized in the perceptive image-consciousness. Turned toward the “image” (not the depicted), we grasp as object not something actual but precisely an image, a fiction.

Through turning the mental gaze away from the fiction, the attentional actuality of the neutralized positing passes over into potentiality: the image still appears but is not “heeded,” not grasped in the mode of “quasi.” In the essence of this state of affairs lie possibilities for actual turnings of the gaze that never produce actualities of **positing**. Similarly with “actual” (non-neutral, genuinely positing) memories: when the remembered, through turning the gaze, still appears but is no longer actual, the potentiality of positing means that through attentional actuality not only grasping cogitationes emerge but thoroughly “genuinely” grasping, actually positing ones. In the neutrality modifications of memories, i.e. mere phantasies, we again have attentional potentialities, but their conversion into actualities yields only neutralized doxic positings in the mode of “quasi.”

Every perception has its perceptual background. The specially grasped thing has its perceptively co-appearing environing world lacking particular existential theses. This too is consciously “actual” surroundings, so that—in the sense of an essential possibility—actually being-positing glances can be directed upon it. It is, as it were, a **unity of potential positings**. In all these cases, actualization of “potential positings” through corresponding turns of the gaze (attentional actuality) necessarily leads to ever new actual positings, and this belongs to the essence of these states of affairs. But if we pass over to the parallel neutrality modifications, everything translates into the modification of the “quasi,” including the “potentiality” itself.

The same holds for modal modifications of the specific belief-theses (the doxic ur-theses)—for conjectures, surmises, questions, etc., as well as for negations and affirmations. The correlates conscious in them—possibility, probability, non-being, etc.—*can* receive doxic positing and thereby specific “objectification,” but while we “live in” conjecturing, questioning, rejecting, affirming, etc., we do not perform doxic ur-theses, though we do perform other “theses” in a necessarily generalized sense—conjecture-theses, dubiousness-theses, negation-theses, etc. But at any time we *can* perform the corresponding doxic ur-theses; in the essence of the phenomenological states of affairs there lies the ideal possibility of actualizing the potential theses contained in them.

## § 114. More on Potentiality of the Thesis and Neutrality Modification

Husserl reiterates that the difference between non-neutral and neutral consciousness concerns not merely experiences in the attentional mode of cogito but also those in attentional inactuality. The difference manifests in the double behavior of these consciousness-“backgrounds” when they are converted into “foregrounds”—specifically, when they pass into attentional actualities in which the original experience becomes a doxic cogito, indeed an ur-doxa.

The neutrality modification is not a special modification attached to actual theses alone, but rather a **fundamental essential characteristic of all consciousness whatsoever**, one that expresses itself in the comportment toward actual ur-doxic positability or non-positability. Therefore it must be exhibited precisely on actual ur-positings and on the modification they undergo.

More precisely: **consciousness in general**, whatever its type or form, **is pervaded by a radical division**. To every consciousness belongs the essential possibility of a modification that converts it into the form of the cogito. Within the mode of cogito, there are then **two fundamental possibilities** of the manner of consciousness-accomplishment. To every cogito belongs an exactly corresponding counterpart, such that its noema has in the parallel cogito its exactly corresponding counter-noema. One of the two is a “genuine” act, the cogito a “genuinely positing” cogito, while the other is a “shadow” of an act, an improper, non-genuinely positing cogito. The one genuinely accomplishes, the other is a mere reflection of accomplishment.

Correspondingly: on one side the constituted noematic accomplishment with the character of unmodified genuine accomplishment; on the other the “mere thought” of the exactly corresponding accomplishment. The genuine and modified correspond ideally with absolute exactness and yet are not of the same essence. The modification transfers itself to the essences: to the original essence corresponds its counter-essence as a “shadow” of that same essence.

Husserl warns against introducing into these metaphorical expressions of shadow, reflection, image anything of mere appearance or deceptive opinion, which would yield genuine acts and positional correlates. Every cogito is in itself either a doxic ur-positing or not. But by a lawfulness belonging to the general fundamental essence of consciousness, every cogito can be converted into a doxic ur-positing—in manifold ways, such that every “thetic character” in the broadest sense takes on the form of a modality of being. Thus the character “probable” becomes probable-being, the character “questionable” becomes questionable-being, the negation-correlate becomes the form of non-being—all forms that have accepted the stamp of the actual doxic ur-thesis.

Husserl extends this further: we will find reasons to extend the concept of thesis to all act-spheres, speaking of theses of pleasure, wish-theses, will-theses, with their noematic correlates “pleasing,” “wished,” “practically obligated,” etc. These correlates too, through the a priori possible conversion of the relevant act into a doxic ur-thesis, take on the form of modalities of being in an extremely extended sense.

The cases divide radically by whether the respective ur-doxa is a genuine one—a really believed belief—or its powerless counterpart, the mere “thinking-to-oneself” of being, possible being, etc. What the doxic conversion of the original experience yields—whether unfoldings into genuine doxic ur-positings or exclusively into ur-doxic neutralities—is absolutely predetermined by the essence of the relevant intentional experience. The terms **“positional”** and **“neutral”** serve from now on as terminological designations. Every experience falls under this opposition. Positionality does not mean the presence or performance of an actual position; it expresses only a certain potentiality for the performance of actually positing doxic acts. But the case where an experience is from the outset an accomplished position is also included. The difference between positionality and neutrality is a **universal consciousness-difference**.

## § 115. Applications. The Extended Concept of Act. Act-Accomplishments and Act-Stirrings

Husserl notes that the cogito is the explicit intentionality. The concept of intentional experience presupposes the opposition of potentiality and actuality: only in the transition to the explicit cogito and in reflection on the non-explicit experience and its noetic-noematic components can we recognize that it harbors intentionalities and noemata. The explicit intentional experience is an accomplished “I think.” But it can also pass, through attentional modifications, into an “unaccomplished” one. The experience of an accomplished perception, judgment, feeling, or act of will does not vanish when attention turns “exclusively” to something new.

The cogito thus designates the **proper** act of perceiving, judging, taking-pleasure, etc. But the entire structure of the experience remains the same when this actuality is lacking. Husserl distinguishes more clearly between **accomplished acts** and **unaccomplished** ones; the latter are either acts that have “fallen out of accomplishment” or **act-stirrings** (*Aktregungen*). Such act-stirrings are experienced with all their intentionalities, but the ego does not live in them as an “accomplishing subject.” The act-concept thus extends in a determinate and indispensable sense. The accomplished acts, or act-accomplishments, constitute **“position-takings” in the broadest sense**.

It is then clear that acts in the broader sense carry the differences of neutrality and positionality just as specific cogitationes do. They are already noematically and thetically accomplishing before their conversion into cogitationes, except that we can bring their accomplishments into view only through cogitationes. The positings, or the positings in the mode of “quasi,” are already really present in them, together with the full noeses to which these positings belong.

Husserl observes that in all the discussions regarding neutrality, the doxic positings were privileged. The neutrality had its index in potentiality. Everything rested on the fact that every thetic act-character harbors within its essence a character of the genus doxic thesis that “covers” it in certain ways. According to whether the relevant act-intention is non-neutralized or neutralized, so too is the doxic thesis contained in it.

## § 116. Transition to New Analyses. The Founded Noeses and Their Noematic Correlates

Husserl summarizes the results so far: a series of general structural features of noeses and noemata have been studied within a large but restricted framework. The studies concerned a mere sub-layer of the stream of experience, involving relatively simply built intentionalities. The preference (apart from the last anticipatory considerations) was for sensuous intuitions, especially those of appearing realities, and the obscure presentations arising from them.

Two directions of advance now present themselves, both leading to intentionalities founded in representations: either toward noetic **syntheses**, or toward novel but **founded “positing” types**. Taking the latter direction, one encounters the (initially as simple as possible) **feeling**, **desiring**, **willing noeses**, which are founded in “representations”—perceptions, memories, sign-representations, etc.—and display manifest differences of layered founding.

With the new noetic moments, new **noematic moments** also appear in the correlates. On one side there are new characters analogous to belief-modes but possessing doxological positability in their own right; on the other, new “apprehensions” are connected with the novel moments, constituting a new sense that is founded in the sense of the underlying noesis while simultaneously encompassing it. The new sense introduces a totally **new dimension of sense**: through it, not new determinative pieces of mere “things” are constituted but **values of things**, worthinesses, and concrete value-objectivities—beauty and ugliness, goodness and badness, tools, artworks, machines, books, actions, deeds, etc.

In every full experience of the higher level, the full correlate shows a similar structure to what we observed on the lowest level of noeses. In the noema of the higher level, the valued-as-such is a sense-kernel surrounded by new thetic characters. The “worth,” “pleasing,” “delightful,” etc. functions similarly to “possible,” “probable,” or “null,” “indeed real”—though it would be wrong to place it in these series. The consciousness is again a **positional** consciousness with respect to this new character: the “worth” is doxically positible as worth-being. Manifold deep-reaching changes arise through attentional modifications as the attentive gaze passes through the various intentional layers.

## § 117. The Founded Theses and the Conclusion of the Doctrine of the Neutralization Modification. The General Concept of Thesis

Husserl now considers the relation of the new noetic and noematic layers to neutralization. Acts of pleasure, feeling, and will are “acts,” “intentional experiences,” possessing an “intention,” a “position-taking.” In a broadest but essentially unified sense, they are “positings”—but not doxic ones. The essential analogy of the specific pleasure-noeses with belief-positings is manifest, as is that of wish-noeses, will-noeses, etc. Also in valuing, wishing, willing, something is “posited,” apart from the doxic positionality that “lies” in them.

To the essence of every intentional experience belongs it to have at least one, and normally several, “positing characters,” “theses,” connected in the manner of founding. In this plurality, one is necessarily the **archontic** thesis, uniting and governing all others. The highest generic unity binding all these specific “act-characters” does not exclude essential generic differences. Thus the sentiment-positings are related to the doxic as positings but not so closely affiliated as all modalities of belief.

Husserl is thus led back to the **generalized title “thesis,”** and formulates: **Every consciousness is either actually or potentially “thetic.”** The earlier concept of actual positing and of positionality undergoes a corresponding extension. The doctrine of neutralization and its relation to positionality transfers to the extended concept of thesis. On one side, positional theses are characterized as either actual theses or convertible into actual ones; on the other, the improper “quasi”-theses, the powerless reflections, incapable of admitting any actual-thetic accomplishments regarding their neutralized noemata.

The preferment of the specific doxic positings has its deep foundation in the things themselves. The doxic modalities and especially the doxic ur-thesis of belief-certainty have the unique privilege that their positional potentiality embraces the entire sphere of consciousness. Every thesis of whatever genus can, by virtue of the doxic characterizations inseparably belonging to its essence, be converted into an actual doxic positing. Every “proposition,” e.g. every wish-proposition, can be converted into a doxic proposition.

It follows that **all acts whatsoever—including the acts of sentiment and will—are “objectivating,” originally “constituting” objects**, necessary sources of different regions of being and thus of the corresponding ontologies. The universality of the logical, ultimately of predicative judgment, is rooted here. In further consequence, the possibility and necessity of formal and material noetic, noematic, and ontological disciplines essentially related to sentiment- and will-intentionality become intelligible.

## § 118. Syntheses of Consciousness. Syntactic Forms

Husserl now turns to the forms of **synthetic consciousness**. Manifold formations of experiences through intentional connection enter the horizon, belonging as essential possibilities partly to all intentional experiences and partly to the peculiarities of particular genera. Consciousness binds with consciousness not merely in general but into **one** consciousness whose correlate is **one** noema founded in the noemata of the connected noeses.

Husserl sets aside the unity of immanent time-consciousness (though he acknowledges it as the all-embracing unity), and likewise the continuous syntheses essential to all spatial-thing-constituting consciousness. His interest turns to the **articulated syntheses**—the peculiar ways in which discretely separated acts bind into an articulated unity of a synthetic act of higher order. Examples include the **relating willing** (“for the sake of another”), the **relating pleasure**, acts of **preference**, and especially the universal group encompassing **colligating** (collecting), **disjoining**, **explicating**, **relating** syntheses—those that determine the formal-ontological forms according to the pure forms of the synthetic objectivities constituted in them, and that are mirrored in the apophantic significance-forms of formal logic.

-----

## § 119. Conversion of Polythetic Acts into Monothetic Ones

For all kinds of articulated syntheses, of polythetic acts, every synthetically unitary consciousness possesses its **total object**. Every distinctive noesis, even if an unselfstanding layer, contributes to the constitution of the total object. The synthetically constituted total object is “objective” in a quite different sense from the constituted object of a simple thesis: the synthetic consciousness is **multi-rayed** in its directedness, while the simply thetic consciousness is single-rayed.

A fundamental law obtains: to every multi-rayed (polythetic) constitution of synthetic objectivities—which by their essence can “originally” become conscious only synthetically—belongs the essential possibility of converting the multi-rayed conscious content into something **simply conscious in a single ray**, i.e. making the synthetically constituted object “objective” in a **monothetic** act in the specific sense. Thus the synthetically constituted collection becomes genuinely objective through a simple doxic thesis. **Plural consciousness can essentially be converted into singular consciousness** that takes the plurality as one object, as a single thing.

In logic, this lawfulness manifests in the **law of “nominalization”**: to every proposition and every partial form distinguishable in it there corresponds a nominal expression—to “S is p” the nominal that-clause, to “is p” the P-being, to the relational form “similar” the similarity, to the plural form the plurality, etc. The concepts arising from these nominalizations, thought of as determined exclusively by the pure forms, yield **formal-categorial modifications of the idea of objectuality in general** and furnish the fundamental conceptual material of formal ontology.

-----

## § 120. Positionality and Neutrality in the Sphere of Syntheses

All proper syntheses build upon simple theses and are themselves theses of higher level. The findings about actuality and inactuality, neutrality and positionality, transfer to syntheses. A closer investigation would be required to establish how positionality and neutrality of the founding theses relate to that of the founded theses.

In the group of syntheses now under consideration, **every synthesis is dependent in its positional character on that of the founding noeses**: it is positional (and can only be so) if all sub-theses are positional, and neutral if they are not. A colligating is either genuinely colligating or colligating in the mode of “quasi.” Pure neutrality can never function for positional syntheses; it must at the very least undergo conversion into “assumptions”—e.g. hypothetical antecedents or consequents, hypothetically assumed nominals.

-----

## § 121. The Doxic Syntaxes in the Sphere of Sentiment and Will

Husserl asks how the syntheses of this group come to express themselves in the syntactic forms of assertive sentences. These are **doxic syntheses**, or **doxic syntaxes**. But the acount is not the whole truth. These syntheses of “and,” “or,” “if”/“because” belong not merely to the doxic but also to the **non-doxic theses** in their own essence.

There is undoubtedly such a thing as collective joy, collective pleasure, collective willing. Alongside the doxic “and” (the logical) there is an **axiological and practical “and.”** For example: a mother lovingly embracing her group of children performs a unity of collective love directed at each child individually and at all together. The syntactic forms enter into the essence of the sentiment-acts themselves.

The parallelism between the doxic syntaxes and those belonging to all other theses is a special case of the essential kinship between doxic theses and theses in general. The synthetic sentiment-acts constitute **synthetic sentiment-objectivities**, which come to explicit objectification through corresponding doxic acts. All these syntactic forms are parallel forms belonging both to the sentiment-acts with their specific components and to the parallel doxic positionalities extractable from them.

## § 122. Modes of Accomplishment of Articulated Syntheses. “Theme”

To the realm of theses and syntheses belongs an important group of general modifications. A synthesis can be accomplished step by step in **original production**. The thesis and synthesis comes into being as the pure ego actually performs each step; it lives in them not as a passive being-in-them but as their source in an **origin of productions**. Every thesis begins with a **point of inception**, a punctual **originary positing**. This “inception” is like a *fiat*, like the inception-point of willing and acting. But spontaneous self-resolving and willful executing is one act among others; **every** act can begin in this spontaneity-mode of creative beginning.

This mode of inception immediately passes into another mode: the perceptive grasping transforms into the “having in one’s grip.” A further modal change occurs when the thesis was merely a step for a synthesis and the pure ego performs a new step, now “still holding in its grip” what it had just grasped while grasping the new thematic object.

These differences represent, over against the differences of clarity and unclarity, a **wholly new dimension**, though both intertwine closely. The new differences stand under the law of the correlation of noesis and noema. Through the changes, an identical what—an identical **sense**—is preserved noematically, and on the noetic side the correlate of this sense and the whole form of articulation.

A new essential modification arises: the pure ego can wholly **withdraw** from the theses, **releasing** the thetic correlates from its “grip”: it **“turns to another theme.”** The possibility of simultaneous, mutually interfering and “disturbing” themata and thematic syntheses points to further possible modifications. The title **“theme”**—referred to all fundamental types of acts and act-syntheses—constitutes an important topic of phenomenological analyses.

-----

## § 123. Confusion and Distinctness as Modes of Accomplishment of Synthetic Acts

There are also modalities of accomplishment lying, so to speak, in the reverse direction from the preferred mode of originatively springing actuality. A thought—simple or articulated—can emerge as a **confused** thought. It initially presents itself like a simple representation without any actual-thetic articulation. We recall a proof, a theory, a conversation—it “occurs to us.” A new process can then begin: the confused recollection passes into **distinct** and **clear** recollection, step by step. This distinction between **confusion** (*Verworrenheit*) and **distinctness** (*Deutlichkeit*) plays an important role in the phenomenology of “expressions.”

-----

## § 124. The Noetic-Noematic Layer of the “Logos.” Meaning and Signification

With all the acts considered so far, **expressing** acts—the specifically “logical” act-layers—interweave, for which the parallelism of noesis and noema is equally demonstrable. Husserl ties into the familiar distinction between the sensuous (bodily) side of the expression and its non-sensuous (“mental”) side. His attention focuses exclusively on **“meaning” (*Bedeuten*) and “signification” (*Bedeutung*)**.

Originally these words relate only to the linguistic sphere. But it is nearly unavoidable and an important cognitive step to extend their meaning so that they apply in a certain way to the entire noetic-noematic sphere. For the sake of distinctness, Husserl reserves the word **“Bedeutung”** (signification) for the old concept, especially in the complex expression “logical” or “expressive” signification, and uses the word **“Sinn”** (sense) in the more encompassing breadth.

**Logical signification is an expression.** The word-sound can be called expression only because the signification belonging to it expresses; the expressing lies originally in it. “Expression” is a remarkable form that can adapt to all “sense” (the noematic “kernel”) and raises it into the realm of the “logos,” of the **conceptual** and thereby of the “universal.” A peculiar intentional medium lies before us that has the distinction of reflecting, as it were, every other intentionality in its own coloring and impressing upon it its own form of “conceptuality.”

The layer of expression is—and this constitutes its peculiarity—apart from lending expression to all other intentionalities, **not productive**. Its productivity, its noematic accomplishment, exhausts itself in expressing and the form of the conceptual that comes with it. The expressive layer is perfectly one in thetic character with the layer receiving expression, so thoroughly absorbing its essence in the covering that we call expressive representing itself representing, expressive believing itself believing, etc. The **expressive layer cannot have a differently qualified positional or neutral thesis than the expression-receiving layer**, and in the covering we find not two theses to be distinguished but **only one thesis**.

-----

## § 125. The Modes of Accomplishment in the Logico-Expressive Sphere and the Method of Clarification

The distinctions of act-accomplishment modes apply to expressive acts in a **double** way: they concern the signification-layer itself, and they concern the founding sub-layers. In the accomplishment of significational acts in the mode of genuine production, one gains complete **distinctness of “logical understanding.”** This distinctness can pass into confusions: the sentence just read sinks into darkness, loses its living articulation. But such distinctness must be distinguished from that which concerns the expressed sub-layers. A distinct word- and sentence-understanding is compatible with the **confusion of the substrata**.

This has a most important methodological significance. What is needed to move from confused thinking to genuine and fully explicit cognition is: first, all “logical” acts must be converted into the mode of originary spontaneous actuality, i.e. complete **logical distinctness** must be established. Then the analogous work must be done in the grounding **sub-layer**: all unvitality converted into vitality, all confusion into distinctness, all non-intuitiveness into intuitiveness. Only then does the previously described method of clarification come into action. It depends on the **kind of evidence** sought: all evidences relating to purely-logical relations require only the givenness of the significations; the sub-layers need not be brought to clarity for a purely-logical insight.

-----

## § 126. Completeness and Universality of Expression

Husserl notes the distinction between **complete** and **incomplete expression**. The unity of the expressing and expressed is a certain covering, but the upper layer need not extend expressingly over the entire sub-layer. Expression is complete when it conceptually-significationally stamps all synthetic forms and matters of the sub-layer; incomplete when it does so only partially.

A totally different incompleteness belongs to the essence of expression as such, namely its **universality** (*Allgemeinheit*). Whole dimensions of variability in the sub-layer do not enter into the expressive meaning at all—e.g. the modifications of relative clarity and distinctness, the attentional modifications, etc. The layer of meaning is not, and in principle is not, a kind of reduplication of the sub-layer.

## § 127. Expression of Judgments and Expression of Sentiment-Noemata

The central problem: how does **asserting** as expression of judging relate to the expressions of other acts? We have expressive predications, expressive conjectures, questions, doubts, wishes, commands, etc. The original dispute concerned whether these are coordinate kinds of signification or whether all such sentences are in truth assertive sentences in their signification.

Husserl insists that the whole reference to acts (noeses) is insufficient; the persistent overlooking of the noemata is detrimental. The **radical problem** is: **Is the medium of expressive meaning a specifically doxic one? Does it, in adapting meaning to what is meant, cover itself with the doxic element lying in all positionality itself?**

This would not exclude multiple ways of expressing, e.g. sentiment-experiences. One mode would be direct—simple expression of the experience through immediate adaptation, where doxic covers doxic. Other modes would be indirect, involving detours. Every act can give rise to various relational explications; accordingly, the expression is then not directly adapted to the original phenomenon but to the predicate derived from it. **Explikative or analytic synthesis**, **assertion or judgment in the ordinary sense**, and **doxa (belief)** must be carefully distinguished.

-----

## Fourth Section: Reason and Reality

## First Chapter: The Noematic Sense and the Relation to the Object

## § 128. Introduction

The phenomenological wanderings of the last chapter led through nearly all intentional spheres. The radical point of view of the division into real and intentional, noetic and noematic analysis, revealed ever newly branching structures. This division turns out to be a **fundamental structure pervading all intentional structures**, forming a governing leitmotif of phenomenological method. Two radically opposed yet essentially interrelated regions of being came to differentiation. The noematic proved to be an objectuality belonging to consciousness yet peculiar in kind: while objects simpliciter stand under fundamentally different highest genera, all object-senses and all fully taken noemata belong to a **single highest genus**. The essences noema and noesis are inseparable from each other.

The recognition of the essential two-sidedness of intentionality has the consequence that a systematic phenomenology cannot one-sidedly aim at a real analysis of experiences. The temptation is great, since the historical path from psychology to phenomenology leads one to understand the immanent study of pure experiences as one of their real components. In truth, large fields of eidetic research open up on both sides.

Husserl announces the guiding point of view for the next considerations: the **phenomenological problem of the relation of consciousness to an objectuality** has above all its noematic side. The noema in itself has objective reference through its own “sense.” Pursuing this leads ultimately to the question of the “pretension” of consciousness to “refer” genuinely to something objective, to be “valid”—and thus to the great **problems of reason**, whose formulation as phenomenological problems on the transcendental ground is the goal of this section.

-----

## § 129. “Content” and “Object”; Content as “Sense”

In previous analyses, a universal noematic structure played a constant role: the separation of a **noematic “kernel”** from the changing **“characters”** associated with it. This kernel has not yet received its scientific due. Husserl now sets out to bring it closer. He takes as his thesis: **Every noema has a “content,” namely its “sense,” and refers through it to “its” object.**

Husserl reflects on the recent habit of praising the distinction between act, content, and object as a great advance (especially since Twardowski’s treatise). While acknowledging Twardowski’s merit, he notes that a radical advance was impossible before a systematic phenomenology of consciousness. The first step was the phenomenological separation of “matter” and “quality” and the idea of “intentional essence” in its distinction from “cognitive essence” (in the *Logical Investigations*).

The full noesis relates to the full noema as its intentional what. But this relation cannot be the one meant when one speaks of the relation of consciousness to its intentionally objective correlate. For in the noema, a noematic “kernel” is separated from the complex of thetic characters, and the ego’s “glance” goes through the noematic kernel. This directs us to an **innermost moment of the noema**—not the kernel itself but something constituting the necessary central point of the kernel, functioning as a “bearer” of noematically modified properties. Thus not only for consciousness but also for the **noema in itself** the distinction between “content” and “object” must be made. The noema too refers to an object and possesses a “content” through which it refers to the object.

-----

## § 130. Delimitation of the Essence “Noematic Sense”

Husserl simplifies by setting aside attentional modifications and restricting to positional acts. A living cogito has essentially, in a distinguished sense, “directedness” toward an objectuality. A quite **fixed content in every noema** is thereby delimited. Every consciousness has its what and every one intends “its” objectuality; with every consciousness such a noematic description of the same, “exactly as it is intended,” must be in principle performable. Formally-ontological expressions are used (“object,” “property,” “state of affairs”), material-ontological ones (“thing,” “figure,” “cause”), and determinate content-characterizations (“rough,” “hard,” “colored”)—all with their quotation marks, i.e. in noematically modified sense. Excluded are expressions like “perceptual,” “memorial,” “clearly intuited”—these belong to the manner *how* something is conscious, not to the what.

-----

## § 131. The “Object,” the Determinable X in the Noematic “Sense”

The predicates are predicates of **“something,”** and this something belongs inseparably to the kernel: it is the central unity-point, the “bearer” of the predicates, but not a unity of the predicates. It is necessarily distinguished from them, though not to be placed alongside or separated from them. In the continuous or synthetic progression of consciousness, the intentional object is continually conscious as “the same” but “giving itself otherwise”—in other predicates, from various sides, with previously indeterminate predicates becoming more determinate. This identical intentional **“object”** separates itself as a **central noematic moment**: the “object,” the “identical,” the “determinable subject of its possible predicates”—the **pure X in abstraction from all predicates**.

Two concepts of object are to be distinguished noematically: this pure unity-point, the noematic **“object simpliciter,”** and the **“object in the how of its determinacies.”** The **“sense”** is this noematic “object in the how” with everything that the characterized description can evidently find and conceptually express in it. Through the sense-bearer (as an empty X) and the possibility of harmonious combination of senses into sense-unities, not only has each sense its “object,” but different senses refer to the **same** object.

-----

## § 132. The Kernel as Sense in the Mode of Its Fullness

The sense as determined is not a concrete essence in the total composition of the noema but a kind of **abstract form** dwelling in it. Holding the sense fixed, one obtains a **second concept** of the “object in the how”—in the how of its **modes of givenness**. Setting aside attentional modifications and modes of accomplishment, the differences of **clarity-fullness** come into consideration, which are so determinative epistemically. A darkly conscious something and the same clearly conscious are noematically quite different in their concretion, yet nothing prevents the determinative content from being absolutely identical. As the **full kernel** we reckon the full concretion: the **sense in the mode of its fullness**.

-----

## § 133. The Noematic Proposition. Thetic and Synthetic Propositions. Propositions in the Domain of Representations

Husserl now introduces the **noematic proposition (*Satz*)**. In the *Logical Investigations*, quality and matter were taken together into the concept of sense (“significational essence”). It now seems more fitting to define the term “sense” as merely that “matter” and to designate the unity of sense and thetic character as a **proposition**. We then have **single-membered propositions** (as in perceptions and other thetic intuitions) and multi-membered, **synthetic propositions** (predicative doxic propositions/judgments, conjecture-propositions, etc.), as well as **pleasure-propositions**, **wish-propositions**, **command-propositions**, etc. These concepts of sense and proposition contain nothing of expression or conceptual signification, though they encompass all expressive propositions under themselves.

Even in the simple **intuitions**, these concepts have their necessary application: **intuition-sense** and **intuition-proposition** must be coined. In the domain of outer perception, one can extract from the “perceived object as such” the object-sense—the **thing-sense of this perception**—which differs from perception to perception. Taking this sense in its full intuitive fullness yields a determinate and very important concept of **appearance** (*Erscheinung*).

-----

## § 134. Apophantic Morphology

A main task: to design a systematic “analytic” **morphology of “logical” significations**, of **predicative propositions**, of “judgments” in the sense of formal logic, which takes into account only the forms of analytic or predicative synthesis and leaves the sense-terms entering into these forms indeterminate. This task has universal reach because the title “predicative synthesis” designates a class of operations universally possible for all kinds of senses.

These forms, with doxic ur-positings, yield various forms of judgments in the logical sense (**apophantic propositions**). The goal of determining all these forms a priori designates the idea of a **morphology of apophantic propositions or syntaxes**. When the positings are doxic modalities, the concept of form extends, determined partly by the properly syntactic forms and partly by the doxic modalities.

The extension to **founded theses** in the sentiment and will sphere is likewise indicated. It can be seen that in a suitably extended morphology of doxic propositions, the morphology of all propositions is mirrored. Phenomenology’s task is not the systematic development of these morphologies but the analysis of the a priori discoverable in immediate intuition and its descriptive cognition in the systematic web of all layers of transcendentally pure consciousness.

-----

## § 135. Object and Consciousness. Transition to the Phenomenology of Reason

As every intentional experience has a noema and thereby a sense through which it refers to the object, so conversely everything we call **object** is already object of consciousness. Whatever world and reality may be, it must be represented within actual and possible consciousness through senses and propositions filled with more or less intuitive content. Everything phenomenologically excluded belongs, in a certain change of sign, back within phenomenology—represented by the corresponding total manifolds of senses and propositions.

To every real thing of nature there correspond all senses and variously fulfilled propositions in which it is the correlate of possible intentional experiences. This constitution refers initially to a possible individual consciousness, then also to a possible community consciousness.

Husserl then notes that a **double meaning** lurks in the characterized essential connections between object and consciousness. We assign manifolds of propositions to an object such that syntheses of identification are a priori possible, through which the object can stand as the same. But **is** it really the same? **Is** the object itself “real”? Could it not be unreal while the manifold harmonious propositions ran their conscious course? The consciousness-subject itself judges about reality, asks about it, conjectures, doubts, and performs “adjudications of reason.” Must not the essence of this right, and correlatively the essence of “reality,” be clarifiable within the essential nexus of transcendental consciousness? We thus stand before a great **turning point**: the problems of reason and reality.

-----

## Second Chapter: Phenomenology of Reason

Husserl observes that speaking of objects simpliciter, one normally means actual, truly existing objects. Whatever one says about them—speaking rationally—must be “grounded,” “verified,” directly “seen” or mediately “seen into.” Principally, in the logical sphere, **“truly-” or “actually-being” and “rationally-demonstrable-being” stand in correlation**.

-----

## § 136. The First Basic Form of Reason-Consciousness: Originarily Giving “Seeing”

Husserl asks what rational demonstration means, i.e. what **reason-consciousness** consists in. Intuitive exemplification and the beginning of essential analysis immediately yield several distinctions:

**First**, the distinction between positional experiences in which the posited comes to **originary givenness** and those in which it does not: between **“perceiving,” “seeing” acts—in the broadest sense—and non-“perceiving” acts**. A memory-consciousness is not originarily giving; the landscape is not perceived as when we actually see it. An analogon of this opposition appears for all types of positional experiences.

The distinction does not concern the pure sense or proposition, which is identical in both members of every pair. It concerns the **manner in which** the mere sense or proposition is a **fulfilled or unfulfilled** sense and proposition. An experiential mode of the sense is the intuitive, and a specially distinguished case is the **originarily giving** one. A **specific reason-character** belongs to the positing-character as a **distinction** that essentially accrues to it when and only when it is a positing on the basis of a fulfilled, originarily giving sense. To every bodily appearing of a thing, the positing “belongs”—it is through the appearing “motivated” and indeed “rationally motivated.” The positing has in originary givenness its **original ground of right**.

**Evidence**, **insight** (*Einsicht*) is a quite distinguished occurrence; at its “kernel” it is the **unity of a reason-positing with what essentially motivates it**.

-----

## § 137. Evidence and Insight. “Originary” and “Pure,” Assertoric and Apodictic Evidence

What we usually call evidence and insight is a positional doxic and adequately giving consciousness that “excludes other-being.” But closer consideration of the contrasted examples reveals a **double differentiation**: in one example we deal with essences, in the other with individuals; secondly, originary givenness is in the eidetic example **adequate**, in the experiential example **inadequate**.

The **“assertoric” seeing of an individual** (e.g. the “perceiving” of a thing) differs in its reason-character essentially from an **“apodictic” seeing**, from the **insight into an essence or essential relation**. Husserl proposes to separate the terms: **evidence** for the most general concept (embracing assertoric seeing and apodictic insight), **originary evidence** for every reason-thesis characterized by a motivational relation to originariness of givenness, and **insight** (*Einsicht*) reserved for the specific designation of apodikticity. Further: **pure** and **impure** evidence, **formal** (“analytic,” “logical”) and **material** (synthetic-a priori) evidence.

-----

## § 138. Adequate and Inadequate Evidence

The positing based on the bodily appearance of a **thing** is rational, but the appearance is always one-sided, “imperfect.” In principle, a thing-real can appear in a closed appearance only “inadequately.” Essentially connected with this: no reason-positing based on such an inadequately giving appearance can be “final” or “unconquerable.” Rational motivation comes from the appearance taken in isolation, but the phenomenology of reason in the sphere of principally only inadequately givable types of being must study the various a priori prefigured occurrences: how inadequate givenness-consciousness progresses through ever new appearances with one and the same determinable X; the cases of harmonious fulfillment with its steadily increasing reason-force; the opposing possibilities—the cases of fusions or polythetic syntheses of **disharmony**, of “other-determination” of the ever-same X, with annulment, “explosion” of perception, “conflicting thing-apprehensions,” etc.

To every **region and category** of pretended objects there corresponds a basic type of originarily giving consciousness and an associated basic type of originary evidence. Every such evidence is either **adequate** (in principle not further “confirmable” or “disconfirmable,” without gradation of weight) or **inadequate** (capable of increase and decrease). Which type is possible depends on the generic type and is a priori preformed.

-----

## § 139. Intertwinings of All Types of Reason. Theoretical, Axiological, and Practical Truth

A positing of whatever quality has its right when it is rational. The reason-character is the character of rightness belonging to it essentially. Correlatively, the **proposition** is called justified. But something can also “speak for” a proposition even when the proposition is not itself rational. All lines of reason ultimately lead back to **ur-belief and its ur-reason**, i.e. to **“truth.”**

**Truth** is the correlate of the perfect reason-character of the ur-doxa, of belief-certainty. Evidence is not merely a title for reason-occurrences in the belief-sphere but for **all thetic spheres** and especially for the significant reason-relations running between them. The highly difficult problem-groups of reason in the sphere of sentiment- and will-theses, and their intertwinings with “theoretical” (doxic) reason, arise. **“Theoretical” or “doxological” truth/evidence has its parallel in “axiological and practical truth/evidence,”** where the “truths” of the latter come to expression and cognition in doxological truths. The problems of reason in the doxic sphere must in their solution precede those of axiological and practical reason.

-----

## § 140. Confirmation. Justification Without Evidence. Equivalence of Positional and Neutral Insight

Further studies concern the **connections of “covering”** between acts of the same sense and proposition but of different reason-value. An evident act and a non-evident one can come to covering, with the insightful positing functioning as “confirming.” The “proposition” “verifies” or “confirms” itself, the imperfect givenness-mode transforms into the perfect. For every genus of propositions, the forms of principally possible verification must be phenomenologically clarified.

Husserl distinguishes between **motivated possibility** (determined by what the proposition, as fulfilled as it is given, contains) and **empty possibility**. Either the possibility coincides with actuality (the insight into the possibility carries with it the originary givenness-consciousness and reason-consciousness), or it does not. **Actual experience** (not mere running-through of “possible” perceptions) furnishes actual verification of positings directed at reals. But for every **essence-positing**, the intuitive re-presentation of its perfect fulfillment is **equivalent to the fulfillment itself**.

-----

## § 141. Immediate and Mediate Reason-Positing. Mediate Evidence

All mediate grounding leads back to immediate. The **primal source of all right** lies in immediate, and more narrowly, in **originary evidence**. Clear **memory** has in a certain way original, immediate right: it has “weight.” But it has only a relative and imperfect right. Memory posits the past and necessarily co-posits a horizon that must explicate itself in a connected chain of memories terminating in **actual perceptions**. With each advance from memory to memory, the memory is corroborated; the memory-weights are functionally interdependent. When the explication is carried through to the actual now, **something of the light of perception and its evidence radiates back over the whole series**.

But there are also essentially other forms of drawing from the source of originary givenness: a proposition **mediately** in an at every step **evident synthetic nexus** referred to immediately evident grounds yields a new general type of reason-positing—**“mediate evidence”** in the usual exclusive sense. This derived evidence-character can arise only in the end-term of a positing-nexus proceeding from immediate evidences, running in various forms, and sustained at every further step by evidences.

-----

## § 142. Reason-Thesis and Being

With the general essential understanding of reason must come the general clarification of the essential correlations binding the idea of truly being with the ideas of truth, reason, and consciousness. A general insight: not only are “truly existing object” and “rationally positible object” equivalent correlates, but also “truly existing object” and an object positible in an **originary perfect reason-thesis**. In such a thesis, the object would not be given incompletely or merely “one-sidedly”; every aspect of the determinable X would be determinately given in originary fashion.

Principally, to every **“truly existing” object** there corresponds the **idea of a possible consciousness** in which the object itself is originarily and completely adequately graspable. Conversely, if this possibility is guaranteed, the object is thereby truly existing.

Of special significance: in the essence of every **apprehension-category** (correlate of every object-category) it is determinately prefigured what configurations of concrete, complete or incomplete apprehensions of objects of that category are possible. Each object-category is a general essence that can itself be brought to adequate givenness. **In its adequate givenness** it prescribes an **insightful general rule** for every particular object becoming conscious in manifolds of concrete experiences.

-----

## § 143. Adequate Thing-Givenness as Idea in the Kantian Sense

Husserl addresses an apparent contradiction: he said that there are in principle only inadequately appearing objects, but now speaks of adequate givenness as a correlate of true being. The resolution: objects of the kind “thing” cannot be given in a closed, completed appearance. But **as an “Idea” (in the Kantian sense)**, perfect givenness is nonetheless **prefigured**—as an absolutely determinate system of endless processes of continuous appearing, a continuum of appearances with different but determinate dimensions, governed by fixed essential lawfulness.

This continuum determines itself more precisely as an in all directions infinite continuum, all phases consisting of appearances of the same determinable X, ordered in such coherent fashion that every line yields a harmonious appearance-nexus in which the one and same X continuously-harmoniously determines itself more closely and never “otherwise.”

A closed unit of traversal, a finite act, is not thinkable (due to the all-sided infinity of the continuum). Yet the **idea** of this continuum and the idea of the perfect givenness prefigured by it **lies insightfully before us**—insightfully in the way an “Idea” can be insightful, designating by its essence an **own type of insight**. The idea of an essentially motivated infinity is not itself an infinity; the insight that this infinity cannot in principle be given does not exclude but rather demands the insightful givenness of the *idea* of this infinity.

-----

## § 144. Reality and Originarily Giving Consciousness: Concluding Determinations

It remains that the eidos truly-being is correlatively equivalent to the eidos adequately-given and evidently-positible—either in the sense of finite givenness or givenness in the form of an Idea. In one case, being is “immanent”—being as a closed experience or noematic experience-correlate; in the other, **transcendent** being, whose “transcendence” lies in the infinity of the noematic correlate required as its being-“matter.”

Where a giving intuition is adequate and immanent, originary fulfilled sense and object coincide—the object is precisely what is grasped in adequate intuition as the originary self. Where the giving intuition is transcending, adequate givenness is impossible; only the **Idea** of such an objectuality can be given, together with an a priori rule for the lawful infinities of inadequate experiences.

On the basis of the actually performed experiences and this rule (or the manifold system of rules it contains), what the further course of experience must be cannot be univocally determined. Infinitely many possibilities remain open, though they are preformed in type by the very substantive a priori regulation. How experience-based empirical thinking helps further, how scientific determination of things as experientially posited unities (involving infinite ambiguities) becomes possible—this belongs to a new layer of research: the phenomenology of specifically experiencing, and in particular physical, psychological, natural-scientific reason.

-----

## § 145. Critical Remarks on the Phenomenology of Evidence

From the preceding, it is clear that the **phenomenology of reason**, the noetic in the pregnant sense, presupposes the general phenomenology throughout. That thetic consciousness of every genus stands under norms is itself a phenomenological fact; the norms are nothing other than essential laws. Naturally, “unreason” as the negative counterpart of reason must everywhere be taken into account, just as the phenomenology of evidence includes that of **absurdity**.

Evidence is not some consciousness-index affixed to a judgment, like a mystical voice from a better world calling “Here is truth!” Husserl rejects the index- and feeling-theories of evidence, as well as the skeptical worries (the Cartesian demon fiction) they cannot overcome. In the study of the relevant phenomena within phenomenological reduction, one recognizes that evidence is a peculiar positing-mode (not an appended content of any kind) belonging to eidetically determinate essential constitutions of the noema. One further recognizes that essential laws regulate the relation of those positional acts lacking this distinguished constitution to those possessing it—there is consciousness of “fulfillment of intention,” of specific confirmation and corroboration, as well as the corresponding counter-characters of “disconfirmation” and “invalidation.” The logical principles demand deep phenomenological clarification. The principle of contradiction leads back to essential connections of possible verification and possible annulment.

These studies are conducted in the “transcendental,” phenomenological attitude. No judgment presupposes the thesis of natural reality as background. Everywhere we pursue the formations of noeses and noemata, drafting a systematic eidetic morphology, highlighting essential necessities and essential possibilities. “Object” is everywhere a title for essential connections of consciousness.

-----

## Third Chapter: Levels of Generality of the Reason-Theoretical Problematic

The meditations on the problematic of a phenomenology of reason have so far moved at a height of generality that did not let the essential branchings of problems and their connections with formal and regional ontologies emerge. To come closer: only then will the full sense of the phenomenological eidetics of reason and the entire richness of its problems disclose themselves.

-----

## § 146. The Most General Problems

The problem-title encompassing all of phenomenology is **intentionality**. Phenomenology begins with problems of intentionality—initially in generality, without drawing in the questions of the truly-being of what is conscious in consciousness. That positional consciousness with its thetic characters can be designated in the most general sense as a “meaning” (*Vermeinen*) and as such stands necessarily under the reason-opposition of validity and invalidity remains out of consideration. In all eidetic spheres, the systematic way leads from higher to lower generality.

-----

## § 147. Problem-Branchings. Formal Logic, Axiology, and Praxis

The general phenomenology of reason branches when further structural differences determining the reason-characters are considered: differences according to fundamental types of theses, differences between simple and founded theses, and the cross-cutting differences between single-membered theses and syntheses. Husserl refers back to his earlier discussions of the pure morphologies of propositions and specifically of synthetic propositions, related to the predicative doxic synthesis, to the doxic modalities, and to the sentiment- and will-acts.

As soon as we raise the question of reason-validity for propositions determined exclusively through their pure forms, we stand in **formal logic** and in the formal parallel-disciplines of **formal axiology and praxis**. In the synthetic forms lie a priori conditions of possible validity expressed in the essential laws of the disciplines in question. Specifically, in the pure forms of predicative (analytic) synthesis lie a priori conditions of the possibility of **doxic reason-certainty**, noematically: of possible **truth**.

The same applies to the syntheses belonging to the sentiment and will sphere. The conditions of possibility of axiological and practical “truth” lie in the pure synthetic forms of these spheres. Through “objectification,” all axiological and practical reasonableness turns into doxic reasonableness and noematically into truth, objectively into **reality**: we speak of true or actual purposes, means, excellences, etc.

-----

## § 148. Reason-Theoretical Problems of Formal Ontology

From the formal disciplines, a turn leads to the corresponding **ontologies**. Every formal-logical law is equivalently convertible into a formal-ontological one. Instead of judgments, one now speaks of states of affairs; instead of judgment-parts, of objects; instead of predicate-significations, of features, etc.

Formal ontology goes far beyond mere conversions of formal apophantic truths. Great disciplines accrue through **nominalizations**: in plural judging, the plural arises as a plural thesis; through the nominalizing turn, it becomes the object “set”—the fundamental concept of set-theory. The same holds for the concepts relation, number, etc. as fundamental concepts of mathematical disciplines. The concept of “formal ontology” has thus **expanded**: values and practical objectivities fall under the formal title “object,” “something in general.” Their corresponding “formal” ontologies are (from the standpoint of universal analytic ontology) material disciplines. Deep problems of the phenomenology of reason attach to the radical clarification of the analogies and parallels between the doxic and the axiological/practical.

-----

## § 149. The Reason-Theoretical Problems of the Regional Ontologies. The Problem of Phenomenological Constitution

The transition to **material** disciplines and first to the **regional ontologies**. Every objective region constitutes itself in consciousness. An object determined by the regional genus has, insofar as it is actual, its a priori prefigured ways of being perceivable, intuitively or darkly representable, thinkable, demonstrable. Each region provides the **guiding thread for its own closed investigation-group**.

Taking the region “material thing” as guiding thread: the **problem of the general “constitution” of the objectualities of the region Thing in transcendental consciousness**. An individual thing—intuited, followed through its movements, turnings, form-changes, quality-changes, causal behaviors—gives rise to continua of intuitions covering and unifying into the unity-consciousness of one identical X. But we know nothing thereby of the intuition-processes themselves: the various hyletische, noetic, noematic “reflections” are needed to open the great research-field.

The guiding question: **How are the noeses and noemata belonging to the unity of intuitively representing thing-consciousness to be systematically described?** The regional idea of the thing—its identical X with its determining sense-content, posited as being—**prescribes rules for manifolds of appearances**. The idea of the region prescribes quite determinate, determinately ordered, in infinitum progressing, as ideal totality firmly closed series of appearances. Within this organization, the unity of a mere res extensa is thinkable without the unity normed by the idea of a res materialis, though no res materialis is thinkable that is not a res extensa.

What we phenomenological naives take for mere facts—that a spatial thing always appears in a certain “orientation,” that it can only be seen at a certain “depth,” “distance,” all relative to an invisible center—prove, with minor empirical particularizations, to be **essential necessities**. Something like a spatial thing can be intuited—even for God as the ideal representative of absolute knowledge—only through appearances in which it is given “perspectivally” in manifold determinate ways of variation and in changing “orientations.”

The **problem of constitution** means: the regulated and necessarily co-belonging appearance-series can be intuitively surveyed and theoretically grasped despite their infinities, analyzed and described in their eidetic peculiarity, and the lawful correlation between the determinate appearing-unity and the determinate infinite manifolds of appearances fully seen into and stripped of all riddles.

-----

## § 150. Continuation. The Region Thing as Transcendental Guiding Thread

After understanding in the most general terms the infinities that thing-intuition harbors: the question is how the **region Thing can serve as a guiding thread** for phenomenological investigations. While intuiting an individual thing and following its movements, we perform continua of intuiting—covering, closing into the unity of one identical X. But with all this, we know nothing of the processes of intuition themselves. The change of attitude is required—the various hyletische, noetic, noematic “reflections.”

In the noematic sphere: **What do the manifold positing intuitions, the “intuition-propositions,” look like in which an “actual” thing comes to givenness and demonstrates its actuality in originary “experience”?** What do the mere appearances look like that bring one and the same thing “to appearance”—the thing that belongs to this appearance-manifold as a **necessary correlate**?

The regional idea of the thing, posited as being, **prescribes rules for manifolds of appearances**—not arbitrary manifolds but determinately ordered, in infinitum progressing series of appearances, whose inner organization is essentially connected with the partial ideas composing the regional thing-idea. Within this organization, the thing-schema—the spatial shape filled merely with “sensuous” qualities without any determination of “substantiality” or “causality”—constitutes an own layer. Already the idea of a mere res extensa is the title for a wealth of phenomenological problems.

What we naively take for mere factual contingencies of spatial intuition—perspective, orientation, depth—prove to be essential necessities. The **problem of the “origin of the representation of space”** reduces to the phenomenological essential analysis of all noematic (and noetic) phenomena in which space intuitively presents itself and is “constituted” as the unity of appearances.

-----

## § 151. Layers of the Transcendental Constitution of the Thing. Supplements

These investigations are essentially determined by the various **levels and layers of thing-constitution** within the framework of originarily experiencing consciousness. Each level and each layer within a level is characterized by constituting **its own unity**, which in turn is a **necessary intermediate member** for the full constitution of the thing.

Taking the level of simply perceptive thing-constitution (whose correlate is the sense-thing endowed with sensory qualities), we are referred to a single stream of consciousness and the possible perceptions of a single perceiving ego-subject. Various unity-layers appear: the **sensual schemata**, the “seen-things” of higher and lower order, the **substantial-causal thing** at the top. The next higher level is the **intersubjectively identical thing**—a constitutive unity of higher order referred to an open plurality of subjects standing in “mutual understanding,” constituted through **empathy**-mediated experience.

Husserl emphasizes the need for completeness: not only the ideal system of harmonious appearance-manifolds must be studied but also the counter-cases—**partial breaches of harmony**, the “corrections” needed to maintain consistency, the distinctive thetic occurrences, revaluations and devaluations, “illusion,” “conflict,” etc. The phenomenology of “null semblance” is indispensable for a phenomenology of “true reality.”

-----

## § 152. Transfer of the Problem of Transcendental Constitution to Other Regions

What was said exemplarily for the constitution of the material thing transfers to **all object-regions**—in problem and in method. For “sensory perceptions,” the types of originarily giving acts essentially assigned to the relevant regions now enter. Very difficult problems attach to the **interweaving of the different regions**: the thing is not isolated from the experiencing subject; the subject is itself constituted in experience as a real being, as a human being or animal; intersubjective communities, though essentially founded in psychic realities (which in turn are founded in physical ones), prove to be **objectualities of a novel, higher order**. There are also value-objects, practical objects, all concrete cultural formations (state, law, custom, church, etc.).

All these objectivities must be described as they come to givenness, and the **problems of constitution** must be posed and solved for them. Their constitution leads back to that of spatial thinghood and psychic subjects. As the lowest level, material reality underlies all other realities; hence the **phenomenology of material nature** has a distinguished position. But the founded unities are genuinely novel; they can never be reduced to mere sums of other realities. **Every distinctive type of such realities carries with it its own constitutive phenomenology** and thereby a new concrete doctrine of reason.

## § 153. The Full Extension of the Transcendental Problem. Articulation of the Investigations

The tremendous extent of the researches just recognized can scarcely be conveyed in so general a discussion. Nevertheless, it is clear that these are serious problems and that fields of investigation open up that concern **all that is principial in all material sciences**. The “principial” is nothing other than what groups around the regional ideas in fundamental concepts and fundamental cognitions, finding its systematic unfolding in corresponding regional ontologies.

What has been said extends from the material to the **formal sphere** and to its ontological disciplines. The framework of constitutive researches thereby extends so far that it can finally encompass the whole of phenomenology.

The problems of object-constitution are first of all referred to the manifolds of possible **originarily giving** consciousness (e.g. for things, to perceptions). To this is joined the supplementary consideration of reproductive positional consciousness-types and the investigation of their constitutive reason-performance. We move initially in the mere sphere of **“representation.”**

But corresponding researches, related to the performances of the **higher, properly so-called “understanding” or “reason” sphere**—with its explicating, relating, and other “logical” (also axiological and practical) syntheses—join in. Objectualities first given in monothetic acts can be subjected to the play of **synthetic operations**, thereby constituting synthetic objectualities of ever higher levels. The phenomenological investigation must clarify the various givenness-modes and their significance for “actual being” of such objectualities.

The logical syntheses are grounded in lowest theses with simple matters, but the essential lawfulness of the synthetic level—and specifically the reason-laws—are in a wide, determinately delimited “formal” sphere **independent of the particular matters** of the synthetic members. Thereby a general and formal logic becomes possible. **Constitutive investigations** accordingly divide into those following **formal** fundamental concepts as “guiding threads” of the reason-problems, and those following **regional** fundamental concepts with the question of how an individual of such a region comes to givenness.

This extends to all act- and object-spheres, including the objectualities for whose constitution **sentiment-acts** with their specific theses and matters must a priori contribute.

The intimate relation of the constitutive phenomenologies to the a priori ontologies and finally to all eidetic disciplines becomes evident. The **sequence of the formal and material doctrines of essences prefigures in a certain way the sequence of the constitutive phenomenologies**, determines their levels of generality, and provides them in the ontological and material-eidetic fundamental concepts and propositions with their “guiding threads.” For example, fundamental concepts of the ontology of nature (time, space, matter) are indices for layers of the constituting consciousness of material thinghood, and the corresponding fundamental propositions are indices for essential connections in and between the layers.

Husserl expressly notes that in these connections between constitutive phenomenologies and the corresponding ontologies, **nothing of a grounding of the former by the latter is implied**. The phenomenologist does not judge ontologically when recognizing an ontological concept or proposition as an index for constitutive essential connections; he sees in it a guiding thread for intuitive demonstrations that carry their right and validity purely in themselves.

A comprehensive, equally noetic and noematic solution of the constitution-problems would be equivalent to a **complete phenomenology of reason** in all its formal and material formations and in its anomalous (negatively-rational) as well as normal (positively-rational) forms. Further, such a complete phenomenology of reason would **coincide with phenomenology as a whole**: a systematic execution of all consciousness-descriptions demanded by the total title “object-constitution” would have to encompass all consciousness-descriptions whatsoever.
