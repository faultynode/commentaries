# J. N. Mohanty, *The Philosophy of Edmund Husserl: A Historical Development*

## Paragraph-by-Paragraph Commentary

-----

## Preface

Mohanty opens the Preface by telling us that the idea for this book had been germinating since the 1980s. A Humboldt Prize in 1992 gave him the opportunity to spend several months, and then many subsequent summers, in Germany, where he was able to read the relevant secondary literature and, more importantly, to work directly with Husserl’s *Nachlass* (the unpublished literary estate) at the Husserl Archive in Freiburg. He expresses gratitude to the Directors of the Archive for granting him access and permissions. This paragraph situates the book within a long biographical arc of engagement with Husserl’s thought: Mohanty is not writing a quick survey but the product of decades of immersion, including firsthand contact with primary manuscript sources that go well beyond the published Husserliana volumes.

In the second paragraph, Mohanty recounts how a young philosopher named Sigfried Rombach drove him around the Black Forest, visiting the small, picturesque villages where Husserl used to vacation and compose parts of his *Nachlass*. This personal pilgrimage was deeply moving: visiting Husserl’s grave outside Freiburg was both inspiring and saddening. Mohanty reads the grave itself as a symbol — it represents Husserl’s refusal to capitulate to the irrationalism that was closing in around him (a reference to the political and intellectual climate of the 1930s, including the rise of National Socialism), even as he suffered personally and continued to defend the life of thinking. This passage reveals the deep personal reverence Mohanty holds for Husserl, not merely as a philosopher to be studied, but as a figure whose life embodied the philosophical commitment to rationality under duress.

In the third paragraph, Mohanty notes that his own Husserl studies began in 1950 and that the present book, together with a planned second volume, will bring those studies to a close. He concludes with a tribute to Husserl as a teacher who showed him what it means to live a life devoted to philosophy and how to follow that path steadfastly. This final remark reinforces the book’s character as both a scholarly and existential testament: Mohanty writes not just as an interpreter but as someone whose entire intellectual life was shaped by the encounter with Husserl.

## Part I. Anticipations: The Halle Period, 1886–1900

## 1. Toward a Philosophy of Mathematics

**Paragraph 1 (p. 3).** Mohanty begins the chapter by reporting the contents of a letter Husserl wrote to Franz Brentano on December 29, 1886. In this letter, Husserl tells his former teacher that he is occupied with logical investigations into the fundamental concepts and principles of arithmetic and higher analysis. To amuse Brentano, Husserl quotes a sentence on “continuity” by Herman Cohen, which he presents as an example of what at that time passed for scientific philosophy while really being what he calls “tiefsinnigen Blödsinn” (profound nonsense) or “blödsinnigen Tiefsinn” (nonsensical profundity). This is contrasted with Brentano’s “Gesundbrunnen” — his wellspring of philosophical health and rigor — to which Husserl says he will always return. The passage establishes the Brentanian context in which Husserl’s early philosophical career unfolded: Husserl began as a mathematician drawn to philosophical foundations, and his first intellectual orientation was shaped entirely by the clarity and sobriety that Brentano represented, in deliberate contrast to the neo-Kantian tradition of Cohen.

**Paragraph 2 (p. 3).** Mohanty notes that Husserl’s *Philosophie der Arithmetik* appeared in 1891 and that, according to its author, it consists of a series of psychological and logical investigations intended to lay the groundwork for a system — though without constructing the system itself. It remains unclear what sort of system Husserl envisioned, and he never actually went on to build it. Husserl adds, however, that for understanding the *Philosophie der Arithmetik*, knowledge of philosophy is not required, though some mathematical knowledge may be needed for the second volume — a volume that was eventually abandoned. This paragraph establishes a key fact about the *Philosophie der Arithmetik*: it was conceived as a preparatory, foundational work rather than a self-contained treatise, and the larger systematic ambition it was meant to serve never materialized.

**Paragraph 3 (pp. 3–4).** In the first volume, which is the only one we have, Husserl tells us that he will deal with psychological questions connected with the analysis of the concepts of plurality, unity, and number. He will treat these first insofar as they are authentically (i.e., intuitively) given, and then insofar as they are symbolically represented. Through this analysis, he hopes to demonstrate that the meaning and purpose of an arithmetic of cardinal numbers are determined by the fact that we are limited to symbolic representation of numbers. Mohanty is here setting out Husserl’s basic framing of the project: the investigation splits into two levels — the authentic or intuitive givenness of number concepts, and their symbolic or sign-mediated representation — and the relationship between these two levels is what makes arithmetic necessary in its particular form.

**Paragraph 4 (p. 4).** Husserl goes on to inform us that in the second volume he will investigate the logic of “arithmetical algorithm” and of computation with “quasi-numbers” such as negative, imaginary, and irrational numbers. He will also discuss the idea of a universal arithmetic that can be applied to quite distinct conceptual domains, whose main concepts are “series,” “quantity,” and so on. He expresses the hope that he can prepare the way for a philosophy of calculus and will develop a new philosophical theory of Euclidean geometry. On each of these topics, Husserl will have something to say, even though the second volume was never completed. On the topic of geometry, he did leave a manuscript which Mohanty promises to review. This paragraph outlines the larger scope of Husserl’s unrealized ambitions — the planned second volume was to extend the analysis of number into the domain of algorithmic logic, non-natural number systems, and eventually into a full philosophical treatment of calculus and geometry. These are themes that would recur, transmuted, throughout Husserl’s later work, especially in his thinking about formal ontology and the *mathesis universalis*.

**Paragraph 5 (p. 4).** Before proceeding to an exposition of the *Philosophie der Arithmetik*, Mohanty pauses to correct a widespread misunderstanding. He states that it would be fundamentally mistaken to regard Husserl’s position in this work as “psychologistic.” Ever since Frege’s well-known review of the *Philosophie der Arithmetik*, the standard story has been that Husserl held a wrongheaded psychologistic position and then, partly under the influence of Frege’s criticism, retracted it. Recent researchers have shown this story to be mistaken. The thesis of the *Philosophie der Arithmetik* is not “psychologistic” in the pejorative sense: it does not *reduce* numbers to subjective processes. Frege, in fact, did not understand the nature of Husserl’s theory. Yet even if the thesis is not psychologistic, neither is it what could be called “purely logical.” More appropriately, using a characterization not yet available to its author, the *Philosophie der Arithmetik* is rather a first attempt at a *phenomenology* of arithmetic. This is an important interpretive intervention by Mohanty: he is challenging the received narrative (going back to Frege and perpetuated through much of the 20th century) and aligning himself with more recent scholarship that reads the *Philosophie der Arithmetik* as proto-phenomenological rather than psychologistic.

**Section I heading (p. 4).** The Roman numeral “I” marks a new section within the chapter. No further title is given.

**Paragraph 6 (pp. 4–5).** Husserl begins his discussion of number concepts by arguing for the priority of cardinal numbers. The word “cardinal” itself means “basic” or “fundamental,” which Husserl uses to support this thesis. If cardinals are numbers of sets and ordinals are related to series, the priority of cardinals might be established by reducing series to ordered sets. In assigning priority to cardinals, Husserl follows his teacher Weierstrass and rejects the view held by distinguished scholars such as Helmholtz and Kronecker, who, Husserl argues, do not clearly distinguish between numbers and numerical signs and consequently misinterpret the symbolic process of counting. Husserl therefore proceeds to analyze the concept of cardinal numbers such as “one,” “two,” and so on. But this analysis presupposes an analysis of the concept of multiplicity — that is, every determinate number, such as “five,” is a determination of the indeterminate concept of plurality. One answers the question “How many?” with regard to a multiplicity; when a multiplicity is given, one recognizes that it must consist of determinate objects, each given for itself and all collectively apprehended together. This dense paragraph lays out the foundational structure of Husserl’s analysis: cardinal numbers are primary; they presuppose multiplicities (pluralities); and multiplicities in turn presuppose the collective apprehension of individually given objects. The chain of conceptual dependency runs from concrete totalities up through plurality to determinate cardinal number.

**Paragraph 7 (p. 5).** Husserl begins with multiplicities that are authentically represented — that is, which are intuitively given and not merely symbolically referred to. Since anything, or rather any things, can be counted, the totality upon which abstraction may be exercised in order to generate number concepts may consist of any objects whatsoever, without any constraints as to the specific nature of these objects. Husserl therefore rejects all those views that restrict the origin of number concepts to physical phenomena. One can equally well count mental acts, such as two judgments. In any given case, it is true that a totality must consist of specific contents, but besides such specific contents, there must be present in a totality a certain mode of combination of those contents. One may distinguish between various kinds of combination: “continuous combination” (as in a totality of points on a line or moments in a stretch of time — a continuum); and a second kind, the way a spatial extension is combined with a color, or a color with a certain intensity — which Brentano calls “metaphysical” combination. This paragraph establishes two crucial points: first, that Husserl’s theory of number is maximally general (any objects whatsoever can be counted, not just physical ones); and second, that the question of how the members of a totality are *combined* is central to understanding what kind of totality is at stake. Husserl here introduces Brentano’s typology of modes of combination as a backdrop against which his own concept of “collective combination” will be defined.

**Paragraph 8 (pp. 5–6).** Mohanty explains that in order to form a concept of totality, we must be able to reflect on the mode of combination of the parts in that sort of totality. On Husserl’s view, the concept of number arises from reflection upon the totalities formed by the mode of combination he calls “collective combination.” We need to look more closely at this relation of “collective combination” which produces the totalities (or *Inbegriffe*) pertinent for the production of number concepts. One account, which Husserl rejects, explains “collective combination” as the relation of belonging to one comprehensive unity of consciousness. This account cannot distinguish between all contents of one’s consciousness at any moment (which one may especially focus upon by a spontaneous act of “noticing” or even thinking) and only those contents that are selectively combined. This means that not consciousness in the widest sense of the totality of all mental contents, but rather an especially spontaneous act of focusing, is needed for collective combination. Likewise, it would be a mistake to identify collective combination with temporal simultaneity — on this account, a multiplicity would be nothing but all or some of those contents that are simultaneously given to a consciousness. This paragraph is working through rival accounts of collective combination and showing why Husserl rejects them. The key philosophical point is that collective combination cannot be reduced to mere co-presence in consciousness or temporal simultaneity; it requires something more specific — an active, selective act of gathering.

**Paragraph 9 (p. 6).** Mohanty presents a continuation of the argument against identifying collective combination with simultaneity. Husserl considers and rejects a third account, which holds that a multiplicity can only consist of contents that consciousness entertains successively. The elements constituting a totality can then be given only one after another. Such an account, Husserl argues, is based on too restricted a view of consciousness. Consciousness is not restricted, at any moment, to only one specific content. If this were so, a combination of successive contents would have been impossible. Husserl concedes that for representing totalities, temporal succession is necessarily required. However, this is not the same as saying that the contents of those representations themselves involve temporal succession. When one infers a conclusion from given premises, this act of thinking involves temporal succession, but the relation between the premises and the conclusion is not one of temporal succession. No one would want to identify the concept of plurality with that of succession. The idea of a plurality of simultaneous contents is not an absurdity; were it so, we would not be able to explain the origin of the concept of temporal coexistence. Even if a series of contents were successive, a psychic act is needed that comprehends those contents together so that the representation of a totality may be possible. This extended argument clinches Husserl’s case that the combination constitutive of number is not temporal (neither simultaneity nor succession). The temporal dimension of mental acts is a psychological precondition for representing totalities, but the logical structure of the totality itself is independent of time. This distinction between the psychological conditions of representation and the logical content of what is represented is already a proto-phenomenological move.

**Paragraph 10 (p. 6).** Regarding the relevance of time for the concept of number, Husserl agrees with Herbart that number has no more in common with time than with a hundred other kinds of representations that can be produced only gradually. He also agrees with Beneke that the fact that time elapses while we are counting cannot prove anything — for when, over what, does time not elapse? Time plays the role only of a psychological precondition for our concepts. However, neither the simultaneity nor the succession in time of the contents enters into the logical nature of our concepts of totality and number. In this respect, Husserl differs from Kant, who, as is well known, had related the origin of numbers to time. This paragraph rounds off the discussion of time by marshaling the authority of Herbart and Beneke on Husserl’s side, while also marking the crucial break with the Kantian tradition. For Kant, number is constituted through the successive synthesis in time (the “schema” of quantity). Husserl denies this: time is a psychological accompaniment of counting, not a constitutive element of number itself.

**Paragraph 11 (pp. 6–7).** Mohanty reports that Kant writes that the pure schema of magnitude (*quantitatis*), as a concept of understanding, is *number*, a representation which comprises the successive addition of homogeneous units. Number is therefore simply the unity of the synthesis of the manifold of a homogeneous intuition in general, a unity due to the generating of time itself in the apprehension of the intuition. Husserl considers this text “dark” and finds it incompatible with what Kant himself says about “schema.” If, as Kant says, a schema (of a concept) is the representation of a universal procedure of imagination in providing an image for that concept, then the schema for “quantity” has to be the procedure of counting, and not number itself. “Number” and “counting” must be different representations. This paragraph contains Husserl’s specific internal critique of Kant. The point is precise: Kant confuses the schema (the procedure of counting) with the number concept itself. If a schema is a rule for generating images, then the schema of quantity is the process of successive addition (counting), and what that process yields — the number — must be something distinct from the process. This is an important critique because it separates the act-structure (counting as a temporal procedure) from the conceptual content (the number as an ideal object).

**Paragraph 12 (p. 7).** Most thinkers who understand the concept of multiplicity from the representation of series — and who therefore assign priority to ordinal numbers over cardinals — support the Kantian thesis regarding the relation of number to time. Notable mathematical scholars belonging to this group are Helmholtz and Hamilton. By understanding “series” as “ordered set” and by assigning priority to cardinals, Husserl avoids such a Kantian-type theory. This short paragraph draws out the systematic connection: the priority of cardinals over ordinals, which Husserl established at the outset, is what enables him to break with the Kantian derivation of number from temporal succession.

**Paragraph 13 (p. 7).** Another group of thinkers prefer to derive the number concepts from the representation of space. Unlike time, which can be represented only as a continuous quantity, space allows “discrete” quantities to be represented. A group of objects in space, such as the fingers in one’s hand, allow us to generate appropriate number concepts. Such a view, held by Mill and Lange, is in consonance with the reduction of arithmetic to geometry. Again, Husserl finds this theory too restrictive for number concepts. We count not only spatially separated contents, but also, as he insists, four virtues and two premises (of a conclusion). Furthermore, even when we count several spatial objects, the relations of position and ordering amongst them are not relevant, and can be freely varied, the number remaining the same. This paragraph disposes of another rival account — the spatial derivation of number. Husserl’s objection is the same in structure as his objection to the temporal account: spatial arrangement is contingent with respect to number, since we can count non-spatial things and since rearranging spatial objects does not change their number.

**Paragraph 14 (p. 7).** We thus find that neither spatial nor temporal relations are necessary for the sort of plurality that can serve as the basis for deriving the concept of number. Husserl draws a still stronger conclusion: the combination that produces the required sort of totality is not grounded in the nature of the contents that are combined. The act of collective combination does not produce a new content besides the contents that are combined. There is only the act of combining, but *no* new relational content. This paragraph states the decisive positive thesis about collective combination: it is purely formal. It adds no new content to the items combined; it is not grounded in any feature of the combined contents (neither spatial, temporal, physical, nor qualitative). It is nothing but the act of holding-together itself. This is why the concept of number can apply to absolutely any objects whatsoever.

**Paragraph 15 (pp. 7–8).** Husserl next examines a group of theorists — chief amongst them being Jevons, Sigwart, and Schuppe — who hold that for the concept of number to be possible, the objects counted must be given as different. As Schuppe held, what I cannot distinguish, I cannot count. The concept of plurality, which underlies the concept of number, is then “the empty form of difference.” So also Jevons: number is another name for diversity. Husserl rejects this theory. Difference of contents is not necessary for representing a totality: the contents need not be represented as different. To differentiate the given contents requires comparison and analysis, which are not needed for representing a plurality consisting of those contents. The same sort of considerations show that similarity amongst the given contents — similarity based on comparison of their nature — is no more necessary than difference for constituting a collective combination. As a matter of fact, the most heterogeneous contents can be collectively combined into a totality. One may suppose that we can form the collection only after recognizing some similarity amongst them. This paragraph addresses and rebuts the “difference” theory of number. The key philosophical insight is that neither difference nor similarity among the counted items is a prerequisite for collective combination. Collective combination is prior to and independent of any comparative act: you do not need to compare objects to count them. This reinforces the purely formal character of the collective act.

**Paragraph 16 (p. 8).** Mohanty continues the argument from the previous paragraph. One might think that objects can only be collected together — as “clock, pen, and ink” — after recognizing some similarity amongst them. Husserl’s point is that truth is just the reverse: in order to compare these objects and then recognize their similarity, we must already have formed the collection. This closes off the suggestion that similarity is a precondition for collective combination: the collection is logically and genetically prior to any recognition of similarity or difference among its members.

**Paragraph 17 (p. 8).** The conclusion Husserl draws is that collective combination is a unique, further unanalyzable relation. It is not one of the primary relations, which are, in Brentano’s sense, physical phenomena, or, as Husserl calls them here, primary contents. Such primary relations include similarity, continuity, metaphysical connections like those between color and spatial extension, and logical connections as between premises and conclusion. Sharply distinguished from these are what Husserl calls psychic relations. Primary relations belong, in a certain sense, to the same level as their relata. Psychic relations are not co-given with the relata; they are objectified only through reflection upon the relating act. Collective combination is not a part of the totality of contents amongst whom it obtains. Nothing of this relation is to be found amongst the contents related. Besides, in the case of primary relations, the variability of the relata, consistently with the relation obtaining, is limited. In the case of collective combination, as we saw, there is unlimited variability. Any content can be collectively combined with any other. Not any content can be similar to any other. Besides, no matter how many contents are collected together, the relation of collective combination is a simple relation and does not consist of many simpler, two-termed relations. The logical word “and” signifies this relation, provided we do *not* analyze the totality of *a*, *b*, and *c* into “*a* and *b*,” “*b* and *c*,” and “*a* and *c*.” This is a dense and important paragraph in which Mohanty lays out Husserl’s full characterization of collective combination. It is (a) unique and unanalyzable — not reducible to any other type of relation; (b) “psychic” rather than “primary” — meaning it is not found among the contents themselves but only disclosed through reflection on the act of combining; (c) subject to unlimited variability of relata — any objects whatsoever can be collectively combined; (d) simple and not decomposable into pairwise sub-relations — the “and” in “a and b and c” is a single collective act, not a conjunction of binary relations. This analysis essentially defines the formal-ontological character of what will later become the concept of a “manifold” or “set” in Husserl’s mature philosophy.

**Paragraph 18 (p. 8).** The fact that Husserl, in the *Philosophie der Arithmetik*, characterizes collective combination as a *psychic* relation, has led some to understand his theory of number to be “psychologistic,” as though a psychic relation is a relation that obtains in a mind. The designation is no doubt misleading, and should be taken to mean exactly the way Husserl defines it — that is, as a relation that is consistent with infinite variability of the contents combined. This paragraph directly addresses the source of the “psychologistic” misreading: Husserl’s own terminology. Mohanty argues that the word “psychic” as Husserl uses it does not mean “mental” in the sense of subjective or psychological; it means simply a relation that is disclosed by reflection and that places no constraints on the nature of the relata. The misleading terminology is what gave Frege and others the hook for the psychologism charge, but the actual content of the definition is entirely formal.

**Paragraph 19 (pp. 8–9).** Husserl now wants to say that the number concepts are abstracted from a collective totality. By saying this, he means the following process: the given collective totality consists of several determinate contents. In order to abstract the number concept from it, we do not consider those contents as such-and-such determinate contents. Our interest focuses upon their collective combination, while the given contents are thought of as *any* contents, that is to say, each one as “some one.” What we then have is “some one, some one, some one.” We then have a determinate number concept. Each given concrete totality will fall under one and only one determinate number concept. An important stage in the development of this account is the use that is made of the concept of “something” (*Etwas*). “Something” is a name that applies to any thinkable content. It is not arrived at by comparing all different contents. The concept of “something” arises from reflection upon the act of presentation, as whose content any and every object can function. The concept of plurality is analyzable as “something and something and something, etc.” where the “etc.” points to an indefiniteness that belongs to the concept. By eliminating this indeterminacy, we get the different number concepts: “something and something,” “something and something and something,” and so on. This is the culmination of Part I of Husserl’s account. The process of abstracting number concepts works by disregarding the specific nature of the collected contents and attending only to the collective combination itself. Each content is replaced by the maximally indeterminate concept “something” (*Etwas*), and the resulting structures — “something and something,” “something and something and something,” etc. — are the number concepts. The concept of “something” is itself not arrived at by abstraction from features common to all objects (which would be an empty intersection), but rather by reflection on the act of presentation as such. This is a crucial move: the concept of “something” is a formal-reflexive concept, not an empirical generalization.

**Paragraph 20 (p. 9).** It is not as though the concrete objects belonging to a totality would disappear from our consciousness. Here “abstracting” means “not to attend, or not to notice specifically.” Without this concept of “something,” there would be no concept of number. Mohanty adds that to be noticed is that in all this, Husserl has not attempted to give a *definition* of number. As a matter of fact, while he has developed a theory about how we derive our *concepts* of numbers, he considers numbers themselves to be indefinable. It is in this light that we have to understand his rejection of the definition of “number” in terms of equinumerosity, and the definition of the latter in terms of reciprocal one-to-one correlation of the elements of the sets. Definitions of this kind are regarded by Husserl to be “unjustified and scientifically useless,” although because of their formalism they have appealed to many mathematicians and philosophers. This paragraph makes two key points. First, “abstraction” in Husserl’s sense is not an act of destruction or elimination but one of selective inattention: one stops attending to the specific natures of the contents, without those contents ceasing to be given. Second, and more importantly, Husserl does not offer a *definition* of number — he offers a genetic-descriptive account of how number concepts arise. Numbers themselves are indefinable because they are basic, primitive concepts. This is why Husserl rejects the Frege-Russell style definition of number in terms of one-to-one correspondence and equivalence classes: such definitions are formally correct but philosophically uninstructive, because they operate with concepts (like “equivalence”) that are no clearer than the concept of number itself.

**Paragraph 21 (p. 18).** One entire chapter (chapter 6 of the *Philosophie der Arithmetik*) is devoted to examining and clarifying the concept of reciprocal one-to-one correlation, in which one of the claims Husserl makes is that even if similarity or *Gleichheit* be defined in terms of such equivalence, that is not identity. Furthermore, defining *Gleichheit* of two pluralities in terms of equivalence of the two sets is defining what is familiar and well understood in terms of what is difficult to grasp. Even if it is conceded that the definition in terms of equivalence gives, in the logical sense, a necessary and sufficient criterion for *Gleichheit*, determining such *Gleichheit* does not depend upon ascertaining the needed equivalence. “Equivalence” and “having the same number” are not the same concepts, although their extensions may coincide. This paragraph is about Husserl’s critique of the equivalence-class definition of numerical identity. Husserl insists on the distinction between the *sense* (Sinn) and the *extension* of a concept: two concepts can have the same extension (be coextensive) without having the same meaning. Defining number through one-to-one correspondence may give an extensionally correct criterion, but it does not illuminate the *meaning* of numerical identity. This anticipates the sense/reference distinction that would later become central in analytic philosophy (and in Husserl’s own mature theory of meaning).

**Paragraph 22 (pp. 18–19).** The next chapter of the *Philosophie der Arithmetik* develops an argument against defining number through the notion of equivalence, first in general and then with special reference to Frege’s definition. In general, the argument proceeds as follows: let us begin with an arbitrary set *M*. Let us then collect all those sets that are equivalent to *M*. Let this class of all sets equivalent to *M* be *K*. *K* may be called the set of all sets equivalent to *M*. *M* belongs to *K*, and may be taken as its representative. *K* includes all conceivable sets of a certain number. To all members of *K* we assign one and the same number. We need not, for our present purpose, recall the procedure for constructing this number for a given *K*. Now if the proposed definition of number in terms of equivalence classes is correct, then assigning a number to a given class *M* is none other than bringing it under a class *K*, that is, classifying it in this way, which is the same as relating it to other sets belonging to *K*. But this, according to Husserl, is not at all the meaning of statements assigning numbers to concrete pluralities. This paragraph presents Husserl’s reconstruction and critique of the equivalence-class approach to defining number (which is essentially Frege’s approach, and later Russell’s). Husserl’s objection is that on this account, saying “there are nine planets” would mean nothing more than “the set of planets belongs to a certain equivalence class K.” But this, Husserl insists, is not what we mean when we make ordinary numerical ascriptions. The definition captures an extensional fact but misses the intuitive, concrete meaning of numerical predication.

**Paragraph 23 (p. 19).** What Frege attempts is to ground arithmetic on a series of formal definitions, from which the theorems of this science can be logically derived. In Husserl’s view, this is a false ideal. Fundamental concepts such as likeness, identity, whole and part, unity and plurality, intensity, location, time, quality, do not allow logical definition. What, in such cases, one can do is to exhibit the concrete phenomena from which these concepts are abstracted, and then to clarify the process of abstraction. In the case of number concepts, then, what needs to be given — instead of purely logical definitions — is a descriptive account of the way one arrives at these concepts. This precisely is what Husserl claims to have done. This paragraph states the methodological heart of Husserl’s philosophical approach in the *Philosophie der Arithmetik* and explains why Mohanty regards it as proto-phenomenological. For Husserl, the most fundamental concepts cannot be *defined* (i.e., analyzed into still more primitive components) because they are themselves primitive. What philosophy can and must do is *describe* the experiential origins of these concepts — exhibit the phenomena from which they are abstracted and clarify the process of abstraction. This is precisely what phenomenological description will later become.

**Paragraph 24 (p. 19).** Husserl recognizes that Frege’s actual definition goes through several steps. Frege, as is well known, takes number to be predicated of concepts, not of things. Saying that there are nine planets is saying of the concept of “planet” that it has nine instances. The number of *F*’s is the same as the number of *G*’s if and only if there are precisely as many *F*‘s as *G*‘s, and the latter is the case if there is a one-to-one correlation between the two concepts. This account, given in §§62–74 of the *Grundlagen der Arithmetik*, is soon reformulated using the analogy of “direction.” Frege now introduces “classes” into his account, especially the concept of *extension of concepts*. Using this technique, the direction of a line *a* may be defined as “the extension of *the concept parallel to line a*” — this last being a second-order concept. Analogously, the number belonging to the concept *F* is defined as the extension of *the concept equal to the concept F*. This paragraph is a compact but precise reconstruction of Frege’s strategy in the *Grundlagen*: the move from the question of numerical identity to the invocation of one-to-one correlation, the analogy with the concept of “direction,” and the final definition of number as the extension of a second-order concept. Mohanty presents this clearly as background for Husserl’s criticisms.

**Paragraph 25 (pp. 19–20).** There are lots of difficult questions here, especially regarding a proper interpretation of Frege’s concept of extension of a concept, and the related concept of course-of-values of a function. But, from Husserl’s perspective, one question remains central: does this account give a satisfactory reconstruction of the intuitive meaning of statements of numerical identity? This question is asked, besides Husserl, also by Crispin Wright, whose book on Frege’s *Conception of Numbers as Objects* Mohanty has found extremely helpful in understanding Husserl’s point. Is “extension” a purely logical concept? Further, the following question raised by Wright is pertinent (actually, Frege himself recognized this as one of the difficulties with his definition of “number”): let Ø be the concept of number that we all understand. If *q* were any singular term that we already understand, the judgment whether *q* is a Ø would be an intelligible question and can be decided in terms of the definition of Ø. If *q* is Caesar, and its extension is the same as the extension of “1,” then Caesar is identical with “1.” This paragraph brings in a further technical and philosophical difficulty with Frege’s definition — the so-called “Julius Caesar problem.” If number is defined as the extension of a concept, how do we know that Caesar is not the number 1? The definition in terms of extensions does not, by itself, rule this out. Mohanty cites Crispin Wright’s work on this problem, showing that Husserl’s critique anticipates (or at least resonates with) difficulties that would become central in analytic philosophy of mathematics decades later.

**Paragraph 26 (p. 20).** Husserl’s point is that the definition in terms of “extension” and a second-order concept does not clarify the *sense* of “number,” in spite of the “Scharfsinn” (acuity) and “unfruchtbare Hypersubtililitäten” (unfruitful hyper-subtleties) of the definition. It is perhaps unjustified to say that the Fregean subtlety was fruitless as evidenced by its influence on the development of logic; however, philosophically it was. Frege is speaking here as a philosopher, not as a mathematician. Husserl’s account conforms better to the everyday ascriptions of number: the subject of such ascription (in case of natural numbers greater than 1) is a plurality, composed of different objects, and the number predicate “being 9” is a collective — not a distributive — predicate. This final paragraph of Chapter 1 delivers Mohanty’s concluding assessment. Husserl may have been unjust in dismissing Frege’s work as fruitless — it bore enormous fruit for logic and the foundations of mathematics. But *philosophically*, Husserl’s objection stands: Frege’s definition does not illuminate the *sense* of number, only its extensional structure. Husserl’s own account, by contrast, cleaves more closely to ordinary numerical experience: when we say “there are 9 objects,” the subject is a concrete plurality and the number predicate is collective (it applies to the group as a whole, not distributively to each member). This is the core of Husserl’s proto-phenomenological alternative to logicist foundations of arithmetic.
