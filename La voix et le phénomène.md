# A Reader’s Guide to Jacques Derrida’s *La voix et le phénomène* (1967)

## Introduction au problème du signe dans la phénoménologie de Husserl

-----

## Preliminary Note

This guide follows the structure of Derrida’s text chapter by chapter and, where the density of the argument demands it, paragraph by paragraph. It is intended for philosophy students who have some familiarity with Husserl’s *Logical Investigations* (1900–1901) and with the basic vocabulary of phenomenology (intentionality, noesis/noema, reduction, constitution, etc.), but who may find Derrida’s compressed prose and layered argumentation difficult to enter. All references are to the text as found in the 1989 Quadrige/PUF edition.

Three epigraphs open the book — one from Husserl’s *Logical Investigations*, one from Husserl’s *Ideas I*, and one from Edgar Allan Poe’s *Extraordinary Tales*. The first reminds us that the word “I,” when written by an unknown hand, is “a word, if not devoid of *Bedeutung*, at least foreign to its normal *Bedeutung*.” The second evokes an infinite regress of representations within representations: paintings within paintings within paintings. The third is the voice of a dead man who says “I have slept, and now — now I am dead.” Together, these set the stage: the sign, representation, the voice, death, and the question of presence.

## INTRODUCTION (pp. 1–16)

### The Continuity of the *Logical Investigations* in Husserl’s Corpus

Derrida opens by asserting the fundamental continuity of Husserl’s philosophical project from the *Logical Investigations* (1900–1901) all the way through to the *Crisis* and *The Origin of Geometry*. The basic conceptual framework — intentional sense, the noematic, the distinction between the two strata of formal analytics, the concept of science in general — is laid down in the *Investigations* and never fundamentally displaced. Even the reductions (eidetic and phenomenological) are already at work in the *Investigations*, implicitly if not yet thematically. Derrida insists that a “patient reading” would uncover in the *Investigations* “the germinal structure of all of Husserl’s thought.”

**What the student should grasp:** The *Logical Investigations* are not merely an early, pre-transcendental work. They contain in embryo the entire problematic of phenomenology. This is why Derrida chooses to analyze them: whatever is found at their root will be found throughout the later Husserl.

### The First Investigation and the “Double Sense” of Sign

Derrida focuses on the opening chapter of the First Investigation (“Expression and Meaning”), which begins with what Husserl calls “essential distinctions.” The most fundamental is announced in the very first paragraph: the word “sign” (*Zeichen*) has a “double sense” (*ein Doppelsinn*). A sign can be either an *expression* (*Ausdruck*) or an *indication* (*Anzeichen*). This distinction, Derrida warns, commands everything that follows — not just in the First Investigation, but in the whole of phenomenology.

### Derrida’s Guiding Question

Derrida now states the guiding question of the entire book: does the phenomenological rigor of Husserl’s analysis — his insistence on the absence of presuppositions (*Voraussetzungslosigkeit*), his bracketing of metaphysics, psychology, and the natural sciences — nevertheless conceal a *metaphysical presupposition*? Not an accidental one, not a lapse of vigilance, but one that *constitutes* phenomenology from within, in its critical project and its founding premises: namely, in what it will call the “principle of principles” — the original, giving intuition, the presence of sense to a full and originary intuition.

In other words: is the *form* of phenomenological vigilance already commanded by metaphysics? Even Husserl’s suspicion toward metaphysical presupposition already presented itself as the condition for an authentic “theory of knowledge” — as if the project of a theory of knowledge did not itself belong, from the outset, to the history of metaphysics. “Is not the idea of knowledge and of the theory of knowledge *in itself* metaphysical?”

**What the student should grasp:** Derrida is not accusing Husserl of making a mistake. He is asking whether the very *form* of the phenomenological project — the demand for presence, evidence, the given — is the metaphysical gesture par excellence. This is a critique from *within*, not from without.

### The Project: Phenomenological Critique as a “Moment Within” Metaphysics

Derrida’s aim is to show, through the “privileged example” of the concept of sign, that the phenomenological critique of metaphysics is itself a moment *inside* metaphysical assurance. More precisely: the *resource* of phenomenological critique is the metaphysical project itself, in its historical completion and in the “merely restored purity of its origin.” Husserl, in criticizing metaphysical speculation, was targeting only its “perversion” or “degeneration,” never the authentic *philosophia prote* that he wished to restore. Even in the *Cartesian Meditations*, Husserl opposes “authentic metaphysics” to “metaphysics in the usual sense.”

### Presence, Ideality, and the Living Present

Derrida identifies the single, permanent motif behind everything Husserl denounces in “degenerate metaphysics”: it is always a *blindness to the authentic mode of ideality*. This ideality is what can be *indefinitely repeated in the identity of its presence* precisely because it does not exist as a real thing. Its non-reality (as essence, noema, intelligible object) guarantees that presence to consciousness can be indefinitely repeated. The ultimate form of ideality — “the ideality of ideality” — is the *living present* (*lebendige Gegenwart*), the *self-presence of transcendental life*.

Presence, Derrida argues, “has always been and will always be, to infinity, the form in which the infinite diversity of contents will be produced.” The originary opposition of form and matter, which inaugurates metaphysics, finds its ultimate justification in the “concrete ideality of the living present.”

**What the student should grasp:** The “living present” is the hinge of the entire argument. For Husserl, it is the ground of all evidence, all ideality, all truth. For Derrida, it is the unquestioned metaphysical presupposition of phenomenology — and of all Western metaphysics.

### Temporalization, Intersubjectivity, and Non-Presence

Derrida now points to what he calls phenomenology’s internal tension. Two areas of Husserl’s own descriptions resist the privilege of presence: (1) the analysis of *temporalization*, in which the constitution of the presence of a temporal object requires the passage from *retention* to *re-presentation* (*Vergegenwärtigung*); and (2) the analysis of *intersubjectivity*, in which the relation to the other ego requires *appresentation*, not direct presentation. In both cases, what Husserl calls a “modification of presentation” (re-presentation, appresentation) does not merely *happen to* presentation but *conditions it from the start*, “fissuring it a priori.”

This does not, Derrida notes, “call into question the apodicticity of the phenomenological-transcendental description.” But it does reveal an “original and non-empirical space of non-foundation” — an irreducible void upon which the security of presence is decided.

### Phenomenology as a Philosophy of Life and the Voice

Phenomenology, Derrida argues, is a philosophy of life. Not only because it treats death as an empirical and extrinsic accident, but because the *source of meaning* is always determined as the act of a living being (*Lebendigkeit*). The unity of “life” — which diffuses its light through all the fundamental concepts (*Leben, Erlebnis, lebendige Gegenwart, Geistigkeit*) — *escapes* the transcendental reduction and even *paves the way* for it. When empirical life is bracketed, it is still a *transcendental life*, a *living present*, that is discovered.

This concept of life is intimately linked to the voice. “The element of signification — or the substance of expression — which seems best to preserve at once ideality and living presence under all its forms is the *living word*, the spirituality of the breath as *phonè*.” The voice can seem to guard presence even in the absence of the world. This is the privilege of the *phonè* that the entire history of metaphysics implies and that Husserl radicalizes.

### The Parallels: Phenomenological Psychology and Transcendental Phenomenology

Derrida devotes several dense paragraphs to the extraordinary difficulty of the relation between phenomenological psychology and transcendental phenomenology. Husserl speaks of a “parallelism” (*Parallelität*) and even an “overlapping” (*Deckung*) between them: the domain of pure psychological experience *covers* the totality of the domain of transcendental experience. And yet a “radical difference” remains — one that “distinguishes nothing in fact,” separates no being, no lived experience, no determinate signification; a difference that nevertheless “changes all the signs” and in which alone the possibility of a transcendental question resides. “That is to say, of freedom itself.”

This “nothing” that distinguishes the parallels — this “invisible distance” — is what makes transcendental phenomenology possible. Without it, the phenomenological project would be destroyed at its root. But this “nothing” must not be substantialized or turned into something in the world. “If language never escapes analogy, if it even is analogy through and through, then it must, having arrived at this point, freely assume its own destruction and hurl metaphors against metaphors.”

**What the student should grasp:** The “parallels” between the psychic and the transcendental are a central and deeply puzzling feature of Husserl’s thought. Derrida uses this puzzle to show that the “difference” on which phenomenology rests is not a difference between things in the world but a difference *within language*. “Language guards the difference that guards language.”

## CHAPTER I: THE SIGN AND THE SIGNS (*Le signe et les signes*) (pp. 17–27)

### Husserl’s Opening Distinction: Expression vs. Indication

Derrida begins his close reading of the First Investigation. Husserl opens by denouncing a confusion: the word “sign” (*Zeichen*) covers, in ordinary and sometimes in philosophical language, two heterogeneous concepts — *expression* (*Ausdruck*) and *indication* (*Anzeichen*). Some signs “express nothing” because they carry nothing that could be called *Bedeutung* or *Sinn*. Such is the indication: it is *bedeutungslos*, *sinnlos* — but this does not mean it is a sign *without signification*. There can be no sign without signification of some kind. The problem is translational: in German one can say without absurdity that a sign (*Zeichen*) is devoid of *Bedeutung*; in French one cannot without contradiction say that a sign is devoid of *signification*.

Derrida proposes to render *bedeuten* as *vouloir-dire* (“to mean” in the sense of “wanting to say”), capturing the idea that *Bedeutung* is always what someone or a discourse *wants to say* — always a discursive content.

### Sinn and Bedeutung in Husserl (vs. Frege)

In the *Investigations*, Husserl does not distinguish between *Sinn* and *Bedeutung* (unlike Frege). Later, in *Ideas I*, he reserves *Bedeutung* for the ideal content of verbal expression (discourse), while *Sinn* covers the whole noematic sphere, including its non-expressive layers. *Bedeutung* is always linked to the linguistic sphere; *Sinn* is wider. This confirms Derrida’s point: *Bedeutung* belongs essentially to spoken discourse (*Rede*).

### Functional, Not Substantial, Distinction

The difference between indication and expression is *functional* (or intentional), not substantial. The same phenomenon can function as expression or as indication depending on the intentional experience that animates it. Two functions can *intertwine* (*verflechten*) in the same chain of signs. Husserl speaks first of “addition” or “juxtaposition,” then of “intimate intrication” (*Verflechtung*). This word will reappear at decisive moments throughout, and its recurrence is not accidental.

The crucial point: in communicative discourse, expression is *always* interwoven with indication. This is a factual necessity. But Husserl insists it must not compromise the possibility of a *rigorous essential distinction*. The entire analysis will proceed within the gap between fact and right, existence and essence, reality and intentional function.

### The Problem of the Genre/Species Relation

If all expressions are enmeshed in indication but the reverse is not true, one might be tempted to make expression a *species* of the genus “indication.” This would mean speech is merely a form of gesture. Husserl contests this: expression (*bedeuten*) is not a species of the *being-sign* (*Zeichensein*) in the sense of indication. To prove this, Husserl must find a situation in which expression is *not* entangled with indication — a language without communication, a monologue, “the solitary life of the soul” (*in einsamem Seelenleben*). The purity of expression can only be found when the relation to a certain *outside* is suspended.

### Two Possible Readings

Derrida offers two readings of Husserl’s move:

**First reading (critical):** Husserl represses, with “dogmatic haste,” the question of the sign *in general*. Before distinguishing two types of sign, shouldn’t one ask what a sign is *in general*? The concept of sign must have some unity of essence. “What is a sign in general?” — a question Husserl seems to evade.

**Second reading (charitable):** Husserl may be *deliberately* refusing to presume the unity of the sign. Perhaps the apparent unity is merely verbal; perhaps there are two irreducible concepts that have been abusively attached to a single word. To ask “what is the sign in general?” would be to subject the sign to an ontological project, to assign signification a place in an ontology — a classically metaphysical gesture. It would submit the sign to truth, language to being, speech to thought. But what if the sign *precedes* truth, *constitutes* it? What if “the sign is not a thing (*Sache*), not a being (*Seiendes*) about whose being one could pose a question”?

**What the student should grasp:** This chapter introduces the two sides of Derrida’s reading throughout: Husserl is both trapped by the metaphysical tradition (in subordinating the sign to a logic of presence) and, at certain moments, on the verge of escaping it (in recognizing the constitutive role of expression). The tension between these two tendencies is the engine of the entire book.

## CHAPTER II: THE REDUCTION OF INDICATION (*La réduction de l’indice*) (pp. 28–33)

### Indication as Extrinsic and Empirical

Husserl devotes only three paragraphs to “the essence of indication,” versus eleven to expression. Indication must be treated briefly and reductively because the goal is to isolate the originality of expression as *vouloir-dire*. Indication is to be excluded as an extrinsic and empirical phenomenon, even though it is tightly interwoven with expression in fact. But this reduction is difficult: indicative adherences keep reappearing, and their effacement is an “infinite task.”

### What Is an Indicative Sign?

Indication can be natural (the “canals of Mars” indicating possible intelligent beings) or artificial (a chalk mark, a brand). The opposition of nature and convention is irrelevant; what matters is the unity of the indicative function. Husserl describes this as a *motivation* (*Motivierung*): something that moves a thinking being to pass from one thought to another. This passage can be from conviction to conviction or from conviction to presumption, and it always connects an actual knowledge to an inactual one.

Crucially, Husserl uses very general concepts (*Sein*, *Bestand*) that cover both ideal objects and empirical existents. The motivation so defined is broader than indication in the strict sense: it covers both the indicative allusion (*Hinweis*) and demonstrative proof (*Beweis*). In proof, the connections are between ideal necessities and are *evident*; in indication, they link empirical existents and are *non-evident*. Even if an indication gives empirical certainty, it will never be an apodictic demonstration.

### Indication, Existence, and the Foundations of Reduction

Indication always falls on the side of empirical existence, contingency, non-evidence. All the psychic lived experience — the acts, convictions, motivations — belongs to the indicative order. Only the *content* of ideal truths escapes it. This means: everything that will later fall under the phenomenological reductions (factuality, worldly existence, non-essential necessity) is already associated with indication. The entire problematic of reduction — and all the conceptual distinctions in which it operates (fact/essence, transcendentality/worldliness) — “unfolds within the gap between two types of signs.”

Derrida pushes this thought: could the concept of “parallelism” that defines the relation between pure psychic and pure transcendental (gathering the whole enigma of Husserl’s phenomenology) be “announced here in the form of a relation between two modes of signification”? If so, the “reduction,” before becoming a method, would coincide with the most spontaneous act of spoken discourse — the simple practice of speech. This conclusion would “constitute, in our eyes, in a certain sense, the ‘truth’ of phenomenology” — but it would contradict Husserl’s express intention for two reasons: (1) Husserl believes in a pre-expressive, pre-linguistic layer of sense; and (2) while there is no expression without discourse, not all of discourse is “expressive.”

## CHAPTER III: MEANING AS SOLILOQUY (*Le vouloir-dire comme soliloque*) (pp. 34–52)

### What Is Expression?

Suppose indication has been excluded. What remains is expression: a sign charged with *Bedeutung*. Derrida identifies three intertwined reasons why Husserl calls these signs “expressions”:

**(A) Expression as Exterioration.** *Ex-pression* is a going-outside: it imprints in a certain “outside” a sense that is first in a certain “inside.” But this inside and outside are not the ordinary ones: the *bedeuten* aims at an *ideal object*, which is then ex-pressed, “going outside itself” into another outside that is still “within” consciousness — i.e., discourse. Expression, as Husserl will develop in *Ideas I*, is an “unproductive” layer that comes to *reflect* (*widerspiegeln*) all other intentionality. Expression is thus: “the going-outside-itself of an act, then of a sense, which can only remain within itself in the voice, and in the ‘phenomenological’ voice.”

**(B) Expression as Voluntary.** Expression is a *voluntary* exterioration, entirely conscious and intentional. In indication, animation has two limits: the body of the sign (which is not a breath) and the indicated (which is an existence in the world). In expression, the intention is absolutely *express* because it animates a voice that can remain entirely interior. This leads to a key equation: in the order of expressive lived experience, “intentional consciousness and voluntary consciousness are synonymous in Husserl’s eyes.” Derrida suggests this reveals a deep “voluntarist metaphysics”: “Sense *wants* to signify itself; it expresses itself only in a *vouloir-dire* that is nothing but a *vouloir-se-dire* of the presence of sense.”

Everything that escapes this pure spiritual intention is excluded from *bedeuten* and therefore from expression: facial expressions, gestures, the totality of the body and of worldly inscription, “the totality of the visible and the spatial *as such*.” Visibility and spatiality would “lose” the self-presence of the will and spiritual animation that opens discourse. “They are literally its death.”

**(C) Expression and the Exclusion of Communication.** Even within spoken discourse, there remains a massive non-expressivity. Not just the physical side of the sign, but everything related to *communication* or the *manifestation of psychic lived experiences* is excluded from pure expressivity under the heading of indication. Derrida shows that what ultimately separates expression from indication is “what one could call the *non-immediate-self-presence of the living present*.”

### Indication and the Relation to the Other

In communication, sounds are animated by a subject who gives them sense, and another subject must simultaneously understand the speaker’s intention. But this animation can never be pure and total: it must traverse “the non-transparency of a body.” The *Kundgabe* (manifestation) is thus always indicative because the *presence* of the other’s lived experience is *refused to our originary intuition*. The other’s subjectivity, the acts by which they give sense to their signs, “are not immediately and originally present to me as they are for them and as mine are for me.” This is an “irreducible and definitive limit.”

Whenever the immediate and full presence of the signified is *withheld*, the signifier is indicative in nature. Communication is indicative precisely because it involves non-presence. And since “as soon as the other appears, indicative language — another name for the relation to death — can no longer be effaced.”

### The Solitary Life of the Soul

To reduce indication and regain pure expressivity, Husserl suspends the relation to the other. In “the solitary life of the soul” (*im einsamen Seelenleben*), the physical event of language seems absent: the identical unity of the word is ideal, and its expressivity needs only the ideal and identical form of the word’s body insofar as it is animated by a *vouloir-dire*. In soliloquy, we use not real words but only *represented* words (*vorgestellt*). Their place is the *imagination* (*Phantasie*).

Husserl argues that in soliloquy, the subject does *not* communicate anything to itself, does not indicate anything to itself, because it has no need to do so: the acts in question “are lived by us *in the same instant* (*im selben Augenblick*).” The present of self-presence would be “as indivisible as a blink of the eye.”

Derrida raises the key question: doesn’t Husserl here reveal that “only the indication is truly a sign for Husserl”? The full expression — i.e., the fulfilled intention of *vouloir-dire* — “escapes in a certain manner the concept of sign.” The sign (*Zeichen*) is, *in truth*, indication (*Anzeichen*). This apparent lapsus reveals a deep truth: expression in its plenitude would no longer be a sign at all.

### The Comparison with Saussure

Derrida briefly compares Husserl’s analysis to Saussure’s *Course in General Linguistics*. Saussure, too, distinguished between the real word and its image, recognizing only the “acoustic image” as the signifier. But Saussure, lacking the “phenomenological precaution,” made the acoustic image — the signifier as “psychic imprint” — into a *reality* whose only originality is being interior. Husserl’s analysis goes further: the *noematic content* — the sense — is a non-real (*reell*) component of consciousness. The interiority of solitary discourse has a highly differentiated structure.

## CHAPTER IV: MEANING AND REPRESENTATION (*Le vouloir-dire et la représentation*) (pp. 53–66)

### Two Arguments for the Exclusion of Indication from Soliloquy

Derrida recapitulates Husserl’s two arguments: (1) In interior discourse, I do not communicate anything to myself; I can only *represent* (*vorstellen*) myself as doing so. (2) I do not need to, because the existence of psychic acts is *immediately present* to the subject in the present instant.

### The First Argument: The Problem of Representation

Husserl seems to apply to language a simple distinction between reality and representation: between *effective* communication and *represented* communication, there would be a clean exteriority. But can this distinction be applied to language? Derrida argues it cannot.

A sign is *never* a pure event, an unrepeatable empirical singularity. “A sign that would take place only ‘once’ would not be a sign.” A signifier must be recognizable as the same despite empirical variation. This identity is *ideal*, and it necessarily implies *representation*: as *Vorstellung* (the locus of ideality in general), as *Vergegenwärtigung* (the possibility of reproductive repetition), and as *Repräsentation* (substitution). Since this representative structure *is* signification itself, “I cannot begin an ‘effective’ discourse without being originally engaged in an indefinite representativity.”

If discourse essentially belongs to the order of representation, then the distinction between “effective” discourse and “represented” discourse becomes suspect: “there are every reason to think that ‘effective’ language is as imaginary as imaginary discourse, and that imaginary discourse is as effective as effective discourse.” The maintaining of this difference — throughout the history of metaphysics and still in Husserl — answers “the obstinate desire to save presence and to reduce or derive the sign.”

### The Second Argument: Presence, Ideality, and Death

Derrida connects the structure of ideality to the question of death. Ideality is “only the name of the permanence of the same and the possibility of its repetition.” It depends entirely on acts of repetition. “Being is determined by Husserl as ideality, i.e., as repetition.” The “principle of principles” — the value of originary presence to intuition — means that the universal form of all experience (*Erlebnis*) *has always been and will always be the present*. “Being *is* presence or modification of presence.”

To think presence as the universal form of transcendental life “is to open myself to the knowledge that in my absence, beyond my empirical existence, before my birth and after my death, the present *is*.” Therefore: “it is the relation to my death (to my disappearance in general) that hides in this determination of being as presence, ideality, absolute possibility of repetition. *The possibility of the sign is this relation to death*.”

If the possibility of my disappearance must in some way be *lived* for a relation to presence in general to be instituted, then one cannot say this experience comes to *affect* a subject who already exists. “I am” already supposes the relation to presence in general. “*I am* means originally: *I am mortal*.” The statement “I am immortal” is an *impossible* proposition — not merely false, but structurally contradictory.

### The Instability of the Distinction

Derrida shows that the sign is “originally worked by fiction.” Once this is admitted, no reliable criterion exists for distinguishing between exterior and interior language, or between effective and fictive language within the interior. “Such a distinction is nevertheless indispensable to Husserl for proving the exteriority of indication to expression, with everything it commands.”

## CHAPTER V: THE SIGN AND THE BLINK OF AN EYE (*Le signe et le clin d’œil*) (pp. 67–77)

### The Punctuality of the Instant

The “point” of Husserl’s argument rests on the identity of the lived experience present to itself “*in the same instant* (*im selben Augenblick*).” Self-presence must take place in the undivided unity of a temporal present, in order to have nothing to make known to itself by the proxy of a sign. If the punctuality of the instant is “a myth, a spatial or mechanical metaphor, an inherited metaphysical concept” — then Husserl’s entire argument is threatened at its foundation.

### Husserl’s Lectures on Time-Consciousness

Derrida turns to Husserl’s *Lectures on the Phenomenology of Internal Time-Consciousness* (1905), which Heidegger praised as the first in the history of philosophy to break with the concept of time inherited from Aristotle’s *Physics*. Derrida identifies three key points:

**(1) The persistence of punctuality.** Despite acknowledging that “no phase can be isolated as a punctual instant,” Husserl still describes temporal flow from the *point-source* (*Quellpunkt*): “The ‘now-apprehension’ is like the nucleus vis-à-vis a comet’s tail of retentions.” The “actual now is necessarily and remains something punctual (*ein Punktuelles*), a form that persists while matter is always new” (*Ideas I*, §81).

**(2) The constitution of the present through non-presence.** Despite this, Husserl’s descriptions show that the presence of the perceived present can appear as such only insofar as it “composes continuously with a non-presence and a non-perception”: retention and protention. These are not accidents added to the now; they participate “indispensably and essentially” in its possibility. Husserl himself calls retention a “perception” — but a unique one whose perceived is not a present but a *past*. The passage between perception and non-perception is *continuous*: “perception and non-perception pass continuously into one another.”

“Once one admits this continuity of the now and the non-now, of perception and non-perception, in the zone of originarity common to originary impression and retention, one *welcomes the other into the self-identity of the Augenblick*: non-presence and inevidence in the blink of the eye.” The *différance* between retention and reproduction is not the radical difference between perception and non-perception that Husserl wants it to be, “but between two modifications of non-perception.”

**(3) Trace, différance, and the ruin of the im selben Augenblick.** Without the non-identity to self of the so-called originary presence, how could the possibility of reflection and of re-presentation belong to the *essence* of every lived experience? Their common root — “the possibility of re-petition in its most general form, the *trace* in its most universal sense” — must not only *inhabit* the pure actuality of the now but *constitute it*. “Such a trace is — if one can maintain this language without contradicting and erasing it at once — ‘more originary’ than phenomenological originarity itself.”

**What the student should grasp:** This chapter is the philosophical heart of the book. Derrida uses Husserl’s own analyses of time to show that the punctual “now” — on which the distinction between indication and expression depends — is *always already* fissured by retention (non-presence). There is no pure, undivided self-presence; therefore, the sign (and indication) cannot be excluded from the innermost structure of consciousness.

## CHAPTER VI: THE VOICE THAT KEEPS SILENCE (*La voix qui garde le silence*) (pp. 78–97)

### The Double Exclusion

Phenomenological “silence” requires a double reduction: (1) the exclusion of the relation to the other in indicative communication, and (2) the exclusion of expression itself as a layer *added to* a pre-expressive sense. The voice exerts its “strange authority” in the relation between these two exclusions.

### The Theoretical Determination of Language

Derrida notes that Husserl’s examples of interior discourse (“You have acted badly, you cannot go on like this”) are *practical*, not theoretical: they involve blame, exhortation, remorse. These are chosen precisely because they are *not* cognitive propositions. But Husserl has always determined the model of language *in general* from the *theorein*. The pure form of the expression is “S is P” — the indicative present of the verb “to be” in the third person. The *Bedeutung* is always determined by a relation to the object. Everything in language that is not immediately a judgment of the form “S is P” tends to fall on the side of the non-expressive, and thus the indicative.

This leads Derrida to reflect on the verb “to be.” Its privilege “cannot resist the deconstruction of the word.” The word *being* is “the first or the last word to resist the deconstruction of a language of words.” The epoch of the *phonè* is the epoch of being in the form of presence.

### Expression as “Unproductive” Medium and the Privilege of the Voice

For Husserl, pure expression is an “unproductive medium” that reflects the pre-expressive layer of sense. Its only productivity is to pass sense into the ideality of a conceptual, universal form. Expression must protect, respect, and restore the presence of sense both as *being-before* (the object available to a gaze — *Gegenstand*, *Gegenwart*) and as *proximity to self* in interiority.

Now the voice enters. An ideal object is one whose showing can be indefinitely repeated. The voice seems to accomplish this operation “in time,” without breaking with the order of the *Zeigen* (showing) and indeed completing its function. “The passage to infinity in the idealization of the object is one with the historial advent of the *phonè*.”

### The Power of the Voice: Auto-Affection

Derrida provides his analysis of *why* the voice has this power. In the operation of “hearing oneself speak” (*s’entendre-parler*), there occurs an “auto-affection of an absolutely unique type.” On one hand, it operates in the medium of universality: the signifieds must be idealities repeatable and transmittable ad infinitum. On the other hand, the subject can hear/speak to itself, be affected by the signifier it produces, *without any detour through exteriority*, the world, or the non-proper. Every other form of auto-affection — seeing oneself, touching oneself — must either pass through the non-proper (the surface of the body, the mirror) or renounce universality. The voice seems to reduce even the interior surface of the body itself: it “seems, in its phenomenon, to be able to dispense with this exteriority within interiority.” This makes it the most *pure* auto-affection, and therefore apt for universality.

“The voice is the *name* of this element.” The voice is being-beside-itself in the form of universality, as *con-sciousness*. “The voice *is* consciousness.”

### The “Transcendent Appearance” of the Voice

The “transcendence” of the voice over every other signifying substance holds to the fact that the signified (*Bedeutung*) is immediately present to the act of expression. This immediacy rests on the seeming self-effacement of the phonetic signifier at the very moment it is produced: it seems to belong already to the element of ideality, to reduce its own worldly opacity into pure transparency. “This effacement of the sensible body and its exteriority is *for consciousness* the very form of the immediate presence of the signified.”

But this “transcendence,” Derrida warns, “is only apparent.” This “appearance” is the essence of consciousness and its history.

### Auto-Affection and Différance

Auto-affection through the voice supposed that a *pure difference* (*différance*) came to divide self-presence. In this pure différance is rooted the possibility of everything one thought one could exclude from auto-affection: space, the outside, the world, the body. “Once one admits that auto-affection is the condition of self-presence, no pure transcendental reduction is possible.”

This movement of *différance* “does not befall a transcendental subject. It *produces* it.” Auto-affection is not a modality of experience characterizing a being that is already itself (*autos*). “It produces the *same* as the relation to self in the difference from self, the same as the non-identical.”

The temporalization that constitutes the living present is itself “auto-affection” in which the impression originaire — the “point-source” — “affects itself, without empirical resource, with a new originary actuality in which it will become a non-now.” This auto-affection is pure because nothing external affects the originary impression. And yet it introduces *impurity* into presence: “The living present springs forth from its non-identity to itself, and from the possibility of the retentional trace.”

“The *self* of the living present is originally a *trace*.” “One must think being-originary from the trace and not the reverse.”

### Spacing and the Ruin of Interiority

Since temporalization is from the outset “spacing” — interval, difference, opening to the outside — “there is no longer any absolute interiority.” Space is “in” time, the pure going-outside-itself of time. The theme of a pure interiority of speech or of “hearing-oneself-speak” is “radically contradicted by ‘time’ itself.”

### The Originary Supplement

Expression does not come to “add itself” as a layer to the presence of a pre-expressive sense. Indication does not accidentally affect the inside of expression. “Their *Verflechtung* is originary”; it is not a contingent association. “If indication does not add itself to expression which does not add itself to sense, one can nevertheless speak of ‘originary supplement’ (*supplément originaire*): their addition comes to *supply a lack*, a *non-self-presence* that is originary.”

## CHAPTER VII: THE SUPPLEMENT OF ORIGIN (*Le supplément d’origine*) (pp. 98–117)

### Supplementarity as Différance

The supplementarity thus understood *is* *différance*: the operation of differing that simultaneously fissures and delays presence, “submitting it at once to originary division and delay.” *Différance* must be thought *before* the separation between differing-as-delay and differing-as-active-work-of-difference. It is “unthinkable” from the standpoint of consciousness (presence) or its opposite (absence, non-consciousness).

The structure of the “supplement” is strange: “a possibility produces belatedly what it is said to add itself to.” The *for-itself* (*für sich*) of self-presence, traditionally determined in its dative dimension as auto-donation, arises in the movement of supplementarity as substitution: the *for-self* would be an *in-the-place-of-self*.

### The Husserlian Separation of Intention and Intuition

Derrida now turns to the last and most audacious “reduction” in the First Investigation: the exclusion from expression, as “inessential components,” of the acts of cognitive intuition that “fulfill” the *vouloir-dire*. The *Bedeutungsintention* — the intention that gives the *Bedeutung* — is always the aiming-at of a relation to the object. But it suffices that this intention animate the body of a signifier for discourse to take place. Fulfillment by intuition is not indispensable. “It belongs to the originary structure of expression to be able to do without the full presence of the object aimed at in intuition.”

Derrida traces the remarkable consequences: the ideality of the *Bedeutung* and the non-coincidence between expression, *Bedeutung*, and object. Two identical expressions can have the same *Bedeutung* but a different object. Two different expressions can have different *Bedeutungen* but the same object. Two different expressions can have the same *Bedeutung* and the same object. Without these distinctions, no pure logical grammar would be possible.

### The Freedom of Language and the Telos of Truth

Husserl demonstrates, against the philosophical tradition, that speech remains speech even when it is false or contradictory, provided it obeys certain grammatical rules that are not immediately rules of knowledge. The distinction between *Widersinnigkeit* (absurdity of contradiction: “the circle is square” — intelligible but objectless) and *Sinnlosigkeit/Unsinn* (“Abracadabra,” “green is or” — not even discourse) is the basis of pure logical grammar.

Derrida shows that one could be tempted to argue that *vouloir-dire* essentially *excludes* the fullness of intuition — that its very structure is *Gegenstandslosigkeit* (absence of object given to intuition). When intuition and intention fuse in full presence, the specificity of language “effaces itself or melts its originality.”

But Husserl does not draw this consequence. The motif of “full presence” — the intuitionist imperative — continues to command the entire description “at a distance.” The true and authentic *vouloir-dire* is the *vouloir-dire-vrai* (“wanting to say truly”). The *eidos* is recaptured in the *telos*, and language is recaptured in knowledge.

### The Case of “I” and the Structural Necessity of Death

Derrida takes the personal pronoun *I* (*Je*) as a test case. Husserl classifies it among “essentially occasional” expressions: its *Bedeutung* shifts with each occasion, each speaker, each situation. In soliloquy, Husserl claims, the *Bedeutung* of *I* is “realized” in the immediate representation of one’s own personality. But Derrida argues: can’t I give sense to “I am” only by *implying*, as with every expression, the *possible absence* of the object of discourse — here, of myself? “When I say ‘I am’ to myself, this expression… has the status of discourse only if it is intelligible *in the absence of the object*, that is, here, of myself.”

“My death is structurally necessary to the pronouncement of ‘I’.” The *Bedeutung* “I am” or “I am alive” has its ideal identity only if it is not undermined by the possibility that I may be dead at the moment it functions. “The statement ‘I am alive’ is accompanied by my being-dead, and its possibility *requires* the possibility that I be dead.”

This is not an extraordinary tale by Poe — “it is the ordinary history of language.” The anonymity of the written “I,” the impropriety of “I write,” is, “contrary to what Husserl says, the ‘normal situation.’” The autonomy of *vouloir-dire* with respect to intuitive knowledge “has its norm in writing and the relation to death.”

### The Infinite Deferral

In its ideal value, the entire system of “essential distinctions” is a purely *teleological* structure. The possibility of distinguishing between sign and non-sign, indication and expression, ideality and non-ideality, etc. is “deferred to infinity” (*différée à l’infini*). These distinctions are “caught in the following aporia: *in fact, really*, they are never respected, Husserl admits it. *In right and ideally*, they efface themselves, since they live, as distinctions, only from the difference between right and fact, ideality and reality. *Their possibility is their impossibility.*”

### Closure

That Husserl always thought infinity as an “Idea in the Kantian sense” — as the indefinite of an “to infinity” — suggests he never derived difference from the plenitude of a parousia, of a full presence of a positive infinite. And yet “the entire phenomenological discourse is caught, as we have sufficiently seen, in the schema of a metaphysics of presence that exhausts itself tirelessly in trying to derive *différance*.”

Derrida closes with what amounts to a thesis statement for his entire early philosophical project: “We believe quite simply in absolute knowledge as the closure if not the end of history… The history of being as presence, as self-presence in absolute knowledge, as consciousness (of) self in the infinity of the parousia, this history is closed.” Because full presence, having a “vocation of infinity,” its accomplishment would be “the end of the infinite, which can only be the unity of the concept, of the logos, and of consciousness in a voice without *différance*.”

“A voice without *différance*, a voice without writing, is at once absolutely alive and absolutely dead.”

What begins “beyond” absolute knowledge requires “unheard-of thoughts” that seek themselves “through the memory of old signs.” And the final sentences invoke the infinite regress of the second epigraph — the gallery of paintings within paintings — and the conclusion: “contrary to what our desire cannot not be tempted to believe, the thing itself always slips away.”

“Contrary to the assurance that Husserl gives us a little further on, ‘the gaze’ cannot ‘remain.’”

## Summary of the Book’s Central Arguments

1. **Husserl’s distinction between expression and indication** is the foundation of his entire theory of meaning. Expression is linked to the *vouloir-dire*, to ideality, to the *Bedeutung* of logical discourse. Indication is linked to empirical existence, contingency, the body, communication, the relation to the other.
1. **Husserl’s proof that expression can exist without indication** depends on the thought experiment of “the solitary life of the soul” — interior monologue. In soliloquy, there is supposedly no need for indication because psychic acts are *immediately present* to the subject “*in the same instant*” (*im selben Augenblick*).
1. **Derrida’s counter-argument** proceeds on multiple fronts: (a) The structure of the sign as such (its necessary repeatability, its ideality) implies representation from the outset, making the distinction between “effective” and “represented” discourse untenable. (b) Husserl’s own analyses of time-consciousness show that the punctual “now” is always already fissured by retention and protention — by *non-presence*. The *Augenblick* is not indivisible. (c) The privilege of the voice — the medium in which expression supposedly achieves its purity — rests on an auto-affection that itself presupposes *différance*: the pure difference that divides self-presence. (d) Expression itself, even when “fulfilled” by intuition, structurally implies the *possible absence* of its object (and of its subject): “My death is structurally necessary to the pronouncement of *I*.”
1. **The conclusion** is that the “essential distinctions” on which phenomenology rests are caught in an aporia: in fact they are never respected; in right they efface themselves. Their possibility is their impossibility. The concepts of *sign*, *representation*, *supplement*, *trace*, *différance*, and *writing* designate what is “more originary” than phenomenological originarity itself — what makes presence possible by fissuring it from within.

## Key Terms

- ***Ausdruck*** — Expression (in Husserl’s technical sense: a sign charged with *Bedeutung*)
- ***Anzeichen*** — Indication (a sign that points but does not “mean” in the logical sense)
- ***Bedeutung*** — Meaning, *vouloir-dire* (what a discourse or a speaker “wants to say”)
- ***Verflechtung*** — Intertwining, entanglement (the factual interlocking of indication and expression)
- ***Kundgabe / Kundnahme*** — Manifestation / apprehension of manifestation (the communicative function)
- ***Vergegenwärtigung*** — Re-presentation, presentification (reproductive, as opposed to originary presentation)
- ***Gegenwärtigung / Präsentation*** — Presentation (originary, perceptual)
- ***Augenblick*** — Instant, blink of an eye (the punctual now of self-presence)
- ***Différance*** — Derrida’s neologism: the operation of differing that is at once *temporal delay* and *spatial differentiation*, constitutive of and prior to presence
- ***Supplément*** — Supplement: what adds itself to something supposedly complete, thereby revealing that the thing was never complete; originary supplementarity
- ***Trace*** — The quasi-originary mark of non-presence within presence; “more originary” than the originary
- ***Lebendige Gegenwart*** — Living present (Husserl’s term for the ultimate form of temporality and self-presence)
- ***Phonè*** — Voice (the element in which the metaphysics of presence achieves its fullest expression)
