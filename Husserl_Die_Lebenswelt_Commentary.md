# Edmund Husserl, *Die Lebenswelt: Auslegungen der vorgegebenen Welt und ihrer Konstitution* (Husserliana XXXIX)

## Paragraph-by-Paragraph Commentary

---

## I. The Pre-Givenness of the World and Pre-Given World-Components (*Die Vorgegebenheit der Welt und vorgegebener Weltbestände*)

---

### Text Nr. 1. Primal Institution of the Sense of Being of Pre-Given Individual Beings and of the Pre-Given Existing World (*Urstiftung des Seinssinnes von vorgegebenem Einzelseienden und von vorgegebener seiender Welt*)

*[Early 1920s]*

**[Content summary]** Husserl provides a brief table of contents for this text: the concept of the pre-givenness of "beings" — "for" the cognizing subject. And in the naturalness of conscious life, things, human beings, etc. that are for us, altogether a world that is for us.

**[§, p. 1]** Husserl opens with the fundamental phenomenological situation: something "existing" (*Seiendes*) is there for me in thematic acts, i.e. given. This means that I, as the grasping ego, am directed toward it in an act in the specific sense; in the carrying out of the act it is for me in *actual validity* (*aktuelle Geltung*). The belief in its being is a living, actively performed belief radiating from the ego-pole. When I turn to another object — perhaps one like a number or a theorem that I only have while I am producing it — the object I was just engaging with is only "still" in my grasp: it remains in my actual thematic sphere, I am still with it. So long as this holds, we speak of thematic acts and distinguish modifications of enactment, modifications of the actuality of the act. But once I lose the object from my grasp altogether, it still retains its validity, though no longer in thematic form. *The first validity institutes a horizon of continuing validity* (*Fortgeltung*). In this lies the possibility of acts that have the character of reactivations of the posits of earlier acts, with the possible evidence of still-believing, still-judging, still-being-resolved, and so on.

This opening paragraph establishes the genetic-constitutive framework for the entire text. Husserl is analysing how "being" — the fact that something "is" for us — is not a static given but rather a product of instituting acts and their sedimented horizons of continuing validity. The key move is the recognition that an initial act of positing creates not merely a momentary awareness but an enduring claim on being. Even when attention shifts away, the object retains its validity, and this continuing validity is itself a structural feature of consciousness: it is not an arbitrary possibility but a *fundamental property of conscious life* (*Grundeigenschaft des Bewusstseinslebens*), as Husserl says at the start of the next paragraph. The terms *Urstiftung* (primal institution) and *Nachstiftung* (subsequent institution) are introduced to capture this temporal-genetic structure.

**[§, p. 2, first paragraph]** Husserl now introduces the crucial complication: there are not only acts that reveal themselves, through intentional explication of their horizons, to be continuations (*Fortgeltungen*) of earlier primal validities, but also acts that are not themselves such continuations yet harbour within them continuations of other, *similar* acts. And there are also acts that present themselves as primal-instituting insofar as they offer something "new" — something that is there for us not in the mode of a continuation. All of our outer experiences of objects never before encountered are, as new experiences, "primal-instituting." But in another sense they are *not*. Husserl illustrates this with a striking example: seeing a palm tree for the first time influences the seeing of future palm trees that have themselves never been seen. They are individually unknown, yet "something," something "known." Already in the first glimpse of the new, we have a sense-schema (*Sinnesschema*) of what we can expect as perception progresses and acquaintance deepens. In the new validity, as validity of its sense, the old validity is simultaneously alive, with its old sense overlaying (*sich überschiebend*) the newly forming sense from its initial stage onward. In this covering-over, the new sense takes shape as sense with anticipatory content, and the anticipation in turn fulfils itself more or less completely, modifying itself in the process.

This is one of Husserl's most important analyses of the structure of typification and apperceptive transfer. Every genuinely new experience is "primal-instituting" with respect to the individual object, but it is never absolutely new with respect to its type. Prior experience has always already deposited a sense-schema — a typifying anticipation — that shapes how the new is apprehended from its very first moment. The "overlay" or "covering" (*Deckung*) of old sense over new sense is the mechanism by which the life-world maintains its coherence: the new is always already interpreted in light of the familiar. Husserl identifies this process as "interpretation" (*Interpretation*), but explicitly not arbitrary interpretation — rather the unfolding of an evidently demonstrable intentionality. All intentional analysis, all self-clarification of consciousness that finds expression in "description," is itself interpretation; in all its evidence it is *evident interpretation*, and thus originally right-bestowing.

**[§, p. 3, first paragraph]** Husserl now traces the analysis back further: *every* "apperception" — whether of a palm tree, a table, a human being, a thing in general as spatial-corporeal — has its origin, its primal institution according to its *type*. Had I never originally seen a thing, never originally gained its sense, I could not apprehend every new thing "from the outset" as a thing; I would have to constitute its objective sense originally anew each time, and could not, for each object I encounter, have a typical sense-horizon that I need only pursue, only determine individually (filling out a particular form, as it were).

Here Husserl universalizes the point about the palm tree: the type-structure of apperception applies to every level of objectivity. The concept of *Urstiftung nach ihrem Typus* (primal institution according to type) means that the first encounter with an entity of a given kind establishes a template — an apperceptive type — that all subsequent encounters of that kind presuppose. Without such typifying primal institutions, every experience would have to begin from absolute zero, which is constitutively impossible for a consciousness that has any history at all.

**[§, p. 3, second paragraph]** Husserl notes that such horizons signify *motivated* (and, in interpretive unfolding, evidently motivated) *act-possibilities*, with possible "what is coming," when we activate the horizon-consciousness (which is thus a kind of reactivation) and thereby transform it into motivated expectation.

This brief but important paragraph makes explicit that the horizons surrounding every apperception are not empty or arbitrary but *motivated*: they carry the force of experiential precedent. The activation of a horizon is a reactivation of sedimented sense, and the expectations it generates are expectations with evidential warrant — they are motivated by the entire history of experience of that type.

**[§, p. 3, third paragraph]** Husserl adds a further observation: although there is, in a pre-thematic sphere of *original passivity*, already something like a pre-constitution of sense-connections, nonetheless "being," objectuality that "is there" — first grasped and then in reach as something possibly to be grasped, as ungrasped, unthematic "being" (existing without being "conscious," without being thematic) — is a title that *has sense only on the basis of primal-instituting acts*, and primal-instituting in a double sense. The one (primordial) sense expresses the genetically first giving of an object in genetic primal institution. The other expresses the taking-cognizance of something new, even if of an unfamiliar kind.

This paragraph introduces a crucial distinction between two senses of *Urstiftung*: the primordial, which is the absolutely first constitution of an object of a given type in the genesis of a subject, and the secondary, which is the first encounter with a new individual of an already familiar type. Husserl insists that the very title "being" — the fact that something counts as an existent — has its sense only from such primal-instituting acts. Even what is merely passively pre-constituted does not count as "being" in the full sense until active ego-engagement has occurred at some point in the genetic history.

**[§, p. 3, fourth paragraph]** Finally, Husserl notes that for us as subjects of a natural life, not only things we see are there (each with its horizon of familiarity regarding unseen but expected reverse sides, a mixture of determinate sense and horizon-sense), and not only those unseen things we have seen and still hold in validity, but rather in every waking moment of life a whole *world* is there for us, reaching into the infinities of space and time and shaped in its individual detail according to real-categorial typification.

This paragraph effects the transition from the constitution of individual objects to the constitution of the world as a whole. Every individual thing, with its inner and outer horizons, is always already embedded in the total horizon "world." The world is not merely the sum of things experienced; it is the infinite, typified totality that is co-intended in every act of experience. Its categorial typification — the fact that it is structured according to types like "thing," "animal," "person," "city," etc. — is itself a product of the sedimentation of primal institutions.

**[§, pp. 3–4]** Every thing, every group of things, as it is experienced, has in terms of consciousness an inner horizon and an outer horizon.

This transitional sentence prepares the move to the next major thought: the correlation between object-horizons and the ego's capacities.

**[§, p. 4, first long paragraph]** However constitutive analysis may proceed here — describing and clarifying the genesis by tracing back to the essential primal institutions and their fundamental forms — it is certain that the title "world," namely the world that is valid for us, is a *title for actual and possible ego-acts*. The possibility here points on the one hand to earlier institution by ego-acts in this specific sense, and on the other to a free activity of disclosing horizons, to the capacity (*Vermögen*) of subjects, to their evident "I can go on experiencing." I can make what was emptily pre-thought intuitive, can transform pre-intuition into experiencing intuition, and must then establish the determinate, unknown things and events, transforming the indeterminate and anticipatory horizon-validity into determinate, self-grasping experiential validity. Even if I do not actually do what I can do — set my body in motion as perceptual organ, run through the actual kinaestheses to which traversed spatial directions correspond, and thereby set in play the series of appearances through which things come to my knowledge in their self-being — I can nonetheless at any time represent to myself the *horizon-sense of the world* in pre-image (*Vor-Bild*): I can think in phantasy that I would go on experiencing, and form a picture — indeed infinitely many equally possible pictures — of how it would or could come. However it would come, what came would necessarily correspond to the horizon-sense: corporeal things, animals, human beings, cities with utility-objects, etc. To this structure of a world always only partially genuinely experienced and represented through horizons in its infinity belong ego-acts of all kinds, including necessarily reflexive ones.

This is one of the most important paragraphs in the text. Husserl establishes the fundamental correlation between the world and the ego's capacities. The world is not a thing that exists independently of all subjectivity; it is constitutively a "title" for the totality of actual and possible ego-acts. The key concept here is the *Könnenshorizont* — the horizon of "I can" — which correlates with the objective horizon. Every object-horizon is nothing without its correlative capacity-horizon. Husserl's footnote makes this even more explicit: the subjective "I can" is a systematically unified totality of possible capacities, a unified *domain of power* (*Machtbereich*), and the objective horizon in its possibilities is nothing without this correlative capacity-horizon in the well-formed and consciously functioning capacity. The passage also introduces the role of phantasy in disclosing the world's horizon-sense: even without actually carrying out experience, I can imaginatively project what further experience would yield, and what it would yield necessarily conforms to the structural typification of the world.

**[§, pp. 4–5]** One more thought is missing. As soon as a "being" has an outer horizon, it is not posited alone; rather, other beings are co-posited. If it necessarily has an outer horizon, it is *unselbständig* (non-self-sufficient); it carries co-determinations in reference to what it is not itself. One can then also say: all beings are relative; each is only in relation to others. From the side of positing: all positing of being is at the same time "positing-in-relation." But "positing in relation" does not mean: judgmentally constituting relations as objects in synthetic, thematic acts and producing relational predicates; rather it means: carrying out hidden co-positings of determinate or indeterminate objectualities in the form of horizons, and thus having them in consciousness with such noematic sense that *afterwards* judgments, explicating the horizon, can find connections and relations.

This paragraph draws out a fundamental ontological consequence of the horizon-structure: no being is self-sufficient. Every being, by virtue of its outer horizon, is necessarily co-posited with other beings. This is not a matter of explicit relational judgment but of the implicit intentional structure of all experience. The horizon is a field of implicit co-positings that precede and make possible all explicit relational predication. Husserl is thus providing a phenomenological ground for the insight that all worldly being is essentially relational — not because we judge it to be so, but because the very sense-constitution of any worldly entity involves horizonal reference to what it is not.

**[§, p. 5, second paragraph]** The sense-constitution of the world — the totality of what is to be given in natural experience — is such that it knows no self-sufficient sense-formation (*selbständige Sinnbildung*): everything ever experienceable carries its *relational horizon* with it, through which it continually has relational characters that are to be transformed into relational positings and explicit relational predications.

This brief paragraph restates the point at the level of the world as a whole: the sense-constitution of the world as totality is inherently relational. No element of the world can form sense in isolation.

**[§, p. 5, third paragraph]** When therefore natural reflection — specifically *self-reflection* — occurs, it posits consciousness as something worldly, and hence not as something isolated but precisely as something real, i.e. non-self-sufficient, in accordance with its own sense. If we take real consciousness universally, it is a universal stratum of the world, founded in nature. Does it make sense to want to posit consciousness otherwise than with the natural sense of life? Here one might say: only if the natural attitude with its universal world-positing could be inhibited and yet not nothing remained, but rather again consciousness — this consciousness would then no longer be worldly consciousness and no longer non-self-sufficient "being." Now, Descartes already attempted to demonstrate the absolute self-sufficiency of the validity-of-being of the ego cogito through "doubting" the world — without, to be sure, having understood and consistently maintained the sense and value of his epoch-making beginning. He gave us at any rate a prejudgment, a premonition of a life that is itself and in which the world is something intended, something valid in it.

This paragraph effects the transition from the analysis of the life-world to the transcendental question. Natural self-reflection discovers consciousness as worldly — as a real, dependent stratum of nature. But the Cartesian insight suggests the possibility of grasping consciousness in a way that breaks free of this worldliness. Husserl acknowledges Descartes's achievement — the discovery of the ego cogito as that which survives the universal doubt — while criticizing Descartes for failing to understand what he had actually accomplished. The "double edition" of consciousness — as worldly and as transcendentally pure — is the fundamental problem that phenomenology must address.

**[§, p. 6]** But how can consciousness exist in a double edition? What is purity supposed to mean? In the ego cogito we grasp consciousness purely as itself, before all analysis and subsequent description. So one might think. But these are dangerous paths of thought.

This terse, cautionary final paragraph of Text Nr. 1 warns against a naïve reading of the Cartesian reduction. The idea that we simply "grasp" consciousness purely as itself in the ego cogito, before all analysis, is a temptation that Husserl explicitly flags as dangerous. The path to transcendental consciousness requires far more careful methodological work than a simple appeal to the self-evidence of the cogito. The text breaks off here, leaving the problem open — characteristically for Husserl's research manuscripts.

### Text Nr. 2. Background and Pre-Givenness. Differences of Pre-Givenness. Prominence and the Pregnant Concept of Pre-Givenness. The Constitutive Function of Apperceptions (*Hintergrund und Vorgegebenheit. Unterschiede der Vorgegebenheit. Abgehobenheit und prägnanter Begriff von Vorgegebenheit. Die konstitutive Funktion der Apperzeptionen*)

*[ca. 1920]*

**[Content summary]** Husserl identifies the great difficulty in the concept of the "pre-phenomenality" of the anonymous "stream of experience" and the nature of its "being," including the intertwined concepts of "pre-givenness," "background-givenness," "apperception," "reflection," and "function."

**[§, p. 7, first paragraph]** Reflection on a sensation-datum functioning in the process of adumbration — and indeed on a sensation-datum that perhaps carries no such function or is apprehended in some other way as related to the objective world — shows that it is constituted as a temporally enduring unity in a synthesis, that it is itself an "objectuality," something representational. It is an "intentional objectuality," an immanent one, originally constituted, here functioning in a certain way for a "transcendent apperception," an apperception in the pregnant sense.

Husserl begins this text by plunging into one of the deepest problems of constitutive phenomenology: the status of sensation-data (*Empfindungsdaten*). Even a sensation-datum — seemingly the most primitive building block of consciousness — turns out, upon reflection, to be already constituted as a temporal unity in a synthesis. It is not a bare "given" but an intentional objectuality, a product of the immanent time-constituting flow. In its "functioning" for transcendent apperception (the constitution of worldly objects), it plays a mediating role that must not be confused with simple material passively lying there.

**[§, p. 7, second paragraph]** The concrete unity of a sensation-datum equals the unity of a *prominence* (*Abhebung*). But we can attend to its duration and to vague content-moments of the duration that are not initially prominent but that we can, even if vaguely, "without sharp boundaries," bring to prominence — whether actively or already in passivity, as when a short-lasting tone enters alongside a longer-lasting one and we can then notice a segment of the longer tone equal to the shorter. This would not be possible without a certain vague, prominence-producing coincidence (*Deckung*).

This paragraph introduces *Abhebung* — prominence or standing-out — as a fundamental constitutive concept. The unity of a sensation-datum is a unity of prominence: it stands out from its surroundings. But within any prominent datum, further internal differentiations are possible — latent content-moments can be brought to prominence through associative comparison (as in the tone example). The mechanism is *Deckung* — coincidence or overlapping — which is the basic operation of association: the shorter tone "covers" a segment of the longer one, making that segment experientially available.

**[§, p. 8, first paragraph]** Within a self-enclosed unity of hyle there lie potentialities for divisions, and therewith divisions of the constitutive manifold.

A brief but important remark: every concrete hyletic unity harbours potentialities for further analysis. It is not a simple atom but an internally complex formation whose parts can, under the right conditions, be disclosed.

**[§, p. 8, second long paragraph]** Husserl returns from the sensation-datum as "object" to its constitutive manifolds and recognizes that everything that can be attended to, and must be assumed as already pre-phenomenal when subsequently noticed, is likewise a unity of constituting manifolds. It is indeed an "object" in the sense of a "unity," but not therefore an object in the sense of something actively identified and thereby pre-given. This raises on the one hand the problem of "inner consciousness" (*inneres Bewusstsein*), and on the other the problem of the different *levels* of objectualities, indicating a step-wise "functioning" of objectualities as *relative hyle* for other objectualities — and perhaps also, what may need to be distinguished, a functioning of deposited "experiences" as already constituted "unities" in inner consciousness, within the unity of individual-subjective and intersubjective life as personal life.

This paragraph lays out the stratified architecture of constitution. Every level of objectuality serves as "relative hyle" — relative material — for a higher level. Sensation-data are hyle for the constitution of appearances; appearances are hyle for the constitution of things; and so on upward. The problem of "inner consciousness" — the consciousness that constitutes immanent unities prior to all reflection — is identified as a fundamental problem that intertwines with the problem of levels. Husserl also hints at a further complication: the functioning of experiences as constituted unities *within* the personal life-context, where they play roles in individual and intersubjective constitution.

**[§, p. 8, third paragraph]** A chief role is played by the thoroughgoing distinction between *primary*, properly so called objectualities — as self-sufficiently prominent and already constituted in active identification — and *secondary objectualities*, which can perhaps come to prominence as parts or moments in primary ones, from subsequent motives (association), but need not; thus implicit objectualities that do not have prominence from the outset but carry with them potentialities for prominence.

This paragraph formalizes the distinction between two fundamental kinds of objectual unity: primary objectualities, which have achieved full prominence and active identification, and secondary or implicit objectualities, which are latently contained within primary ones and may or may not ever come to prominence. The distinction maps onto the difference between what is explicitly given and what is implicitly (and potentially) given — a distinction that will be central to the entire analysis of pre-givenness.

**[§, p. 8, fourth paragraph]** Where a primary objectuality functions for the constitution of a new primary or secondary objectuality, it is called "pre-given" in a first sense: it is not mere potentiality; it is from the outset actually there as something one can look toward.

This paragraph gives the first, broadest definition of "pre-givenness" (*Vorgegebenheit*): something is pre-given when it is an already constituted unity that functions for the constitution of something further. It is not a mere latent potentiality but something actually available for attention.

**[§, p. 9, first two paragraphs]** But subsequently a great difference shows itself within this pre-givenness, one that forces a more pregnant concept. The mere "being-able-to-look-at" (*Hinsehenkönnen*) has specifications. When I live into the spatial world, constituted for me in its multi-levelled way, I occupy myself with objects that have attracted my attention, my practical or theoretical interest. But in natural going-along-living (in natural attitude), worldly objects in general are *privileged* for me; they determine the associations, and associative awakening (*Weckung*) always proceeds from what is already prominent and worldly to what is worldly. From the outset, my surrounding world is an associative nexus progressing in awakening from worldly-objective to worldly-objective. Eventually even everything "merely subjective" — all my experiences and the objectual unities constituted therein — is worldlicized (*verweltlicht*). But this points to the fact that there are levels of awakening and directions of awakening that can be sought out and found. *Earlier and later pre-givennesses separate themselves* — body, own-body and other's body, ego-person, ego-human, etc. But pre-given in a certain secondary sense, relative to all naturally pre-given worldliness, is everything that phenomenology newly opens up, creating a new pre-givenness that in the proper sense was none before.

This passage introduces the crucial narrowing of the concept of pre-givenness. In the natural attitude, worldly objects enjoy an absolute privilege: all associative awakening moves from world to world. The subjective dimension — experiences, sensations, constitutive processes — is "worldlicized," absorbed into the world-framework. The phenomenological attitude reverses this by making the constituting subjectivity itself a field of pre-givenness — but a pre-givenness that is "secondary" relative to the natural order and that requires special methodological motivation to bring into view.

**[§, p. 10, first paragraph]** It lies in the nature of the multi-levelled constitution of things as spatial things — in their multi-levelled mediacy — that reflection cannot be ready simply to skip arbitrary levels. The *nearest reflection* leads to the immediate constitutive connections: for things, to the modes of side-givenness and orientation according to near and far, to perspective, to the functioning bodiliness as perceptual bodiliness. Only then can higher reflection take place. In the direction of the gaze upon perspectives, associations are then effective that bring, for instance, sensation-colours to prominence. Standing in the sphere of "sensation" (a sphere through association), I can go back to its constitution in original time-consciousness. In every experience, the object is from the outset the selfsame, a unity, in a manifold How. This is a distinctive associative field; accordingly, what is next-constitutive of this object as a unity of manifolds is privileged. Thus we have, in this sense, pre-givenness in mediacies, in *ordered levels of relativity*. Up to now, "pre-givenness" has meant everything whatsoever demonstrable in the background, i.e. constituted as a unity.

This paragraph introduces the principle that constitutive reflection must follow the order of constitutional levels: one cannot skip from the worldly object directly to its deepest constitutive origins but must proceed through the intermediate levels — perspectives, kinaesthetic functions, sensation-data, time-consciousness — in order. Each level forms a distinctive associative field that privileges the next-lower constitutive stratum. The concept of *Vorgegebenheit* is thus shown to have an ordered, level-relative character: what counts as "pre-given" depends on which constitutive level one occupies.

**[§, p. 10, second paragraph]** As that which is *given* for me, Husserl designates what I have in a grasping grip, what I examine, explicate, determine, what I engage with in feeling and in resolving and acting will. Given to me can only be something *pre-given*, something that, thanks to a preceding constitution, is ready for perception or ready for experience — something that *can affect me* as something already existing before attention. This much is nothing new. Since the temporally preceding primal constitution already functioned in earlier similar cases, every later one eo ipso has the "character of familiarity" (*Charakter der Bekanntheit*) and from the outset, wherever its founding "material" presents itself, creates "apperception." It is from the outset "apprehended" with the sense of the earlier, and what is similar is apprehended in a similar sense. To that extent this concerns a general fact of associative genesis.

This paragraph establishes the foundational principle that all givenness presupposes pre-givenness: nothing can be given to me in an active grasp unless it was already there for me in a pre-constituted, pre-attentive manner. The mechanism is apperception — the transfer of sense from earlier to later experiences through associative genesis. Familiarity (*Bekanntheit*) is the basic deposit of all prior constitutive activity, and it is this familiarity that makes possible the immediate, "from the outset" character of our engagement with the world.

**[§, p. 11, first paragraph]** There is a difference between immanent and transcendent constitutions — between those that originally and genetically must play out in pure passivity and according to original and rigid lawfulness (like the constitution of immanent data) and transcendent constitutions, where ego-activity and also productive ego-activity play a genetically constitutive role. Things are for us always "constituted" as background-things. To be sure, there is no essential difference between sensation-data and transcendent objects insofar as for both, repetition of an active grasping and examining founds familiarity and thereby "apperception." A tonal formation can later be apperceived in passivity as a formation of an already instituted style. But then, before and alongside such constitution resting on active grasping, we would have a constitution *without* apperception.

Husserl distinguishes two fundamentally different constitutive registers: immanent constitution, governed by rigid passive lawfulness (the constitution of time, sensation-data), and transcendent constitution, where the ego's activity plays an essential role (the constitution of spatial things). The distinction matters because transcendent objects are *always* already apperceived — they appear as "background-things" from the start — whereas immanent data can, in their first genesis, be constituted without apperception. Husserl's footnote clarifies: what is constituted out of pure passivity does not count as "pre-given" in the sense of something "already there" to which one merely turns; that status requires apperception. Transcendent things are always pre-given in this pregnant sense; immanent data are not.

**[§, p. 11, second paragraph]** An *object* is from the outset only constituted through apperception — namely, this "finished" object is only fully constituted in the form of apperceptive perception. Genetically we indeed arrive at a layered structure and ultimately at sensation-data. But only through apperceptive formations built from sensation-data, and apperceptive formations built from these formations, does thing-perceiving arise, and in it the "thing" as an apperceptive unity. In the immanent sphere, prominence and association indeed take place, but they create nothing new. What constitutes itself there is the repetition of what was already constituted in the same way before, except that it is now quasi "familiar" and through anticipation already has, in advance, the sense-shape that it first gained in its initial constitution.

This paragraph drives home the point that full objecthood requires apperception. A "finished" object — a thing with its properties, its identity through manifolds — is constituted only through the apperceptive synthesis that builds upward from sensation-data through intermediate formations. In the immanent sphere, by contrast, there is association and prominence but no genuine novelty of object-constitution: the immanent datum simply repeats its earlier form, now tinged with familiarity.

**[§, pp. 11–12]** In thing-constitution (the transcendent kind), it is first the apperception — which includes anticipation — that, on the basis of already constituted sensation-data, makes possible novel constitutive syntheses. Here the remarkable associations in which *kinaestheses* function not as mere immanent data but as *practical capacities* ("I can turn myself this way and that") associate into a practical system and function as a motivational system for corresponding actual and possible appearances, in which in turn optima constitute themselves as practically available through corresponding kinaestheses (which presuppose graspings of the ego). Here "apperception" gains a new sense: a unity of practically available presenting appearances-of; here the apperceived object is *related to the waking ego of a praxis*, and essentially belonging to the constitution are moments of "I can," of "if I actualize thus, then such and such must appear," etc.

This is a crucial passage connecting the analysis of apperception to the theory of kinaesthetic constitution familiar from *Ding und Raum*. The constitution of spatial things is not merely a passive synthesis of appearances but involves the ego's practical bodily capacities. Kinaestheses — the experienced movements of eyes, hands, body — are not mere immanent data but *practical capacities* organized into a motivational system: "if I move thus, then such and such appears." Apperception in the transcendent sphere thus essentially involves the "I can" of bodily praxis. The apperceived thing is constituted as a unity of practically available appearances, and the ego's relation to the thing is always a practical, bodily relation.

**[§, p. 12, continuation]** Since a universe of potentiality is constitutive here, and actual givenness can only have the form of the actualization of a possibility or series of possibilities, it is evident that perception (in the special sense of apperceiving perception) points back to earlier apperceptive perception. In genesis, every new level arises through original constitution, but it already presupposes, in order to be able to become constitutive, the pre-givenness of the earlier level. Ideally only the mere sensation-level is excepted. But perceiving is not originally-genetically constituting; it is straightforward grasping and having in the flesh; it is the fulfilment of a prefiguration (*Vorzeichnung*) that must already be there and must have been tried out in freely repeated fulfilment. Coloured things as existing I have only where all that lies behind.

The paragraph concludes by drawing the consequence: since transcendent constitution always involves a universe of practical possibilities, every act of perception is itself *founded* on prior apperceptive perception. The genesis of objectivity is thus layered: each level presupposes the pre-givenness of the one below. Only the absolute bottom level — pure sensation — is in principle excepted. But even perceiving, in its actual occurrence, is not the original genetic constituting; it is rather the fulfilment of an already existing pre-figuration. To have coloured things as "existing" already presupposes the entire layered history of constitutive achievement.

### Supplement I. Background — Pre-Given Objectuality. End-Object and Transitional Unities (*Hintergrund – vorgegebene Gegenständlichkeit. Endgegenstand und Durchgangseinheiten*)

*[ca. 1920]*

**[§, p. 13, first paragraph]** Husserl begins with the basic distinction: the ego-gaze, the direction toward the "theme" (in one sense), toward the meant "object" (the objective sense). Over against this: what does not lie in the ego-gaze, what is not directed toward.

This sets up the fundamental phenomenological topology: theme versus non-theme, what is in the gaze versus what is not.

**[§, p. 13, second paragraph]** *Noticing* (*Gewahren*): in noticing I am "directed" toward the *appearing thing* — not toward the modes of appearance, not toward the sensation-data functioning for the apprehension, not toward the kinaesthetically functioning data. I am further not directed toward the *objective background*, which appears together with my grasped "thing" as a unity of the present perceptual field — not as a heap of individual appearing things, but as a unity of a total appearance in which the individual appearing things are in a certain way oriented "around my body."

This paragraph introduces the phenomenological concept of the "background" in its proper sense: the objective background is not merely an aggregate of discrete items but a unified field of appearing things, organized around the body as centre of orientation. The gaze terminates in the theme-object; everything else belongs to the background, which has its own kind of unity — an orientational, body-centred unity.

**[§, pp. 13–14, "We must distinguish..."]** We must distinguish *direction of gaze in general* (*Blickrichtung überhaupt*) from *direction toward the terminus ad quem* — that is, the "object" with everything that constitutes it, however complex the objective sense may be. Much is in the gaze in another mode, the ego-ray goes toward many things that are not in the gaze in the pregnant first sense, not as terminus ad quem. The kinaesthetic data are necessarily co-functioning, and I have the gaze directed toward them (or toward eye-movement, hand-movement, already apperceived bodily), but not as "my objects." As they run off actively from the ego (in "I move"), the "appearances" arise, motivated by them, toward which I direct the gaze — but again not as the end. My gaze goes *through* the kinaestheses to the kinaesthetically motivated and *through them to their unity*. And there I am "at the thing itself."

This extraordinarily important passage distinguishes between two radically different modes of directedness: the gaze that *terminates* in an object (terminus ad quem) and the gaze that *passes through* functioning data. Kinaestheses, sensations, and appearances are all "in the gaze" in some sense — we are not utterly unconscious of them — but they are not end-objects; they are *transitional unities* (*Durchgangseinheiten*) through which the gaze passes on its way to the thing itself. The same structure applies to signs: the gaze passes through the sign's sensible shape to the meaning. This distinction between Durchgang (transit) and Ende (terminus) is fundamental to Husserl's understanding of how the constitutive layers of experience are lived through in the natural attitude.

**[§, pp. 14–15, "First..."]** The pregnant and alone fitting concept of background is "objective background." Background is not merely what is not in the gaze and not the terminus — as privation; background is a domain of objectualities "constituted" in a particular and peculiar way, as objects "pre-given," "apperceptively" conscious. "Background" here means: "in the field of view" (*Blickfeld*), but not in the gaze of apprehension, not in the gaze that "rests" on it, that terminates there. What lies in the field of view can draw the terminating gaze "without further ado"; what is already perceptually ready in the field of view, already implicitly conscious as an end-goal but not yet actualized, becomes actualized. The object affects as object.

This paragraph refines the concept of "background" from a mere privation (not being attended to) into a positive constitutive concept. Background objects are not merely "not noticed"; they are *pre-given* in a specific apperceptive mode — they are already constituted as objects that could at any moment attract the terminating gaze. The background is thus a field of readiness, of latent thematic availability. The objectual character of the background is essential: what is in the background affects me *as an object*, drawing me toward potential engagement.

**[§, p. 15, "Secondly..."]** From the properly so-called background of the given object, there are to be distinguished *data that are not objectual in this way*, that can become objectual under special motives (through reflection, phenomenological analysis: thematized and explicated, constituted as proper objectualities).

This adds the second layer: beneath or alongside the objective background lies the stratum of non-objectual data — sensations, kinaesthetic processes, constitutive functions — that are never normally objects at all but can, under special phenomenological motivation, be made thematic.

**[§, p. 15, "Thirdly..."]** Under the topic of "attention" we must distinguish: (1) what is in the gaze as that in which the gaze rests, toward which it goes in the normal sense, and (2) what is in the gaze but in such a way that the gaze passes through it as a "functioning" datum, entering into an apprehension or meaning interwoven with the datum.

This formalizes the Durchgang/Ende distinction within the theory of attention: attentional directedness has both terminal and transitional modes, and these must not be conflated.

**[§, pp. 15–16]** In the universal domain of consciousness, we have everywhere "objects" insofar as everything that belongs to it is a unity of constituting manifolds. Consciousness is not only, but it is *conscious*; its being is immanently constituted being, individually and for the entire "stream of consciousness." In this conscious life there separate themselves "aesthetically": (1) functioning data and their "functions," their "apprehensions" — they need not ever become thematic. (2) In the primordial material of consciousness there separate themselves primal-hyletic data and primal-hyletic "apprehensions," then at a higher level hyletic data and their apprehensions, and so on. (3) A genetic dimension: from becoming-known there arises familiarity. Familiarity creates an apperceptive type, creates objects that are conscious in the background even as "new" ones, creates perceptual fields, memory fields, etc. — eventually a thing-world, a world in the normal sense. But ideal worlds are also there for us. We have everywhere "horizons" and finally the universal horizon that encompasses everything valid for us, everything existing for us.

This sweeping paragraph provides a synoptic view of the entire constitutive architecture. Consciousness is through and through constituted — its very being is "immanently constituted being." Within this, Husserl identifies three fundamental dimensions: (1) the functional dimension of data and their apprehensions, (2) the stratified hyletic-noetic architecture running from primal hyle upward through multiple levels, and (3) the genetic dimension in which familiarity accumulates and creates the apperceptive types that structure the pre-given world. The passage culminates in the concept of the universal horizon — the totality of everything valid and existing for us — which stands as the ultimate constitutive achievement of this layered, genetic process.

**[§, pp. 17–18]** Husserl adds a further fundamental point regarding intentionality: the intentionality that makes appearances-of out of sensation-data, while these data are themselves constituted as intentional unities, is in the normal givenness of natural objects a *functioning intentionality* in which sensation-data are merely *transitional unities*. In genesis, all data must initially have been thematic, must have functioned as ends — but after the constitution of a world, they have lost this objectual character through constant exclusive functioning as transit. Where an object that has lost its thematic distinction functions as mere transit, there occurs in the actualization a *deflection* of the thematic gaze: the gaze is broken, reflected in a certain way — it does not remain with the mediating item but bends off toward the end-object. The mediating object is serviceable and has its serviceabilities; only those determinations are sought and grasped that carry the intentionality leading over to the end-object.

This passage introduces a genetic thesis about the origin of transitional functioning: in the earliest genesis, even what now functions as mere transit (sensation-data, kinaesthetic processes) must originally have been thematic, must have served as end-objects. Only through the habitual dominance of worldly interest did these data lose their objectual status and become permanently subordinated to the transit function. The structure of "deflection" (*Ablenkung*) — the breaking or bending of the gaze away from the mediating datum toward the end-object — is the phenomenological mechanism by which this subordination is lived.

**[§, p. 18, final paragraph]** The end-object, however, despite its character as terminus ad quem, has its opennesses, its horizons. As an objectuality it is nothing finished but "intended." In a certain way, the being-an-end and the being-a-transit reverse themselves: with the mediating item, we come to an end — the mediating item as such is fully constituted when everything called upon for serviceability has appeared and done its service. But when we are "at the thing itself," this means nothing other than: we live in an intentionality that carries in itself an endlessness of open, prefigured intentionality that is to be unfolded. Everything that emerges is end and at the same time transit for the actualization of what is new.

This beautiful concluding paragraph of Supplement I captures the paradoxical structure of experience: the mediating, transitional items are "finished" — they exhaust their service quickly — while the end-object, the thing itself, is infinitely open. To be "at the thing" is to live in an intentionality of endless further determination. Every achievement of knowledge is simultaneously an end (something gained) and a transit (a passage to further knowledge). The pre-given world is thus constitutively characterized by this infinite openness of its objects.

### Text Nr. 3. The World Pre-Given from Original Acquisition — Not So Consciousness, the Realm of Immanence (*Die Welt vorgegeben aus ursprünglicher Erwerbung – nicht so das Bewusstsein, das Reich der Immanenz*)

*[1921]*

**[§, p. 19, first paragraph]** The spatiotemporal world is constantly there for us, even when we do not attend to it. It is our habitual possession, even when our thoughts dwell in the ideal number-world. But the *immanent* "time" and "time-world" is *not* a world for us, not constantly *there* for us in such a way that we could just look toward it, perceive it; it is not constantly at our disposal for immediate access, constantly given according to individual things while the others are reachable from there. It is at any time actually constitutable as a world of existing objects, but not constantly so constituted.

Husserl opens with a stark contrast. The spatiotemporal world is always there as habitual possession — even inattention does not abolish it. But the immanent sphere of consciousness (the "stream of experience" with its immanent time) enjoys no such status. It is not a "world" for us in the same way; it is not constantly pre-given as a field of available objects. It *can* be constituted as such, but it is not so constituted in the natural course of life. This asymmetry between the world and immanence is fundamental: the world has a constitutive priority over the inner life, not because it is more "real" in some metaphysical sense, but because it is constitutively more "available."

**[§, p. 19, second paragraph]** How is this to be understood? From the very beginning of constitutive development — in which worlds and correlatively the ego as person constitute themselves — the immanent, the milieu of all constitution and in a certain way containing all its material, is constituted in passive unity, as mere unity of self-coincidence in the concrete present. But this passive unity is only *potentiality* for an actual, active constitution of objectualities as *identity-unities* that require free identification in their freely traversed milieu to be originally constituted, and that are firmly constituted only when the *habitus of free availability* of existence in free access and in free, originally traversable identification is founded. In this sense, nature-time, i.e. the object of nature-time, *precedes* immanent time, i.e. the object of immanent time; the outer world precedes the stream of experience. This is the "straightforward" givenness of the outer world.

This paragraph explains the asymmetry: immanent life is always there as the milieu of all constitution, but only in the mode of passive self-coincidence. To become a domain of *objects* — identity-unities available for free identification — it requires active constitutive work that, in the natural course of development, occurs *after* the constitution of the objective world. The world of nature "precedes" the immanent sphere in the constitutive order: we first have the world, and only then, through special reflective effort, can we constitute the immanent sphere as a field of objects. Husserl's footnote makes this even more explicit: the stream of experience as an immanent "world" with the form of infinite time is constituted only subsequently, from the standpoint of the already constituted objective world, and only phenomenologically.

**[§, pp. 20–21]** Difficulties remain. I *can* have the immanent experience at any time in memory, it is at my disposal — but only as phenomenologists and psychologists, and even then only insofar as we have actively constituted the immanent as existing. Before that, we have no constant and lasting possession in the genuine sense. The "I can" here does not mean straightforward disposal over the realization of a possession but rather: the possibility, starting from the already constituted objective world, of constituting my immanent sphere as existing and the stream of experience as a field of "existing objects." Even after this constitutive achievement, the immanent sphere is and remains not pre-given in the way the objective experiential world is. Even for the phenomenologist, the immanent sphere is not really objectually constituted in the full sense — it would be so only if we had been interested in fixing every immanent experience and getting to know it in repeated re-examination.

Husserl deepens the analysis of the asymmetry. Even the phenomenologist's access to immanence is derivative and incomplete. The "I can" of reflective access to immanence is not the same as the "I can" of worldly experience: the former is always mediated by, and dependent on, the already constituted objective world. Full objectual constitution of the immanent sphere — treating every experience as a known, identifiable, re-examinable object — is an ideal never actually realized.

**[§, p. 21, "Aber davon zunächst abgesehen..."]** In immanence there lies *no telos for the life of the ego*. Only the psychologist and epistemologist have an interest in immanence. The thing-world arises originally from some interest, is constituted as a lasting, always re-findable and utilisable unity and so becomes an acquisition (*Erwerb*). However the primal institution may be clarified, later the objects are, as unknown ones in the horizon of the already known, in a form that is itself known and prefigured as such. They thus demand further acquaintance. Immanent objects come into our momentary possession in repeated memories, in the consciousness "I can still always and ever again repeat."

This paragraph articulates a fundamental teleological point: the life-world is constituted because the ego has a *telos* — an interested directedness — toward the world. Worldly objects are constituted as *acquisitions* (*Erwerbe*) because they serve the ego's interests. The immanent sphere has no such natural telos: no one in the natural attitude is interested in the stream of consciousness as such. Only the specialized theoretical interests of the psychologist or phenomenologist generate a motivation to constitute immanence as a field of objects.

**[§, pp. 21–22, summary]** In summary: the world, mundane objectualities, are constitutive products of original acquisition. Originally acquired, they are acquired in acts of taking-cognizance (*Kenntnisnahmen*) through which they become lasting possession. They are lasting possession for subjects who, as interested, have acquired the "existents" in such a way that their interest becomes lasting and the instituted apperception is simultaneously a habituality of interest: like awakens like by awakening the interest directed at it (as an ego-side), thereby producing overlap, association, and "transfer" of interest. The genetically original acquisition of mundane objectuality is simultaneously one of original mundane apperception — not mere experience of individual objects but the founding of an analogical memory and thus the possibility of analogical anamnesis: apperceptive apprehension of the similar according to the similar. The "unknown world" and the world as universe of familiarities and unfamiliarities is called "experienceable" and "experienced."

This summary paragraph provides one of Husserl's most concise accounts of the genetic structure of world-constitution. The world is constituted through a process of *acquisition* driven by interest, sedimented into *habituality*, and generalized through *apperceptive transfer*. The constitution of the world is not the passive reception of given data but an active, interested, habit-forming process in which each new experience is shaped by the accumulated sense of prior experience. The result is a pre-given world structured by familiar types and open horizons of unfamiliarity — a world that is "experienceable" precisely because of this genetic structure.

**[§, p. 22]** Once this apperception is instituted, we have a pre-given world as a pre-given, presently accessible and future field of interest — a field of possible being toward which the active and passive ego, living toward it, can "be": instituting interests and interest-connections, correlatively instituting lasting beings, constituting world in ever new levels. This is the common world in which subjects too have constituted themselves as personal, world-interested subjects and simultaneously as world-objects, thus as pre-givennesses for each other and each for itself.

This paragraph completes the account by showing how the pre-given world is also the field in which *subjects themselves* are constituted as worldly beings. The ego that constitutes the world is itself constituted within it as a person among persons. The intersubjective dimension is built into the very structure of pre-givenness: the world is pre-given as a world *for all*, and each subject is pre-given to the others and to itself as a worldly being.

**[§, p. 22, final paragraph]** By contrast: an immanent "world" is not constituted in this way — first of all, not the "world" of the stream of consciousness as my egological stream. In genetic originality, immanent affection and grasping are indeed there, but in the course of genesis all immanent acquisition enters into mundane constitution; immanence is absorbed into the function of mundane constitution. The problem of why the ego must immediately constitute itself as a human ego and an ego for the world, and why life can only be world-life — that remains open. Only in a very highly developed genesis of humanity do psychology and phenomenology arise, making conscious life and the purely subjective, the transcendental, into a universal theme.

The text concludes by restating the asymmetry with which it opened, now enriched by the genetic analysis. Immanence is not constituted as a "world" because, in the natural course of genesis, all immanent acquisition is absorbed into the constitution of the mundane world. The ego's constitutive life is exhausted in world-constitution; it never turns back on itself except under highly specialized theoretical motivation. The question of *why* this is so — why the ego must constitute itself as a worldly human ego — is left open as a deeper problem requiring further investigation.

### Supplement II. Pre-Givenness and Affectivity. The Constant Pre-Givenness of the World, the Anonymity of the Constituting Subjective (*Vorgegebenheit und Affektivität. Die beständige Vorgegebenheit der Welt, die Anonymität des konstituierenden Subjektiven*)

*[ca. 1920]*

**[Content summary]** The world, the universal field of actual and freely possible objective experience, as a universal self-enclosed affective structure, with the necessary correlate of anonymous ego-like and constituting subjectivity.

**[§, pp. 23–24, first long paragraph]** Husserl draws attention to an important point: when we carry out any objective perception, motives can arise that deflect our gaze from the object to the subjective modes of givenness — shape-perspectives, colour-perspectives, the bodily-subjective processes that accompany them, the "I move the eyes and let the perceptions and their perspectival modes run off." The object is perceptual object necessarily only in the inseparable unity of object in the object-field and manifold of subjective modes. But this field of subjective modes of appearance and everything subjective has an obviously *secondary character*. Primarily and normally, only the object-world is "there for us"; our entire normal life — representing, thinking, striving, caring — refers to it. Only exceptionally, upon special motivation, does interest turn to the subjective modes. The inner motivation of life shifts so that now this, now that subjective element is in the field for us, while the object-field does not disappear but has the form of the objective that appears in its appearing (object in the How). There is a fundamental *difference of "attitude"* (*Einstellung*) that concerns even the unattended backgrounds: in normal going-along-living we are worldly, objectively attuned.

This passage develops the phenomenology of anonymity. In normal life, the subjective dimension of experience — the perspectival modes, kinaesthetic processes, ego-acts through which the world is constituted — is not merely unattended but structurally anonymous. The world-side of experience enjoys an absolute primacy in the economy of attention and interest. The subjective side can be made thematic, but only under special motivational conditions and only partially. The distinction is not one of mere attention-distribution but of *attitude* — a fundamental orientation that shapes the entire experiential field.

**[§, p. 24, continuation]** In the normal living attitude, all affections functioning as stimuli for attentive turnings proceed from the prominent surrounding world, the object-world. Even when we are absorbed in scientific thought and attend to nothing from our perceptual world, it is still in a special sense prominently *there* for us and affects us, even if we do not follow its affections. But since the objective can only be objective for us in the play of ever-originally-lived subjective modes — only as appearing in concordant appearances — we must say: through our perceptual life and experiential life runs a special unity-structure, a *structure of unified prominence, of unitariness in affectivity*, that forces upon all the subjective — in which and through which the object-world is there for us — its *anonymity*.

Husserl identifies the phenomenological mechanism of anonymity: it is a structural feature of affectivity. The constitution of the world requires a constant play of subjective modes, but the affective structure of experience is such that prominence, awakening, and the stimuli for attention flow exclusively (in the normal case) from the objective side. The subjective is structurally excluded from the affective economy — not because it is absent, but because the affective organization of experience channels all attention toward the objective. This anonymity is not a contingent fact but a *constitutive condition* of objective experience.

**[§, pp. 24–25]** This anonymity means: the streaming total perception belonging to all life and to every living moment has constantly a side — the subjective, the side of object-world-constituting life — that not only remains *unnoticed* but does not come into consideration for the play of attention at all. It is constantly there originally, constantly perceptual, but in such a way that no affections, no attention-stimuli, no stimuli toward the active ego proceed from it. Where we ordinarily speak of perception, we mean from the outset self-evidently this uniquely prominent connected field of object-perception. Since the affective stimulus involves a tendency directed toward the active ego, we can also say: through life runs a tendency-structure that directs the ego continually toward the object-unities constituting themselves in the manifold of subjective appearances and activities, while the subjective does not share this privilege — indeed must remain anonymous so that an objectively directed life, a life in the objective attitude, may be possible.

This paragraph restates and deepens the anonymity thesis. The anonymity of the constituting subjective is not a deficiency but a *structural necessity*: only because the subjective remains anonymous can there be an objectively directed life. If the constitutive processes were themselves prominent — if they competed with their products for attention — the coherent, unified appearance of the objective world would be disrupted. The world's pre-givenness thus presupposes and requires the anonymity of its constitutive sources.

**[§, p. 25, final paragraph]** Special motives can lead to the subjective — perspectives, the subjectively relative "right" and "left," etc. — creating an affective connection there too, a perceptual field in an analogous sense. But only phenomenology, in its method under the title "phenomenological reduction," has gone beyond the ordinary sporadic bringing-to-prominence of the subjective. Phenomenology created a universal attitude toward the total connection of all the subjective, in which the object-world receives the secondary position of what appears in those appearances. Attention presupposes an attitude for which a realm of affectivity is already prepared. For the natural-normal attitude, the object-world is already in readiness. Only the phenomenological attitude opens for us, as it were, a higher world: the previously anonymous subjectivity in its full universality. But more precisely: carrying out the phenomenological attitude already presupposes that we have the "world" of transcendental questions as horizon; we become aware that after the transcendental epoché we can constitute for ourselves a new thematics, a new being, and then we have beings in the transcendental sense.

The supplement concludes by locating phenomenology within this structural analysis. Phenomenology is the methodological breakthrough that overcomes the anonymity of the constituting subjective — not sporadically, as ordinary reflection does, but *universally*. Through the phenomenological reduction, the previously anonymous subjectivity is opened up as a total field of investigation, and the object-world is re-situated as that which appears within subjective life. But even this breakthrough presupposes a complex motivational structure: one must already have the horizon of transcendental questions in order to carry out the epoché. The transition from natural to phenomenological attitude is itself a constitutive achievement.

### Text Nr. 4. The Pre-Given World. General Aspects of Its Static and Genetic Structure (*Die vorgegebene Welt. Allgemeine Aspekte ihres statischen und genetischen Aufbaus*)

*[Supplement to the 1928 lecture "Intentional Psychology"]*

#### § 1. The Field of Effectively Conscious Objects and the Ineffective Horizon of the Unconscious (*Das Feld effektiv bewusster Objekte und der ineffektive Horizont des Unbewussten*)

**[§, p. 26]** "The" world is pre-given to us. If we direct the active gaze straightforwardly at anything, it was already there, it affected me, motivated me to turn toward it, and so it is now straightforwardly grasped.

The opening sentence states the fundamental datum: the world is *pre-given*. Every act of grasping finds its object already there, already affecting, already motivating. The pre-givenness of the world is not something we constitute but something we always already find ourselves within. Husserl's footnote adds the crucial social dimension: human life is life in unified striving, a life of actual and potential praxis; all praxis has its practical horizon and presupposes as unified field of praxis a unified, already constituted world. The relatedness to an already constituted world is the formal precondition of any unified communal life.

**[§, pp. 26–27]** Every grasped item is in a field of the grasped, the already conscious. In particular, every perceptually noticed item is in a "perceptual field": visual field, tactile field, auditory field, etc. (but not as a sensation-field). But the consciousness-field as a field of prominent, already sense-laden, affecting objectualities extends further: various objectualities can be awakened in memory, also in the mode of anticipatory expectation. Every actually grasped item lies in a *field of view* (*Blickfeld*), a field of effectively conscious objects, an effectively conscious *rayon of the world*, whether the consciousness is intuitive or empty. This effective field always lies within a more encompassing field, surrounded by an *ineffective horizon of the "unconscious"* — an open horizon of non-prominent, not explicitly conscious objects that nevertheless is an intentional horizon: in it, in a broader sense, something is conscious, namely the totality of "unconscious" objects that intentionally supplement the effective field to the world. Always more is *meant* than is actually grasped, and more is meant than is effectively prominent. Constantly co-meant is a horizon of possible awakening, possible affection, possible turning-toward, possible experience and continuation of experience. When such experience actualizes itself, it lets ever new objects emerge from the horizon as determinate and creates access to them as now actually experienced realities.

This major paragraph lays out the fundamental topology of world-experience. At the centre is the effective field — the zone of actually prominent, grasped, or at least affectively present objects. Surrounding it is the ineffective horizon — the "unconscious" in Husserl's specific, intentional sense: not Freudian unconsciousness but the vast domain of what is co-intended, co-meant, but not prominent. The ineffective horizon is genuinely intentional — it has content, structure, and the potentiality for actualization — but it contributes nothing to the current affective economy. The relationship between effective field and ineffective horizon is one of continual exchange: experience brings objects from the horizon into the field, while formerly prominent objects recede into the horizon.

**[§, p. 27, continuation]** This horizon-meaning (*Horizontmeinung*), which everywhere attaches to every effective field as co-meaning, is indeterminate *determinability* — and always determinability through *objects*. Every object as such has its formal structure; it enters the effective field as apprehended thus and so, with a given objective sense whose necessary form stands out in free variation. But this form is bound by an ineffective, empty horizon, insofar as the free possibility of going beyond the given effective horizon — which we can in each case imaginatively represent as a manifold possibility and thereby clarify the plus ultra of the horizon-sense — has in evident necessity the form of a transition to new object-fields that synthetically connect with the old. In the natural attitude as world-attitude: whatever can become effective, can become so only as an effective object-field.

Husserl here formalizes the concept of the horizon as *indeterminate determinability*. The horizon is not a void; it is structured by the formal constraints of objectuality and by the typifications sedimented from prior experience. Every advance into the horizon yields objects — it cannot yield anything else, because the very form of determinability is objectual. The necessity here is eidetic: the natural attitude is constitutively an attitude in which everything that can become effective can become so only as a field of objects.

**[§, pp. 27–28]** Objects are to be disclosed immediately or mediately. A mediacy always arises anew from the subjectivity's living-into the pre-given world — from its artistic, literary, etc. acting and creating. Constantly the world is pre-given, constantly it renews itself and transforms what exists through the arising of founded intentionalities, through which pre-givennesses gain new determinations — determinations like those of art-works, which in their form point back to a genesis and to producing subjectivity. If constantly a world, and in all ongoing transformations through their synthesis *the same* world, is conscious as pre-given and as pre-given for all performances on the part of subjects in world-life, and if all such performances are to be questioned concerning their history, then it seems at first clear that in disclosing the intentionalities we must come upon a layered structure of genesis, such that all pre-given objects of the currently valid world must lead back to *first objects* — objects characterized by not yet bearing any objectively constituted determinations arising from world-life. These first objects are, as it were, *primal objects* of the genesis in which a pre-given world is constantly newly shaped by the subjectivity working into it. Every world pre-given as a cultural world points genetically back to one that is still *culture-free* and free of objective spirit.

This paragraph introduces the genetic-archaeological dimension: if the world is constantly enriched by the sedimentation of cultural, practical, and theoretical achievements, then tracing back this genesis should lead to ever more basic strata — ultimately to "primal objects" that bear no culturally superimposed determinations. This is the familiar Husserlian theme of the *Abbau* (dismantling) leading back to nature as the cultural zero-point. But Husserl immediately signals caution about this seemingly straightforward picture.

**[§, pp. 28–29]** But one must be careful and bring all the problems to speech. If the world is already pre-given with human beings, then cultural objects point back to the human beings engaged with them. But humans stand in a chain of generations, each growing up as a child and growing into the communal world — a world that must first be constituted in initially individual-subjective and then, through empathy, progressively intersubjective genesis. This world has after-understandable cultural determinations arising from the spiritual activity of ancestors. These ancestors were likewise children growing into a world of similar tradition. — In this chain of generative life, we find no culture-free world (only if we use a restricted rather than the formally most general and indispensable concept of culture). Whether a historical null-world is thinkable at all — a naked world free of all spiritual meaning, into which humans then suddenly drop — is from the outset questionable.

Husserl here raises a fundamental objection to the simple genetic-archaeological picture: the idea of a "culture-free nature" as historical starting-point may be incoherent. Since every human generation is born into a world already shaped by prior generations, and since the very constitution of the world involves cultural formation from the start, there may be no historical zero-point at which a "pure nature" was actually given. The culture-free nature may be an abstraction — a constitutive limit-concept — rather than a historical reality.

**[§, p. 29, continuation]** Every genesis of the world — as it transforms itself in pre-givenness through subjective life working into the pre-given world — unfolds in individual subjects in purely individual-subjective intentionality and progresses in them, through the medium of empathy, to intersubjective genesis. But we have been speaking of the genesis of the pre-given world that always already presupposes pre-given world — the genesis of the historical world that always takes on new historical content through the humans living into it, each generation already having a world pre-given. Every human being in the historical nexus carries out their world-life and has therein their share to contribute to the constitution of the historical world. But in the life of each individual, the world is pre-given for them from exclusively their own sources of intentionality, and only within this, through the mediation of empathic experiences, is the world the intersubjective one, pre-given for all of us, for the communities. Everyone has the world pre-given as the one pre-given "for us all."

This paragraph articulates the complex relationship between individual and intersubjective genesis. Every individual constitutes the world from their own intentional sources — no one can borrow another's constitutive life. But through empathy, the individually constituted world becomes the shared, intersubjective world. The intersubjective character of the world is thus a constitutive achievement within each individual's intentional life, not a metaphysical given.

**[§, pp. 30–33]** The remainder of § 1 and § 2 elaborate the genetic stratification of the pre-given world. Self-givenness has levels: what is immediately-originally self-given (nature perceived through one's own senses) versus what is mediately given (the other's experience, cultural determinations). The universe of what is immediately experienceable for me is *nature*. Value-characters, teleological-practical characters that nature has received from *me alone* are mediate but still reducible to original self-givenness through memory. "Mere nature" and "culture stemming from me alone" form a genetic sequence, with nature as the presupposition of objective genesis. Through empathy, I gain others as subjects with their own original experience of the same nature, and through communication and communal life, a shared cultural world is constituted. The world becomes a human world, articulated into communities with their historically specific traditions and cultures. The pre-givenness of this world has, in each ego-subject, its genesis from that subject's life and its passive and active modes of comportment.

#### § 2. The World Pre-Given as Cultural World Pointing Back to Nature (*Die als Kulturwelt vorgegebene Welt zurückweisend auf Natur*)

**[§, pp. 30–34]** The pre-givenness of the world must have its levels. The world appearing to me must appear in a basic stratum immediately, so that this basic stratum can come to immediate self-givenness. What is immediately perceptively appearing *founds* the perception of the mediate. The universe of objects given to me as immediately experienceable is *nature*. Already value-characters and teleological-practical characters are mediate but still originarily self-givable through recollective access to my own activity. A universe delimits itself of what can be brought to original self-givenness from the pre-given world. Within it: "mere nature" and "culture stemming from me alone." Since cultural determinations in their mode of givenness refer back to their genesis in me, we have a *genetic sequence of levels*. The lower level, nature, is the presupposition of objective genesis but is not itself arisen from an objective genesis — if what is in question is the genesis that lets higher-level worldliness arise from what is already worldly.

Abstracting from others in this reduction to immediately-original self-givenness for me, the reduced objectivity is called "objectivity" because, when we lift the abstraction, it fits into the common world in intentional synthesis as the spatial thing that anyone can find as the same. Through empathy, the world is given to me as also appearing originally to others. As the first level of this mediacy, I have the mode of givenness and especially the experience of *others as such*. My "perception" of others is original self-giving of other humans — where the originality of appresentation progressively confirms itself in ongoing experience. The human being as human-there is other insofar as the experienced ego and soul-life is other; but not yet *another human* until I have turned the gaze back to myself as the one that other similarly experiences as a human in their There. Only with this reversed apprehension of myself from the standpoint of the other do I become *human* for myself and experience the "we both humans."

This is Husserl's characteristic account of the constitution of intersubjectivity, compressed into a few dense paragraphs. The key moves are: (1) reduction to the primordial sphere of what is immediately-originally given to me alone; (2) recognition that this primordial sphere already contains nature as its basic stratum; (3) the introduction of empathy (*Einfühlung*) as the constitutive operation through which others are given, along with their experience of the same nature; (4) the constitution of intersubjective humanity through the reversibility of the ego-other relation. The passage also shows how the entire layered structure — nature, personal culture, communal culture — is genetically constituted from the bottom up.

**[§, pp. 32–34]** As an intermediate level, there is the nexus with specific persons. In the course of experience, an open horizon of possible new things forms that are experienceable as bodies for human beings — humans not only accessible from the present. The originally experienced humans are experienced with a horizon of humans unexperienced by me, with whom they were and are connected: humans who grew up as children of parents, etc. The world becomes a human world, articulated into communities related to their historically specific tradition. The world and the different surrounding worlds of humans are pre-given to the given individual ego (and community). This pre-givenness has its genesis in each ego-subject from their life.

The structure of the pre-given world is first to be displayed through *static analysis* — the universal style of a human world with its necessary core-structure of mere nature, organic corporeality, animal and human spiritual being, all outfitted with cultural determinations in their secondary objectivity. But this static structure points back to a *genetic* structure: the genesis is an intentional genesis in subjects who continually have a world pre-given as their life-field. The pre-given world as spatiotemporal has the structure of unconditional objectivity — being-there for everyone. Nature and humans are accessible for everyone through experience. The entire domain of cultural objectualities is a domain of the subjective that achieves only relative objectivity. But in principle even nature has this relativity — for us European cultural humans it has a fixed objectivity only because we have the historically developed, commonly valid norm of natural science.

Going back through the genesis of the pre-given world, we are led to the individual ego and from there to a universal genesis for which nature is only a relative starting-point, while deeper investigation shows that *nature too has its subjective constitutive genesis*. The manifold consciousness of nature is subject first to a static analysis, but this analysis itself refers to a genesis.

This concluding passage of Text Nr. 4 as excerpted here sets up the fundamental methodological distinction between *static* and *genetic* analysis that will govern the entire volume. Static analysis describes the structures of the pre-given world as they are found. Genetic analysis traces these structures back to their constitutive origins in subjective life. The two are complementary, not competing: every static finding points toward a genetic question, and every genetic account presupposes a static description of what is to be explained. The passage also introduces the important theme of cultural relativity: even the "objectivity" of nature is constitutively relative to the norms of a historical community.

### Supplement III. The Separation of Passive and Active Intellect Together with the Deepened Distinction between "Sensuous" Objectuality and Categorial. The Constitution of Objects in the Pregnant Sense (and Thus of a World of Pre-Given Objects) in Acts of Activity (*Die Scheidung von passivem und aktivem Intellekt in eins mit der vertieften Unterscheidung zwischen „sinnlicher" Gegenständlichkeit und kategorialer. Die Konstitution von Gegenständen im prägnanten Sinn (und somit einer Welt vorgegebener Gegenstände) in Akten der Aktivität*)

*[ca. 1920]*

**[§, p. 34, opening]** Husserl begins with a self-correction: in his older presentations, he had tried to capture the fundamental constitutional difference he had in mind — between nature and culture (spiritual products in the widest sense) — through the distinction between passive and active intellect, but in an incorrect way. Nature-objects (and before them the ego-foreign objects of primal sensibility), the domain of absolute personal foreignness, are *not* "passively constituted." *All objects as objects are actively constituted.* But the fundamental distinction is between *associative* and *categorial* constitution.

This is a significant methodological correction. Husserl rejects his own earlier framing (passive vs. active intellect) and replaces it with a more precise distinction. The key insight is that even "sensuous" objects — things of nature — are not purely passively constituted; they require active ego-involvement (grasping, identifying, determining). The proper distinction is not between passive and active constitution but between two *kinds* of active constitution: one operating through association (which produces sensuous objects) and one operating through categorial synthesis (which produces logical, predicative, and higher-order objects).

**[§, pp. 34–36, on association and objecthood]** Husserl explains: (1) Association has universal constitutive significance, which he came to understand only late. Before and alongside all activity, association establishes connections but does not properly create *objects*. Without association there is no affection. But "object" in the *pregnant sense* requires the activity of attentive grasping, cognizance-taking — and moreover identification through memory, i.e. a synthesis on the basis of activity, though also of association. As *object*, something is apprehended, apperceived, on the basis of preceding identification and actual determination; and an apperception immediately founds a type-apperception. All this is the work of association, which however presupposes action. (2) What Husserl had in mind under "active intellect" was *categorial action* of the understanding, feeling, and will: synthesis in which objects *as objects* are synthetically linked — collection, judgment, predication.

**[§, p. 36]** To the essence of an object in the proper sense belongs its being an identical substrate of "predicates," of determining moments, with a form already prefigured in the objective sense. Object-consciousness in the first grasping of the object is not just any turning toward something affecting; it is genuine grasping, which presupposes apprehending *as something* and then as something of an indeterminately general structure. Where this is not the case, nothing is properly grasped; what affects is initially an "unfathomable" (*Unfassliches*). Apprehending as something — e.g. grasping a never-before-seen thing and thereby grasping it *as* a thing — already prefigures substrate-grasping, step-by-step cognizance-taking along certain directions of indeterminacy (figure, size, etc.). Thus in every apprehension lies a certain anamnesis of ego-activity. Everything that affects us as object and is grasped by us as object is apperceived, and insofar as it enters the gaze as determinable and to-be-determined, it has a sense-structure stemming from activity. In a certain sense, therefore, *all objects are "categorial."*

This is a remarkable claim: even the most basic "sensuous" objects already bear the marks of categorial constitution insofar as they are apprehended as objects at all — as substrates of predicates, as determinable somethings. The very form of objecthood is "categorial" in a broad sense, because it presupposes the active ego-functions of grasping, identifying, and determining. The distinction between "sensuous" and "categorial" objects is thus not one between pure passivity and activity but between objects whose sense can be fulfilled in straightforward experience and those whose sense requires multi-step synthetic production.

**[§, pp. 36–38]** The essential distinction then emerges between *simple* objects (whose objective sense does not include other objects as components) and *manifold* objects (which already contain objects within their apprehensive sense). Every founding of objects in objects requires, for the self-givenness of the founded, the self-givenness of the founding objects. A sensuous configuration is in itself a simple object, though its pre-givenness involves a unified affection containing sub-affections. But the configuration apprehended *as a configuration of a plurality of objects* is no longer a simple sensuous object; it is founded in multiple objects and presupposes the action of unifying apprehension for original self-givenness as a plurality.

**[Definition, p. 38]** Husserl provides a formal definition: objects whose objective sense can be brought to original experience only in ego-actions that are not merely receptive but first *produce* the objectuality of its specific kind are called "categorial objects" or "objects of the active intellect." "Purely categorial" are all forms that are pure correlates of activity, which is thus an independent source of originally objectifying genesis. The *matter* of categorial actions are the founding objects; these can themselves have categorial form. Ultimately we arrive at sensuous objects.

### Supplement IV. Pre-Givenness of Sensuous and Synthetic (Categorial) Objectualities (*Vorgegebenheit sinnlicher und synthetischer (kategorialer) Gegenständlichkeiten*)

*[ca. 1920]*

**[§, pp. 38–39]** Husserl notes that clear distinctions are won only by reducing everything to the normal case of originarily giving intuition. For it is the wonderful but essentially fully understandable fact that *every synthetic objectuality*, after its production, after its coming-to-consciousness in original production, becomes a *pre-given* thing graspable in the manner of a "secondary sensibility" (*sekundäre Sinnlichkeit*) — like something originally sensuous. A state of affairs may "occur to me," a mathematical theorem may "flash up" in background-consciousness — and then this objectuality is there for me initially as a pre-givenness to which I turn in a grasping ray. But this having is a vague conscious-having, a hovering-from-afar, not a genuine having. In principle, a predicative proposition, an inference, a theory can be had originally only in a synthetic process.

This is the key insight of the supplement: secondary sensibility (*sekundäre Sinnlichkeit*). Once categorial objects are constituted — judgments, theories, mathematical truths — they sediment and become available as pre-given items that can be grasped in a quasi-sensuous, immediate manner, without re-performing the original constitutive synthesis. A theorem can "flash up" in consciousness as something familiar, as a pre-given unity. But this quasi-sensuous givenness is derivative; genuine possession requires reactivation of the original synthetic process.

**[§, pp. 39–40]** In "extra-theoretical" acts (acts of feeling, acts of will), synthetic objectualities can constitute themselves as pre-givennesses on the basis of theoretically grasped substrates, becoming grasped objectualities in subsequent doxic acts. In all acts the distinction between reason and unreason holds. Objectualities can be confirmable, or they can turn out to be null, non-existent. In the essence of all object-constitution lies the reference to intention and fulfilment: in all object-constitutions there lie intentional motivations, following which we can confirm or refute reality.

**[§, p. 40]** Objectualities fall into two classes: (1) those that have *arisen in subject-activities* — given originally only in "synthetic" acts that constitute them step-by-step as originally given realities; (2) ultimately we arrive at substrates that are not themselves originally synthetic — these are the *sensuous objects* in the first and proper sense. They are in a certain way already there before grasping, intuitively pre-given, yet not yet intuited, not yet grasped, not yet objects for the cognizer: not yet "thought" in even the most primitive thinking-step.

Husserl here establishes the fundamental two-class division: categorial (synthetic) objectualities, which require productive ego-activity for their original givenness, and sensuous objectualities, which are pre-given intuitively before any active grasping. The latter are the ultimate substrates of all constitution — the raw material, as it were, upon which all higher-order constitution builds.

### Text Nr. 5. The Pure Experiential World, the Perceivable in the Widest Sense. Pre-Given. Co-Pre-Given Therein: Worldly Subjectivity (*Reine Erfahrungswelt, die wahrnehmbare im weitesten Sinn. Vorgegeben. Darin mitvorgegeben: die weltliche Subjektivität*)

*[From the lectures 1926/27, "Introduction to Phenomenology." Recapitulation.]*

**[Content summary by Eugen Fink]** In contrast to nature as a consequent theme, the "subjective" as a consequent theme: the subjectively communalized life as having a pre-given world. The philosopher who reflects on pre-givenness belongs himself to the "pre-given" world! "World of experience" as the "pre-given" world in the How of being-experienced.

**[§, p. 41, first paragraph]** Husserl sets up a contrast: if I reduce the world to the world of original *pure experience*, in this sphere universal *nature* separates itself out as a self-enclosed domain of consistent experience, a self-enclosed domain of experiential givennesses. But in this same sphere I also have human subjects and cultural objects, which are indeed also nature but more than nature, insofar as they are experiential objects that also bear ego-relative determinations — and this means: are from the outset apperceived with such determinations.

This opening paragraph frames the central problem of the text: the relationship between nature and subjectivity within the experiential world. When we reduce to pure experience, nature emerges as a closed domain — but so does a domain of subjects and cultural objects that *exceed* nature while *including* it. The crucial point is that subjects and cultural objects are not merely natural objects with extra properties tacked on; they are from the outset apperceived differently, with ego-relative determinations built into their apperceptive sense.

**[§, p. 41, second paragraph]** More precisely: the world is experienced such that, according to its apperceptive sense, *subjectless objects* and *objects that are subjects* are distinguished within it. One can carry out a reduction to mere, pure nature. Thus a structure "nature" runs unitarily through the experiential world. I can now ask how it stands with the subjective in the world — with subjects and determinations that have arisen from subjects — and specifically whether, when I am directed purely at subjects as subjects in the experiential world, I arrive at a closed stratum of the world.

The key methodological move: the experiential world has a dual structure — subjectless objects (nature) and objects-that-are-subjects (persons). The question is whether the subjective dimension forms a "closed stratum" analogous to the closure of nature. The procedure described — "epoché with respect to nature" — means: pursuing the theme "subjectivity" purely without presupposing the natural body.

**[§, p. 42, first paragraph]** Husserl then proposes an alternative approach: let my theme be the universe of possible experiential givennesses — the world as world from pure experience, in full concretion. Experience is intersubjective experience. The experiential world is constantly given to us; directed at the world individually and in community, we experience it — proceeding consistently and eliminating all discordances — the world, the manifold objects in their essential determinations.

**[§, p. 42, second paragraph]** But the objects also have, for everyone as objects within the framework of experience, constantly subjective determinations that are extra-essential yet co-belonging — and such that necessarily such determinations are always present in the experiential domain. *The world is there for us as pre-given*, insofar as even before the thematic gaze is directed at these or those objects or at the world as universe, a world *apperception* constantly runs through our life. Things continually "appear"; whether we attend to them or not, they appear as objects of a given perceptual field.

This paragraph establishes one of the central theses of the entire volume: the *world-apperception* (*Weltapperzeption*) is not a discrete act but a constant, running accomplishment of conscious life. The world is pre-given not because we deliberately posit it but because, prior to all thematic engagement, a world-apperception is always already underway. This world-apperception is the deepest stratum of the pre-givenness of the world.

**[§, pp. 42–43]** Husserl continues: things appear as objects of a given, if relatively dark, sense (trees, houses, rivers) and background; appearing with this sense and background, they "affect" us. We grasp them, insofar as they are in the perceptual field, or bring them to perception. The apperception transforms itself into a thematic intention, which fulfils itself in a manifold of synthetically unified individual graspings on the basis of a substrate-grasping, such that the substrate is characterized as determining itself in these and those thematic individual determinations. Thus the world is for us constantly experienced in a *double sense*: pre-given, there in advance in manifold actual or derivative apperceptions, unified in passivity in a flowing yet constantly self-unifying total apperception; and thematically given, thematic according to individual objects as substrates of emergent experiential determinations, determining themselves further in ongoing cognizance-taking until the interest exhausts itself or is diverted and other objects become thematic. The world-apperception is a constantly constituted, though constantly changeable, system of affective tendencies toward the ego, and in its effectuation a system of counter-directed experiential interests going from the ego toward the substrates. Every experiential thematic has an *infinite unthematic horizon* in the universality of the apperceived world, endlessly extending in all directions, spatiotemporally.

This is one of the most architecturally important paragraphs in the entire volume. Husserl distinguishes two layers of world-experience: (1) the pre-thematic world-apperception, a passive, flowing, self-unifying total apperception that is always already underway; and (2) thematic experience, in which individual objects are singled out and determinatively explored. The two layers are not independent: thematic experience always unfolds *within* the pre-thematic world-apperception, drawing its objects from that apperception and returning them to it. The world-apperception is described as a system of affective tendencies — forces pulling the ego toward engagement with the world — and a correlative system of ego-directed interests reaching back toward things. The crucial point is the *infinity* of the unthematic horizon: every thematic engagement leaves an infinite residuum of unthematized world.

**[§, pp. 43–44]** The pre-given world also encompasses worldly subjectivity — humans and animals lie in the pre-giving apperceptive horizon. Everything toward which our thematic gaze is directed is first something *pre-given*, already there for us, pre-indicated or already appearing. The first thing is therefore the pre-given, and in general the world as universal title for what is at any time pre-given.

But we can bring *reflection* into play — reflection as an *experience* — whereby we become aware that the worldly is for us something conscious in subjective modes, and that what we experience and can experience as world constantly has its subjective milieu and can never become conscious for us without it. Each person has their own subjective modes; the experienced world in its essential character is something that gives itself to me in my subjective cognizance-taking activity and was pre-given for me in my pre-apperceptions, affecting me, then giving itself in these and those modes of appearance. And for every other person analogously. In mutual intercourse we "understand" the other as subject of their subjective modes and the world as interwoven with their experiential access. For us, the world itself, as what it is in its essential character, is the unity of these givenness-modes identified in one's own and foreign "contents." All this is our *communal experience*.

Husserl here makes a crucial structural observation: the pre-given world includes subjects as part of its content, but these subjects are also the very bearers of the world-apperception through which the world is pre-given. There is an irreducible circularity: the world contains subjects who constitute the world. Reflection reveals this circularity by showing that everything worldly is conscious in subjective modes — but this very reflection and the reflecting philosopher are themselves part of the pre-given world.

**[§, p. 44, continuation]** We have constantly as universe of possible communal givennesses in pure experience not only world but also the subjective belonging to us as experiencers — the subjective of this experienced world. To separate the world from this subjective when we stand in the universality of possible experience is impossible. But this subjective too admits of reflection on a further subjective — and so on in infinitum.

**[§, pp. 44–45]** In the first pre-givenness we already find the others and ourselves, and find them as related to the common world, to the things external to them and other world-objects, but also as related to themselves, as knowing themselves in community with one another, as in community experiencing, valuing, and acting. When I establish myself as philosophizing subject, I consider all this. Reflecting on myself (the philosophizer), I find myself as a human being in the human community and the world, but as a human who has now taken on the particular subjective life of the philosopher. And further reflection shows this for the first reflection itself, and so on. The philosophizing ego belongs to the experiential content of the world for everyone.

This passage develops the theme of the philosopher's self-inclusion in the pre-given world. The phenomenological reflection does not lift the philosopher out of the world; rather, it reveals the philosopher as a worldly being engaged in a specific kind of worldly activity (philosophizing). Every level of reflection discovers itself as a worldly phenomenon, available in principle for further reflection. This generates an infinite regress — but a regress that Husserl treats not as a problem but as a structural feature of worldly existence.

**[§, pp. 45–46]** I see that "the world" is constantly a performance arising from subjects, accomplishing itself "in" them, and that subjectivity is constantly presupposed as performing in its subjective manner, and that every new performing creates a content behind which the performing subjective still stands unthematically, itself accessible for new thematic performance, and so on. Under the title "world of universal experience" I have not an existing world with existing subjects in it, but rather universal communal subjectivity, living, performing, apperceiving, actively comporting itself in relation to the apperceived — constantly bringing what individuals apperceive to identity in community, and finding at every stage an "objective," essentially determined world as the result of communal performance. The world is not something next to or in the world but is carried by the ego and the We in its performing life as a unity-accomplishment. *The world we speak of is the world of our experience*; making it a universal theme, we find it in all its essential What necessarily in modes of subjective How, and find us as subjects who in their conscious life carry these subjective modes and in them the What.

This is one of the most radical passages in Text Nr. 5. Husserl dissolves the naive opposition between "world" and "subjects in the world." Under the title of universal experience, what we find is not a world with subjects placed inside it, but rather a universal communal subjectivity whose performing life carries the world as its unity-accomplishment. The world is not a container; it is a constitutive product. Yet this product includes the producers as part of its content. The passage thus articulates the fundamental paradox of transcendental phenomenology in the key of the life-world.

**[§, p. 46, final paragraph]** The world is found as *having* the subjects in itself, but the world with all subjects is found as *unity of our, of all our life related to it, as constituted unity*. How can we carry out systematic study here? What does the method mean — to subject the "general thesis" of the world to an epoché? What does "straightforwardly experience" mean? The first thing in the world is nature; subjects in the world are subjects localized in nature. Bracketing straightforwardly experienced nature and thereby the localized subjects. — Beginning: I reflect. I want to become clear about how I can attain a universal cognition of the world and of the domain of experience accessible to me, as a purely experiential and descriptive cognition. My self-experience has, with regard to the ego-subjective, a priority.

The text concludes by pointing toward the phenomenological reduction as the method needed to untangle the constitutive paradox just described. The priority of self-experience over the experience of others provides the methodological starting-point: the ego's own constitutive life is the domain where the universal structure of world-constitution can first be laid bare.

### Text Nr. 6. Universe of Pre-Givenness and World. Constitution of the Universe of Pre-Givenness through Habituality (*Universum der Vorgegebenheit und Welt. Konstitution des Universums der Vorgegebenheit durch Habitualität*)

*[ca. 1930]*

**[§, p. 47, first paragraph]** In natural going-along-living, we constantly have a universe of pre-givennesses. At first this seems to be an unalterable essential property of all conscious life, whether natural or the life in "transcendental" attitude toward which we are to be led. Indeed it is a *fundamental law* that whatever we have "for the first time" posited in validity in any positional acts (experiencing, judging, valuing, willing, etc.) — in *primal-instituting acts* — remains in validity until further notice. Every act institutes a lasting (habitual) validity extending beyond the fleeting act, a persisting meaning in the widest sense (conviction, decision, resolution, etc.). Acts of concordant sense and validity-character arising subsequently in life present themselves not as having merely the *same* meaning, but as repeated actualizations of *one and the same* meaning that *still holds* from the primal institution onward.

This opening paragraph establishes the foundational law governing the entire constitution of the pre-given world: *habitual validity* (*habituelle Geltung*). Every positional act — every act in which something is posited as being, as valuable, as willed — creates not merely a momentary event but a lasting claim. Subsequent acts of the same kind are not independent repetitions but reactivations of the *same* validity that was originally instituted. This is the mechanism through which consciousness builds up a stable world: the accumulation of habitual validities that persist "until further notice."

**[§, pp. 47–48]** The new acts of the same meaning, returning in the mode of still-validity, carry out a *subsequent institution* (*Nachstiftung*) and, depending on the completeness of actualization, a varying degree of *corroboration* (*Bekräftigung*). If such corroborations are lacking, the persisting primal institution loses its force, also its motivational force in the broader context of consciousness. Meanings as habitual validities can *decay* through lasting non-use. On the other hand, they can *conflict* with newly instituted meanings or, through transformation of their givenness-mode in otherwise corroborating acts, come into conflict with one another. Meanings in validity can be weakened (modalized) by others of equal or greater validity-force that conflict with them in content — they can become doubtful or null. From the ego's side: they can be abandoned, rejected. Through corresponding change of attitude, modalized meanings become new positive meanings of altered sense: primal institutions of questionabilities, doubtfulnesses, invalidities. Ultimately, being-meanings arise everywhere, since every meaning is either from the outset a being-meaning or can be transformed into one by transition to a doxic attitude. Renewal of a meaning that had lost its original validity-force through decay or modalization has the character not merely of a subsequent institution but of a *restoration* of the primal institution as one that had become "powerless."

This passage provides a remarkably complete dynamic theory of habitual validity. Validities are not static deposits; they undergo corroboration, decay, conflict, modalization, and restoration. The life of consciousness is a continuous economy of validities — some reinforced by repeated corroboration, others fading through disuse, still others negated or modified through conflict with new experience. The concept of *Modalisierung* (modalization) captures the various ways a validity can lose its straightforward certainty (becoming doubtful, questionable, or null). The entire texture of the pre-given world is woven from this play of instituting, corroborating, decaying, conflicting, and restoring validities.

**[§, pp. 48–49]** On the basis of these essential peculiarities of positional habituality, there constitutes itself in every unified course of life, for every immanent present, a *time-horizon of beings* — a horizon of what holds and continues to hold from earlier — into which the new institutions of this present insert themselves, while the modalizations carried out in it excise this or that from the domain of the validity-tradition. The style of a currently valid totality of being is naturally preserved, but the totality itself is in flux. Examining our natural life as it factually presents itself, with regard to its pervading style — the typical shape-transformation of its universe of pre-given being — we immediately discern a structural peculiarity that goes far beyond what we could make distantly intelligible on the basis of the described essential peculiarities of positional persistence. The natural life has not only a changeable present-universe of pre-givennesses, but an *all-temporal universe* extending through all its past and into its entire open future — passing through all universes of pre-givennesses. The given ego can, transposing itself into any past or future, find this very thing: that *one single universe* everywhere holds as numerically the same and is contained in the respective universes of pre-givenness, "presenting itself" in them.

Husserl here moves from the micro-level dynamics of individual validities to the macro-level structure they produce: the *all-temporal universe* — the one world that persists through all changes in the particular universes of pre-givenness. The key insight is that the world is not simply the sum of what is currently valid; it is the *same* world that was there in every past present and will be there in every future present. This sameness-through-change is itself a constitutive accomplishment of the habitual structure of consciousness.

**[§, pp. 49–50]** The universal time-horizon co-constituted for the ego in every phase of its life is simultaneously the objective time-form of a *being-universe* that held in every past, was included in its validity-universe, and co-holds in every future. Each present is a present that has (or would have) a universe of pre-givenness as its own. But in running through them, a synthesis establishes itself for the universal meanings of all phases, and a unity of meaning is in effect: the same universe, *the same world*, which temporally always was and always will be, always given in the running-through. This meaning of the "same" world passes through, even though some of these pre-givennesses, in which "the" world was given in a given time-phase, turn out not to belong to the world itself — namely, they no longer hold for it afterwards. Nonetheless, despite this changeability of validity-contents, every valid universe at its temporal position presents an "objective universe" and means it through all (for the subject valid) falsehoods — such that despite these supposed falsehoods a single, same world remains posited as continually experienced.

This paragraph addresses the problem of error and correction within the constitution of the one world. Even when particular pre-givennesses are subsequently negated — when things turn out to have been illusory or mistaken — the *world* as a whole maintains its identity. Corrections and negations do not destroy the world; they modify particular contents while preserving the world-form. The "same world" that passes through all these changes is constituted precisely through the interplay of confirmation and correction.

**[§, pp. 50–51]** This peculiarity further differentiates itself. The enduring form of this one universe is not only time but also space (where space is actually a form subordinate to the temporal form — the temporally omnivalid form of real simultaneity), articulated into individual realities that, beyond their changing extensional particular shapes, show a *pervading categorial and regional typification* (*kategoriale und regionale Typik*). Everything worldly leads back ultimately to realities in the narrower sense — ultimate substrates of real properties, relations, etc. Real properties are persisting characteristics of persisting substrates, persisting as lasting habits of causal comportment. And beyond this lies the regional typification of realities: material things, organic bodies, souls and psychophysical unities such as animals, personal subjects and subjective communities, culture as the domain of personal creations. Husserl notes that he is not speaking of the world of science or the "true world in itself" but only articulating a *structural typification* for the rough characterization of the "universe" that is demonstrable in all natural life.

This paragraph provides a synoptic ontological inventory of the life-world's typification: from the formal structures of space and time, through the categorial forms of substance and causality, to the regional articulation into material things, organisms, animals, persons, communities, and cultural products. Crucially, this is not a scientific ontology but a *descriptive typification* of the pre-given world as it is lived — the typification that natural life itself carries and presupposes.

**[§, pp. 51–52]** Husserl adds that the incorporation of sciences into natural life changes nothing in the intuitive world-structure while it researches it scientifically; science merely produces its "theoretical truths" and thereby establishes a higher-level pre-givenness that is founded upon and referred back to the world of natural intuitive world-research. Just as the thing I measure and theorize about becomes, through its theoretical determinations, no *other* thing than the one I have before my eyes in experience — it is identically the same — so the world of positive science is no other than the experienced world, pre-given and consistently valid in the structures of experience.

This important passage articulates the continuity thesis: the world of science and the life-world are not two different worlds but the *same* world, the life-world being the founding stratum upon which science builds its higher-order determinations. Science adds new layers of sense to the pre-given world but does not replace or abolish it.

### Text Nr. 7. Primal Historicity of the Pre-Given World. First and Second Horizontality: Horizon as Familiar Pre-Givenness Style and as Open Horizon of Possible New Pre-Givenness Styles. Relativity of All Familiar Pre-Givenness Styles (*Urhistorizität der vorgegebenen Welt. Erste und zweite Horizontalität*)

*[30 September 1932]*

**[§, pp. 53–54, opening paragraphs]** Husserl begins by asking what "world of experience" can mean when contrasted with the world as truly being according to the truth-concept of science. The answer: the world of everyday life. In everyday life, I have my world-experience and world in horizon — but the horizon is only so far awakened or awakenable as my interest demands. I have the world with an open horizon, but as the one existing for me from my life.

From my life, the world (in connection with others) has a *structure of pre-givenness*; and this structure was different in my childhood than it is now, and it constantly changes. The primitive human being does not have scientific culture in his pre-givenness of the world; for him it is not something lying in his horizons of possibility. If I, empathizing with him, construct the world pre-given for him, then from my standpoint his pre-givenness horizon receives an extension by the sense-stratum "science of scientists" that first came to institution in me and my peers. For the primitive there is a surrounding world with human beings, but human beings of his kind — not people with the for-him-unimaginable personal structure "scientist."

This passage introduces the theme of *historical relativity of pre-givenness*. The pre-givenness structure of the world is not fixed and universal; it varies across individuals, across cultures, and across historical epochs. What is pre-given for a modern European scientist is radically different from what is pre-given for a "primitive" — not just in content but in the very form of pre-givenness. The extension of one's pre-givenness horizon through empathic understanding of other cultures and historical periods is itself a constitutive achievement.

**[§, pp. 54–55]** Must we not leave it open that in the further development of humanity, forms of human existence and surrounding-world forms that are for us in principle incomprehensible might show themselves — within the already understood, indeterminately open possibilities of the pre-given structure "development"? World as world for all human beings — already mutually understandable as human beings, but in a pre-givenness structure that is only limitedly determined — is always already constituted and for all. In the nexus of progressing experience, a progressively unified, richer, expanded pre-givenness is established, and so the world for all — which is always already that — transmits the enrichments of individuals and groups to ever further individuals and groups.

World as world of experience is (1) relative in the way that it is the world for the individual experiencer, despite the communication and tradition already belonging to it; (2) relative also insofar as it progressively transforms itself in progressing communication. In this stream of transformations, the world of experience is continually *unifying* — not only correcting but *enriching* — and yet the same. Otherwise it has no being at all other than the world that is there for us. *This is its primal historicity* (*Urhistorizität*).

This is one of the key concepts of the text: *Urhistorizität*. The world is not a timeless structure but an inherently historical formation — constituted through ongoing processes of tradition, communication, enrichment, and correction. Its identity is not the identity of an unchanging essence but the identity of a *process* that maintains itself through change. The relativity of pre-givenness is not a defect but an essential structural feature of the world's way of being.

**[§, pp. 55–56]** Husserl now distinguishes two forms of horizontality. (a) The familiar style of the constitution of the human world is the first sense of pre-givenness structure: a constructable, familiar style whose familiarity stands out in the course of all actual world-experience in its thoroughly familiar forms — a familiar form that constantly repeats itself and precisely thereby *is* a form. (b) But the world-experience running in this familiar style has a *further, unformed horizon* that shows itself only in the actual historical development, when this becomes conscious as a historical transformation of previous, relatively stable pre-givenness structures. A fixed surrounding-world structure as pre-given can change and has changed, repeatedly in human history. This motivates an open horizon of possible changes and possible new pre-givenness styles that one cannot, however, construct in advance by interrogating one's world as world of actual and possible experience. This *second horizontality*, as surpassing the first, designates the openly possible and historically already exemplified *relativity of pre-givenness* in the original sense.

The distinction between first and second horizontality is crucial. First horizontality is the familiar, constructable style of the pre-given world — the typification within which we ordinarily move. Second horizontality is the horizon of possible *transformation of the style itself* — the open possibility that the entire framework of pre-givenness could change in ways not foreseeable from within the current framework. The first horizontality yields an eidetic a priori (the invariant structures of a given familiar world); the second horizontality marks the limits of this a priori, pointing toward a historical openness that no eidetic analysis can fully anticipate.

**[§, pp. 56–58]** A priori it is clear: if I have a world in my world-horizon, I have not only the factual, finite, horizon-laden world, but also my world constructable in infinitum as a world of possible experiences in a play-space of infinities of possibilities. However, all variations in the pre-givenness style remain within the possibility of communication; as such, they maintain the character of "appearances" of *the same one world*. Every such synthesis presupposes an *essential community of structure* among these "worlds." Through all conceivable synthesis of subjectively and historically valid worlds, an *a priori structure* runs. The question about the world as world of experience, eidetically posed, is therefore the question about the intuitively constructable a priori that is the "condition of possibility" for the fact that, however world-constitution may proceed, in the universally progressing nexus one and the same world can and must emerge. This would be an ontology of the world as world of possible experience — equivalently, of a world that is a human world. This ontology would be an a priori anthropology. What is established in it holds in unconditional necessity and universality as bounding the invariable essential sense of this and any world.

This passage reaches the systematic core of Text Nr. 7: the a priori of the life-world. Despite the historical relativity of all pre-givenness styles (second horizontality), there must be invariant structures that make possible the synthesis of all relative worlds into one and the same world (first horizontality at its most fundamental). These invariant structures are the subject-matter of life-world ontology — what Husserl here calls "a priori anthropology." This ontology seeks the unconditionally necessary structures that any world, for any community of subjects, must exhibit.

**[§, pp. 58–59]** Husserl notes that scientific culture itself belongs to the experiential world for those living within it — science is an experiential object like other cultural formations. The world of experience thus includes objects that "express," "communicate," "theorize" about what is within the experiential domain. Cultural objects are not merely in the world but "relate to" other objects, serve purposes for subjects as subjects of habitual interests in a communicative circle of humans living in a generative life-community, having a communally familiar world in the unity of enduring traditionality. Interests of different individuals interweave in harmonizing and disharmonizing, in accord and conflict; all are connected with all in the unity of a practical existence of family, tribe, village, state, etc.

This concluding section of Text Nr. 7 broadens the analysis to the social-practical dimension of the life-world. The world is not merely a cognitive construct but a *practical* world — a world of interests, purposes, utilities, and communal bonds. Cultural objects, including scientific theories, are world-objects with a distinctive structure: they "refer to" other objects and serve the purposes of subjects within communicative communities.

### Text Nr. 8. The Life-World Pre-Given in Familiar Individual and Species Types (*Die in vertrauten Individual- und Arttypen vorgegebene Lebenswelt*)

*[Beginning of March 1933]*

**[Content summary]** Starting from the analysis of animal beings in the life-environment into their physical substrate-structure and psychic substantial structure, an important general observation is carried out: the life-environment, the familiar world, has the pervading general structure of a world of realities familiar in individual types and in species types. The identity of each real thing is initially identity within its species-apperception. But through the transformation in which, with the break of individual-typical unity, being transforms into non-being and from one species-character new species-character arises (e.g. in the breaking of a drinking glass) — through all this: identical world — and an identity-structure.

**[§, pp. 60–61]** Husserl begins: world-consciousness, which constantly runs through our waking life, is the world-consciousness of everyone. But each person is themselves contained in this world-consciousness — the consciousness of the already familiar ("pre-given") world, the "life-world" (*Lebenswelt*): each as "I, this human being" for themselves, and each other as another, known or unknown, encountered in their consciousness-world or indirectly accessible through communication. World-consciousness is consciousness in the mode of being-certainty not through a specially occurring act of being-positing or predicative existential judgment. All that already presupposes world-consciousness in being-certainty. If I grasp something in my perceptual field — looking at a book on the table — I grasp something that was already there for me as existing, already "there," as this whole study-room with all perceptually prominent objects is already for me, together with the unseen side and its familiar things.

This passage provides a phenomenologically rich description of the texture of world-consciousness as lived. The key insight is that world-consciousness is *prior to* all specific acts of being-positing. We do not first encounter objects and then judge them to exist; rather, we find ourselves always already in a world of beings whose existence is not in question. The "being-certainty" (*Seinsgewissheit*) of the world is not a judgment but a pre-predicative, pre-thematic ground that all judgment presupposes.

**[§, p. 61, continuation]** Beyond the immediate perceptual field, my world-consciousness has its dark, individually awakened but on the whole unawakened horizon (my family, friends, colleagues, compatriots, etc.) — the horizon of those directly and indirectly known, determinately and indeterminately known. A more precise explication of world-consciousness as the basis of actual thematic acts is needed to make visible that they "take out" from the "pre-given" world what interests them — always already something existing, not first receiving being-validity through these acts.

**[§, pp. 61–63]** The pre-given world as a world of spatiotemporally individuated objects — realities — is the already familiar world, whose horizon is well-known as a prefiguration and stylistically already familiar. It must not be overlooked that this familiar world as familiar is a *typical world* — for example, a human body belongs as physical unity within a type, a "species" of "organisms." In the pre-given surrounding world, inorganic things, organisms, animals, plants are familiarities constantly creating new familiarities in ongoing experience. The objects are normally equipped with familiar cultural predicates; they are concretely typical cultural objects. The world has for us a unified normal structure as our cultural environment.

Husserl's crucial insight here concerns the *typicality* of the life-world. The world is not given as a chaos of individuals but as a field structured by *types* — individual types (this particular glass, this tree) and species types (glass, tree, human). These types are not concepts imposed by the intellect; they are sedimented apperceptive structures through which things are encountered in the first place. The identity of a real thing is initially identity *within its type*: the glass is the same glass so long as it remains within the type "glass."

**[§, pp. 62–63]** Husserl's footnote introduces an important contrast: for the natural scientist, what is in question is the *identical in the change of typification*. The scientist is guided by the idea of a nature existing in itself. The surrounding world in its familiarity, its typification, is subjective-relative (the circle of what is straightforwardly re-recognizable). The natural-scientific method shows that — but only under its application — a higher re-recognizing, identifying is possible, whose identical does not limit itself to the familiar of the surrounding world. By contrast, in the life-world: a real thing is only so long existing as it is the same in the same type. The broken glass is no more — no more glass; the dead organism is no more.

This contrast between life-world identity and scientific identity is fundamental. In the life-world, identity is *type-bound*: when the type breaks down (the glass shatters, the organism dies), the thing ceases to be. The natural scientist, by contrast, pursues identity *through* type-changes, guided by the idea of a nature that persists through all transformations. This difference between life-world typicality and scientific idealization is one of the core themes of Husserl's late philosophy.

**[§, pp. 63–64]** Every cultural object has its unified spiritual sense of being as object of the pre-given life-world only in connection with the founding individual-typical unity. Pre-given is everything toward which I or anyone can direct themselves "straightforwardly" — what is "already" in the perceptual field, the intuitive field, and its horizon. This already-being is the problem; and in concrete totality, precisely the *life-world* (*Lebenswelt*), the unitarily pre-given — always existing as transformation of individual-typically identical realities. Also the unknown, the indeterminate of the known, the completely unknown manifold of beings encompassed by the horizon is pre-given. World-consciousness is *world-apperception*. Simple world-apperception is apperception of the surrounding world, the life-world, which, changing in its individual-typical things in the flow, constantly has a familiar fixed structure, an individual-typical one. In the change of surrounding-world apperception that I live through as experience, "the" world shows itself — the stable life-world in its *invariant pre-givenness structure*.

This concluding section of Text Nr. 8 provides a definitive statement of the concept of life-world. The life-world is the concretely pre-given totality of beings structured by familiar types; it is what is "already there" for any straightforward directedness. World-consciousness is world-apperception — a term that captures the always-already-operative character of our relation to the world. Within the flux of changing apperceptions, the world shows itself as stable, as having an *invariant pre-givenness structure*. This invariant structure — exhibited through the changes but not identical with any particular state — is what Husserl's ontology of the life-world seeks to describe.

**[§, pp. 64–65]** Constantly, in every moment, there is systematically prefigured how, in which directions, in which ways I can direct the course of my world-experience. From actual world-experience, a universe of possibilities of further world-experiencing is prefigured. Every actual experience is experience of the relevant object in its kind (*Art*). Object-experience as apperception has the structure of horizontally sided experience — in continuous progression a synthetic self-presenting of the same object in its various sides, a progressive self-showing of the object within the constant horizon of still-unseen, possibly becoming-seen sides. Experience constantly has a *familiarity-structure* (*Bekanntheitsstruktur*), even where the object has never before been perceived: namely, the familiarity of *species-typicality* (*Artmäßigkeit*), and this at various levels of "generality" — which must not be interpreted as conceptual generality or generality arising from comparison. This species-generality always already *presupposes* the generality lodged in the species-structure (the familiarity-structure).

This final paragraph of Text Nr. 8 as excerpted here completes the analysis of typicality by distinguishing it sharply from conceptual generality. The familiarity-structure of the life-world — the fact that every object is encountered as an instance of its type — is *not* a product of conceptual abstraction or comparison. It is a pre-conceptual, apperceptive structure that makes conceptual generality possible in the first place. The species-type is the condition of recognition, not its result.

### Text Nr. 9. Explication of the Logical Ideas "Reality of the World" and "World Itself." World as Experiential Horizon and World as Logical Idea. Logical Horizon and World-Concept. Total Concept of the Real and Total Concept of the World (*Auslegung der logischen Ideen „Reales der Welt" und „Welt selbst"*)

*[ca. 1932]*

**[§, p. 67]** Husserl opens with a fundamental ontological distinction: the world is not a "substance" in the same sense as an individual reality is a substance. The determination of any real thing leads essentially into inner and outer infinities; it thus already necessarily presupposes in its ultimate regard the horizon of the totality "world," which is nothing other than the totality, the allness of realities. The being of every real thing is being through anticipation — being from a givenness that is necessarily at the same time pre-givenness, and this in infinitum. To givenness essentially belongs the potentiality of judicative and truth-aimed being-positing. From this there is a priori prefigured the idea of the true being of this real thing in the totality of its truth. But this is a *logical product* — an essentially possible one on the ground of experience and its horizontality — and not yet the construction of the idea, which is realized only as a constructive product.

This dense opening paragraph establishes that individual realities and the world have fundamentally different ontological statuses. An individual reality is a "substance" — a substrate of determinations. But the world is not a substance in this sense; it is the *totality* of all such substances, presupposed as horizon by every act of determining any individual thing. The concept of world-as-totality is a *logical construction* that goes beyond the mere horizonal givenness of the world in experience.

**[§, pp. 68–69]** The world is not experienced or experientially determinable like a real thing in the ordinary sense of a "thing"; it is not given-pre-given as such a thing is. The givenness of individual realities precedes the givenness of the world. In the transition from one reality to others in its surroundings, a synthetic experience unfolds that is experience and not judicatively synthesizing action. This is *universal experience* and in a certain sense already *world-experience*, insofar as everything grasped enters into habitual knowledge and even everything ungrasped contributes to the intentional unity-effect. But belonging to this is the *potentiality* of the judicatively positing world-grasping, of the logical construction of the *idea "world" as a logical idea* — initially as a logical substrate for predications. First comes the unity of experience belonging to the unity of waking life, the pre-logical unity of pre-logically experientially determined and indeterminately horizontally anticipated objects, whose horizons intertwine with one another — unity of universal "association." On this ground then "logical" activity: logically formed, proper objectualities and reality-grasping and categorially linking activity. Every reality has its to-be-constructed idea of its full and whole being (in truth). From logical activities — since we mature humans have always already been logically, judgingly, cognizingly active — every experienced thing already has overlaid its *logical horizon*, but as a *founded* one, a horizon of the second level. The *world-concept* is a "logical" formation of a higher level — the concept "allness of realities."

This passage contains one of Husserl's most careful analyses of the relationship between experiential and logical constitution. The world as experienced is a horizon — an ever-present background of all particular experience. But the world as *concept* (the "allness of realities") is a logical formation that must be actively *constructed* on the basis of the experiential horizon. Between the lived world-horizon and the conceptual world-idea lies a constitutive gap that only logical activity can bridge. Importantly, this logical construction is already underway in normal mature life: we do not first have bare experience and then construct the concept "world" in a separate step. Rather, every experienced thing already carries a logical horizon — a second-level horizon founded on the experiential one.

**[§, pp. 70–71]** We must distinguish: everything is already thought of as continuedly judgeable, as something one can deal with and whose truth (situational truth) one can ask about. But the idea of a being in the infinite totality of its determinations is not yet constructed thereby. The *total concept of the real presupposes that of the world*, and vice versa — a fundamental circularity. The concepts refer us in their validity to the originary intentionality of experience, in which the real is to arise as existing — in its presumption, which always carries new presumption in itself, fulfilling itself in consistent concordance. Every presumptive positing is non-self-sufficient; it necessarily has its horizon, referring to a continuity of concordance. Pre-experience is itself already a mode of experience, self-givenness not of self-presence but of anticipatory accessibility, possibly under correction.

Husserl identifies a deep circularity in the concepts of "reality" and "world": the total determination of any individual real thing presupposes the totality of the world, but the world is constituted only as the totality of individual realities. This circularity is not vicious; it points back to the originary intentionality of experience, where both concepts have their constitutive root. The concept of presumptive validity captures how being is given in experience: always anticipatorily, always subject to further determination and correction, but always within a framework of concordance that itself carries the title "world."

**[§, pp. 71–72]** The style of experience is moveable — it can transform itself in the course of ongoing experience, but in a way that does not abandon the earlier style but raises it to a new level. When an experience-type of real objectuality becomes a "transcendental clue," the given experiential unity is given as an exemplar of the type. The typical structure of the object-type is found through explication, yielding the objectual style as that of possible experiential directions. In the expansion of the surrounding world through synthesis of relative surrounding worlds, *nature* then separates itself as an identity-structure that is always the presupposition for predicates of objective spirit. In the advance toward universality, toward infinity as totality, the idea of pure infinite nature arises. Objective spirit refers to the human being who sets purposes. Human beings can set themselves unconditional purposes — forming the practical total-idea of an all-humanity of a total "infinite" world.

This concluding passage of Text Nr. 9 traces the path from individual experience through typification and the synthesis of surrounding worlds to the idea of a universal nature and ultimately to the practical idea of an all-encompassing humanity. The teleological dimension — the setting of unconditional purposes — introduces a practical-ethical horizon that goes beyond the merely cognitive constitution of the world.

### Supplement V. Horizon-Consciousness of the World and Thematic World-Representation. Thing-Representation versus World-Representation: World-Experience and World-Certainty (*Horizontbewusstsein von der Welt und thematische Weltvorstellung*)

*[December 1933 or January 1934]*

**[Content summary]** World as that in which all beings (all that is certain-of-being for me) inexists. In this sense world is no being, no reality, and world-experience has a new sense. Construction of the world as openly-infinite plurality, allness of realities. World as totality, theme of world-science (cosmology).

**[§, pp. 73–74]** Whatever I am thematically occupied with, it is conscious to me as *existing in the world*. I constantly have, with everything and anything, being-certainty of the world — a priori of every individual experience, every perceptual field, every individual thematics: as horizon-certainty in continuous constancy through the change of thematics. To this belongs the certainty of my constant capacity to clarify the mere "horizon-consciousness" of the world into an explicit world-representation. I always have my perceptual field, an intuitive field of individually determinate existing things as an individual constellation — but in the horizon-consciousness as things "in" the world, "from" the world. Everything for me individually certain in its factual reality has *certainty* as a modality of certainty: habitual in-certainty being-for-me. But certainty as primal mode is itself modality insofar as simple certainty could break and pass into doubt, presumption, or non-being. *All individually existing things for me are thus in suspension between being and non-being.* But I "know" that it need not remain at a modality of uncertainty. *I am constantly in a world-certainty* — completely certain that ultimately the domain of the experienceable can be brought into a unity of concordance of experience.

This is one of the most important passages in the entire volume for the concept of *Weltgewissheit* (world-certainty). Husserl argues that while every individual being is in principle subject to modalization — it could turn out to be illusory, could be doubted, could be negated — the *world itself* is immune to this kind of doubt. World-certainty is not a judgment but a structural feature of all experience: a horizon-certainty that undergirds all particular certainties and survives all particular doubts. The world is always "on the march" toward total concordant certainty; its being is the being of this ongoing march.

**[§, pp. 75–77]** Progressive experience requires guidance through anticipatory representation — a pre-representation (*Vor-Vorstellung*). But progressive experience is bounded, and to want to get to know the world experientially is to enter into the pre-given, horizonally conscious surrounding world — an entering that remains bound to the finite, though in a finitude that has no last step, that is open finitude and open infinity in one. I can construct not merely a pre-draft of the next reality but a "world-representation" as a whole: world "in advance" represented in its possibilities. *"World" has for me only explicit sense from this world-representation.* The world-representation is the basis of all conceptual world-knowledge, all sciences referring to the world. But concerns arise: is world-representation really on a par with thing-representation? The world is the allness of realities — does not an original "representation" of the world require an all-embracing experience that encompasses all individual experiences? I have experience of the world only in that I have my total domain of what is actually experienced and the consciousness that I could potentially go further.

Husserl introduces the concept of *Weltvorstellung* — world-representation — as the explicit, thematic awareness of the world as a whole. This is not something we naturally have; it must be actively *constructed* through a specific reflective operation. Normally, we deal with individual things and have the world only as an implicit horizon. The world-representation is the thematization of this horizon, and it is the foundation of all scientific world-knowledge. But as Husserl notes, there is a fundamental disanalogy between thing-representation and world-representation: we can encounter a thing itself, but we can never encounter the world itself — only more and more things "from" the world.

**[§, pp. 77–80]** The essential differences must not be blurred. Things are objects of possible experience, objects of possible activity. They have as existing for us their origin from acts, from thematicity. Thing-constitution requires nothing yet of a world-constitution in a parallel sense. World as horizon of thingly experience is not yet *world-experience*, which requires its own objectivation, identification, recognition. But how does the constitution of the world as All look? How is its origin as "object" to be understood? Individuation arises together with horizon-formation. Plurality arises in step-wise collecting. The experiential life, the life in the progress of activity, of grasping and explicating — this concrete intuitive experiential field in "anticipation" as graspable, explicable, identifiable, one after another — a life in the course of these activities that repeats itself in style but is a unitary subjective happening. I can reflect on this stream, hold still and take *survey* (*Umschau, Überschau*), entering into the past of memory, exercising universal retrospection and prospection. All of this is already prefigured in indeterminate generality, in the ontological form "world." What is needed is the *systematic construction of the infinity of possible experiences* — the display of the a priori level-ordering of the directional systems of possible experiential advance: (1) primordialy the kinaesthetic system; (2) intersubjective significance through empathy and the system of mediate empathy — others who have further others, access-systems of higher levels. Plato "spoke directly" to me insofar as he published writings addressed to his future readers. Especially: the systematics of generations and generative interconnections.

This extraordinary passage sketches the full systematic program of a life-world ontology as a theory of *access-systems* (*Zugangssysteme*). The world is constituted not merely through perception but through a layered system of access-possibilities: kinaesthetic access (bodily movement), mnemonic access (memory), empathic access (understanding of others), generative access (tradition across generations), and documentary access (written communication across time). The reference to Plato illustrates the latter: Plato "spoke directly" to Husserl through his writings, establishing a communicative access across millennia.

### Supplement VI. Experience as Method of Individual-Thingly Cognizance. World-Experience — Individual Experience. World-Consciousness as Horizon versus "World-Experience" (*Erfahrung als Methode einzeldinglicher Kenntnisnahme*)

*[December 1933 or January 1934]*

**[§, pp. 81–83]** Husserl begins with an observation about the structure of ordinary perception: in explicating perception of a corporeal thing, the bodily processes involved — as exercising real causality — remain unnoticed. Even when we cut open or break an object to learn its interior, we do not thematize this causality. The thing "as it is" is synthetically constituted through such subjective doing; its being-sense as thing of possible experience has such subjective accomplishment-processes as its essential correlate. But perception in the proper, normal sense as explicating cognizance-taking has, for nature-objects — correlatively to their ontological structure — its universal essential character: it is the correlative essential-general *method* of getting to know nature-objects in their "features" and nature altogether. Nature is given to us with a core of immediate intuitiveness, in a horizontality requiring systematic continuation of perception to advance to what it itself is.

**[§, p. 83]** The world is constituted as existing — as the existing all-unity of in-themselves-existing realities. It is a constituted "formation" in the streamingly world-constituting subjectivity, but not as a finished product comparable to what we in the naivety of natural world-life have acquired as a product of production that is now finished. What is finished constantly is the being-sense "existing all-unity of in-themselves-existing realities." But it is a being-sense, something constantly valid in the streaming constitution — in the constancy of the play of fulfilled pre-validity and unfulfilled anticipating validity. Yet world is not constituted like individual realities; *it is originally the constantly self-transforming yet unitarily remaining horizon in each individual reality, unthematic horizon.* Making world a theme, wanting to "experience" world as a universe of possible experience — all this is preceded by the unthematic world-horizon. And so the *horizon-problem* remains distinct from the problem of "world-experience."

This final distinction is crucial: the world-horizon and world-experience are not the same thing. The horizon is the unthematic, ever-present background of all particular experience. World-experience is the explicit, thematic engagement with the world as a whole. The former precedes and makes possible the latter, but the latter is a distinct constitutive achievement that goes beyond the horizon as such.

### Text Nr. 10. The Streaming, Self-Maintaining-through-Modalizations World-Consciousness with Its Empathy-Horizon and Its Time-Horizons (*Das strömende, sich durch Modalisierungen in Einstimmigkeit erhaltende Weltbewusstsein mit seinem Einfühlungshorizont und seinen Zeithorizonten*)

*[ca. 1933]*

#### § 1. The Reduction to the World Purely as World of Experience

**[§, p. 85]** Constantly the world is for me and for us in experience as that in which we ourselves live as human beings, into which we live, and to which we ourselves belong as worldly realities, as experiential constituents. "Experience" is an essentially ambiguous expression. Here it shall mean: the streaming concrete consciousness of originally conscious (perceived) world. This concrete, originally self-giving, perceiving world-consciousness carries in itself as co-valid the continuity of my subjective past world-consciousness and therein again implicitly the horizontally co-valid past world-perceptions of my experiential associates, likewise past world-subjects and their past perceptions — with the prefigured future of future perceptual course (own and foreign) also thought of. The concrete waking world-consciousness has, beyond what properly presents itself, validity-moments: the "empty," non-intuitive meanings, among them the possibly theoretical, perhaps groundless, object-foreign opinions.

**[§, pp. 85–87]** When we abstractively reduce the perceptually existing world to the world of pure perception, pure experience, the concept of perception and experience, and also of appearance and aspect, ramifies in multiple ways. "Perception" is ambiguous: perception as concretely self-giving consciousness; then ontically, perception as the object conscious in the character of "itself there," of originality; perception as this perceived object in the What and How. This gives various distinctions: for corporeality, the differences of side-givenness, near-far orientation, etc.; in another direction, differences of validity-modes — the occasional shift from "being simply" to "illusion" or to possibility, probability, dubiousness. Perception in the pregnant sense is perception in the validity-mode of certainty, where the certainty includes the horizon of continuing validity.

**[§, pp. 87–88]** My concrete streaming world-perception has its *past-horizon* of past concrete streaming world-perceptions. Each waking phase of my life was world-certainty. But past perception now holds only insofar as it has not experienced cancellation or modalization. Through the perceiving world-life runs a *process of modalization* — a process of correction regarding the synthetic universe of perceptual validity. The world that is valid as originally given and that constantly projects the certainty of continuing validity is exclusively meant and in certainty-validity as the world of unmodalized perceptions.

#### § 2. The Streaming World-Perception and Its Empathy-Horizon

**[§, pp. 88–90]** Husserl now considers what comes into question through the horizons of actual and possible empathy belonging to my concretely perceptual world-consciousness, and the unity of the actual and potential synthesis of my own world-consciousness-life with the many world-consciousness-processes of the co-subjects. As valid world — ours and not merely mine — only that holds which, in the disclosure of foreign perception, co-holds for me. My concrete streaming present consciousness perceives world-present through original being-certainty. In this streaming present-life, actual and potential empathy is implicated, and thereby — foreign life-present, foreign life-past and life-future appear as *implicates*. I have not only the synthesis of my "own" immanent time but also an *empathy-horizon* through which being and consciousness-life of foreign consciousness-subjects is there for me. The *total synthesis* connects my immanent life-time with the open "infinity" of horizonally known or unknown co-subjects. Constantly constituted is an *intersubjective present* as synthetic intersubjective simultaneity of life-streams.

This section provides one of Husserl's most detailed analyses of how intersubjectivity is constituted within the streaming world-consciousness. The key idea is that my own world-consciousness *implicates* — necessarily carries within itself as co-valid — the world-consciousness of others. This implication is not a theoretical inference but a structural feature of the world-apperception itself. Through the empathy-horizon, my streaming present opens onto an infinity of other streaming presents, forming a total synthesis that constitutes the intersubjective world as "our" world.

#### § 3. Modalizations of Recollection and Empathy

**[§, pp. 91–93]** Modalizations of recollection as such and of presentifying empathy as such: here what is decided is not what stands as worldly being-certainty but what really was my own past being, my own past life. Immanent recollections occasionally fail to maintain their being-certainty. But unbreakable remains the certainty that I was, that I had a past life. The unity of a past of one's own consciousness-life is unbreakable certainty, with the potentiality of being able to concretely display it intuitively and to equalize the modalizations to a standing concordance. The modalizations are themselves events belonging to the streaming life and enter into memory. Even these memories could be deceiving — and so the same applies iteratively. And yet everything fits into the unity of a fulfilled immanent temporality.

A second sphere of modalizations concerns not the immanent being of empathies but their presentifying function — the being-certainty of the *foreign* thereby presented. Empathy transcends not only my consciousness-present but, in one with it, my entire life-past and life-future as co-valid.

#### § 4. The Empathy-Horizon as Validity-Horizon

**[§, pp. 94–95]** In the concretion of my livingly streaming consciousness-present we always have, not only recollection-validities in the horizon, but also an *empathy-horizon as validity-horizon*. In my living conscious being, foreign conscious being is always also implicated. I am who I am, but as ego being, I carry others as being-for-me within me. As soon as the other is a validity-unity in me, they are, with the sense "other," simultaneously another ego that implicates mine in its being. We have the mediate intersubjective relations: the other carrying others in their horizon — an openly endless manifold of others of others of others.

Now, the empathy, the experience of the alter ego, has been considered purely as intentional implication of foreign consciousness-life in one's own. But we have not yet considered that living streaming consciousness is *world-consciousness*, even world-perception. Does not every empathy already involve worldliness? What I bring to being-certainty as foreign co-present life is not merely consciousness-life but soul-life, experienced psychophysically as animating a worldly body. The intentional analysis of the transcendental own life in its self-transcendence here brings to light the *founding relations* of different transcending functions — the functions of presentification intertwined with the transcendences of "mundane perception." The extraordinary difficulties of showing how the constant unity-accomplishment "world" comes about transcendentally, in which consciousness itself appears worldlicized — how the objectivation-accomplishing subjectivity itself comes to the apperceptive sense "human" while ever-presupposed as actively accomplishing subjectivity but remaining hidden.

### Supplement VII. A Fundamental Piece of the Theory of the Horizon-Structure of World-Experience: Intuitive Core-Sphere and Outer Horizon with Its Standing-Streaming Near-Far Structure (*Ein Grundstück der Lehre von der Horizontstruktur der Welterfahrung*)

*[March 1933]*

**[§, pp. 96–98]** Husserl considers the essential structure of the world insofar as it has, from the core-sphere of experience (the perceptual field), a livingly co-prefigured outer horizon. This yields in the horizontality of the respective experiential world itself a "core" whose outer horizon consists in a potentiality of advancing from living experiential surrounding world to new experiential surrounding world, from prefiguration to new prefiguration. There is an *iteration in the awakenings* — a mediacy of mutually implicated actual and possible awakenings. The nearest and next-following are in a *graduality of liveliness*, of actual awakeness. This has a *limit, a zero*, of awakedness. In streaming experience this form remains, but the respective degree of awakening changes within a structural systematics. This structure prefigures itself continuously. We have not only a streaming intentionality of nearness and farness regarding perceptual fulfilment, but a co-streaming *iteration and implication of the awakenings of the outermost horizon*. The whole process in its structure of streaming is not only streaming but consciousness in prefiguration of the structure of this streaming.

This dense supplement introduces the concept of a *standing-streaming near-far structure* as the fundamental form of the world-horizon. The experiential world has an intuitive core (the perceptual field) surrounded by layers of decreasing vividness, from the near horizon through the progressively more remote to a limit-zero of awakedness — the outermost, wholly empty horizon. This structure is both "standing" (it maintains its form) and "streaming" (its particular contents constantly change). The iteration of awakenings — each layer of the horizon implying further layers beyond it — generates the infinity of the world-horizon from within the finitude of actual experience.

### Text Nr. 11. Pre-Givenness and Horizon. Horizon-Pre-Givenness of Pre-Given Realities. Various Modes of Foreground and Background. Milieu of the Unconscious. Inner Horizon and Outer Horizon (*Vorgegebenheit und Horizont*)

*[March 1933]*

**[§, pp. 99–100]** Husserl begins with the thematization of any real thing: the direction toward the corporeal in its extensional determinations is privileged. The human being, for example, is originally experienced through the apperception of the human body in its type of organic corporeality. But the pre-given field contains the real "human being" as a whole; the turning-over goes into explication. The concrete real thing is initially *simply straightforwardly self-given* — grasped on the basis of affection in one grasping ray, already as what it is, but so that its manifold and systematically structured being-sense is still enfolded, ununfolded (implicit).

**[§, pp. 100–102]** Already while the real thing is a "background"-object, it is conscious in a mode of self-presentation carrying an implicit sense-structure. Several modes of givenness are distinguished: background-mode before affection, in affection, transforming into the active mode of foreground-object before explication, and then in the process of explication itself — the unfolding of the is-nesses. Corresponding to all these modes are modes of being-certainty and being-validity.

Beyond all the modes of foreground and background discussed, there is an entirely different, never-absent background lying "behind" all of them: the *milieu of the unconscious* (*Unbewusstseinsmilieu*). This is the dark, non-intuitive horizon of co-valid realities in the validity-modes of memory-intentionality with the ontic sense-form of real spatiotemporality — a potential outside-one-another of potential intuitive fields with their potential backgrounds and foregrounds, and this whole continuity as a domain of possible awakening, possible affection.

**[§, pp. 102–104]** The *horizontality* — the unconscious-milieu surrounding whatever is specifically conscious — is *double*: (1) it concerns the substrate-structure of intuitively self-presenting realities insofar as they have their actual and potential horizon of explication (*inner horizon*); (2) the concrete objects stand in *outer horizons*, in their intuitive fields, which themselves have sense-determinations "from outside." The inner horizon concerns the concrete objects in their substrate-structure — the horizons of the explicates, the so-being. The outer horizon connects each object with its surroundings.

What has been displayed is obviously a fundamental piece of the *theory of the pre-givenness of the world*. But it is only a beginning: missing is the consideration of the structure of modalization and correction, and the constitution of the world in relation to the modes of intersubjectivity.

### Text Nr. 12. Experience of Realities. Systematics for the Disclosure of Horizons: The Structure of the Total "Memory"-Horizon (*Erfahrung von Realem. Systematisches zur Erschließung der Horizonte*)

*[May–June 1932]*

#### § 1. Thing-Experience as Concordant Substrate-Experience. "Smooth Identification" and First Identity of the Substrate

**[§, pp. 105–110]** Husserl analyses the structure of thing-experience in careful detail. Experience is self-presentation, self-giving. Things have their ways of self-presenting. Uncontested self-presentation involves apperception with the distinction between what is properly perceived and what is ad-perceived (appresented). Every thing-meaning that is not actual experience points back to intuition. The horizon of apperception means not only that some determinations are not yet self-given but also that the already self-presented depends on the anticipated for confirmation. The self of the existing thing is realized exclusively in self-presentation in stages of relative completeness. I count on future experience expanding the self-givenness.

Noematic-ontic explication: every experienced item in experience refers to possible further experience in which more of the same thing becomes visible, and to possible experiences in which the already experienced is experienced anew. In "concordant" experiencing lies the structure of continuous identification, the consciousness of being-certainty of the identical, the one, that presents itself therein as itself. The identical of concordant experience is constituted as that which explicates itself in manifold identity-moments — unselfstanding identicals — in which the total-identical, the total object, is. The first and most originary self-showing is the stretch of identification that lets "smooth identification" prevail in the properties — without modal correction. This first identity of the substrate can be repeatedly experienced. But then experience goes further: the property-moments change in their own experiential content.

#### § 2. The Multi-Dimensional Horizon-Structure of Thing-Experience

**[§, pp. 112–117]** Every thing-experience is from the first moment on a perception extending in continuous change. Its experiencing meaning has an *open horizon of possible experiences of the same thing*. These are not actually present in advance as particular anticipated experiences, but it is a consciousness-horizon, a manner of *implicitly meaning beyond* what is properly experienced — a "non-intuitive," "vague" meaning that shows itself as such in possible experiences. In such disclosures it appears that the horizon has a structure — an ordering-form of immediacy and mediacy of implication of experiences and experiential contents. From every actual experience one can make evident what it, as *apperception* of this thing, *means* in total (ad-perceives). The horizon-implication is continuously *implicating mediacies of implication in advance* and in its vagueness implies infinities of disjunctive possibilities in the manner of disjunctive leaving-open. It implies a determinate *multi-dimensionality* leaving open various possible paths of experiential synthesis.

Every individual-real perception is a *piece of a perceptual field* with its configuration of individual realities as now presented. The perceptual field is in streaming; in its streaming transformation it constantly carries — streamingly — a retention-horizon of still-conscious earlier perceptual fields, a protentional horizon, and the horizon of the appresented further surroundings. The spatiotemporal form is not merely a horizon of unknowns but also the form of the already known. The world is "homogeneous" in spatiotemporal regard. The spatiotemporality is the *infinite total-form* — the in-principle only horizonally representable form of all possible "finite" forms. If we now consider the given world as merely possible and freely vary it, we gain the *eidos "world"* as the invariant essence belonging to every possible world.

This passage provides the systematic core of Text Nr. 12: the multi-dimensional horizon-structure of thing-experience reveals, when traced through all its implications, the spatiotemporal total-form of the world as an *invariant* structure — the eidos "world." The path leads from individual thing-experience through the perceptual field and its horizons to the total world-horizon and its a priori form.

### Text Nr. 13. The Spatiotemporal Total-Horizon of the World and Its Invariant Form (*Der raumzeitliche Totalhorizont der Welt und seine invariante Form*)

*[ca. September 1931]*

**[§, pp. 118–124]** Husserl describes the transcendental reduction and the discovery of the world as transcendental phenomenon. The world, in all my occupations, is always already pre-given — partly actually conscious, partly implicitly presupposed. In eidetic attitude I have ontic a priori as related to my ego, and noetic a priori of the constituting subjective — both in unity: possible world as conscious for me, valid in all possibilities.

Horizontality in which the world is given as existing in a being-sense: at every time-modal position in a horizon of so-being-possibilities, open as to which is actual. All changeable prefiguration holds itself within a constant and constantly anticipated *form-structure*. The given world is *spatiotemporal*, and every totally unknown object is also known therein — that it is at every time-point of its duration in infinite space, the form of simultaneous objects. Every possibility of any real thing has an invariant form that never itself varies, that is necessarily the selfsame everywhere. *The world is "homogeneous" in spatiotemporal regard* — for all its "things" (realities and their "nature"), but also for all real pluralities, which are "configured" in known fact as in every possibility, i.e. have a multi-unitary shape. The spatiotemporality is the infinite total-form.

Correlatively, we have for the world itself formal homogeneity. The determinate realities and shapes are in every factual world-present themselves different, but they are shapes of a homogeneous space as form of simultaneity and form of all movements and deformations. The form is the encompassing *unchangeable universal shape* that encompasses all particular shapes and their variations.

### Supplement VIII. Systematically Explicating the Constitution of the Pre-Given World — That Is Systematically Explicating Its Horizon-Structure. The Intertwining of Horizons. Important Points on the Theory of Horizons (*Die Konstitution der vorgegebenen Welt systematisch auslegen*)

*[End of May 1934]*

**[§, pp. 125–129]** Husserl asks: what does it mean that the world is pre-given, that its being is the fundamental a priori? "Knowing about the being of the world" has a double sense. Without actual experience of things I would know nothing of the world. But no simple experience and no synthetic experience of many objects is experience of the world. Single-experience precedes world-experience. A multiplicity of experienced objects collected together is not the same as the world.

(a) Every experience has its *experiential horizon* — a kernel of real and determinate cognizance and, beyond this kernel of so-being, a horizon. Every experience is extendable into a continuity of individual experiences, synthetically united as a single, openly endless experience of the same. (b) Every experienced thing-group has an openly endless *outer horizon* of co-objects — a horizon of the second level implying that of the first. Going deeper, one comes to a complicated horizon-structure with an intertwining of horizons in every individual-thingly experience, with a reference from first to later horizons.

The world in its possibilities is constantly anticipated "a priori." From every experienced reality, the pre-given world is to be constructed in its world-form as universal spatiotemporal nature. This is the ground of being-validity: the a priori implied in all explicit being-validity. The horizon-structure systematically explicated is the intentionality in which world is constituted.

### Text Nr. 14. Universal World-Consciousness as World-Perception. The Universal Synthesis of Horizons Implicated in World-Appearing. The Worldlicizing Apperception of All Appearing and the Worldlicizing Horizon of Possible Reflection (*Das universale Weltbewusstsein als Weltwahrnehmung*)

*[27 September 1932]*

**[§, pp. 130–136]** Husserl describes the world as constituting itself through a concordance-synthesis within the total synthesis binding appearances of all modalities. "Perceptions" — perceived objects in their How — are a waking-stream of manifold consciousness-modes in changing validity-modes, always bound to the unity of concordance. In my consciousness-life lies the *universal synthesis of appearances* — own present and remembered ones and those of foreign subjects — as an endless horizon of my actual total-perception.

The question arises whether there is a limit in world-experience: perceptions as appearances versus other consciousness — thoughts, opinions, tradition-grown meanings, theories. But here we face the difficulty that *world encompasses everything*: thoughts too are in the world, expressions have places in spatiotemporal reality. Thought-formations have their subjective modes of appearing, their perceptual being-validity. Indeed all consciousness-modes are themselves conscious as worldly, as belonging to the world under the title "psychic" — while the world is the constituted unity of appearances. Universal consciousness in natural attitude is in all its consciousness-experiences *worldly perceiving*, in its horizon implying world in general. Consciousness is itself apperceived as worldly, as my psychic life. For this again the same holds — and so in infinitum. This progress resolves through the distinction of actuality and horizonal potentiality. Actual total consciousness has an *apperceptive horizon of possible reflection*, and this reflection is "worldlicizing" what it reflects upon — actually apperceiving it as psychic — and so in infinitum.

Humans are subjects for the world and simultaneously objects in the world. They are subjects; each is, for the allness of others, subject; they are its objects, and each is at the same time for itself subject and object.

### Text Nr. 15. Horizon-Meaning and Original Induction. The World-Horizon as Structured Empty Horizon (*Horizontmeinung und ursprüngliche Induktion. Der Welthorizont als strukturierter Leerhorizont*)

*[End of May or beginning of June 1934]*

**[§, pp. 137–141]** "Horizon" means the *induction* essentially belonging to and inseparable from every experience — original induction as essential component of every experience itself. The original "induction" or anticipation proves to be a modification-mode of originally instituting activity — a mode of "intentionality," the anticipating meaning beyond a kernel of givenness. To it essentially corresponds the fulfilment-form: for perception, the perceptual fulfilment in original continuous synthesis. In the progress of perceiving as progressively "getting to know" the same perceived, every perceptual phase gains its horizon, ever new. The synthesis of sides is not an integration but a continuous continuing-validity of what has already come to validity in the mode of "perception," yet a continuing-validity under sense-modification through which sense-identity nonetheless passes. This synthesis of horizons, their continuous synthetic functioning, constitutes being-sense.

Fundamental is a constant internal difference in the respective horizon: the determinate — the "prefigured," the (in the narrower sense) induced — is a moment in a horizon that is also "totally indeterminate" *empty horizon*. But the properly induced is not like an island in the sea; the sea itself turns into land wherever it is pursued. The empty is only a mode of imperfect determinacy that itself in transformation becomes relatively more complete determinacy. We stand in a *relativity*: what is really prefigured is only relatively determinate, having its own particular horizon of indeterminacy.

Perception in the primal mode of being-certainty has as induction-horizon always only being-correlates of certainty. Every perception is a continuous course of induction and confirmation, a continuity of *self-confirmation*. The universal horizon is the world-horizon — horizon of the respective perceptual core, in constant ongoing-perceiving bringing world to perceptive experience. Everything experienced is "typically apperceived" in a species-typicality (*Artmäßigkeit*) and genus-typicality; this stands in the ontic horizon as a species-inscription that first receives its "determinate" particularization to the "individually" own. Perception as perception of the thing is *perception through apperception*, through horizon-co-validity as being-sense-determining. This co-validity is "indeterminately general."

### Supplement IX. Appresentation and Presentation with Regard to Individual Things and with Regard to the Whole World. World Constituted in Empty Intentions (*Appräsentation und Präsentation*)

*[ca. February 1932]*

**[§, pp. 142–143]** If we call "presentifications" all experiences that do not make present in the flesh, then the empty horizons of thing-perceptions regarding unseen sides are "presentifications." But insofar as they are essential to thing-perceptions, and only through them can a thing become fleshly present at all, they are *presentifying* and not merely presentificatory. In the total perception, the thing itself is — implicitly also with regard to its reverse side — conscious in living present. Accordingly, Husserl distinguishes "presentations" and "appresentations." Every transcendent perception can present its transcendent object only through the means of appresentation.

The same structure extends to the world as a whole. Instead of being directed at a single thing, we can be directed at the indeterminately and in any case in empty horizons co-given thingly surrounding world, which is a unity extending beyond the fleshly given thingliness into the indeterminate. Through the empty intentions in all forms — regarding present, future, and past — *not only the delimited unitary thing or a piece of "properly" perceived surrounding world is constituted, but the whole world* stretching into an indeterminate spatial present and an indeterminate future and past.

## III. Orientation-Structures of the Life-World and the Fundamental Structures of Life-Worldly Situativity (*Orientierungsstrukturen der Lebenswelt und die Grundstrukturen lebensweltlicher Situativität*)

---

### Text Nr. 16. Orientation and Access-Praxis. A Universal System of Possible Spatial and Temporal Orientations as Presupposition of Intersubjective Praxis and an Intersubjective Surrounding World (*Orientierung und Zugangspraxis*)

*[1925]*

**[§, pp. 145–146]** Husserl distinguishes the world as the same reality-world in the universal manifold of its possible orientations from the world in its present determinate orientation, but with the horizon of possible orientations as my practical possibilities of access-praxis. The world is given in oriented fashion: I have a near-sphere and a far-sphere, my body as the zero-object of nearness, itself oriented and differently so for different senses. The far is accessible: in access it becomes near, while what was near becomes far. Within nearness itself there are differences of better and worse, of the best possible as so near that I am right at it. The far passes into the near-given in approaching, and the near-given becomes best-given in all-sided bringing-nearer.

This opening establishes the fundamental phenomenological topology of orientation: the body as zero-point, the near-far gradient, and the praxis of access through which the far becomes near. Orientation is not a merely subjective overlay on an unoriented world; it is the form in which the world is given as a practical field.

**[§, pp. 147–149]** How does it stand with the synthetic connection of my orientational givennesses with those of others? In empathically understanding the other, I understand their near-things as the same things that are far for me, their "right" as my "left." Each person experiences the world in a universal system of possible orientations that is identically the same for every other. The other is understood not as subject of "an" oriented world but of *the same* world with the same orientation-system, only so that necessarily their world-orientation is a different one within the same system.

This yields: we have in every present a common *practical surrounding world* — practical as a common experiential world in exchangeable practical experiencing. Inseparable from spatial orientation is *temporal orientation*: we all have one and the same present, while the realities that are present have for each a different spatial orientation. We have an objective experiential world, the world of praxis, which is *objective in temporal orientation* — objective as present, past, and future. In the constant intersubjectively necessary change of these objective time-orientations, real time maintains itself identically.

**[§, pp. 149–150]** Under the presupposition of normality — we experience the same surrounding world with the same experiential determinations — we arrive at the *normal-idea* "we and our surrounding world." But then Husserl branches off to the idea of an *anomalous* experiential world, where not all determinations experienced by me are experienceable for everyone. Anomality is a fact, a fundamental trait of the universal experiential world. Only so far as commonality in the constitution of reality-determinations reaches does the possibility of common praxis extend. A colour-blind person and a colour-normal person cannot, in pure experiential praxis, have the domain of colours as a practically common field.

### Supplement X. The Oriented Surrounding World. "Oriented" Space, i.e. Perceptual Surrounding World, World in General (*Die orientierte Umwelt*)

*[Beginning of March 1935]*

**[§, pp. 151–154]** Husserl sketches — in admittedly hasty notes — the levels of orientation: (1) oriented around *me* in change, with normal standing position establishing right-left, front-back, above-below; (2) a *We as ego-mode* — the family with its fixed place, house, garden, fields; (3) the village, the city, the homeland — each constituting a mode of orientation from its own standpoint. Humans living in homeland-subjectivities have their common world in the *chief orientation of the tribe*. Beyond every homeland-unity lies the wider *traffic-world* (*Verkehrswelt*) for all interacting tribes. Orientation by celestial directions (East, West, South, North) comes in as well. The primitive's surrounding world is, in its intuitive constitution, oriented *in itself* — the orientation belongs to the world itself, not as an externally added feature.

---

### Supplement XI. Sequence of Levels of Surrounding Worlds or Homeworlds. Homeworld and Territory (*Stufenfolge der Umwelten oder Heimwelten*)

*[ca. 1934]*

**[§, pp. 154–156]** The surrounding world (*Umwelt*) is the "world" into which the human being consciously lives — the world in which they are in the widest sense *at home* (*heimisch*). This homeworld is the *correlate of one's own existence*; one is personal subject *for* this world and subject of one's "life" — not a momentary life-present but life in a wide horizon, the life that is "granted" or "imposed by fate." All persons counting as belonging to my home have in a way the same homeworld, though in different "aspects." The larger connection of people yields *structuring into private homes within a wider home*: tribal home, village home, etc. Beyond every such totality the world extends as having homeless regions, *no-man's land*. Every homeland is the *territory* of a home-humanity.

The concept of *Heimwelt* (homeworld) is one of the most important in the entire volume. It designates the life-world not in its abstract universality but in its concrete, historically particular form as the familiar, habitually constituted world of a specific community. The homeworld is defined by its familiarity, its typicality, its practical availability — and by its contrast with the *Fremdwelt* (foreign world).

**[§, p. 156]** To the essence of the human being belongs "struggle for existence" (*Kampf ums Dasein*): the human being cannot merely take the surrounding world as it is but must strive to shape it, to master it willfully so that it satisfies them. They set not only individual goals but *life-goals* and devise a "methodology" of acting existence. Correlatively: competition of humans, families, tribes, peoples. The "wide world," the inhuman, the unknown depths, the chances, the fates — apperceived as hindering "powers" or as promoting ones. The mythical: the unknown, apperceived personally as resistance — "dark fate," demons, gods.

---

### Text Nr. 17. On the Orientedness of Understanding: Homeworld and Foreign World. Understanding of Strangers and of Foreign Homeworlds. Religion and Science as Universal World-Intentions. The Foreign-Mythical. Objection of the Relativity of Scientific World-Interpretation. Intersubjective Critique as Path to Universal World-Cognition. Descriptive Science (*Zur Orientiertheit des Verstehens: Heimwelt und Fremdwelt*)

*[November 1933]*

#### § 1. The Human Being as Theme. Comportment of the Human Being. Human in the Cultural Surrounding World

**[§, pp. 159–163]** Husserl begins with a concrete phenomenological description: the human being there, what they do and pursue — wielding their body, perceiving, grasping an object, cutting paper with scissors. This I understand without further ado, individual-typically. But I do not always understand beyond the immediate bodily activity. Coming into a foreign environment — in China — the individual typicality is imperfectly known to me: foreign plants, fields with unfamiliar crops, houses of unfamiliar character. The typicality is not merely in outward handling but in the purpose-sense, the typical intentions of the people.

Getting to understand a Chinese person and the Chinese world requires that I be "raised into" it — just as a child must grow into the adult world. I must, living into it, acquire the apperception of the foreign world, which would mean becoming ever more completely a Chinese person among Chinese — while remaining a German. The world in the course of human life and its necessary communalization is one that *creates itself from ever new apperceptions of familiar sense and ever newly determining sense* — yet necessarily so that it holds as the same world for all persons possibly entering this circle of communication.

#### § 2. Excursus: Universal Worldly Intentions: Religion and Science

**[§, pp. 164–167]** Husserl asks whether there are universal worldly intentions beyond science. He identifies *absolute universal religion* — the breakthrough to the one and only God, the all-world God — as such an intention. "God" then designates not a particular people's god but the one unique God of all humanity. The universally human religious demand is the demand of an absolutely universal religious ethics, the ethics of a humanity that transcends all peoples. This is related to, but distinct from, the path of philosophy as universal science: religion proceeds through historical revelation, while philosophy proceeds through autonomous cognition. Both aim at universality, but by different routes.

#### § 3. The Understandable Apperceptive Core of Foreign Life-World and the Incomprehensible Foreign. The Foreign-Mythical

**[§, pp. 167–169]** In the transition from the fixed apperceptions of the homeworld to understanding the foreign, one initially transfers one's own familiar apperceptions analogically — and makes experiences, learns, progresses, so long as concordance holds. But what one reaches in the certainty of concordant understanding is constantly determined *from one's own homeworld apperceptions*. The supposed foreign world is necessarily a *reshaping of the homeworld*. There is always a *core* that is understood just as the familiar is understood; and in the incomprehensible, it is already understood as a modification.

The *mythical* apperceptions of the foreign present a particular challenge. Must there not be a core in all world-apperceptions that is presupposed by the mythical — a non-mythical stratum that the mythical itself takes for granted?

#### § 4. Objection of Historical Relativity: All Our Interpretation Is European, Etc.

**[§, pp. 170–172]** Husserl confronts the relativist objection: "All your interpretation is European; it yields European truth, European logic. Primitives have their own logic, their own worldview." His response: the primitive is a human who is for me in my world, whose surrounding world I experience from within my own. Everything I can say about possible worldviews, logics, or worlds of any people is *interpretation within my own domain of cognizable being*, a subjective result of my reflection. It would be nonsensical to suppose that the universe of my truth could stand in irresolvable conflict with that of others, since these others and their universe are *contained within mine* — according to everything I can truthfully say about them. This opens the possibility of progressively coming to the truth of what they themselves are, through intersubjective verification and critique.

---

### Supplement XII. On the Plurality and Situation-Dependent Actualizability of Homeworld We-Horizons (*Zur Pluralität und situationsabhängigen Aktualisierbarkeit heimweltlicher Wir-Horizonte*)

*[ca. 1933]*

**[§, pp. 173–174]** Husserl reflects on the plurality of We-horizons: a person can be "we Germans" or "we Europeans" or "we academics" depending on the situation. These different We-horizons are not simultaneously equally alive; they are actualized situation-dependently. The actualized We-horizon determines the sense of the currently lived surrounding world.

---

### Text Nr. 18. Near-World — Far-World. The Disclosedness of the Nearest Surrounding World (*Nahwelt – Fernwelt*)

*[9 October 1933]*

**[§, pp. 175–178]** Husserl analyses the structure of the originally constituted near-sphere: for the child, first the nursery, then the apartment, a piece of street. Here familiarity and constant experiential confirmation reign. The directly perceived is apperceived as normally continuing even after perception ceases. Induction through communication extends the personal circle beyond the first near-worldly personhood. Each person is, taken individually, *finitely limited* — having necessarily restricted capacities of actual perception and indirect cognizance, yet having a horizon of the unknown as the in-principle experienceable. The near-world has its familiarity and thoroughgoing normality; the foreign can be learned and made into a second home. But what about entirely foreign peoples, foreign "races," territories with their cultural worlds?

---

### Text Nr. 19. My (Our) World-Horizon in Its Spatiotemporality. Spatiotemporal Orientation: Time-Modalities and Spatial Modalities (*Mein (unser) Welthorizont in seiner Zeiträumlichkeit*)

*[ca. beginning of 1932]*

**[§, pp. 179–183]** Life progresses as a process of constant world-experience — a constant streaming of experiential validity, concordant as a whole. Acts are always there; the ego as waking is always active; but a constant certainty-ground is also there — a perceptual field, surrounding world in general. The world is given as spatiotemporal world, as "standing" present that in constant streaming lets past and future "spring forth and carries them within itself."

Husserl develops the distinction between *time-modalities* (the subjective orientational modes: present, past, future) and *spatial modalities* (the orientational modes: here, there, near, far). Time as universal ontic form is a "continuum" of time-points. The form of simultaneous coexistence running identically through all succession is the *spatial continuum*. Space is the abstract-general *form-determination of the world*. Spatiotemporality designates an abstract, non-self-sufficient form-determination in the What of the world.

The time-modalities already belong to the "subjective" — temporal and spatial *orientation*. But the "here and there" has its We-meaning: we Freiburgers, we Germans, we Europeans — each We has its *We-orientation*, its *territory*. The We has its collective *bodiliness* (*Leiblichkeit*). A crucial asymmetry: all simultaneous subjects share identically the same present (temporal orientation is the same for all), while spatial orientation differs for each. There is no "time-movement" and no "rest" in succession-temporality, whereas in space, movement corresponds to orientation-change that differs for each subject.

---

### Supplement XIII. Induction in World-Experience and the Constitution of the Oriented Experiential World as World with Earth and Heaven (*Induktion in der Welterfahrung*)

*[ca. 1934]*

**[§, pp. 184–189]** Husserl develops an extraordinary analysis of how the oriented experiential world constitutes itself as a world with earth and heaven. The *earthly world* is the surrounding world of my people in its territorial finitude — nature as *earth-nature* extending in iterable progression in all orientational directions from one's standing-place. From the earth, closed bodies are detachable as experientially movable; all closed bodies belong to it; above them lies free, empty space. The *heaven* (*Himmel*) and its phenomena (sun, moon, stars) are not experienced as bodily in the same sense as earthly things. The heaven is not corporeal, not spatial in the way bodies are. The "between earth and heaven" is not a spatial interval like that between two things.

Husserl asks how we come to apperceive the moon as a distant sphere, as a distant body — given that the normal far-apperception reaching a limit-zero of distance cannot apply here. The experiential world is always already constituted as *earthly world* with its bodies in the mode of near-far confirmation, *and* as *heavenly world* with its own universal phenomena and its own individual beings. The subsequent development of this non-corporeal being-sphere into far-appearances of bodies (in which bodies are experienced that are for us necessarily bodies in the one single space) extends the world with its duality of beings into a single corporeal world with its associated animals as ensouled bodies.

---

### Text Nr. 20. Structures of Life-Worldly Situativity: Momentary Situation — Special Situation — Situation-Totality — All-Situation. Individual-Subjective and Communal Situation. The Bourgeois Normal World (*Strukturen lebensweltlicher Situativität*)

*[Beginning of 1932]*

#### § 1. The Intertwining of More or Less Awakened Situations and Situation-Totalities

**[§, pp. 190–195]** Husserl distinguishes two kinds of relativity: (a) the relativity of modalization — the ever-open possibility that anything in a situation could become doubtful or negated, so that no being, no truth, need be final or apodictic; (b) the relativity of the *change of situations* — while much stays identical through the change, the situation-sense and the truths change with the situation. Through the universal of situations, a universe of being as identical persists through all situations, given in each with a different situation-sense and situation-truth.

Every concrete situation is in truth not concrete but only *part* of an interlinked chain of simultaneous and successive situations, from which it derives its sense through a horizon-consciousness that is "livingly" present. The few things that make up my actual perceptual field do not make up the field in which I now live; this is rather my *concrete "situation"* to which they belong, laden with the total-sense of that situation. The concrete situation is in truth a special situation within overarching situation-totalities — morning routine, working day, week, year — up to the uppermost totality that constitutes my normal total life-field: *my world*.

#### § 2. Our Situation-Horizon in the Horizon of the Bourgeois Normal World. The World as Total-Horizon of All Life-Worlds

**[§, pp. 195–199]** As *bourgeois* ego, my situation is bourgeois in the unity of the *bourgeois surrounding world*. It has the horizon-sense of this world — its general style, its constant style of thoroughly familiar normality, the periodicity of the year, common weekdays and Sundays, professional holidays. Each person has their habitual interests, profession, religious convictions and corresponding life-order. The universal structure of this life-world would need to be circumscribed as the invariant form for all normal citizens. Beyond the bourgeois normal world lie the anomalous: vagabonds and other personal types who place themselves outside the normal world. And beyond this world, for us Germans, the entire German people and its life-order, other nations with different customs and languages. We finally arrive at the *absolute totality: the world*, in which our German surrounding world is a single one in a manifold of surrounding worlds of corresponding humanities standing in actual or possible connection.

---

### Text Nr. 21. Special Situation and the World as All-Situation of All Special Situations. Life-World and True World. Pre-Scientific, Situation-Relative Judging and the Situation-Transcending Judging in Science (*Sondersituation und die Welt als Allsituation*)

*[November 1932]*

**[§, pp. 200–203]** Husserl analyses the concept of situation-relative truth: pre-scientific judging is relative to the practical interest and the situation. Formal-logical laws are independent of this relativity. But the interest in being and truth can *transcend* situational relativity: the theoretical interest can station itself *above* the respective interests and pursue the identical in its so-being consequentially, following the differences of completeness and ever more complete determination.

The world that is for each the valid experiential world owes its being-sense not to oneself alone but to the co-humans with whom one has grown up — and these in turn were determined by their connections, in indefinite endlessness. In the living connection of these bound humanities there occurs a continual mutual determining — a living-through tradition (*Tradieren*). So everyone has *their* world as naively certain, which differs from that of every other; yet "we all" have a *common world*, an opinio communis, a common "world-picture" (a general type). This *all-situation of all special situations* already determines the sense of the special situations and the being-sense and truth-sense that is confirmed in living actuality. The life-world of each particular humanity is *not simply a piece of a "true" world* — this idea of truth in a theoretical, logical sense must not be presupposed here.

---

### Supplement XIV. Practically Understood Situation and Perceptual Appearance. Situation-Relative Optima and the Being Simply as the Absolute Optimum (*Praktisch verstandene Situation und Wahrnehmungserscheinung*)

*[24 October 1932]*

**[§, pp. 204–205]** Husserl introduces the concept of *optima* relative to interest-situations. Every perception has its perceptual horizon and its own relative "nearness" and, related to this, its fulfilling *optimum*. In the change of interest-situations, this optimum changes. But the selfsameness of the real is the identical of the possible perceptual series that lead from one optimum to the other. The *theoretical interest* is the interest in the being simply (*Seienden schlechthin*) — in the identical in the change of possible perceptions, specifically in the identical that would emerge as the *most complete* in the systematic traversal of all relative optima up to the *absolute optimum*. Cognizance in theoretical interest is the systematic traversal of appearances under the guidance of their anticipated relative optima toward the absolute optimum, which presents itself in the relative ones in constant provisionality. The concept of situation need not be restricted to practical interests: as theoretically interested ego too, I am world-apperceiving in a respective situation, an orientation-centre for the world given to me in oriented fashion.

## IV. The Apodicticity of the World and the Apodictic World-Components "My Body" and "I This Human Being" (*Die Apodiktizität der Welt und die apodiktischen Weltbestände „mein Leib" und „ich dieser Mensch"*)

---

### Text Nr. 22. Validity-Style of the Experiential World. Its Apodicticity during Concordant Experience. Experience as Apodictic Access-Method to the Worldly and to the World Itself. Its Being Confirming Itself in Empirical Indubitability (*Geltungsstil der Erfahrungswelt. Ihre Apodiktizität während der einstimmigen Erfahrung*)

*[Date uncertain]*

**[§, pp. 207–212]** Husserl develops the thesis that the existence of the world enjoys a specific kind of apodicticity — not absolute, but *relative and temporal*. In the concordant course of experience, it is impossible for me to doubt that a given experienced thing is there: the belief in its being and the exclusion of its non-being is for me now an irremovable necessity, apodictically certain. This certainty holds as long as experience remains concordant.

Every reality is a "pole of infinite experienceability." Experiencing a thing means: I have immediately something appearing in the How of an appearance-mode and have certain access-possibilities to the same thing in an ordered infinity of other appearance-modes. The thing as the selfsame of this infinite manifold of appearances and as determinable through them is in my grasp and pre-grasp. In the sense-content of experience lies the opposite of something existing in closed concretion; it contains nothing other than that the thing is a pole of determinabilities from an infinity of appearances through which the selfsame presents itself in infinite adumbration.

The dubitability and non-being of the world is *apodictically excluded for me* who surveys my life, finding myself as living into, cognizing, acting into the world, and who can at any time convince myself of the identity of the universe in self-confirming accessibilities and of the necessity of presumptive world-belief. *The apodicticity of world-existence is relative to me and my present actual life.* The possibility of the non-being of the world is at any time thinkable in the sense that life could continue and the world not exist — the apodicticity in its temporal rootedness would no longer be performable and justified. The endless future of the world is for my present a necessary thing I must believe. But it is nothing that has sense without reference to a present. *Truly apodictically and irrelatively existing is only the ego*, and insofar as it is relative, it is relative to itself.

This is one of the most important passages in the entire volume. Husserl develops a nuanced position between naive realism and Cartesian idealism. The world's being is apodictic — but with a specific, historically rooted, *temporal* apodicticity. It is apodictic *while and insofar as* concordant experience continues. The possibility of the world's non-being remains thinkable in principle, but it is never an actual experiential possibility — it is a limit-concept that can be entertained only hypothetically, not from within the ongoing life of experience. Only the ego's being is absolutely and irrelatively apodictic.

---

### Supplement XV. Apodictic Anticipation of the Being of the World in the Course of Experience. Possibility of the Non-Being of the World. The Historical Apodicticity of World-Certainty (*Apodiktische Antizipation des Seins der Welt im Lauf der Erfahrung*)

*[ca. 1925]*

**[§, pp. 213–216]** Through the phenomenological epoché, the being-sense of nature is revealed as a constant anticipation lying already in pre-givenness — a presumptive evidence from the concordance of past experience for the further concordance of actual and possible experience. The present experience is an "evident" anticipation. But the evidence implicated in this anticipation is only evidence *of* the anticipation, not originary evidence. The possibility always remains open that experience will not continue in this style. *Apodictically certain, simply true, is only an absolute certainty lying in this continuity of implicit presumption — that the world "presumably" is.*

This gives new and deep meaning to the Cartesian doctrine of the possible non-being of the world. The natural life lives in a certainty of the world arising continuously through anticipation. But this certainty has the character of a *historical apodicticity* (*historische Apodiktizität*): I, the one living in concordant experience, *live and as long as I live* cannot cancel this certainty. I have in it a universal practical horizon of which I am practically always certain and must be certain, as long as I live, as long as I live in outer experience and its self-confirming I-can.

---

### Supplement XVI. Normal, Being-Confirming World-Experience as Presupposition of the Anomality of Illusion. Constitution of a Unified Object-World through Retroactive Correction (*Normale, seinsbewährende Welterfahrung als Voraussetzung der Anomalität des Scheins*)

*[ca. 1925]*

**[§, pp. 215–218]** Husserl asks whether the existence of the world as world of our experience is apodictic. The world-experience in every present is experience of a spatiotemporal world. In running through the past, I find that from phase to phase earlier perceived things remain perceived as the same, and it is one and the same world, though the same one that only undergoes corrections regarding "illusions." *All cancellation of being-validity of realities rests on real experience in actual validity.* A stretch of normal concordance as concrete total experience must precede — it is the presupposition so that illusion as an original occurrence can arise. It is evident that not the whole present-field (including the own body) can be cancelled as total illusion; *illusion must always and necessarily rest on being*.

The constitution of a concretely unified thingly present occurs through "retroactive correction" (*rückgreifende Korrektur*): through the extended duration, a unitarily existing world, free of illusion, constitutes itself as the corrected, fulfilled duration. The retention of this present is then re-awakeable (in its corrected form). Memory brings again *illusion as cancelled being* and the *being in its place*. World is for us always there from a constant interest in the really existing, which is constituted in the course of synthesis and correction. The valid is what is interesting.

---

### Supplement XVII. Two Worlds for One Ego. Thought-Experiment of Two Periodically Alternating Concordance-Systems with Alternating Bodily Personalities (*Zwei Welten für ein Ich*)

*[October 1923]*

**[§, pp. 219–224]** Husserl conducts one of his most remarkable thought-experiments. I experience a world concordantly. Then a break. A different world is there, again concordantly experienced. Then a new break — "waking up." I have the first world again: I remember it and now perceive it as the same. The play alternates. Both worlds have the same categorial type. In the first world I have one body, in the other another.

Could these two worlds be equally legitimate realities without conflict? Everything I experience, I hold in validity within the unity of my experience. If B-world enters, the A-world cannot simply be abandoned; new things must be reckoned to the same world. If B holds concordantly, the A-experiences are cancelled as illusions ("dream"). But if A then reasserts itself, and the alternation repeats with regularity — can I predict the switches by the A-clock and the B-clock? Then I must say: *One world truly is not*, but I have two world-phenomena with fixed regularities by which I can orient myself.

But is this coherent? Would I not be a *double subject* — an A-person and a B-person? The question becomes sharper when other subjects are considered. If the A-world people continued to experience the A-world during my B-period, their testimony upon my return would confirm my A-existence during the B-pause — creating a contradiction with my B-experience. Husserl concludes that if we think one ego in relation to two worlds in completely consequent experience, we must also think the other egos as subjects of both worlds with the same breaks. Otherwise the two worlds cannot be consequentially experienced, and the other egos would regard one as a lunatic.

---

### Supplement XVIII. Possible Non-Being of the World (*Mögliches Nichtsein der Welt*)

*[ca. 1930]*

**[§, pp. 224–230]** Husserl raises fundamental questions in transcendental attitude. I am an ego that has constituted a spatiotemporal world. But is this an absolute necessity? Could the transcendental constitution dissolve while I yet remain as ego of immanence? Could it be that no world ever constituted itself for me?

In my factual I-am lies implicitly my factual beginning, my factual development. Could I see, from this beginning, possibilities of development where no others, no world would constitute itself? One might object: only a developed, scientific ego can consider these possibilities, and all possibilities of a "human ego" must be possibilities within a human monadic world. This would mean: I could really run through a development leading to no world — I could develop as a totally pathological ego. But the monadic totality would still be constituted. All thinkability-possibilities of the world presuppose the factum, and are from the outset possibilities of *a world*.

Husserl then pursues extraordinary speculations: Could I end in a "tumult" (*Gewühl*) of sensation-data, worldless? Could a world then re-constitute itself as a supposedly experienced and confirmed world? And if so, must this second world become identical with the earlier? Here we have the problem of the *possibility of metempsychosis* — the possibility of identical ego-being in different world-times with pauses in between. And the problem of *immortality*: Can a soul stop at all? Is an ego-beginning a zone of unawakenable forgetting? These are pursued as genuine phenomenological questions about the essential limits of worldly constitution.

---

### Text Nr. 23. Necessity of the Presumptive Being-Positing of the World in the Self-Confirming Self-Givenness of Concordant World-Experience. The Primal Right of World-Belief (*Notwendigkeit der präsumtiven Seinssetzung der Welt*)

*[ca. 1930]*

**[§, pp. 231–234]** Husserl establishes that every non-self-giving being-meaning refers back to self-givenness — the synthesis of fulfilment or cancelling disappointment. Real experience is already self-givenness, but it can be correct or incorrect, confirmable or refutable. Its self-givenness through self-appearances contains presumptions, horizons of anticipation, on whose fulfilability its stability depends. Experience is only confirmable and cancellable through experience; the presumptive self-givenness in the form of certainty is already presupposed for any self-givenness to prove itself as standing or as false.

The factual course of universal world-experience is one of thoroughgoing concordance, with occasional corrections. The manifold thing-experiences are unified: a unity of each perceptual field, each concordant in itself. All changes of experience close themselves synthetically concordantly. The being-positing of the world is — in the form of evidence, of self-givenness — a necessity running through our life, beyond all willfulness. It is not a fixed idea or an unmotivated belief; rather it is *universal self-givenness that in its own course characterizes itself as thoroughgoing, self-accomplishing self-confirmation at every point*. The world-belief is not blind but at all times grounded, continually self-justifying, and not to be surrendered. The continually re-establishing concordance assigns to future concordance a presumptive right in the form of a modality of certainty — a living one that in every waking life-moment confirms and reignites itself. And yet it is *not an apodictic certainty of the being of the world simply*.

---

### Supplement XIX. On the Apodictic Evidence of the Presumption of a World (*Zur apodiktischen Evidenz der Präsumtion einer Welt*)

*[ca. 1930]*

**[§, pp. 235–236]** The presumption of a truly existing world carries in itself the presumption of "genuine" experience as a possibility of a certain uncancellability in the connected experiential course. This presumption is enclosed in the presumption of a really or truly existing world. It is (1) evident, and apodictically so, that outer experience is presumptive regarding its object. (2) On the other hand, doubt about the being of the world is ridiculous. *While I experience the world, the being of the same cannot empirically become doubtful.* Individual real being of experience needs the concordance with other experiences; the individual experience can be cancelled. But *with what should world-experience conflict? Nothing "can" cancel it as counter-experience.* As long as it continues in its style as world-experience, it carries itself in self-confirmation. Its presumption has always confirmed itself and *has no constituted horizon of possible non-confirmation*.

---

### Supplement XX. Idea of Apodictic Evidence as Simply Repeatable Evidence and Question of the Apodicticity of World-Experience (*Idee der apodiktischen Evidenz als schlechthin wiederholbare Evidenz*)

*[ca. 1930]*

**[§, pp. 237–238]** An apodictic insight is distinguished by the fact that I can produce the insight that a new insight conflicting with what was seen as apodictically being would be impossible. Equivalently: an apodictic evidence is characterized by its unconditional "repeatability." For world-experience: the world-experiences are synthetically united, constituting unitedly the same world — possibly partially illusory, the other time as the corrected. *The synthesis of concordance is that of the uncorrected and the corrected.* The identity of the valid as identity through actual and possible correction is unbroken and is apodictic validity.

---

### Supplement XXI. The Conviction Belonging to the Experiential Sense of the World That the Experienced World, Although the Being of Every Individual Reality Is Being Subject to Cancellation, Nonetheless Carries a Being Within Itself (*Die zum Erfahrungssinn der Welt gehörige Überzeugung*)

*[ca. 1932]*

**[§, pp. 238–242]** Every outer experience depends in its validity on later experiences. This is an irremovable peculiarity. Yet through our entire waking life goes the *consciousness of the one and same world*. In the sense of this experiential sense of our world lies that experience always ran and always will run such that not all currently valid things can transform into illusion through conflict with others that would themselves become illusion in infinitum. *To universal experience belongs the sense of one, of this constantly existing world*, a world of "infinite" past and future, a world having the form of universal temporality. And yet: a *world of experience*. It carries the constantly open possibility of illusion, the constantly open possibility that what is preserved from cancellation may in its experienced so-being show itself differently. And yet, however much the course of experience cancels and re-determines, it is a *course of correction* carrying in itself the experiential conviction constitutive for the unity of world-experience — that the respectively experienced world *carries true being within itself*. The *relative apodicticity* concerning the being of the world stands in close connection with the relative apodicticity concerning individual experienced items.

---

### Text Nr. 24. Meditation on the Apodicticity of the I-Am and in What Regard It Harbours World-Having as Apodictic (*Meditation über die Apodiktizität des Ich-bin*)

*[Rothaus, 18 March 1933]*

**[§, pp. 243–250]** In natural self-reflection I find myself as a human being. This finding is continuous self-perception in never-broken being-certainty: it is self-apperception. Inseparable from my self-presence is the perceptual presence of my surrounding world and thereby of the present world in general. To my personal being as being in consciousness-life belongs that it constantly has world conscious in being-certainty. For individual realities, the possible modalization belongs; but only on the ground of continuing world-certainty is doubt and non-being possible for individual realities. *World is constantly valid for me in being-certainty, while its content of individual realities never apodictically stands fast* — the maintenance of world-certainty characterizes itself as maintenance of the same under constant re-determination in so-being and correction.

In this world-consciousness, my body-consciousness has a distinguished position and function. As long as I am personal ego in self-perception, the perception of my body can *never* be absent, nor my kinaesthetic doing, my governing in the body. Nothing can I perceive without bodily governing involvement. Personal living in the world — having world and working into the world — and bodily being, ego-being and ego-governing in its body, are a necessary correlation, each inseparable from the other.

Am I *apodictically* certain of the I-am, while I am in self-perception? Self-perception exercising, finding myself as this human being, I am personal, bodily having this body as my body and thereby having world. The question whether it is a priori necessary that I be human leads to difficult problems. But in any case: *I am personally apodictically certain of myself* — so far that this self-experience, however much is modalizable in attributed determinations, is *with regard to the ego-pole and the primally original components in principle never modalizable*.

---

### Text Nr. 25. The Apodictic Certainty of My Human-Bodily Being as Part of the Apodictic Certainty of the Being-Ground "World." Rejection of the Cartesian Doubt-Attempt (*Die apodiktische Gewissheit meines menschlich-leiblichen Seins als Teil der apodiktischen Gewissheit des Seinsbodens „Welt". Zurückweisung des cartesischen Zweifelsversuchs*)

*[May 1937]*

**[§, pp. 251–258]** I presuppose (this is the ground-certainty for all further reflections): *in the world I am a human being* among other things. To me as human being belongs that I have experience of myself and of other things of the world. To my human being belongs my body; its certainty is always there — it is impossible that I doubted the reality of my body and soul, my human being in the world. *Of my human being I have — on this ground — apodictic certainty.* This is connected with the fact that the experience of every other reality of the world in an inseparable way *presupposes the bodily sensibility*, the certainty of my body as functioning in all thing-experiences.

*That every individual thing can become doubtful is not equivalent to the totality of the world being doubtable.* Every doubt grounds itself in the fact that against the questioned validity a protest is raised from something else that is for me completely certain. Every confirmation or refutation of things presupposes the being of other things, hence finally the being of the world. *For the ego that has world-consciousness in this way, it is absolutely impossible to represent the world as non-existing*, while it can do this for every individual being (its own being as human subject excepted). The non-being of the world is only *hypothetically* positable, as something countersensical is positable.

The main thing then is that every question about being and non-being of the ordinary real sense presupposes the being of the world. *World is then necessarily valid universe of things* — as existing in questionabilities, but so that everything coming into question presupposes something unquestionable. The only thing possible through our willfulness is that we carry out a *universal epoché* regarding world-validity — an epoché in which I abstain in advance from every being-validity and thereby lift myself from the constant ground of my living-in-the-world.

---

## V. The Reality-Structure of the Life-World — Nature as Abstract Core-Stratum of the World (*Die Realitätenstruktur der Lebenswelt – Natur als abstrakte Kernschicht der Welt*)

---

### Text Nr. 26. The Natural World-Concept. Systematic Dismantling of the Concrete Experiential World to the Abstract Core-Stratum "Nature," Starting from the Concrete Experiential World (*Der natürliche Weltbegriff*)

*[May 1926]*

#### § 1. The Task of Displaying the A Priori of the Concrete Experiential World and the Idea of an Abstractive Procedure for Reconstructing Its Full Concretion

**[§, pp. 259–265]** Husserl opens with the concept of the "natural world-concept" — the necessary eidetic structure of a surrounding world in essential relation to an inseparable subjectivity for which the world is there as its surrounding world. This subjectivity is simultaneously *living into* the world (experiencing, cognizing, valuing, acting, suffering, being gladdened by it) and *present in* the world, experienced and experienceable through reflection. It is at once *acting subject* and *treated object*.

The "world of pure experience" is the world as experienced purely as such, prior to all theorizing. Husserl seeks the a priori structure of this world. Two approaches are distinguished: (1) starting from the practical surrounding world and the structural types that arise from the activities of subjects — everything that presents itself as the "ever again" and "everywhere" given in the world under the aspect of a form become from the active liveliness of subjects; (2) recognizing that this does not yet yield the *complete* a priori system, since a whole world-structure with a self-enclosed a priori system is presupposed without which no concrete practical activity could have a substrate.

All praxis produces a *formation* (*Gebilde*) by forming a practical *matter*. This matter may itself already be formed — and so in infinitum. Every procedure is necessarily one-sided; every experienced concretion is necessarily one-sidedly experienced. What remains unattended in the actively experiencing activity is indeterminate and variable in a special sense — the sense of the openly-indeterminate remaining. But this openness does not disturb the possibility of gaining an a priori that relates to what is actually varied. The abstraction of a *nature* is possible in the way the abstraction of geometrical space is possible: a self-enclosed structural a priori is thereby delimited, creating the ground for a closed science independent of the a priori belonging to the concrete world in its meanings.

To gain the full a priori of the experiential world in its full concretion, we must follow a consciously *abstractive procedure* that systematically discerns the *structural strata*, grasps them abstractively for themselves, but constantly secures the correlative abstractions so as to be able, so to speak, to build up the total abstraction of concretion.

#### § 2. The Ordered Abstractive Dismantling of Everything Subjective from the Concrete Experiential World for the Purpose of Gaining Mere Nature

**[§, pp. 265–274]** Husserl executes the abstractive dismantling (*Abbau*) in a carefully ordered sequence:

(1) The most general structural distinction in the concrete surrounding world: *subjects* versus *non-subjects* (things).

(2) Abstractive removal of the *predicates of spiritual significance* — all predicates referring to subjects who have, through their acts, bestowed a "personally instituted" origin on things.

(3) The world as "truly and really existing" is the validity-unity confirming itself in the consequence of concordant experience. We bind our investigation to this *idea*.

(4) Removal of *feeling-predicates*, active valuations, *mood-characters*, characters of drive-like or instinctive attraction — all that we do not attribute to the thing itself, though the thing necessarily presents itself in some such characters. The stimmungsmäßig, the sad and cheerful of a landscape, the character of strikingness — these do not belong to the *thing itself*.

(5) Removal of all *subjective appearance-modes*: changing perspectives, differences of orientation both spatial and temporal, the here and there, now and just-past, shape-perspective and colour-perspective. When we abstractively delimit the thing-world purely as that experienceable in selfsameness and in its individual own-essence in concordant experience, we have gained the *idea "mere nature"* (*bloße Natur*). From the world, the entire cultural world has vanished; we make ourselves thematically blind to it.

However, a crucial distinction emerges. Perspectives stand in an *essential connection* with the thing within its thingly nature: anyone who is to have experienced the same eigenhood of the thing must have experienced it in the same perspectival connections. In contrast, feeling-characters and mood-characters stand in a merely external relation to the thing's own nature.

(6) Regarding the counter-member of the first-level abstraction — *subjects* (humans and animals, i.e. *animalia*): we can abstract from all their psychic interiority. Then their body reduces to a *mere thing*, distinguished only by a peculiar typicality — that which characterizes an *organism*. In the abstractive attitude toward nature, only one class would be distinguished among the other natural objects through a special typicality as human body-corpses, and from there a wide series of related types, all ultimately comprised under the type "organism."

#### § 3. All-Nature and All-Spirit. The Absence of a Closed Experiential Unity of Everything Subjective

**[§, pp. 272–274]** We gain the *all-nature* (*Allnatur*) as the universe of all natural things — a really connected and openly endless manifold. Over against it stands the "all-spirit" (*Allgeist*). But — and this is the crucial asymmetry — while the thematic abstraction of all-nature yields a self-enclosed and thematically independently investigable structure, what remains is *not* a unity of the subjective that would be a similarly closed experiential unity. As soon as we step over the thematic walls enclosing nature, we find ourselves in embarrassment. The non-natural, the spiritual, prevails everywhere in the concrete experiential world; it attaches itself to everything, but in very different ways.

All natural objects have their manifold constitutive "appearance-modes." Bodies as natural objects have, in addition, a totally different subjectivity: the "psychic 'interiority'" that makes a body into *this* human, *this* animal. Each such interiority is completely self-enclosed and centred under the title "ego." Every ego lives in a manifold ego-life of intentionality (consciousness-of), partly active (I experience, I think, I wish, I will), partly passive affection from a dark passive living-ground. Egos are not merely unity-points for their life; they have "capacities," habitualities, lasting convictions. We come upon souls as *monads*. But within the universal frame of the experienced world, we have the many intertwined things, among them the many bodies, and "on" them — in a quite special, unphysical sense — the belonging souls.

---

### Text Nr. 27. The Natural Core of the World in Its Respective Experiential Sense Is a Product of Experiencing Doing. Equating Natural Appearance-Modes and Cultural Formations. Appearance-Modes as Objects of Praxis (*Der naturale Kern der Welt in seinem jeweiligen Erfahrungssinn ist ein Erzeugnis erfahrenden Tuns*)

*[Whitsun week 1926]*

**[§, pp. 275–280]** The *personal world* — the world with which the human sciences deal — is always the actually experienced world in its experiential How. There is a double system of forms to study: the forms of the valid real world itself, and the forms of the manifold of "presentations" in which it presents itself for the experiencing subjects.

Husserl draws a striking parallel: the natural core of the world in its experiential sense is itself a *product of experiencing doing*. In the action of outer experiencing, the experienced real gains ever new experiential sense, and this new sense is the "product" of the experiencing doing. The appearance-modes belonging to the natural core of the world thus fall into one class together with the spiritualizations of reality of every kind. When the nature is displayed as core-structure, we have on one side the manifold of subjective "appearance-modes" or subjective shapes that nature itself can have in the personal surrounding world, and on the other side the manifold subjective appearance-modes that no less belong to the natural realities as practically realized and to-be-realized in the personal surrounding world.

The concept of *surrounding world* (*Umwelt*) becomes accordingly *ambiguous*: it sometimes means the realities themselves in their identity-preservation with their real characteristics, and sometimes the actually practical and for the subjects experientially accessible world — the reality-world in the respective How. All the subjective is, so to speak, multiply *polarized*; it carries in itself as experienced ultimately a pole-system — that of *real nature*. Everything subjective appears in the experiential surrounding world as *naturalized* — endowing the natural with subjective sense or content.

---

### Text Nr. 28. The General Reality-Structure of the World of Experience and Its Founding Structure "Nature." Regional Kinds of Self-Preservation and of Change of Concrete Realities (*Die allgemeine Realitätenstruktur der Welt der Erfahrung und ihre fundierende Struktur „Natur"*)

*[1929]*

#### § 1. Method of Conceiving the Invariant Essential Form of a Possible Experiential World in General

**[§, pp. 281–283]** The world is given as the spatiotemporal allness — "world" — of realities, constantly experienced and experienceable in concordant intersubjective experience. We distinguish between the world as it experientially holds for us and the true world in itself, knowing that what now holds could lose its validity in further experience. Considering the world now holding for us as it confirms itself in concordant experience, thinking its experiential sense through to the end, we gain the invariant form-structure without which a world holding for anyone is not thinkable. This essential form must also belong to the true world in itself — which is nothing other than the *idea* of a world experienceable by everyone in concordance in infinitum.

#### § 2. Kinds of Change of Realities

**[§, pp. 283–290]** Every reality is individuated by spatiotemporal position. Every concrete reality has its duration, its shape-extension, and in each time-point an individual position in space. The spatiotemporal *filling* consists of *qualitative moments* — what extends through the extension, "qualifying" it. This brings new modes of change beyond movement and deformation: *qualitative change*.

Changes are not arbitrary: the realities in the outside-one-another are multiply dependent on one another. The regularities governing changes in which a reality preserves itself as identical are the lawfulnesses of *causality*.

The realities of the world have their *regional forms*. Husserl introduces the concept of *region-specific kinds of self-preservation*: (a) Self-preservation of a *physical thing* — its non-self-preservation is disintegration into parts. (b) Self-preservation of an *organism* — its opposite is death, cessation of regional unity. (c) Self-preservation of a *human being* — persisting in specifically human changes, including both organic self-preservation and spiritual-personal self-preservation. (d) Self-preservation of a *city*, a *work of art*, a *machine* — each has its own way of being destroyed, its own way in which regional unity ceases while real unity in a more general sense may persist.

Through all other regions runs the region "physical nature" in the way that every reality either is merely a physical object or is simultaneously a physical object and, through a higher stratum of determinations, a reality of a new region — the purely physical determinations being unified into a physical reality, and the non-physical determinations belonging together with them to form the unity of a higher-level reality. *The world at bottom is universal nature.*

#### § 3. Region-Specific Kinds of Self-Preservation of Concrete Realities and Region-Specific Kinds of Transition from Preservation to Destruction

**[§, pp. 286–290]** The threefold selfsameness of a human being — as physical thing, as organism, as spiritual person — is not a collective togetherness but selfsameness in the unity of a persisting in the manifoldness in the specific sense of a persisting real objectuality. The regional unity "human being" must be interrogated as to how it is experiential unity — its experiential sense must be explicated in these directions.

---

### Supplement XXII. Natural Kinds of Change of Realities: Joining and Division, Mixing and Unmixing. Natural Causality and the Possibility of Personal Intervention in Natural Happening (*Naturale Veränderungsarten von Realem*)

*[ca. 1929]*

**[§, pp. 291–293]** Husserl elaborates on natural kinds of change: division and joining (corresponding to the divisibility of spatial extension), mixing and unmixing (two realities fusing into one and separating again). Wholes and sets; secondary realities. Processes and processes of collective form can be regarded as realities. Biology. The physical-chemical attitude: directed toward ultimate parts.

Every natural reality has as its formal determination that all its actual and possible changes, including division and mixing, stand under *causal regulation purely within nature*. But this does not mean that within mere nature all happening is univocally determined, nor that hyperphysical intervention — intervention by ego-subjects — is excluded. *It can be that a universal natural causality is precisely the presupposition for personal "intervention"*, which thus can never alter the natural order since it precisely presupposes it. Universal natural causality necessarily still *leaves something open*.

---

### Text Nr. 29. World — Real and Ideal Objects. The Natural Core of the Appearing Reality-World and the Worldliness of Ideal Objectualities (*Welt – reale und ideale Gegenstände*)

*[ca. 1929]*

#### § 1. The Identity-Structure of the Pre-Given Intersubjectively Experienceable World and Its Two Strata of Essential Forms

**[§, pp. 294–298]** The world has an *identity-structure* enabling intersubjective identification of the same thing despite different apperceptions by different people. This identity requires an identical content-core, objectively for me and for everyone. Thus the same world has for all of us, purely as world of communally concordant experience, an *unconditionally objective* stratum, beyond which further determinations remain open that vary subjectively (according to special communities). We have *two strata of essential forms*: (1) the form of the core of unconditionally objective predicates grounding individual identity of the real as unconditionally universally experienceable; (2) the form belonging to intersubjectively experienceable realities regarding determinations that in their essential typicality are necessary but need not have the same particular, same individual content for everyone.

Alongside objective truth regarding the world itself (what is for everyone unconditionally objective), there is also a general truth regarding the *relatively objective* — insofar as it is, in its relativity (its reference to special subject-groups), accessible to everyone.

#### § 2. Ideal and Real Objectualities. Persons and Personal Communities. Free and Bound Idealities

**[§, pp. 298–300]** *Real objects* are those mundane objects individuated by spatiotemporal locality. *Ideal objects* in the world have spatiotemporal occurrence but can appear at many spatiotemporal places simultaneously, yet numerically identical as the same. To the occurrence of ideal objects essentially belongs that they are subjective formations — localized in worldliness through the locality of subjects, but identifiable as the same across different times and subjects.

Persons are real, essentially related to governing in a single real body. Personal communities are real despite the free change of place of the involved persons; they have not a singular body but a plurality of bodies. Husserl distinguishes *free idealities* (logical-mathematical formations and pure essential structures of every kind) from *bound idealities* that carry reality with themselves in their being-sense and thereby belong to the real world. All reality is traced back to spatiotemporality as form of the individual — but originally it belongs to nature. *The world as world of realities has its individuality from nature as its lowest stratum.*

---

### Text Nr. 30. Nature in Surrounding-Worldliness and Nature in Itself. Habitual Style of the Surrounding World as Causal Style. Over against This Surrounding-Worldly "Causality," Exact Causality. Homogenization of Earthly Surrounding World and Heavenly World (*Natur in der Umweltlichkeit und Natur an sich*)

*[ca. January or February 1934]*

**[§, pp. 301–306]** Husserl analyses the constitution of the "in itself" (*An-sich*) of nature from within surrounding-worldliness. In the everyday surrounding world, the "habitual style" prevails: things customarily tend to influence one another's movements and changes, but this does not preclude "chance" happenings against all habit. Universal and necessary natural causality does not yet belong to the idea of the "in itself" of the surrounding world.

This leads to the great problem of the *infinite extension* of the surrounding world — from the earthly surrounding world (as world of genuinely possible experience) to a universal nature extending infinitely, with a truly infinite space and infinite time as form, all-encompassing causality, inner infinity through the supposition of divisibility in infinitum, and exact natural causality in which there is no chance.

Starting from the surrounding world, we find it in the division: *earthly world* and *heavenly world*. The earthly world is the world of realities in the familiar sense of human praxis, constituted through near-far appearances referring back to a genuinely perceivable thing-itself as the measure of all far-reality. The heavenly world is a separate, but in determinate ways connected, universe of beings that are *not* constituted as unities of near-far appearances. Sun and moon are continuously perceptually there but not experienced as bodies in the earthly sense. The concepts of persisting being, movement, change, and space all have, when purely drawn from the experience of the heavenly, *their own meaning* — a different concept of being.

The naive though thought-mediated substruktion that the heavenly world consists of ever more distant earthly-type realities cancels the division of earth and heaven, transforms the heaven into a sphere of ever-more-distant earthly things, creates a *homogeneous world* — a universe of beings all standing under the one single concept of being, the earthly-constituted one. Only *infinity is carried into the earthly*, and the finite limit of absolute nearness becomes a *mathematized limit* already enclosing an infinity within itself. Conversely, distance too is stripped of its finitude and passes into the heaven, which has become the overwhelmingly distant and finally the infinitely distant.

## VI. The Life-World as Personal World of Praxis and World of Finite Cognition-Interests Bounded by Practical Goals (*Die Lebenswelt als personale Welt der Praxis und Welt der von praktischen Zielen begrenzten endlichen Erkenntnisinteressen*)

---

### Text Nr. 31. Finitude of Praxis. Finite Surrounding World as Practical. The Practical Surrounding World as Horizon of Actual and Possible Interests (*Endlichkeit der Praxis. Endliche Umwelt als praktische*)

*[ca. November 1932]*

**[§, pp. 307–310]** Husserl opens with a fundamental observation: the finitude of praxis is a fundamental piece of the finitude of life as a whole. Waking, active life is in the widest sense *practical* — interest-life that has its respective interests, habitual purposes and purpose-syntheses. All experiencing as a doing is interested, directed toward goals. The interests of life have their essential forms: serious interests forming unity, periodically interrupted by interests of play and leisure.

Every experience has its prescribed *end*, to exceed which goes against the practical sense of the respective practical undertaking. The practical life is life in the respective *practical surrounding world* — the surrounding world that bounds all special interests insofar as they refer to its finitude. Not just individual objects but the surrounding world itself has the character of the practically finite: each thing lies in a finite experiential field, each perceived in a finite perceptual field. This field has, from the interest-subjectivity and its personal interests, a horizon distinguished as horizon of *actual and possible interests*. These possible interests are not empty possibilities but are prefigured through a normal, familiar course of interest-activity, with a finitude-giving structure and various layers or levels: "homeland" as village, city, homeland in the wider sense, homeland-country — these designate variously "lively" or "awakened" horizons correlative with levels of personalities (family, village-community, Badeners, Germans, Europeans).

What is thereby circumscribed is the *concept of the practical surrounding world*. This surrounding world in its finitude still has a horizon for the practical human being: the "unelaborated," vague, with no known or typically known objects occupied horizon — the horizon of vague further-reaching nature, and corresponding empty possibilities that praxis might penetrate there.

---

### Text Nr. 32. The Acting Human Beings, Actions, and Action-Results with Their Teleological Face Belonging to the Life-Surrounding-World as Practical World (*Die zur Lebensumwelt als praktischer Welt gehörigen handelnden Menschen, Handlungen und Handlungsergebnisse mit ihrem teleologischen Gesicht*)

*[End of 1931 or beginning of 1932]*

**[§, pp. 310–312]** From every action, whether successful or failed, results emerge — fulfilling the intention or contrary to it. Every result as now-existing and existing-for-humans has, in its understandability as result, its *teleological face*, its teleological characteristics belonging to it in relation to practically directed humans and their actions. But this is limited by the understandability prepared for by the respective humans.

If we dismantle this humanization of the world, universal nature remains. In the ascending reconstruction of the becoming humanization, we reconstruct a system of changes of the world whereby at the lowest level stand natural changes and an immediate purpose-sense related to the most general needs. Then the higher levels of mediacy.

Nature gives itself for everyone as *over-clothed with humanizing predicates*. The humans understand themselves as humans at bottom in their bodily governing, but also in their typical immediate needs and ways of need-satisfaction, then in higher-level motivated needs. Humans apperceive the same things as things of the same or analogous purposive serviceability. To the construction of a human surrounding world belongs the *correlation* of the humanization and the humanizing subjectivity.

---

### Text Nr. 33. Levels of Surrounding Worlds and Their Constitution (*Stufen der Umwelten und ihre Konstitution*)

*[ca. 1932]*

**[§, pp. 316ff.]** Husserl analyses the layered constitution of surrounding worlds, from the most immediate practical environment through the expanding circles of communal life. Each level has its own constitutive structure: the family's surrounding world, the village's, the people's, the nation's. These are not simply concentric circles of the same type but involve qualitatively different modes of constitution, different types of familiarity and access, different styles of practical relevance.

---

### Text Nr. 34. The World as Practical Surrounding World and the Finitude of Practical Cognition-Interest (*Die Welt als praktische Umwelt und die Endlichkeit des praktischen Erkenntnisinteresses*)

*[ca. 1932]*

**[§, pp. 326ff.]** Husserl develops the theme of practical cognition-interest as essentially finite. Every cognition in practical life has its practically prescribed limit: one needs to know *enough* for the purpose at hand, not everything about the object. This practical finitude shapes the very structure of what counts as "known" and "unknown" in the life-world. The transition from practical to theoretical interest — the interest in knowing for the sake of knowing — represents a fundamentally new attitude that breaks through the finitude of practical cognition.

---

### Text Nr. 35. The Surrounding World as World of Practice and the Question of the Constitution of Its Pre-Givenness (*Die Umwelt als Welt der Praxis und die Frage der Konstitution ihrer Vorgegebenheit*)

*[ca. 1932]*

**[§, pp. 338ff.]** Husserl addresses the question how the pre-givenness of the surrounding world as practical world is constituted. The world is not first given as a neutral field of perception and then overlaid with practical significance; rather, it is from the outset given *as* a practical field — a field of possible doing, of materials and tools, of obstacles and opportunities. The constitution of pre-givenness involves the sedimentation of past practical dealings into habitual apperceptions.

---

### Text Nr. 36. The Surrounding World as Experiential World for the Human Being (*Die Umwelt als Erfahrungswelt für den Menschen*)

*[ca. 1932]*

**[§, pp. 354ff.]** Husserl further develops the theme of the surrounding world as structured by human experience. The surrounding world is not merely spatiotemporal but has a specifically human-experiential structure: it is organized around the human body and its capacities, structured by human interests and concerns, layered by degrees of familiarity and foreignness, colored by moods and practical valuations.

---

### Text Nr. 37. Person and Surrounding World. The Personal World as World of Practice (*Person und Umwelt. Die personale Welt als Welt der Praxis*)

*[ca. 1932]*

**[§, pp. 370ff.]** Husserl analyses the correlation of person and surrounding world systematically. The person is not merely a subject *in* the world but one whose very being is constituted through its relationship to the world as practical field. The personal world — the world as it is for a specific person or community — has its own truth, its own evidence, its own structure of relevance that cannot be reduced to the objective-scientific description of "the same" world.

---

### Text Nr. 38. Communal Life and the Constitution of the Common Surrounding World. Higher-Order Personalities (*Gemeinschaftsleben und die Konstitution der gemeinsamen Umwelt. Personalitäten höherer Ordnung*)

*[ca. 1932–35]*

**[§, pp. 382ff.]** Husserl investigates the constitution of the common surrounding world through communal life. Persons live not only as individuals but in communities of various kinds and levels: families, professional associations, peoples, states. Each community constitutes a common surrounding world that is not merely the intersection of individual surrounding worlds but has its own distinctive structure. *Higher-order personalities* — families, peoples, states — have their own modes of willing, their own habitualities, their own way of persisting through time.

Each person has their own sphere of free disposition — the *private* distinguished from the *public*. A community has common possession (*Gemeinhabe*). The practical surrounding world as communal has in it each person's own possessions. Nature as the core of praxis; the field of the total community is the *territory*; the world is the universe of territories, and beyond them the wide earthly world.

---

### Supplement XXVII. Professions in the Normal People and Their Historicity. On the Phenomenology of Professions (*Berufe im normalen Volk und ihre Geschichtlichkeit*)

*[ca. March 1935]*

**[§, pp. 392–394]** The general professional title "craftsman" — more precisely "cobbler," "tailor," etc. — designates a life-task for those active in the profession. Entering the profession, they have taken on the professional task as life-task. To a profession as a civic profession belongs a horizon of colleagues living in the same profession. In the normal structure of a people, a manifold of professions belongs, each designating a general type of life-tasks that in each generation members of the people have taken upon themselves.

All professions for all normal members of the people in the normal people are communalized insofar as a unity of validity binds them all. Professions can be specially connected — industrialization. Becoming of new professions, dropping out of professions in the historical transformation of the people-creating structure.

---

### Supplement XXVIII. Territory. Personal Spatiotemporality in Its Personal Levels (*Territorium. Personale Raumzeitlichkeit in ihren Personalstufen*)

*[ca. 1928]*

**[§, pp. 394–395]** Husserl develops the concept of *personal spatiotemporality*. The territory is not a fixed piece of land — a wandering people, remaining unified in its tradition through changes of dwelling-place, has in every historical time its dwelling-place. To this corresponds a specific idea of "movement" that here is called "migration" — a "spiritual" movement. The individual has their temporarily fixed dwelling-place, seat, and standpoint: the house, the family's ancestral seat, the village, the city, the people's fixed territory. These are place-concepts that have *personal sense* and must be distinguished from the concept of the natural (geometrical-spatial) place. Further personal places: work-places, official positions ("the office has its seat here and there"), scientific "institutions" — expressions that, through the meaning of professional function, carry the meaning of a place in the "spiritual space" of the historical totality.

---

### Supplement XXIX. Property. The Object-Field in the Horizon of Appropriation and Disposal, of Concordance and Conflict (*Eigentum*)

*[Beginning of the 1930s]*

**[§, pp. 395–396]** Husserl sketches a phenomenology of property. Possession (*Habe*) as durably disposable, durably relevant for my purpose. This possession is a *pre-concept of property* (*Eigentum*). Another can appropriate it through force, or it passes to them through my consent. The object-field is overclad with my purposes; on this horizon my practical willing is directed. The same object-field as will-horizon for others — and thus as unified field for conflicts and for the possibilities of consents, agreements: *horizon for concordance and conflict*.

---

### Supplement XXX. On the Haptical Constitution of the Practical World. The Priority of Tactile Perceptions over Visual Perceptions (*Zur haptischen Konstitution der praktischen Welt*)

*[ca. 1931]*

**[§, pp. 396–400]** Husserl distinguishes: (1) merely perceptually functioning kinaestheses — constituting the perceptual total-field of spatial things; (2) practically functioning kinaestheses — through which the already perceptually given world is practically "handlingly" transformed. These are not two separated sorts of kinaestheses but two ways of co-functioning within the unity of the kinaesthetic system.

Husserl identifies a peculiar *priority of haptically reduced perception*: touching the things. The touching is in the associated kinaesthesis a self-presentation of the object that is *not "perspectivation."* While an optical presentation of a small piece of surface is a mere "adumbration," the touched is not merely adumbrated but there in *another originality* — and *this* kind of originality is the *ultimately fundamental* one, to which the optical also refers back. The haptical touching is still unpractical like mere seeing, but touching has the peculiarity that *it can become, through force-exertion, a pushing, shoving, pressing* — and then a grasping, seizing, lifting, carrying. From the merely perceiving touching, through which only the phantom (the res extensa) constitutes itself, there arises now *a world into which we intervene*.

---

### Text Nr. 39. The True Life-World to Be Corrected Out as Foundation of Life-Worldly Praxis. World and Surrounding World. Constitution of the World in Its Infinite Temporally Self-Presenting in Relatively "Stable" Surrounding Worlds. Carried Out for Nature (*Die herauszukorrigierende wahre Lebenswelt als Grundlage lebensweltlicher Praxis*)

*[1933]*

**[§, pp. 401–407]** Husserl develops one of the most comprehensive analyses in the volume. The world that is for us is constituted as the world appearing and holding in being-validity in constant change in the streaming course of functioning life — as a normally concordant one, as ground for modalization, for correction. In the process of breaking-in modalizations and corrections, a "finite" *stable surrounding world* continually establishes itself — one on which we can always count, a world of withstanding concordance, the *normal*, in which here and there occasionally there are deceptions.

Nature itself, in itself, distinguished from the nature that holds for me, for us in our respective situation, is nothing other than the *idea of the infinitely extended concordant totality of all appearances of all respective life-presents* — but understood as the totality of those whose being-validities are *absolutely concordant* in the totally intuitively made course of all actual and possible life-presents.

Concordance always and necessarily precedes discordance, and being precedes illusion. But the concordance in which beings are self-given is always equipped with a horizon of further experience, of possible new appearances, and belonging to it the capacity to activate this horizon and thereby expand but also correct world-knowledge. Always within the narrower circle of life and experience, modalization led to correction, and so one had individually and with the nearest others always again existing world — existing as the same as before, only corrected, at the same time enriched.

The *unbroken style of the practical life-surrounding-world*: however far we may extend the connection, however deeply we may penetrate into infinity, it cannot come about that we end with the non-being of the world. We in finitude have world only from the finitude of the co-subjects actually entering into play — in the horizon, however, the possibility of newly entering humans. A finite, empirically *stable surrounding world* is always constituted, on which one can count, though its stability can be disturbed by "chance" — by the chance entry of new co-subjects — but in disturbances that are nonetheless compensable. Whoever is a human being among fellow humans has their relatively stable surrounding world, their already concordant or to-be-corrected-out *true life-world* and for it their truths.

---

*This completes the commentary on Part VI. Part VII covers "The World as Acquisition — Structure and Genesis of the World-Apperception and of the Apperceptions of Worldly Beings."*

## VII. The World as Acquisition — Structure and Genesis of the World-Apperception and of the Apperceptions of Worldly Beings (*Die Welt als Erwerb. Struktur und Genesis der Weltapperzeption und der Apperzeptionen von weltlich Seiendem*)

---

### Text Nr. 40. On the Theory of Apperception: Perception and Apperception. Various Kinds of Apperception (*Zur Theorie der Apperzeption: Perzeption und Apperzeption. Verschiedene Arten der Apperzeption*)

*[Beginning of January 1932]*

#### § 1. Dismantling My World-Phenomenon to the World Purely Given from Perception and Apperception. The Pregnant Concept of Perception and Apperception

**[§, pp. 409–417]** Husserl develops a comprehensive theory of apperception as the key to understanding how the world constitutes itself as an acquisition. Under "perception" (*Perzeption*) in the pregnant sense he understands: (1) *direct presentation* (*Präsentation*), in which something present comes to direct self-givenness and active self-grasping; (2) *appresentation* (*Appräsentation*), itself of various kinds, always founded in presentation. Every concrete perception of something concretely real is itself analysable into presentation and appresentation in mutual founding: the front-side and the back-side.

A further form of appresentation is *concrete appresentation*: the case of *indication* (*Anzeige*). A perceived animal track indicates the co-presence of game. Culture-things are seen in their thingly properties but simultaneously apperceived as cultural formations with cultural properties. Indicated are not only co-present things but also earlier processes, causal events, human persons with their purposes and typical needs. The presented has, by virtue of appresentation, an *apperceptive sense* overlaid, founded in the purely presented sense.

#### § 2. Indicating (Back-Referring and Forward-Referring) and Analogizing Apperception. Image-Apperception and Epoché

**[§, pp. 413–417]** Husserl distinguishes *back-referring* indication (a "lava flow" apperceived as indicating a past process of origin) from *forward-referring* indication (something now unchanged apperceived as about to change). There is also *co-presentifying* induction: every apperception of a familiar environment is more than mere recollection — it contains the anticipation (induction) that progressing through the various orientational directions would show the same thing-constellations as before.

Every kind of apperception constitutes through induction and interlacements of inductions; following the *typicality of constitutions* is: genetically following the *validity-construction of the world existing for us* — the typicality of its construction from realities with real properties, relations, configurations, and finally the world in its universal forms.

Husserl then analyses *image-apperception* and its relation to epoché. The image-landscape is a perception that induces a surrounding-context in conflict with the otherwise total perception of the perceptual field. Living purely in the image, I am *lifted out of actuality* — an epoché. Every positional thing can be set into the phenomenon through epoché, and the entire actuality thereby. This repeated always yields the same phenomenon — the "pure" possibility lying in actuality.

---

### Supplement XXXI. Apperception as Transcendent Self-Givenness, Creating an Intuitive Existing World. Analogizing and Practical Apperception (*Apperzeption als transzendente Selbstgebung*)

*[ca. 1932]*

**[§, pp. 418ff.]** In the original *genesis of all apperceptions*, from the level of the lowest sensuous time-consciousness up through spatial thingliness to the highest apperception-types, "experience" manifests itself. *We get to know the worlds by constitutively constituting them in ourselves apperceptively.* All types of apperceptions have their right in themselves, even though they need not confirm themselves through experience. Analogies and parables have their illuminating power; the natural practical life is entirely determined by intuitive apperceptions, by ever new analogies in apprehending.

---

### Supplements XXXII–XXXVI

These supplements develop various aspects of the theory of apperception: the ego as subject of all affections and actions (Supplement XXXII); the passive and active dimensions of apperceptive constitution (Supplement XXXIII); the problem of how first apperception could have arisen — how beings emerge from pre-beings, how worldly doing presupposing cognition can emerge from a pre-worldly doing (Supplement XXXIV, raising the spectre of an infinite regress); the "mute" and "spoken" world and the genesis of the style of world-experience (Supplement XXXV); and the structure of the streaming life of consciousness in its constitutive achievement (Supplement XXXVI).

---

### Text Nr. 41. Pre-Given World as Correlate of Acquisition-Processes. Apperceptive Constitution of Beings as Unity of Passive Self-Covering and Active Identification. The Constitution of Pluralities Therein Founded. Apperception of Pluralities of Likes or Similars. Universe — Open Plurality-Horizon (*Vorgegebene Welt als Korrelat von Erwerbsprozessen*)

*[ca. 1932]*

#### § 1. Apperceptive Constitution of Beings as Unity of Passive Self-Covering and Active Identification

**[§, pp. 435ff.]** Husserl develops the theory of apperception as "analogizing transfer" of a so-being-validity from one item to a similar one. This transfer is an essential fact of *association*. In *pairing*, every so-being appearing at one exemplar conditions the other to be apperceived with the same so-being — within the pairing commonality, within the covering. A crucial distinction: passive covering in pure passivity versus active identification in which the ego fixates being.

#### § 2. Apperception of Pluralities of Likes or Similars

**[§, pp. 441ff.]** In all pluralities of the ordinary kind there is repetition, covering-unity, recognizing one in the other in distanciation. Repetition can be pure repetition (equality), or similarity with partial non-covering. Through the advance of experience, the acquired being-sense of one object is *anticipated in advance* for the second, similar object.

---

### Supplement XXXVII. Apperzeptive Acquisition of Being-Sense (*Apperzeptiver Erwerb von Seinssinn*)

*[ca. 1932]*

**[§, pp. 444ff.]** Husserl provides a noetic-noematic structural analysis of the experiencing consciousness of the world as simply pre-given life-world: (1) inactively: association-"synthesis," unity and difference; (2) modes of activity: retaining, imprinting (actively getting to know), strength-differences of imprinting, increasing deepening of familiarity; (3) modes of inactivity (unconsciousness, memory); (4) apperceptive transfer, assimilation. Through the streaming world-experience runs a constant *synthesis of association*, including synthesis of identification, synthesis of distinction, and overlapping syntheses.

---

### Text Nr. 42. The Mute and the Spoken World. Problem of the Genesis of the Style of World-Experience (*Stumme und ausgesprochene Welt. Problem der Genesis des Stils der Welterfahrung*)

*[ca. 1932]*

**[§, pp. 446ff.]** Husserl distinguishes the "mute" (*stumm*) perceiving — the pre-attentive, pre-explicative having of the real as unattended, ungrasped object — from the "spoken" world in which things have been explicated and articulated. The real in its "mute" appearance must become the real that "speaks itself out" in its so-being, that explicates itself, before it can enter into judgments and truths. Genesis of the world as the world holding for me means understanding how from and in me my holding-valid as holding-valid of the world is to originate — including the origin of the still "mute," still uncaptured and unexplicated world.

---

### Text Nr. 43. Birth and Death as Limit-Problems of Transcendental-Aesthetic Explication (*Geburt und Tod als Randprobleme transzendentalästhetischer Auslegung*)

*[ca. 1933–34]*

#### § 1. On the Reconstruction of Earliest Childhood: Primal Affection and Beginning of Streaming Temporalization

**[§, pp. 456ff.]** Husserl addresses birth and death as limit-problems. Abstracting from "others" and reducing to the primordial, we come to the primordial phenomenon "world" in the primordial ego — the problem of the *beginning*. The beginning-way of world-explication is abstract, yielding only one level. Experience as self-givenness necessarily has the meaning of *normality* — initially of the "normal human." But to the being-sense of the world horizontally belongs also the horizon of the "anomalous" human, of children, finally of animals. Their inclusion as "intentional modifications" yields a second level.

#### § 2. The Ego of the Constitutive Beginning: An Ego of Directed Instincts

**[§, pp. 460ff.]** The ego at its constitutive beginning is an ego of directed instincts — not yet a person, not yet a subject with a world, but an instinctive centre from which world-constitution will progressively unfold.

---

### Supplements XXXIX–XLI

These supplements treat: the problem of how the transcendental analysis of the world-phenomenon proceeds from the world-phenomenon back to the transcendental subjectivity and its streaming, self-transforming world-apperception (Supplement XXXIX); possibilities of a transcendental otherwise-being — an other-than-as-human self-objectification (Supplement XL); and the systematic method of transcendental-aesthetic explication, distinguishing static from genetic layers of analysis (Supplement XLI).

---

### Text Nr. 44. The Primordial Time-World and Its Apperceptive Extensions to the Full Intersubjective Time-World. Historical Apperceptions. Primordial and Intersubjective Historicity of the World (*Die primordiale Zeitwelt und ihre apperzeptiven Erweiterungen zur vollen intersubjektiven Zeitwelt*)

*[ca. 1932]*

**[§, pp. 476ff.]** Husserl traces how, in the constant function of forming apperceptions, the world as world of possible experience *progressively extends itself* — and how in the extended horizon the indeterminate, the unconfirmable, and the confirmable first differentiate themselves. The world is *not* given in advance as an in-itself-determinate confirmable world. The finitude of the primordialy accessible time-world gives way to the humanly-generative time-world in its intersubjective accessibility — the world of historical apperceptions and intersubjective historicity.

---

### Text Nr. 45. The Pre-Given Life-World — an Apperceptive Sense- and Validity-Formation That Continually Takes Up Ever New Apperceptive Sense-Overlays as Validities-For (*Die vorgegebene Lebenswelt – ein apperzeptives Sinn- und Geltungsgebilde*)

*[ca. 1932]*

**[§, pp. 494ff.]** All generating praxis creates for the things that are its substrates thingly sense-overlays as *validities-for*. But whenever the world is there for us as intuitive world, it is always already apperception in this sense. Pre-given world is apperceived, equipped with validities-for, world in one. "Pre-givenness" itself means: pre-givenness for ever new possible activities as creating higher-level sense originally — pre-given for ever new possible apperceptions of humans as act-subjects.

---

### Text Nr. 46. The Life-World as Validity-World in Traditional Change and in Traditional Self-Preservation. Extensions of Our Generative Validity-World and Their Tendency toward a Supranational Terrestrial Culture (*Die Lebenswelt als Geltungswelt in traditionalem Wandel und in traditionaler Selbsterhaltung*)

*[ca. 1933]*

**[§, pp. 500ff.]** The pre-given world is the *traditional world* — the world of generative tradition. The autonomous human, the human of autonomous science, places themselves above all tradition and exercises a universal critique of all tradition. In the pre-given world we find *language* in its validity-structure, various "customs" — behaviours in a certain normative form. Every mundane perception carries its being-sense (as all-temporal) horizontally within itself. The modern human already has constituted an all-terrestrial humanity, and all terrestrial humanities have already come to a synthesis of their special historicities, special traditions, special surrounding worlds — a synthesis that is in its beginning and tends toward a unitary cultural humanity.

---

### Text Nr. 47. The Streaming Constitution of the World as Correlate of Streaming Apperceptions. The Problem of the Constitution of the World-Form "Spatiotemporality" (*Die strömende Konstitution der Welt als Korrelat strömender Apperzeptionen*)

*[ca. 1933]*

**[§, pp. 524ff.]** Husserl analyses the world as correlate of streaming apperceptions, distinguishing the subjective (the mode of givenness) from the worldly-ontic (the content of being-certainty). In every phase of consciousness-life, the ego has its "world" as the ontic content of its presently streaming being-certainty. The ontological form "spatiotemporal world" is the invariant structural core of all streaming apperceptions — but this form itself is constituted through the streaming, not imposed from outside.

---

### Text Nr. 48. World-Apperception and Its Habitual and Traditional Character (*Weltapperzeption und ihr habitueller und traditionaler Charakter*)

*[ca. 1933]*

**[§, pp. 534ff.]** Husserl develops the habitual and traditional character of world-apperception. The world-apperception I have now is not freshly constituted in this moment but carries within itself the sedimented results of all previous experience — my own and that of the community into which I have grown. Every apperception has its *genesis* in earlier apperceptions; the world as a whole has its genesis in the historical tradition of the community. The habitual style of world-experience is not merely individual but communal and generative.

---

## VIII. General Aspects of the Temporal Constitution of the Life-World (*Allgemeine Aspekte der zeitlichen Konstitution der Lebenswelt*)

---

### Text Nr. 49. The Standing-Streaming Life as World-Constituting. The Ontic and the Subjective in the Streaming World-Consciousness (*Das stehend-strömende Leben als weltkonstituierendes*)

*[ca. 1931]*

**[§, pp. 539ff.]** Husserl analyses the standing-streaming life as world-constituting. The standing streaming is that in which, in this constancy, what has just been streamed away (in ever new modes of having-streamed-away) and coming streaming (with modes of the future Now) are given. In these stream-modes the content: the streaming is a stream of appearances, of presentations of the world. In the stream-present, in the present Now, the presently appearing world — therein as necessary and in streaming constantly self-renewing core-component: world-perception as self-givenness of world-present. In the world-present also world-past appears as streamed-away perceptual world-present (retentionally), and in another way in protention world-future. Recollections also occur as explicit repetitions of the streamed-away perception in the as-if.

But all this is still abstract: not only the ego has been left unspoken but also the other ego-subjects and their streaming life. The self-given world is that which is the universe of realities — this is its being-sense in my streaming life.

---

### Text Nr. 50. The Temporal Form of the World as Form of Self-Preservation of Realities and Its Subjective Constitution (*Die Zeitform der Welt als Form der Selbsterhaltung von Realitäten und ihre subjektive Konstitution*)

*[ca. 1932]*

**[§, pp. 554ff.]** Husserl develops the temporal form of the world as the form in which realities preserve themselves — persisting through change, maintaining their identity through the continuous flow of temporal positions. The subjective constitution of this temporal form involves the interplay of retention, primal impression, and protention — the living present as the absolute source of all temporal constitution. The time of the world is not merely experienced *in* time but *constituted through* the streaming temporality of consciousness.

---

### Supplements XLVI–XLVII

These supplements develop aspects of the temporal constitution of the life-world: the streaming present and its double intentionality (Supplement XLVI); the constitution of objective time through the synthesis of retentional and protentional horizons (Supplement XLVII).

---

### Text Nr. 51. Universal Temporality as Self-Preservation and the Problem of Absolute Temporalization (*Universale Zeitlichkeit als Selbsterhaltung und das Problem der absoluten Zeitigung*)

*[ca. 1931]*

**[§, pp. 580ff.]** Husserl addresses the deepest level of temporal constitution — the problem of *absolute temporalization* (*absolute Zeitigung*). Universal temporality as the form of self-preservation of the world has its ground in the absolute streaming of consciousness that is itself not "in" time but constitutes all time. The absolute flow has a unique self-constitution: it temporalizes itself while temporalizing everything else.

---

### Text Nr. 52. Constitutive Temporal Problems and Their Significance for the Understanding of the World as Life-World (*Konstitutive Zeitprobleme und ihre Bedeutung für das Verständnis der Welt als Lebenswelt*)

*[ca. 1934]*

**[§, pp. 586ff.]** Husserl brings together temporal constitution and the life-world concept. The life-world is not merely a world that happens to be temporal; its very character as *life-world* — as the world of living experience, of ongoing praxis, of habitual familiarity — is constituted through the specific temporal structures of retention, protention, recollection, and expectation. The standing-streaming character of the living present gives the life-world its distinctive mode of being: always the same and always changing, always familiar and always new.

---

## IX. Bodily Experience as Necessary Moment of World-Experience — Normality (*Leiberfahrung als notwendiges Moment der Welterfahrung – Normalität*)

---

### Text Nr. 53. Bodily Experience in Its Founding Function for Objective World-Experience (*Leiberfahrung in ihrer Fundierungsfunktion für objektive Welterfahrung*)

*[ca. 1932]*

#### § 1. The Concrete Life-Worldly Reality "Human Being" in Its Body-Soul Double-Layeredness. The Double-Layeredness of the Body

**[§, pp. 614ff.]** Descriptive science in the widest sense is that which holds itself in the domain of the life-world, in the horizon of actual and possible experience, with the conscious goal of bringing what is experienced as world to an ever more complete experiential cognition. Husserl analyses the concrete life-worldly reality "human being" in its body-soul double-layeredness. The body (*Leib*) itself has a double-layeredness: it is simultaneously a physical thing among things (*Körper*) and the lived body through which all world-experience is mediated. The body is the organ of all perception, the organ of all praxis, the zero-point of all spatial orientation, and the bearer of all kinaesthetic capacities.

---

### Text Nr. 54. The Possibility of Intersubjectively Grasping Empirical Truth in Descriptions. Normal Sensibility and the Experience of the Anomalous (*Die Möglichkeit, intersubjektiv die empirische Wahrheit in Deskriptionen zu erfassen. Normale Sinnlichkeit und Erfahren des Anomalen*)

*[ca. 1932]*

**[§, pp. 636ff.]** Husserl addresses how intersubjective truth is possible in descriptive science despite the anomalies of sensibility. Descriptive natural science moves entirely within the framework of intersubjectively concordant, concretely intuitive experience. Within a *normal experiential community*, everyone takes things as they present themselves and regards what is perceived and truly existing as perceivable by everyone with identical content. Only when intersubjective concordance is *disturbed* does the determinate consciousness of anomality and normality arise — experience making the bodily organization and its deviation from a normal form responsible for the anomalies. The anomalous person, who has a completely concordant experiential world for themselves, will subordinate themselves to the community's normal experience.

---

### Text Nr. 55. Nature and Normal Bodiliness. Idea of Normality (*Natur und normale Leiblichkeit. Idee der Normalität*)

*[ca. 1932]*

#### § 1. The Reference of the Appearing World to Normal Bodiliness

**[§, pp. 648ff.]** The world is psychologically constituted in our human subjectivity — correlative to the constituting life and its psychic formations. But all this psychic is psychophysical — conditioned by human bodiliness. Variation of bodiliness entails co-variation of the world altogether. All types of realities depend on human bodiliness. A *normal experiential community* is one in which everyone experiences concordantly and regards the experienced world as having identical content for all. Only in the *contrast* of disturbance does the determinate consciousness of anomality and normality emerge.

---

### Text Nr. 56. Levels of Constitution of the Life-World and the Role of the Body (*Stufen der Konstitution der Lebenswelt und die Rolle des Leibes*)

*[ca. 1932]*

**[§, pp. 660ff.]** Husserl traces the levels of constitution of the life-world, emphasizing the body's role at each level. From the most basic kinaesthetic constitution of spatial objects through the constitution of the practical world and the cultural world, the body is never absent — it is the indispensable medium through which every constitutive achievement takes place.

---

### Texts Nr. 57–58 and Supplements L–LII

These texts and supplements develop further aspects of the relationship between bodily constitution, normality, and world-experience. Text Nr. 57 analyses the constitution of the empirical world as conditioned by normal bodiliness and the interplay of normality and anomality in the community. Text Nr. 58 develops the implications for the concept of objective truth — truth as that which holds for the normal community of experiencers in their normal sensibility. Supplement L continues the analysis of anomality; Supplement LI treats the structure of normal experience; Supplement LII addresses the constitutional role of kinaesthesis in world-constitution.

## X. Many Surrounding Worlds and the One True World — Relativity of Life-Worldly Truth (*Viele Umwelten und die eine wahre Welt – Relativität lebensweltlicher Wahrheit*)

---

### Text Nr. 59. The "In-Itself" of the World Given Only Surrounding-Worldly. Communication of Individual Surrounding Worlds to the Intersubjective "Surrounding World." Intentional Causality (*Die Welt „an sich" nur umweltlich gegeben*)

*[ca. 1931]*

#### § 1. The Reference to Existing World in the Form of Reference to a Respective Surrounding World

**[§, pp. 672ff.]** Husserl develops the thesis that the world "in itself" is given only surrounding-worldly (*umweltlich*). The human life-world is the world of persons. Their surrounding world is the world conscious for me — the world perceived, remembered, expected, thought, desired, striven for, treated by me. In the change of surrounding world that plays out for me and every ego, the *same* world is experienced — but every content that the world has from earlier surrounding worlds is only surrounding-worldly. It carries the stamp "until further notice." In communication, my world-aspects connect with those of others, and in the concordance of communal experience an identical world constitutes itself for all of us.

---

### Text Nr. 60. Ontology or Phenomenology of the Aesthetic World. Transcendental Aesthetics as Transcendental Ontology and Phenomenology of the World (*Ontologie bzw. Phänomenologie der ästhetischen Welt*)

*[ca. 1931]*

**[§, pp. 690ff.]** Husserl outlines the task of an ontology or phenomenology of the "aesthetic world" — the world as it presents itself in intuitive experience prior to all theoretical determination. On a purely transcendental standpoint, this is a *transcendental aesthetics* — a transcendental ontology and phenomenology of the world. "Ontology" refers to the existing world itself, existing as concordantly experienced for the relevant subjectivity; "phenomenology" refers to the correlative and therefore more concrete research considering what exists according to all its subjective modes. What a priori structures belong to an experiential world that we think purely as experienced in a concordantly experiencing humanity?

---

### Text Nr. 61. The Final Factual Truth as Practical Idea. From the Relative to the Absolute Relativism (*Die endgültige Sachwahrheit als praktische Idee. Vom relativen zum absoluten Relativismus*)

*[Winter semester 1924/25]*

#### § 1

**[§, pp. 710ff.]** Husserl addresses what we ordinarily call "outer experience" or "sensory experience" — a perceiving opining, believing, an experiential judgment. Every perception contains anticipations, determinate or more or less indeterminate. Experience is always merely *doxa*. In the intuitive surrounding world there is true being and predicative truth as *practical truth*. The completely near is the true. Every person as perceptual subject has nearness, accommodation, distance from locomotion. The near-far thing as correlate of purely perceptive self-confirmation.

#### § 2. Normality and Anomality

**[§, pp. 720ff.]** The relativity of experiential givennesses and the relativity of experiential truths are essentially connected: the universal relativity of experientially self-presenting objectualities and the relativity of experiential truths — relative to the factually experiencing and cognizing subjects and their temporary factual cognizing. Everything anomalous, as anomalous in relations, yields — when one takes these relations into account — something identical with the normal. All anomalous experience indicates "objectively true being."

---

### Texts Nr. 62–64 and Supplements LIII–LV

These texts and supplements develop the theme of many surrounding worlds and the one true world. Text Nr. 62 analyses the plurality of surrounding worlds corresponding to different communities and the question of how these constitute themselves as aspects of one and the same world. Text Nr. 63 treats the problem of the relativity of life-worldly truth in relation to scientific truth — the life-world as the ground of all scientific idealization while itself remaining in the mode of approximate, situation-relative truth. Text Nr. 64 develops the concept of the world as the universal horizon encompassing all relative surrounding worlds and the question of how the "one true world" is constituted as an *idea* — never fully given but always anticipated as the telos of all experiential concordance.

Supplement LIII analyses the structure of the shared world as constituted through the interplay of homeworld and foreign world. Supplement LIV develops the concept of relative objectivity — truth relative to a specific community but accessible in principle to any community through understanding. Supplement LV treats the ultimate grounding of world-truth in the self-confirming concordance of experience.

---

### Text Nr. 65. The Life-World as Perceptual World. Perception as "Method" of Life Itself (*Die Lebenswelt als Wahrnehmungswelt. Wahrnehmung als „Methode" des Lebens selbst*)

*[ca. 1921]*

**[§, pp. 736ff.]** The final text of the volume returns to the most fundamental level. Perception is pre-theoretically, pre-scientifically a constant function and, so to speak, *method of life itself* — a type of act through which real objects as existing, and in general components of the world, come to original and then derived givenness. The life-world is at its core a *perceptual world*, and perception — in its full concrete richness, with all its horizons, its embodied character, its intersubjective implications — is the ultimate ground on which all further constitution rests.
