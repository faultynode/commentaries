# Leibniz — *The Monadology* (1714): A Paragraph-by-Paragraph Commentary

---

## Notice on the Monadology (Historical and Analytical) — by Émile Boutroux

### Paragraph 1

Boutroux opens by situating the *Monadology* as a summary of the entirety of Leibniz's philosophy. It was composed in French in 1714, during Leibniz's final stay in Vienna, and was written for Prince Eugene of Savoy — a figure distinguished not only as a military and political leader but as a man of considerable scientific and intellectual culture. Prince Eugene treated the manuscript as a precious treasure: he locked it in a casket and permitted only select visitors even to see it. Boutroux quotes a letter from the Count of Bonneval to Leibniz, comparing Eugene's treatment of the manuscript to the way Neapolitan priests guard the blood of Saint Januarius — letting Bonneval kiss it before locking it away again. This anecdote establishes the extraordinary esteem in which the text was held even before its publication.

### Paragraph 2

The *Monadology* was not published during Leibniz's lifetime. It was first translated into German by Köhler and into Latin by Hansche of Leipzig. The Latin translation appeared in the *Acta eruditorum* of 1721 under the title *Principia philosophiæ seu theses in gratiam principis Eugenii conscriptæ* ("Principles of philosophy, or theses composed for the benefit of Prince Eugene"). This Latin version was included in the Dutens edition of Leibniz's works. The original French text was published for the first time in 1840 by Erdmann, in his edition of Leibniz's philosophical works.

### Paragraph 3

Boutroux emphasizes that the work, as the Latin title indicates, is a series of theses summarizing the principal points of Leibniz's philosophy. Crucially, it cannot serve as an introduction to that philosophy. On the contrary, it presupposes a reader already well versed in Leibniz's thought. For such a reader, however, it teaches the supreme vantage point from which the whole system can be seen in its true light and in its harmony.

### Paragraph 4

Boutroux acknowledges that summarizing what is already a summary is an illegitimate enterprise. He will therefore limit himself to indicating the framework and major divisions of the work, and to making explicit the systematic disposition that is more or less concealed by the numbered-thesis format.

### Paragraph 5

The world, Boutroux explains, is at once a diversity and a harmony. To see it as it truly is, one must simultaneously discern its details and grasp its unity. To achieve this double knowledge, one must succeed in placing oneself at the supreme point of view — as close as possible to the point of view of God himself. The *Monadology* determines this point of view and gives us a sketch of the world as it appears to the observer so situated.

### Paragraph 6

Three parts can be distinguished: (1) the Monads or elements of things (§§1–36); (2) God (§§37–48); (3) the world conceived in its cause, which is God (§§49–90). The movement is first ascending, going from creatures to God, and then descending, going from God to creatures. Equivalently, Leibniz's philosophy is first regressive and then progressive.

### Paragraph 7 — I. MONADS

Boutroux divides the doctrine of monads into two parts: (1) their nature (§§1–17), and (2) their degrees of perfection (§§18–36).

Regarding their nature, the monads can be determined first from an external point of view and then from an internal one.

Externally, the monad is simple, unextended, without figure, indivisible, incapable of beginning or ending naturally, and incapable of being modified in its interior by any other creature (§§1–7).

Internally, the monad is endowed with perception — the representation of a multiplicity within a unity — and with appetition — the tendency to pass from less distinct perceptions to more distinct ones. It is thus like an incorporeal automaton (§§8–17).

### Paragraph 8 — Degrees of Perfection

Monads admit of degrees of perfection, determined by the more or less distinct character of their perceptions. The principal degrees are:

(1) The monad or pure and simple entelechy, possessing perception and appetite in the general sense, but without memory. Such is the life of plants (§§18–24).

(2) The monad endowed with memory, or soul, as it exists in animals. These beings are capable of empirical consecutations that imitate reason, but they cannot rise to reason itself (§§25–28).

(3) The monad endowed with reason, or knowledge of eternal truths, and consequently endowed with apperception or consciousness. Such a monad is called a spirit (*esprit*), and is given to us in human beings (§§29–30). Our reasonings are founded on two great principles — contradiction and sufficient reason — which apply concurrently to the two sorts of truths to which all possible truths can be reduced: truths of reasoning and truths of fact (§§31–36).

### Paragraph 9 — II. GOD

The doctrine concerning God divides into two parts: (1) his existence (§§37–45), and (2) his nature (§§46–48).

Regarding existence, the demonstration proceeds from the theory of the two principles of reason and the two sorts of truths. This demonstration is double: a posteriori and a priori.

A posteriori: a God is necessary as the sufficient reason of the contingent, which does not carry within itself its ultimate explanation (§§37–42).

A priori: (a) a God is necessary as the source of essences or possibilities, i.e. of eternal truths, insofar as there is a reality in them (§§43–44); (b) God exists necessarily if he is possible, that is, if the definition of God implies no contradiction (§45).

### Paragraph 10 — God's Nature

Insofar as God is the source of eternal truths, he does not act by his will (as the Cartesians believed) but by his understanding alone. Insofar as he is the source of contingent truths, on the contrary, God acts by his will, which is regulated — according to a moral, not geometrical necessity — by the principle of fitness or the choice of the best (§46).

Created monads are born by continuous fulgurations of the Divinity (§§46–47).

In God are power, knowledge, and will, corresponding to the three elements of the monad: the subject or base, perception, and appetition (§48).

### Paragraph 11 — III. THE WORLD CONCEIVED IN ITS CAUSE

The progressive doctrine of the world divides into: (1) the nature of the world in general, i.e. universal harmony and optimism (§§49–60); (2) the constitution and hierarchy of created beings (§§61–90).

A creature is said to act when it has distinct perceptions, and to be acted upon when it has confused perceptions corresponding to the distinct ones in another. The influence of things upon one another is, in simple substances, entirely ideal — it consists in a divine preordination. God has from the beginning regulated all monads without exception, so that to each distinct perception of one there correspond, in all the others, confused perceptions, and reciprocally. Each monad is thus representative of the entire universe from its own point of view (§§49–52).

There is an infinity of possible universes, and only one can exist. God's choice was determined, according to the principle of the best, by comparing the perfections enveloped in the various possibles. God infallibly chose the best possible world. The excellence of this world consists in the greatest possible variety combined with the greatest order (§§56–60).

### Paragraph 12 — The Hierarchy of Beings

Every created being necessarily has: a composite body, and a central monad or entelechy.

Composites are analogous to simples: they imitate the entirely ideal reciprocal influence of monads by means of mechanical influence, so that every change in one body is accompanied by an equivalent contrary change in all other bodies. Every body thus resonates with everything that happens in the universe. The entelechy or soul represents the whole universe by representing more distinctly the body to which it is particularly affected. There is therefore no soul without a body in nature (§§61–62).

Considered in terms of their degree of perfection, created beings form three superimposed worlds: the world of living things, the world of animals, and the world of spirits.

Simple living things already have organic bodies — bodies in which, pushing division to infinity, one always encounters variety and fitness. Everything is full of living things; there is nothing dead in nature.

In animals, birth and death are merely development and envelopment. The union of soul and body consists in each following its own laws — the soul the law of final causes, the body those of efficient causes — and in their meeting by virtue of the pre-established harmony between the realm of efficient causes and the realm of final causes. This is the system of pre-established harmony, a natural consequence of the very principles of mechanics, exactly determined (§§63–81).

Above animals are spirits, mirrors not merely of the universe but of God himself, and capable of entering into a kind of society with him. The assembly of all spirits forms the City of God — a moral world in which good and evil receive their just recompense and punishment, and in which the good love God with that true and pure love that consists in taking pleasure in the felicity of the one loved. This moral world or kingdom of grace is in harmony with the natural world or kingdom of nature, God as architect satisfying God as legislator in all things: so that things lead to grace by the very ways of nature (§§84–90).

## Leibniz — The Monadology (1714)

*Principia Philosophiæ seu Theses in Gratiam Principis Eugenii Conscriptæ*

### §1

Leibniz opens with a definition: the Monad is nothing other than a simple substance that enters into composites. "Simple" means without parts. The reference to the *Theodicy* §10 anchors the concept in his broader systematic work. The term "monad" (from the Greek *monas*, unity) is thus introduced as a technical term for the ultimate metaphysical unit. Simplicity is the defining characteristic: unlike extended material bodies, which can always be divided further, a monad has no parts at all. It is not a spatial atom — it has no spatial characteristics whatsoever — but a metaphysical simple. This immediately places Leibniz's ontology at a remove from both Cartesian extended substance and from classical atomism: the ultimate constituents of reality are not tiny bits of matter but non-spatial, non-material simples.

### §2

The existence of simple substances is inferred from the existence of composites. If there are composites (and experience shows that there are), then there must be simples, because a composite is nothing other than an aggregate (*aggregatum*) of simples. This is a key argumentative move: the composite presupposes the simple as its ground. Without genuine simples — genuine unities — an aggregate would be an aggregate of aggregates to infinity, and would never have any real being at all. This argument echoes throughout Leibniz's correspondence, particularly his letters to Arnauld, where he insists that without true unities there would be no true beings. The composite has only a borrowed or derivative unity; real unity belongs to the simple substance alone.

### §3

From the simplicity of the monad — its lack of parts — Leibniz draws three immediate consequences: it has no extension, no figure (shape), and no possible divisibility. These three properties all presuppose parts: extension is the spread of parts in space, figure is the arrangement of parts, and divisibility is the capacity to be separated into parts. Since the monad has no parts, none of these can belong to it. Leibniz then makes a striking claim: these monads are "the true Atoms of Nature" and "the Elements of things." The word "atom" is used deliberately to invoke and transform the atomist tradition: unlike the material atoms of Democritus and Epicurus, which are merely very small extended bodies, Leibniz's monads are genuinely indivisible because they are genuinely simple — not extended at all. They are metaphysical atoms, not physical ones.

### §4

No dissolution is to be feared for the monad, and there is no conceivable way in which a simple substance could perish naturally. Since natural destruction involves the dissolution of a thing into its parts, and since the monad has no parts, natural destruction is impossible for it. The monad is thus naturally imperishable. This does not mean it exists necessarily in the absolute sense — only God exists with that kind of necessity — but that, once created, it cannot be destroyed by any natural process. Only the Creator who brought it into being could annihilate it.

### §5

By the same reasoning, no simple substance can begin naturally, since it could not be formed by composition. Natural generation involves the coming together of parts into a whole — but a simple substance, having no parts, cannot arise in this way. This is the converse of §4: if natural destruction is impossible, so is natural generation. The monad is not the product of any natural process of assembly or combination.

### §6

The conclusion follows: monads can neither begin nor end except all at once (*tout d'un coup*). They can begin only by creation and end only by annihilation. What is composite, by contrast, begins and ends by parts — through gradual assembly and disassembly. This paragraph draws a sharp metaphysical line between the simple and the composite. The monad enters and exits being by a single divine act — creation or annihilation — without any intervening process. This contrasts with the gradualism that characterizes the physical world of composites, where things come to be and pass away through the rearrangement of parts.

### §7

There is no way to explain how a monad could be altered or changed in its interior by any other creature. Nothing can be transposed within it, and no internal movement can be excited, directed, increased, or diminished from without — as is possible in composites, where changes occur among the parts. Leibniz's famous dictum appears here: "Monads have no windows through which anything could enter or leave." Accidents cannot detach themselves and wander outside substances, as the sensible species of the Scholastics once did. Neither substance nor accident can enter a monad from outside.

This is one of the most consequential theses of the *Monadology*. The monad is absolutely self-enclosed with respect to external causal influence. No other created thing can reach into its interior and produce a change there. All of the monad's changes must come from within. This rules out any form of real causal interaction between finite substances — a position Leibniz will later reconcile with the appearance of interaction through the doctrine of pre-established harmony. The polemical reference to the Scholastic theory of sensible species — according to which objects emit tiny likenesses of themselves that enter the soul through the senses — shows what kind of theory Leibniz is rejecting: any theory that has something literally passing from one substance into another.

### §8

Yet monads must have some qualities; otherwise they would not even be beings. If simple substances did not differ by their qualities, there would be no way to perceive any change in things, since what is in the composite can only come from its simple ingredients. If monads were without qualities, they would be indistinguishable from one another — since they also do not differ in quantity — and consequently, assuming the plenum, each place would always receive in motion only the equivalent of what it had before, and one state of things would be indiscernible from another.

This paragraph introduces the principle of the identity of indiscernibles, at least in embryonic form. If monads had no qualitative differences, they would be perfectly alike, and then no real change could ever be registered in the universe. The argument also invokes the Leibnizian plenum: if matter fills all of space (there is no void), and if the basic units were qualitatively identical, then any rearrangement would produce an indiscernible state — nothing would really happen. Therefore, monads must differ qualitatively. The qualities in question are not physical or sensible qualities but internal, metaphysical determinations — the perceptions and appetitions that Leibniz will introduce shortly.

### §9

Each monad must be different from every other. For there are never in nature two beings that are perfectly alike and in which it would not be possible to find an internal difference, or one founded on an intrinsic denomination. This is the explicit statement of the *principium identitatis indiscernibilium* — the principle of the identity of indiscernibles. If two things were indiscernible in all their intrinsic properties, they would not be two things but one and the same thing. Every monad is an individual, qualitatively unique, distinguishable from every other monad by its own internal nature. Leibniz insists on "intrinsic denomination" — not a difference of mere position or external relation, but a difference grounded in the thing's own nature.

### §10

Leibniz takes it as given that every created being is subject to change, and consequently the created monad too — and that this change is continual in each one. This introduces temporality and dynamism into the monad. The monad is not a static unit but a perpetually changing one. Its states succeed one another continuously. This continual change is the inner life of the monad — the flow of its perceptions, as Leibniz will make explicit in the following paragraphs.

### §11

It follows from what has been said that the natural changes of monads come from an internal principle, since an external cause could not influence their interior. This is the direct consequence of the "windowlessness" of §7 combined with the fact of change established in §10. If the monad changes, and if nothing can enter from outside, then the source of change must be internal. Every monad carries within itself the principle of its own alteration. This internal principle will soon be identified with appetition.

### §12

But besides the principle of change, there must also be a detail (*détail*) of what changes, which constitutes, so to speak, the specification and variety of simple substances. The monad is not merely something that changes; it must have a differentiated internal content — a "detail" that specifies and varies what is happening within it. If the monad were a bare principle of change with no articulated content, there would be nothing for it to change between. The use of the word *détail* is significant: it suggests an internal richness, a fine-grained texture of states, within what is nonetheless a simple (partless) substance.

### §13

This detail must envelop a multiplicity within the unity or within the simple. For since all natural change occurs by degrees, something changes and something remains. Consequently, there must be in the simple substance a plurality of affections and relations, even though it has no parts. This is a crucial and somewhat paradoxical thesis. The monad is simple — without parts — and yet it contains a multiplicity internally. The multiplicity is not a multiplicity of parts (that would make it composite) but a multiplicity of *affections and relations*. The monad is a unity that envelops plurality — a mirror of the many in the one. The principle that natural change is gradual (continuous) is also invoked: at any given moment, some aspects of the monad's state are changing while others persist, which requires an internal differentiation of states.

### §14

The passing state that envelops and represents a multiplicity in the unity or in the simple substance is nothing other than what is called *Perception*. Perception must be distinguished from apperception, or consciousness, as will appear in what follows. The Cartesians went badly wrong here, having counted for nothing the perceptions of which one is not aware. This also led them to believe that only Spirits were monads and that there were no souls of animals or other entelechies. They confused, along with the common people, a prolonged stupor with death in the strict sense, which in turn led them into the Scholastic prejudice of entirely separated souls and even confirmed ill-disposed minds in the opinion that souls are mortal.

This paragraph is pivotal. Perception is defined in a highly general, technical sense: it is the transitory internal state of the monad that represents a multiplicity within a unity. This is not perception in the ordinary psychological sense — it does not require consciousness or awareness. Leibniz's most important critical move here is the distinction between perception and apperception (conscious awareness). The Cartesians, by identifying all mental life with conscious thought (*cogitatio*), were unable to account for unconscious perceptions — what Leibniz elsewhere calls "petites perceptions." This led to a cascade of errors: denying souls to animals, confusing unconsciousness with death, and entertaining the notion of disembodied souls. Leibniz's broader concept of perception allows him to attribute a form of inner life to all monads, not just to conscious minds.

### §15

The action of the internal principle that brings about the change or passage from one perception to another may be called *Appetition*. It is true that appetite cannot always attain entirely the whole perception toward which it tends, but it always obtains something and arrives at new perceptions. Appetition is the dynamic principle, the striving or tendency that drives the monad from one perceptual state to the next. It is the internal spring of the monad's life. Leibniz is careful to note that appetition is not always fully realized — the monad does not always achieve the perception it tends toward in its entirety — but it always achieves something, always arrives at some new state. This introduces a kind of teleology into the monad's inner life: each state tends toward a subsequent state, and this tendency is the motor of change. Appetition and perception together constitute the complete inner life of the monad: perception is the representative aspect (what the monad represents at a given moment), and appetition is the dynamic aspect (the drive toward the next representation).

### §16

We ourselves experience a multiplicity in the simple substance when we find that the slightest thought of which we are aware envelops a variety in its object. All those who recognize that the soul is a simple substance must therefore recognize this multiplicity in the monad. Monsieur Bayle should not have found difficulty in this, as he did in his *Dictionary*, article "Rorarius." Leibniz here appeals to introspective evidence: even the most minimal conscious thought involves a manifold content — a variety of aspects, qualities, or relations in its object. If the soul is a simple substance (as most philosophers concede), then this internal multiplicity-within-unity is a datum of experience. The reference to Bayle's objection (in the *Dictionnaire historique et critique*) is significant: Bayle had raised difficulties about how a simple substance could contain such multiplicity, and Leibniz is responding that the difficulty is misconceived — we experience it directly in every act of thought.

### §17

One is moreover obliged to confess that Perception and what depends on it is inexplicable by mechanical reasons, that is, by figures and movements. If one imagines a Machine whose structure makes it think, feel, and have perception, one could conceive it enlarged while preserving the same proportions, so that one could enter it as one enters a mill. On inspecting its interior, one would find only parts pushing one another, and never anything to explain a perception. It is therefore in the simple substance, and not in the composite or in the machine, that perception must be sought. Moreover, nothing else can be found in the simple substance — that is, perceptions and their changes. It is in this alone that all the internal actions of simple substances can consist.

This is the famous "mill argument," one of the most celebrated passages in the history of philosophy of mind. Leibniz argues that no mechanical explanation — no arrangement of parts in motion — could ever account for perception. Even if we imagine the machine enlarged to the scale of a mill and walk through its interior, we will find only mechanical parts pushing one another — never perception itself. Perception is therefore not a mechanical phenomenon and cannot emerge from mechanical arrangements. It belongs essentially to the simple substance. This argument anticipates later debates about the "hard problem" of consciousness and the explanatory gap between physical processes and subjective experience. Note that Leibniz draws a positive conclusion as well: since only perceptions and their changes can be found in the simple substance, these constitute the entire inner life of the monad. The monad is, at bottom, nothing but a perceiving and striving unity.

### §18

One could give the name *Entelechies* to all simple substances or created monads, for they have in themselves a certain perfection (*echousi to enteles*); there is a self-sufficiency (*autarkeia*) that makes them sources of their own internal actions and, so to speak, incorporeal automata. The Aristotelian term "entelechy" — meaning that which has its end or perfection within itself — is appropriated by Leibniz for his own purposes. Each monad is an entelechy because it carries its own principle of activity and perfection internally. The term *autarkeia* (self-sufficiency) underscores the monad's independence from external causation: it is the source of all its own changes. The striking phrase "incorporeal automata" captures the paradox of the monad: it is like a machine (an automaton) in that it operates according to an internal program, unfolding its states from within — yet it is incorporeal, having no extension, figure, or parts. It is a self-running spiritual mechanism.

### §19

If we wish to call *Soul* everything that has perceptions and appetites in the general sense just explained, then all simple substances or created monads could be called souls. But since feeling (*sentiment*) is something more than a simple perception, Leibniz consents that the general name of monads and entelechies suffice for simple substances that have only that, and that the name *Souls* be reserved for those whose perception is more distinct and accompanied by memory. This paragraph establishes a terminological hierarchy. In the broadest sense, every monad has a kind of "soul" because it perceives and strives. But Leibniz reserves the term "soul" (*âme*) for monads that have risen to a higher level — those with distinct perceptions and memory. The lower monads are called simply monads or entelechies. This terminological precision reflects a real ontological distinction: there are degrees of perfection among monads, and the names track these degrees.

### §20

For we ourselves experience a state in which we remember nothing and have no distinguished perception — as when we fall into a faint or are overwhelmed by a deep, dreamless sleep. In this state the soul does not differ perceptibly from a simple monad. But since this state is not lasting and the soul emerges from it, the soul is something more. Leibniz appeals to the experience of dreamless sleep or fainting as evidence for the existence of states of perception below the threshold of consciousness. In such states, we have no distinct or memorable perceptions, and our condition approximates that of a bare monad. But the fact that we recover from these states — that our soul reasserts its higher capacities — shows that the soul is more than a bare monad, even when it temporarily sinks to that level.

### §21

It does not follow that in such a state the simple substance is entirely without perception. This is impossible, for the reasons given above: the monad cannot perish, nor can it subsist without some affection, which is nothing other than its perception. But when there is a great multitude of petty perceptions with nothing distinguished among them, one is stunned — as when one turns continuously in the same direction several times in succession, producing a vertigo that can make one faint, leaving nothing distinguishable. Death can give this state to animals for a time.

This paragraph introduces the doctrine of *petites perceptions* — the tiny, unconscious perceptions that are always present even when no distinct perception reaches consciousness. The monad can never be entirely without perception, because perception is its very nature (as established in §14). When we are unconscious, we are not without perceptions but rather overwhelmed by a confused mass of tiny perceptions, none of which stands out. Leibniz's analogy of vertigo — the dizzying confusion produced by spinning — illustrates how a multitude of perceptions can exist without any single one being distinguishable. This doctrine is philosophically momentous: it means that the inner life of the monad is continuous and never interrupted, even in apparent unconsciousness or death. Death in animals is not an annihilation but a temporary reduction to the level of confused petites perceptions.

### §22

And since every present state of a simple substance is naturally a consequence of its preceding state, the present is pregnant with the future (*le présent y est gros de l'avenir*). This is a statement of thoroughgoing internal determinism within the monad. Each state follows naturally from the one before it. There is no discontinuity, no gap, no random leap. The monad's history is a continuous, internally determined series. The vivid metaphor — "the present is pregnant with the future" — expresses the idea that the future states are already contained in germ in the present, just as the mature organism is already implicit in the embryo. This connects to Leibniz's broader metaphysical commitment to continuity and to the idea that a sufficiently powerful intelligence could read the entire past and future of a monad from its present state.

### §23

Therefore, since upon waking from stupor one becomes aware of one's perceptions, one must have had perceptions immediately beforehand, even though one was not aware of them. For a perception can naturally come only from another perception, just as a motion can naturally come only from a motion. This paragraph draws the consequence of §22 for the doctrine of petites perceptions. The continuity of the monad's perceptual life means that the perceptions we become aware of upon waking cannot have sprung from nothing — they must have been preceded by unconscious perceptions. The analogy with motion reinforces the principle: just as a body in motion must have been set in motion by a prior motion (nothing moves from a state of absolute rest spontaneously), so a perception must arise from a prior perception. The monad's perceptual life is an unbroken chain, even when large stretches of it fall below the threshold of awareness.

### §24

One sees from this that if we had nothing distinguished and, so to speak, elevated and of a higher flavor in our perceptions, we would always be in a state of stupor. This is the condition of entirely bare monads (*Monades toutes nues*). If every perception were equally indistinct — if no perception stood out from the mass — we would be permanently in the stunned, unconscious condition described in §21. This, Leibniz says, is the perpetual state of "bare monads" — the lowest grade of monads, those without memory or distinctness. They perceive, but all their perceptions are equally confused and undifferentiated. They exist in a kind of perpetual twilight of awareness, never rising to distinctness or consciousness.

### §25

Nature has given heightened perceptions to animals by providing them with organs that gather together many rays of light or many undulations of air, so as to make them more efficacious by their union. There is something similar in smell, taste, and touch, and perhaps in many other senses unknown to us. Leibniz will later explain how what happens in the soul represents what takes place in the organs.

Here Leibniz introduces the role of bodily organs in elevating perception from the confused baseline of bare monads to the distinct level enjoyed by animals. The organs are instruments of amplification and concentration: they gather many small stimuli (rays of light, air waves) and unite them into a single, more powerful impression. This is how nature lifts certain monads above the stupor of bare perception. Note that Leibniz does not say the organs *cause* the perceptions (that would violate the windowlessness of §7); rather, they are the corporeal counterpart of the soul's heightened perceptions, coordinated with them by pre-established harmony. He also leaves open the possibility of senses beyond the five we know.

### §26

Memory furnishes to souls a kind of consecutation (*consécution*) that imitates reason but must be distinguished from it. Animals, having perceived something that strikes them and of which they have had a similar perception before, expect — through the representation of their memory — what was previously joined with that perception, and are moved to similar feelings as they had then. For example, when one shows a stick to dogs, they remember the pain it caused them, and they cry out and flee.

Memory introduces a new power into the monad: the ability to connect present perceptions with past ones and to anticipate the future on the basis of past experience. This is not reason — it is empirical association, a kind of habit-based expectation. The dog does not reason that the stick will cause pain; it simply associates stick with pain through the mechanism of memory. Leibniz's example of the dog and the stick is vivid and precise: the animal's response is driven by remembered experience, not by rational insight into causes. This consecutation "imitates" reason because it often leads to the same practical results, but it operates on entirely different principles — association rather than analysis, habit rather than understanding.

### §27

The strong imagination that strikes and moves animals comes either from the magnitude or the multitude of preceding perceptions. For often a single strong impression produces all at once the effect of a long habit or of many repeated mediocre perceptions. This paragraph supplements the account of memory by noting that the strength of an imaginative association depends on two factors: the intensity of the original perception and the frequency of repetition. A single overwhelming experience can produce the same associative bond as many weaker ones. This is a proto-psychological observation about the mechanisms of learning and conditioning — the formation of strong associative connections in the animal soul.

### §28

Human beings act like animals insofar as the consecutations of their perceptions are governed only by the principle of memory — resembling empirical physicians who have mere practice without theory. We are nothing but empiricists in three quarters of our actions. For example, when we expect that day will come tomorrow, we act as empiricists, because it has always happened thus far. Only the astronomer judges this by reason.

This paragraph is a striking act of philosophical humility: most of human life is governed not by reason but by the same empirical association that governs animal behavior. We are "three quarters" empiricists. Our expectation that the sun will rise tomorrow is not (for most of us) a conclusion from astronomical theory but a habit of association — the same mechanism as the dog flinching from the stick. Only the person who understands the astronomical reasons for the regularity of day and night acts from reason rather than from habit. This draws a clear line between rational knowledge (grounded in the understanding of necessary connections) and empirical expectation (grounded in the memory of past regularities).

### §29

But it is the knowledge of necessary and eternal truths that distinguishes us from mere animals and gives us Reason and the sciences, elevating us to knowledge of ourselves and of God. This is what is called in us the rational Soul, or Spirit (*Esprit*). The decisive distinction between human beings and animals is access to necessary and eternal truths — truths of mathematics, logic, and metaphysics that hold in all possible worlds, not just in the actual one. This access constitutes Reason in the strict sense and is the basis of the sciences. It also makes possible self-knowledge (reflexive awareness of oneself as a thinking subject) and knowledge of God. The monad that possesses this capacity is no longer merely a soul but a *spirit* — a rational soul. This is the highest grade of monad in the natural order.

### §30

It is also through the knowledge of necessary truths and through their abstractions that we are elevated to reflexive acts, which make us think of what is called *I* (*moi*) and to consider that this or that is in us. And thus, in thinking of ourselves, we think of Being, Substance, the simple and the composite, the immaterial, and God himself — conceiving that what is limited in us is without limits in him. These reflexive acts furnish the principal objects of our reasonings.

Self-consciousness — the ability to think of the "I" — arises through the same rational capacity that gives us access to eternal truths. By reflecting on ourselves, we discover the fundamental metaphysical concepts: being, substance, simplicity, composition, immateriality, and God. Each of these concepts has a reflexive origin: we find in ourselves instances of being, substance, etc., and then extend them beyond ourselves — recognizing that what is limited and finite in us is unlimited and infinite in God. This is a kind of Leibnizian version of the Augustinian *interior intimo meo* — the discovery of the highest truths through inward reflection. The reflexive acts thus generated are the objects and the instruments of our highest reasonings.

### §31

Our reasonings are founded on two great principles: that of *Contradiction*, by virtue of which we judge as false what envelops a contradiction, and as true what is opposed or contradictory to the false. The principle of contradiction (or non-contradiction) is the first and most fundamental principle of reasoning. It governs the domain of necessary truths: a proposition is necessarily true if its negation involves a contradiction, and necessarily false if it itself involves one. This is the principle that underlies all of logic and mathematics. Leibniz's formulation is bilateral: it tells us both how to identify falsehood (by detecting contradiction) and how to identify truth (as the contradictory of the false).

### §32

And the principle of *Sufficient Reason*, by virtue of which we consider that no fact can be found to be true, or existing, and no proposition can be true, without there being a sufficient reason why it is so and not otherwise — although most often these reasons cannot be known to us. The principle of sufficient reason is Leibniz's second great principle. It states that nothing exists or is true without a reason for its being so rather than otherwise. This principle is broader than the principle of contradiction: it applies not only to necessary truths but to contingent ones — truths of fact that could have been otherwise but are in fact the case for a reason. Leibniz's important caveat is that these reasons are most often unknown to us. The principle is an ontological and epistemological regulative ideal: we may not always be able to discover the reason, but there always is one. This principle will shortly be used to establish the existence of God.

### §33

There are also two sorts of truths: those of *Reasoning* and those of *Fact*. Truths of reasoning are necessary and their opposite is impossible. Truths of fact are contingent and their opposite is possible. When a truth is necessary, its reason can be found by analysis, resolving it into simpler ideas and truths until one arrives at primitives.

This paragraph pairs the two principles with two kinds of truths. Truths of reasoning (logical and mathematical truths) are necessary — they hold in all possible worlds, and their denial is self-contradictory. Truths of fact (empirical and contingent truths) are not necessary — their denial is logically possible, even if it does not actually obtain. Necessary truths can be demonstrated by analysis — by breaking them down into their constituent concepts and tracing them back to primitive, self-evident truths. Contingent truths, as Leibniz will explain, require a different kind of grounding: they cannot be resolved into identities by finite analysis but require an infinite one, which only God can perform.

### §34

Thus among mathematicians, speculative theorems and practical canons are reduced by analysis to Definitions, Axioms, and Postulates. Leibniz illustrates the analytical method with reference to mathematics, where all theorems are ultimately reducible to definitions (which fix the meaning of terms), axioms (self-evident truths), and postulates (basic assumptions of construction). This is the model of rational knowledge: a deductive system grounded in primitives that are grasped immediately by the intellect. The implication is that all truths of reasoning have this structure, even if we do not always carry the analysis to completion.

### §35

And there are finally simple ideas of which no definition can be given; there are also Axioms and Postulates, or in a word, primitive principles, which cannot be proved and have no need of proof. These are identical propositions, whose opposite contains an express contradiction. At the bottom of the chain of analysis one arrives at primitives — simple ideas that cannot be decomposed further and primitive propositions (axioms) that are self-evident. These primitive propositions are "identical" — that is, their truth is immediately apparent from the meaning of their terms, and their denial is an outright contradiction. They require no proof because they are the ground of all proof. This is Leibniz's foundationalism for the domain of necessary truths: the entire edifice rests on primitives that are transparent to reason.

### §36

But sufficient reason must also be found in contingent truths, or truths of fact — that is, in the sequence of things spread throughout the universe of creatures. Here the resolution into particular reasons could go to infinite detail, on account of the immense variety of things in nature and the infinite division of bodies. There is an infinity of past and present figures and movements entering into the efficient cause of my present writing, and an infinity of small present and past inclinations and dispositions of my soul entering into its final cause.

This paragraph marks the transition from the domain of necessary truths to the domain of contingent truths and from the world of monads to the question of God. Contingent truths also require sufficient reasons, but these reasons are vastly more complex. The chain of reasons for any contingent fact — say, Leibniz's writing at this moment — extends infinitely: an infinity of physical states (figures and movements) compose the efficient cause, and an infinity of psychological states (inclinations and dispositions) compose the final cause. No finite analysis can exhaust these reasons. The resolution goes on forever, deeper and deeper into the infinite detail of nature. This sets up the need for a reason outside the entire series — which is God.

### §37

And since all this detail envelops only other prior or more detailed contingents, each of which in turn needs a similar analysis to account for it, one is no further advanced. The sufficient or ultimate reason must be outside the sequence or series of this detail of contingents, however infinite it may be.

The chain of contingent reasons, however far one follows it, never reaches a self-explanatory ground. Each contingent fact is explained by prior contingent facts, which themselves need explanation, and so on to infinity. But an infinite series of contingent explanations does not explain itself — it merely pushes the question back indefinitely. Therefore, the ultimate sufficient reason must lie outside the entire series. This is a version of the cosmological argument: the totality of contingent beings requires a ground that is not itself contingent. The argument depends on the principle of sufficient reason: if everything has a reason, then the whole series must have a reason, and that reason cannot be found within the series itself.

### §38

And this is how the ultimate reason of things must be in a necessary substance, in which the detail of changes is contained only eminently, as in its source. This is what we call God.

The ultimate sufficient reason is identified with a necessary substance — one that exists not contingently but necessarily, carrying the reason for its own existence within itself. This necessary substance contains the detail of all changes "eminently" — that is, it contains the ground of all contingent reality within itself, not as a sum of details but as a unified, all-encompassing source. This necessary substance is God. The argument moves from the insufficiency of contingent explanations to the necessity of a self-grounding, necessary being. God is introduced not as a hypothesis but as a requirement of the principle of sufficient reason applied to the totality of contingent existence.

### §39

Since this substance is a sufficient reason for all the detail of the world, and since everything in the world is interconnected, there is only one God, and this God suffices. The uniqueness of God follows from his being the sufficient reason of everything: if everything is interconnected and one substance suffices to ground the whole, there is no need for — and no room for — a second. The interconnectedness of all things in the universe (which Leibniz has already established through the doctrine that each monad represents all the others) means that the ground of any part is the ground of the whole. One God is therefore both necessary and sufficient.

### §40

One may also judge that this supreme substance, being unique, universal, and necessary, and having nothing outside itself that is independent of it — being a simple consequence of possible being — must be incapable of limits and must contain as much reality as is possible.

God's nature follows from his metaphysical status. As the unique, universal, and necessary being, there is nothing external to or independent of God. He is the simple consequence (*suite simple*) of possible being — that is, his existence follows directly from the very nature of possibility. Since nothing limits him from without, he must be unlimited — he must contain the maximum of reality. This is a reformulation of the classical theological thesis of divine infinity: God is the being in which all perfections are realized without limitation.

### §41

From this it follows that God is absolutely perfect, perfection being nothing other than the magnitude of positive reality, taken precisely, setting aside the limits or bounds in things that have them. Where there are no bounds — that is, in God — perfection is absolutely infinite.

Perfection is defined quantitatively as the magnitude of positive reality. Imperfection consists in limitation — in the finitude of a being's reality. Since God has no limits (as established in §40), his perfection is absolutely infinite. This definition of perfection as positive reality without negation is important for Leibniz's theodicy: evil and imperfection are privations, not positive realities. They result from the essential finitude of created beings, not from any deficiency in God.

### §42

It also follows that creatures have their perfections from the influence of God, but that they have their imperfections from their own nature, which is incapable of being without limits. For it is in this that they are distinguished from God. This original imperfection of creatures is noticeable in the natural inertia of bodies.

The asymmetry of perfection and imperfection in creatures is explained: whatever is positive and real in a creature comes from God; whatever is negative and limiting comes from the creature's own finite nature. This is a version of the Neoplatonic principle that all being and goodness flow from the One (or God), while privation and limitation belong to the creature as such. The reference to "natural inertia" is telling: inertia — the tendency of matter to resist change — is Leibniz's physical correlate of metaphysical finitude. It is the bodily expression of the creature's original limitation.

### §43

It is also true that in God is not only the source of existences but also that of essences, insofar as they are real — that is, of what is real in possibility. This is because the understanding of God is the region of eternal truths, or of the ideas on which they depend, and without him there would be nothing real in the possibles — not only nothing existing but also nothing possible.

God is the ground not only of actual existence but of possibility itself. The eternal truths — the truths of logic, mathematics, and metaphysics — subsist in the divine understanding. Without God's understanding, there would be no reality even in possibilities. This is a strong metaphysical claim: it is not just that God creates existing things, but that the very framework of possibility — the space of what could be — depends on God. The eternal truths are not free-floating abstract objects but are grounded in the divine intellect. This positions Leibniz against both the Platonic view (eternal truths as independent of any mind) and the Cartesian voluntarist view (eternal truths as products of God's will, which Leibniz addresses in §46).

### §44

For it must be the case that if there is a reality in essences or possibilities — or indeed in the eternal truths — this reality is founded in something existing and actual; and consequently in the existence of the Necessary Being, in which essence includes existence, or in which being possible suffices for being actual.

This is the a priori argument for God's existence, closely related to the ontological argument. The reality of eternal truths requires a ground in something actual — and that ground is the Necessary Being, in whom essence and existence are identical, or in whom being possible is sufficient for being actual. In all other beings, being possible does not entail being actual — a possible horse is not yet an actual horse. But in the Necessary Being, the very possibility of its existence entails its actuality. This is because the Necessary Being's essence just is existence: to be the Necessary Being is to exist.

### §45

Thus God alone (or the Necessary Being) has the privilege that he must exist if he is possible. And since nothing can prevent the possibility of what encloses no limits, no negation, and consequently no contradiction, this alone suffices to know the existence of God a priori. Leibniz has also proved it a posteriori from the existence of contingent beings, which can have their ultimate or sufficient reason only in the Necessary Being, who has the reason of his existence in himself.

Leibniz brings together the two lines of argument — a priori and a posteriori — for God's existence. The a priori argument: God must exist if he is possible, and since his concept involves no negation or limitation (and hence no contradiction), he is possible, and therefore he exists. The a posteriori argument: contingent beings exist, and their sufficient reason must lie in a necessary being. Both arguments converge on the same conclusion: God necessarily exists. This paragraph serves as the summary and culmination of the section on God's existence.

### §46

However, one must not imagine with some that eternal truths, being dependent on God, are arbitrary and depend on his will, as Descartes appears to have held and afterwards M. Poiret. This is true only of contingent truths, whose principle is fitness or the choice of the best. Necessary truths, by contrast, depend solely on God's understanding and are its internal object.

Leibniz here takes direct aim at the Cartesian doctrine of the creation of eternal truths. For Descartes, God freely wills even the truths of mathematics and logic — he could have made 2+2=5 if he had wished. Leibniz rejects this as conflating two domains: necessary truths depend on God's understanding (intellect), not on his will. They are the internal objects of the divine intellect and could not have been otherwise. Only contingent truths — truths about which actual world God creates — depend on God's will, which is guided by the principle of the best. This distinction between the roles of intellect and will in God is fundamental to Leibniz's rationalism: the structure of possibility is fixed by reason, not by arbitrary decree.

### §47

Thus God alone is the primitive unity, or the original simple substance, of which all created or derivative monads are productions, born, so to speak, by continuous fulgurations of the Divinity from moment to moment, limited by the receptivity of the creature, to which it is essential to be limited.

God is the source-monad — the original and underived simple substance. All created monads are derived from God through "continuous fulgurations" — continuous flashes or emanations of divine power. The metaphor of fulguration (lightning flash) conveys the idea of a continuous creative act: God does not create once and withdraw, but sustains creation at every moment. Yet the creatures that result from this fulguration are essentially limited — bounded by their own receptivity, their capacity to receive and reflect the divine perfection. The creature is necessarily finite because its very nature involves limitation. This is a continuous creation doctrine with a Neoplatonic flavor: all being flows perpetually from the One, and the degree of being received depends on the capacity of the recipient.

### §48

In God there is Power, which is the source of all; Knowledge, which contains the detail of ideas; and Will, which effects changes or productions according to the principle of the best. These correspond to what in created monads constitutes the subject or base, the perceptive faculty, and the appetitive faculty. But in God these attributes are absolutely infinite or perfect; in created monads or entelechies (or *perfectihabies*, as Hermolaus Barbarus translated the word) they are only imitations, in proportion to the perfection each monad possesses.

This paragraph establishes a structural analogy between God and created monads. God has three attributes — power, knowledge, and will — which correspond to the three aspects of the monad: its substrate (the subject or base), its perception, and its appetition. The analogy is not an identity: God's power, knowledge, and will are infinite and perfect; the monad's corresponding faculties are finite imitations. The degree of imitation is proportional to the monad's degree of perfection. The learned aside about Hermolaus Barbarus (the Renaissance humanist who coined "perfectihabia" as a Latin translation of the Greek *entelecheia*) is characteristic of Leibniz's erudition.

### §49

A creature is said to act outwardly insofar as it has perfection, and to be acted upon by another insofar as it is imperfect. Thus action is attributed to the monad insofar as it has distinct perceptions, and passion insofar as it has confused ones.

This paragraph defines action and passion (activity and passivity) in terms of the monad's perceptual clarity. A monad acts — is the metaphysical ground of what happens — to the extent that its perceptions are distinct. It is acted upon — is metaphysically dependent — to the extent that its perceptions are confused. Since monads cannot really act on one another (they have no windows), "action" and "passion" are redefined in terms of explanatory priority: the monad with the more distinct perception of a given situation is the one that "acts" with respect to that situation, because it contains a better reason for what happens.

### §50

And one creature is more perfect than another in that one finds in it what serves to render a reason a priori for what happens in the other; and it is for this reason that one says it acts on the other. Perfection is here linked to explanatory power: the more perfect creature is the one whose internal states provide the a priori reason for the states of the other. "Acting on" another is redefined as providing the rational ground for the other's states. This is a thoroughly intellectualist and non-causal conception of inter-monadic relations: real physical influx is replaced by rational explanation.

### §51

But in simple substances this is only an ideal influence of one monad on another, which can have its effect only through the intervention of God, insofar as in the ideas of God one monad rightly demands that God, in regulating the others from the beginning of things, have regard to it. For since a created monad can have no physical influence on the interior of another, it is only by this means that one can be dependent on another.

The inter-monadic "influence" is entirely ideal — it exists only in the divine plan, not as real causal interaction. God, in creating the system of monads, arranged each one from the beginning so that its states would correspond appropriately to those of all the others. One monad "demands" that God take it into account in regulating the rest. This is the core of the doctrine of pre-established harmony: the coordination of monads is not achieved through real causal interaction but through God's original design. Each monad is programmed, as it were, to unfold in perfect concert with all the others, without any of them ever actually affecting any other.

### §52

And it is through this that actions and passions among creatures are mutual. For God, comparing two simple substances, finds in each reasons that oblige him to accommodate the other to it. Consequently, what is active in certain respects is passive according to another point of consideration: active insofar as what is known distinctly in it serves to render a reason for what happens in another; and passive insofar as the reason for what happens in it is found in what is known distinctly in another.

The mutuality of action and passion is explained through God's perspective. When God considers any two monads, he finds in each some distinct perceptions that ground what happens in the other, and some confused perceptions whose ground lies in the other. This means that every monad is both active and passive relative to every other — active in certain respects (where it has the clearer perception), passive in others (where the other monad has the clearer perception). The entire network of inter-monadic relations is thus a network of mutual rational dependencies, orchestrated by God.

### §53

Now, since there is an infinity of possible universes in the Ideas of God and only one of them can exist, there must be a sufficient reason for God's choice, determining him to one rather than another.

This paragraph introduces Leibniz's doctrine of possible worlds. God's intellect contains an infinity of possible universes — complete, self-consistent systems of monads and their histories. But only one can be actualized. The principle of sufficient reason requires that God's choice of this one over all the others be grounded in a reason. God does not choose arbitrarily or without grounds. The question is: what is the reason for God's choice?

### §54

And this reason can be found only in fitness (*convenance*), or in the degrees of perfection that these worlds contain — each possible having the right to claim existence in proportion to the perfection it envelops.

The reason for God's choice is the relative perfection of the possible worlds. Each possible world has a kind of claim or "right" to existence proportional to its perfection. God, being perfectly good and rational, selects the world with the greatest perfection. This is a metaphysical principle of selection: among all the possibles competing for existence, the one with the most perfection wins. The term *convenance* (fitness, suitability) indicates that God's choice is governed by rational evaluation, not by arbitrary will.

### §55

And this is the cause of the existence of the best, which wisdom makes known to God, which his goodness makes him choose, and which his power makes him produce.

The existence of the best possible world is the result of the cooperation of God's three attributes: wisdom discerns the best among the possibles, goodness chooses it, and power brings it into existence. This is Leibniz's optimism in its most compressed form. The actual world is the best possible world — not merely a good one, but the best that could exist. This thesis is the foundation of the *Theodicy* and the target of Voltaire's later satire in *Candide*.

### §56

Now this connection or accommodation of all created things to each and of each to all makes each simple substance have relations that express all the others, so that it is consequently a perpetual living mirror of the universe.

The universal accommodation — the pre-established harmony of all monads — means that each monad has internal relations (its perceptions) that express or represent every other monad. Each monad is therefore a "perpetual living mirror of the universe" — it reflects the entire cosmos from its own particular point of view. The mirror metaphor is central to Leibniz's metaphysics: the monad does not merely represent some things; it represents everything, because the universe is an interconnected whole and each part is related to every other part. The mirror is "living" because the monad is not a static reflection but a dynamic, perceiving, striving substance.

### §57

And, just as the same city viewed from different sides appears entirely different and is as if multiplied perspectively, it happens similarly that through the infinite multitude of simple substances, there are as it were as many different universes, which are however only the perspectives of a single one, according to the different points of view of each monad.

Leibniz's celebrated analogy of the city: a single city viewed from many different vantage points appears to be many different cities. Similarly, the one actual universe, viewed from the infinitely many perspectives of all the monads, generates an infinity of apparently different universes — but these are all perspectives on the same single reality. Each monad's representation of the universe is partial and perspectival, colored by its own position and degree of perceptual clarity. The unity of the world is preserved through this perspectival multiplicity: there is one world, but infinitely many ways of representing it.

### §58

And this is the means of obtaining as much variety as possible, with the greatest possible order — that is, it is the means of obtaining as much perfection as possible.

The perspectival multiplicity of monads is not merely a metaphysical curiosity but the very means by which the world achieves maximal perfection. Perfection, for Leibniz, consists in the greatest possible variety combined with the greatest possible order. The infinity of monadic perspectives maximizes variety (each monad is unique), while the pre-established harmony among them maximizes order (every monad is perfectly coordinated with every other). The best possible world is therefore the one with the most variety and the most order — the richest content under the most comprehensive lawfulness.

### §59

Moreover, it is only this hypothesis (which Leibniz ventures to call demonstrated) that properly extols the greatness of God. This is what Monsieur Bayle recognized when, in his *Dictionary* (article "Rorarius"), he raised objections to it — even feeling tempted to believe that Leibniz attributed too much to God, more than is possible. But Bayle could adduce no reason why this universal harmony — whereby every substance exactly expresses all the others through the relations it bears to them — should be impossible.

Leibniz defends his system as the one that does the greatest justice to God's greatness. Bayle's objection — that the system attributes too much to God — is turned into a compliment: if even the critic is impressed by the scope of what is being claimed, that speaks to the system's grandeur. And Bayle's inability to show that universal harmony is impossible is taken as evidence that it is possible — and, given the principle of the best, actual. This is a characteristic Leibnizian rhetorical move: the objection is reframed as supporting the very thesis it was meant to undermine.

### §60

One sees moreover, in what has just been reported, the a priori reasons why things could not go otherwise. For God, in regulating the whole, had regard to each part, and particularly to each monad, whose representative nature means that nothing could limit it to representing only a part of things. Yet it is true that this representation is only confused in the detail of the whole universe, and can be distinct only in a small portion of things — namely, those that are either nearest or greatest relative to each monad. Otherwise each monad would be a Divinity. It is not in the object but in the modification of the knowledge of the object that monads are limited. They all go confusedly to the infinite, to the whole; but they are limited and distinguished by the degrees of their distinct perceptions.

This paragraph resolves a tension: if each monad represents the whole universe, why isn't each monad omniscient — why isn't each monad a God? The answer lies in the distinction between confused and distinct representation. Every monad represents everything, but most of its representations are confused — unclear and undifferentiated. Only a small portion of its perceptions are distinct — namely, those relating to things that are nearest to it or most important for it. The monad's limitation is not in what it represents (it represents everything) but in how clearly it represents it. The degrees of distinct perception are what differentiate monads from each other and from God, who alone has perfectly distinct knowledge of everything.

### §61

And composites symbolize with simples in this regard. For since everything is full (plenum), making all matter connected, and since in the plenum every movement has some effect on distant bodies in proportion to their distance — so that each body is affected not only by those that touch it and in some way feels everything that happens to them, but also by their means feels those that touch the first ones it is immediately touched by — it follows that this communication extends to any distance whatsoever. Consequently, every body feels everything that happens in the universe, so that one who sees all could read in each body what happens everywhere, and even what has happened or will happen, by observing in the present what is distant in time and place: *sumpnoia panta* ("all things conspire"), as Hippocrates said. But a soul can read in itself only what is represented there distinctly; it cannot unfold all its folds at once, for they go to infinity.

This is one of the richest paragraphs in the *Monadology*. Leibniz extends the principle of universal expression from monads to bodies (composites). In the plenum (the fullness of space, with no void), every motion propagates through the whole: every body is affected by every other, however distant, because the medium is continuous and filled. An omniscient observer could therefore read the entire history of the universe — past, present, and future — in any single body, because the traces of all events are impressed upon it. The Hippocratic formula *sumpnoia panta* ("all things conspire" or "everything is in sympathy") encapsulates this universal interconnection. However, a finite soul can only read what is distinctly represented in it; it cannot unfold its infinitely complex content all at once. The monad's infinite representational content is like an infinitely folded cloth: each fold contains further folds, and no finite act of awareness can unfold them all.

### §62

Thus, although each created monad represents the whole universe, it represents more distinctly the body that is particularly affected to it and of which it constitutes the entelechy. And since this body expresses the whole universe through the connection of all matter in the plenum, the soul also represents the whole universe by representing this body, which belongs to it in a particular way.

The monad's privileged body — the body of which it is the entelechy or dominant soul — serves as a window (metaphorically, despite the "no windows" of §7) onto the universe. The soul represents the whole universe, but it does so primarily by representing its own body distinctly, and through the body's connections with everything else, it indirectly represents everything. The body, being part of the material plenum, is connected to every other body; by tracking its own body's states, the soul tracks (confusedly) the states of the entire universe. This establishes the monad-body relation as a relation of representational privilege, not of causal interaction.

### §63

The body belonging to a monad that is its entelechy or soul constitutes, together with the entelechy, what may be called a living thing; and together with the soul, what is called an animal. Now the body of a living thing or animal is always organic. For since every monad is a mirror of the universe in its own way, and since the universe is regulated in a perfect order, there must also be an order in the representer — that is, in the perceptions of the soul — and consequently in the body, according to which the universe is represented in it.

The monad-body pair is identified with the living thing (or animal). Crucially, the body is always *organic* — organized, structured, purposive. This is not accidental but follows from the monad's representative nature: since the monad mirrors a perfectly ordered universe, its body must itself be ordered. The order of the universe is reflected in the order of the body, which in turn is reflected in the order of the soul's perceptions. Body and soul are thus isomorphic representations of the same cosmic order — one material, one spiritual.

### §64

Thus each organic body of a living thing is a kind of divine machine, or natural automaton, which infinitely surpasses all artificial automata. For a machine made by human art is not a machine in each of its parts. For example, the tooth of a brass gear has parts or fragments that are no longer anything artificial and have nothing in them that marks them as machines relative to the use for which the gear was intended. But the machines of nature — that is, living bodies — are still machines in their smallest parts, to infinity. This is what makes the difference between Nature and Art, that is, between divine Art and ours.

The distinction between natural and artificial machines is one of Leibniz's most celebrated doctrines. A human-made machine is a machine only at a certain scale: look closely enough at its parts, and you find mere raw material — metal, wood, etc. — with no further mechanical organization. But a living body is a machine all the way down: every part of a living body is itself an organized machine, composed of yet further organized machines, to infinity. There is no level at which you reach mere unstructured matter. This infinite mechanical complexity is the hallmark of divine art and the ground of the absolute difference between natural and artificial things. The thesis has a dual significance: it establishes the irreducible complexity of living bodies and it points to God as the infinitely skilled artisan who alone could produce such machines.

### §65

And the author of nature was able to practice this divine and infinitely marvelous artifice because each portion of matter is not only divisible to infinity, as the ancients recognized, but is actually subdivided without end — each part into further parts, each of which has some motion of its own. Otherwise it would be impossible for each portion of matter to express the whole universe.

The actual infinite subdivision of matter — not merely potential divisibility, as Aristotle held, but real, completed infinite division — is what makes the universal expression possible. Each portion of matter, however small, is itself a complex organized system in motion. If there were any portion of matter that was not further subdivided and organized, it would be incapable of expressing the infinite complexity of the universe. The actuality of the infinite division is therefore required by the representative nature of matter within the Leibnizian system.

### §66

From which one sees that there is a world of creatures, of living things, of animals, of entelechies, of souls, in the smallest portion of matter. Every bit of matter, no matter how tiny, contains within itself a world of living beings — a cosmos in miniature. The implications are staggering: nature is infinitely populated at every scale, and the distinction between the "small" and the "large" is merely relative. There is no bottom level of brute, unorganized matter; at every scale, there is life, perception, and organization.

### §67

Each portion of matter can be conceived as a garden full of plants, or as a pond full of fish. But each branch of the plant, each limb of the animal, each drop of its humors, is again such a garden or such a pond. This is Leibniz's most vivid expression of the infinite nesting of living things within living things. The garden and the pond are images of teeming, organized life. And the image is recursive: within the garden, each plant is itself a garden; within the pond, each fish is itself a pond. The metaphor communicates the inexhaustibility of nature's complexity and the impossibility of ever reaching a level of mere dead matter.

### §68

And although the earth and air between the plants of the garden, or the water between the fish of the pond, are neither plant nor fish, they nonetheless still contain them — but most often of a subtlety imperceptible to us. Even the apparently empty or inert space between the obvious living things is itself populated with living beings, too subtle for our senses to detect. There is no truly empty, dead, or inert portion of nature. The plenum is not merely full of matter; it is full of life.

### §69

Thus there is nothing uncultivated, nothing sterile, nothing dead in the universe; no chaos, no confusion except in appearance — much as a pond at a distance might appear to show only a confused swarming movement, without our being able to discern the individual fish.

Apparent chaos and confusion are merely the result of our limited perceptual resolution. From a distance, or at a scale too fine for our senses, the organization and vitality of nature are invisible. But they are there nonetheless. This is a fundamentally optimistic and rationalist vision of nature: disorder is always merely apparent, never real. The universe is thoroughly ordered, organized, and alive at every level.

### §70

One sees from this that each living body has a dominant entelechy, which is the soul in the animal; but the members of this living body are full of other living things — plants, animals — each of which has in turn its own entelechy or dominant soul.

Every living body has a hierarchy of monads: one dominant monad (the soul of the organism as a whole) and a host of subordinate monads, each of which is the dominant soul of its own smaller organic body. The members, organs, and fluids of an animal are themselves composed of living things with their own entelechies. This establishes a nested hierarchy of dominance: the soul of the whole animal presides over the souls of its organs, which preside over the souls of their cells (though Leibniz does not use that term), and so on without end.

### §71

But one must not imagine, with some who misunderstood Leibniz's thought, that each soul has a mass or portion of matter proper to it and assigned to it forever, and that it therefore possesses other inferior living things permanently devoted to its service. For all bodies are in a perpetual flux, like rivers, and parts enter and leave them continually.

This paragraph corrects a misunderstanding: the monad-body relation is not a permanent, fixed ownership of a specific portion of matter. Rather, the body is in constant material flux — like a river that retains its form while its water constantly changes. Parts are always entering and leaving the body. The monad's body is not a fixed set of material particles but a dynamic, flowing organization. This anticipates modern biological thinking about metabolism and the constant turnover of an organism's material constituents.

### §72

Thus the soul changes its body only little by little and by degrees, so that it is never stripped all at once of all its organs. There is often metamorphosis in animals, but never metempsychosis or transmigration of souls. Nor are there entirely separated souls, nor spirits without bodies. God alone is entirely detached from body.

The soul's body changes gradually — never abruptly or completely. This rules out metempsychosis (the transmigration of a soul from one body to an entirely different one) and the existence of disembodied souls or spirits. Every created monad always has some body, however reduced or transformed. Only God is entirely without body. The gradualness of bodily change ensures continuity of the soul-body relationship even through dramatic transformations (metamorphosis, as in a caterpillar becoming a butterfly).

### §73

This is also why there is never entire generation or perfect death in the strict sense — that is, consisting in the separation of the soul from the body. What we call generations are developments and growths; what we call deaths are envelopments and diminutions.

Birth and death, as ordinarily understood, are not absolute beginnings and endings but relative changes: "generation" is the development and unfolding of an already existing organism, and "death" is the envelopment and contraction of the organism to a simpler state. The soul never separates from its body; it merely presides over a body that has contracted or simplified. This is consistent with Leibniz's earlier argument that monads cannot begin or end naturally (§§4–6): what appears as birth or death is really a change in degree of organization, not an absolute origination or destruction.

### §74

Philosophers have been much embarrassed about the origin of forms, entelechies, or souls. But today, when exact researches on plants, insects, and animals have shown that organic bodies in nature are never produced from chaos or putrefaction but always from seeds — in which there was undoubtedly some preformation — it has been judged that not only was the organic body already present before conception, but also a soul within that body, and in short the animal itself. Through conception, this animal was merely disposed for a great transformation to become an animal of another species. One sees something similar outside of generation, as when worms become flies and caterpillars become butterflies.

Leibniz invokes the biological theory of preformation — the idea that organisms are not generated de novo but develop from preexisting structures within the seed. This aligns with his metaphysics: since monads cannot begin naturally, the soul and its organic body must have existed before what we call "conception." Conception is not the creation of a new organism but the transformation of an already existing one. The analogy with metamorphosis (worms into flies, caterpillars into butterflies) illustrates how dramatic transformations can occur without the creation or destruction of the underlying substance.

### §75

The animals that are elevated to the rank of larger animals through conception may be called spermatic. But those that remain in their species — the great majority — are born, multiply, and are destroyed like the larger animals, and only a small number of the elect pass to a greater theater.

Most of the microscopic spermatic animals remain at their original level, living and dying like any other creatures. Only a few are "elected" — chosen, as it were — to undergo the transformation into a higher form of life through conception. The theological language of "election" is deliberate: it echoes the Calvinist doctrine of election and connects the biological process to the larger theme of divine governance.

### §76

But this was only half the truth. Leibniz therefore judged that if the animal never begins naturally, it also never ends naturally — that not only will there be no generation, but also no entire destruction, nor death in the strict sense. And these reasonings made a posteriori and drawn from experience accord perfectly with Leibniz's principles deduced a priori as above.

The symmetry between beginning and ending is completed: just as the animal never truly begins (it was always already there in the seed), so it never truly ends. Death is not an absolute destruction but an involution, a contraction. Leibniz finds that the a posteriori evidence from biology (preformation, the persistence of organisms through apparent death) confirms the a priori metaphysical arguments (monads cannot begin or end naturally). This convergence of empirical and rational evidence is presented as a powerful vindication of the system.

### §77

Thus one may say that not only is the soul (mirror of an indestructible universe) indestructible, but also the animal itself, even though its machine often perishes in part and takes on or sheds organic coverings.

Both the soul and the animal (the soul-body composite) are indestructible. The body may lose and gain parts — shedding and acquiring "organic coverings" — but the core organism persists. The animal is like a being that changes its clothes without ceasing to exist. The indestructibility of the animal follows from the indestructibility of the soul (which, as a monad, cannot be annihilated naturally) and the principle that every soul always has some body.

### §78

These principles have given Leibniz the means to explain naturally the union — or rather the conformity — of the soul and the organic body. The soul follows its own laws and the body also its own; and they meet by virtue of the pre-established harmony among all substances, since they are all representations of the same universe.

The mind-body problem is solved — or rather dissolved — by the doctrine of pre-established harmony. Soul and body do not interact causally; they run in parallel, each following its own laws, and they agree because God has pre-coordinated them from the beginning. The soul follows the laws of final causes (purposes, goals), and the body follows the laws of efficient causes (mechanical motions). Their agreement is not the result of interaction but of a shared origin in the divine plan and a shared object of representation — the same universe. Leibniz speaks of "conformity" rather than "union" to emphasize that the relation is one of correspondence, not of causal connection.

### §79

Souls act according to the laws of final causes, through appetitions, ends, and means. Bodies act according to the laws of efficient causes, or of motions. And the two realms — that of efficient causes and that of final causes — are harmonious with each other.

This paragraph states the parallelism of the two causal orders with maximal clarity. The realm of final causes (the order of purposes, intentions, and goals) governs the soul; the realm of efficient causes (the order of mechanical motions and forces) governs the body. These two orders run in perfect harmony — every event in the soul has a corresponding event in the body, and vice versa — but neither causes the other. This double-aspect parallelism is the core of Leibniz's solution to the mind-body problem.

### §80

Descartes recognized that souls cannot give force to bodies, since there is always the same quantity of force in matter. However, he believed that the soul could change the direction of bodies. But this is because in his time the law of nature was not known that also requires the conservation of the same total direction in matter. Had Descartes noticed this, he would have arrived at Leibniz's system of pre-established harmony.

Leibniz critiques Descartes's attempted solution to the mind-body problem. Descartes had proposed that the soul, while unable to add or subtract force (which is conserved), could redirect bodily motion — specifically by tilting the pineal gland. Leibniz points out that not only the quantity of force but also the total direction (what we would now call momentum) is conserved in nature. Therefore, the soul cannot change even the direction of bodily motion. If Descartes had known this further conservation law, Leibniz argues, he would have been forced to adopt pre-established harmony as the only viable account of mind-body coordination.

### §81

This system makes it so that bodies act as if (per impossible) there were no souls, and souls act as if there were no bodies, and both act as if each influenced the other.

This is the summary formula of pre-established harmony. The physical world is causally complete — it runs as if no souls existed. The mental world is likewise complete — it runs as if no bodies existed. And yet the two worlds are so perfectly coordinated that they behave as if each influenced the other. The phrase *per impossible* ("assuming the impossible") indicates that the hypothetical scenarios are counterfactual: there are souls and bodies, but their respective causal orders are self-contained. The appearance of interaction is the product of divine pre-coordination, not of real causal connection.

### §82

As for rational souls or spirits, although Leibniz finds that fundamentally the same holds for all living things and animals as stated above — namely, that the animal and the soul begin only with the world and do not end any more than the world does — there is nonetheless something particular about rational animals. Their small spermatic animals, so long as they are merely that, have only ordinary or sensitive souls. But when those that are elect, so to speak, attain through actual conception the human nature, their sensitive souls are elevated to the rank of reason and to the prerogative of spirits.

Rational souls (spirits) share the basic metaphysical structure of all monads — they have always existed and will always exist — but they have a special feature: their elevation from sensitive to rational occurs at a specific moment (conception). Before that moment, the spermatic animal has only a sensitive soul; at conception, this soul is elevated to the rank of a rational spirit. The language of "election" again echoes theological themes of predestination and grace. The elevation of the sensitive soul to reason is a kind of natural miracle — a transformation within the natural order that corresponds to the infusion of rationality.

### §83

Among other differences between ordinary souls and spirits — some of which have already been noted — there is also this: that souls in general are living mirrors or images of the universe of creatures, but spirits are in addition images of the Divinity itself, or of the Author of nature — capable of knowing the system of the universe and of imitating something of it through architectonic models, each spirit being like a small divinity in its department.

Spirits are distinguished from mere souls by their capacity to know and imitate God. While all souls mirror the universe of creatures, spirits additionally mirror God himself — they can understand the system of the world and reproduce aspects of it in miniature. The phrase "small divinity in its department" expresses the special dignity of rational beings: each spirit, within its own domain, exercises a kind of creative and cognitive sovereignty analogous to God's governance of the whole. This is the basis for the moral dignity of rational beings and for their special relationship with God.

### §84

This is what makes spirits capable of entering into a kind of Society with God, and he is to them not only what an inventor is to his machine (as God is with respect to other creatures) but also what a Prince is to his subjects, and even a father to his children.

The relation between God and spirits is qualitatively different from his relation to other creatures. Other creatures relate to God as artifacts to their maker — as machines to their inventor. But spirits relate to God as subjects to a prince and as children to a father. These are personal, moral, and political relationships, not merely technical or mechanical ones. This elevates the status of rational beings within the cosmic order and grounds the possibility of a moral community between God and spirits.

### §85

From this it is easy to conclude that the assembly of all spirits must compose the City of God — that is, the most perfect State possible under the most perfect of Monarchs.

The totality of rational spirits constitutes the "City of God" (*Cité de Dieu*) — a phrase with deep resonances in the Augustinian tradition. This is not merely a metaphor: it is a real metaphysical community, the most perfect possible political order, governed by the most perfect possible ruler. The City of God is the supreme moral and political reality — the end toward which the entire creation is ordered.

### §86

This City of God, this truly universal Monarchy, is a Moral World within the Natural World, and the most elevated and most divine of God's works. It is in this that the glory of God truly consists, since there would be none if his greatness and goodness were not known and admired by spirits. It is also in relation to this divine City that God properly has Goodness, whereas his wisdom and power are displayed everywhere.

The City of God is a moral world embedded within the natural world — a realm of freedom, justice, and love within the realm of mechanical necessity. God's glory consists not merely in the existence of the natural world but in its being known and admired by rational spirits. Without spirits to recognize God's greatness, there would be no glory. God's goodness, specifically, is directed toward the City of God — toward the moral community of rational beings. His wisdom and power are displayed in nature as a whole, but his goodness is properly realized only in his relationship with spirits.

### §87

As we established above a perfect harmony between two natural realms — that of efficient causes and that of final causes — we must here note yet another harmony: between the physical realm of nature and the moral realm of grace — that is, between God considered as Architect of the machine of the universe and God considered as Monarch of the divine City of spirits.

There are two levels of harmony in the Leibnizian system. The first is the harmony between efficient and final causes within nature (the parallelism of body and soul, established in §§78–81). The second, introduced here, is the harmony between the natural order as a whole and the moral order — between God as Architect (governing nature by physical laws) and God as Monarch (governing spirits by moral laws). The natural world and the moral world are not independent or conflicting but are perfectly coordinated: the physical laws of nature serve the moral purposes of grace.

### §88

This harmony makes it so that things lead to grace by the very ways of nature, and that this globe, for example, must be destroyed and restored by natural means at the moments demanded by the government of spirits — for the punishment of some and the reward of others.

The coordination of nature and grace means that natural events serve moral ends. Even seemingly "natural" catastrophes — the destruction and restoration of the world — occur at the moments dictated by the moral governance of spirits, serving as instruments of punishment and reward. This is a bold thesis: the physical history of the world is orchestrated to serve the moral government of the City of God. Natural events are not morally neutral but are woven into the fabric of divine justice.

### §89

One may further say that God as Architect satisfies God as Legislator in all things, and that sins must therefore carry their punishment with them by the order of nature and by virtue of the very mechanical structure of things; and similarly, good actions will attract their rewards by mechanical means with respect to bodies — although this cannot and should not always happen immediately.

The harmony of nature and grace entails a natural form of moral causality: sins naturally produce their own punishments, and good actions naturally produce their own rewards, through the mechanical order of nature itself. God the Architect has designed the physical world so that its mechanical operations serve the moral law of God the Legislator. This is not a matter of arbitrary supernatural intervention but of the inherent structure of things. However, Leibniz adds the qualification that this natural moral causality does not always operate immediately — justice may be delayed but is never ultimately defeated.

### §90

Finally, under this perfect government there would be no good action without reward, no bad action without punishment. Everything must turn out for the good of the good — that is, of those who are not malcontents in this great State, who trust in Providence after having done their duty, and who love and imitate, as one should, the Author of all good, taking pleasure in the contemplation of his perfections, following the nature of that true and pure love that takes pleasure in the felicity of the one loved. This is what makes wise and virtuous persons work toward everything that appears conformable to the presumptive or antecedent divine will, while contenting themselves with what God actually brings about by his secret, consequent, and decisive will — recognizing that if we could understand the order of the universe well enough, we would find that it surpasses all the wishes of the wisest, and that it is impossible to make it better than it is — not only for the whole in general but also for ourselves in particular, if we are attached as we should be to the Author of the whole, not only as the Architect and efficient cause of our being but also as our Master and the final cause who must be the whole aim of our will, and who alone can make our happiness.

The final paragraph is the culmination and peroration of the entire work. Under God's perfect government, justice is ultimately complete: every good is rewarded and every evil punished. The good are those who trust in Providence, who do their duty, and who love God with the pure love that finds its joy in God's own felicity — not in any selfish expectation of personal benefit. The distinction between God's "antecedent" will (what God wishes ideally, prior to consideration of the whole) and his "consequent" will (what God actually decrees, taking everything into account) is invoked: the wise person acts in accordance with the former and accepts the latter with equanimity. The deepest insight is that the actual order of the universe, if we could fully understand it, would turn out to exceed all our wishes — not only for the world as a whole but for each of us individually. This requires attachment to God not merely as the maker of the world but as its final cause — the end to which all things tend and the only source of true happiness. The *Monadology* thus ends where it began, with the aspiration to the divine point of view — but now enriched by the full architectonic of the system, from the simplest monad to the City of God.
