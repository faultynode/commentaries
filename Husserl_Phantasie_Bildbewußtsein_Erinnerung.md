# Phantasy and Image Consciousness

## (Third Main Section of the Lectures from the Winter Semester 1904/05 on "Main Pieces from the Phenomenology and Theory of Knowledge")

### Text Nr. 1 from *Husserliana* XXIII: *Phantasie, Bildbewußtsein, Erinnerung*

---

## 1st Chapter: The Question of the Phantasy Presentation in Contrast to the Perceptual Presentation

### [Chapter Introduction]

The opening paragraph situates the present investigations within the broader lecture course. Husserl notes that the preceding lectures dealt with the phenomenology of perception, but that such a phenomenology cannot be completed in isolation. It necessarily requires attention to closely related phenomena. Thus what has already been learned about perception will receive new illumination, supplementation, and enrichment through the analyses now to be undertaken. The next goal, Husserl announces, is the phenomenology of phantasy (*Phantasie*). This framing is significant: it signals that perception and phantasy stand in an essential, not merely accidental, relationship. The phenomenological analysis of one demands the co-analysis of the other, because both belong to the broader field of objectifying acts — of acts that bring objects to appearance.

### §1. Ambiguity of the Concept of Phantasy in Ordinary Discourse — The Phantasy Experience as Foundation of Phenomenological Essence-Analysis and Concept-Formation

Husserl begins by observing that we all bring a certain concept of phantasy, phantasy appearance, and phantasy presentation from ordinary life, and that — as with nearly all class-concepts of psychic phenomena drawn from common life — this concept is vague and ambiguous. The word "phantasy" sometimes denotes a certain mental disposition or talent and at other times denotes certain actual experiences, activities, or products of activity that spring from this disposition. He draws an analogy: just as one distinguishes understanding (*Verstand*), activities of understanding, and works of understanding, so one can distinguish phantasy-as-faculty, exercise of phantasy, and works of phantasy. Phantasy in the first sense means a mental faculty, a capacity — as when we speak of a person of strong or weak phantasy, or, hyperbolically, of a person devoid of phantasy.

On the other hand, we also speak of the phantasies of an artist, and here we have in mind certain psychic experiences that the artist accomplishes in himself or that he awakens in us through his works. The externally visible works themselves — paintings, sculptures, literary compositions — are not normally called phantasies, but rather the figures brought to appearance by means of them: the human beings or fabulous creatures, the actions, passions, situations, etc. that the poet invents (*fingiert*) for us. These figurations are also designated as works of phantasy (in the first sense), and works in this sense are themselves often called phantasies.

Husserl sharply delimits the phenomenological interest. Phantasy as a faculty lies outside the scope of investigation, as does phantasy activity insofar as we consider it as a causal process unfolding within psychic objectivity — i.e., as an activity in the genuine sense, as a psychic action. The same holds for the product of such action. What interests phenomenology are phenomenological data serving as foundations for an essence-analysis to be carried out — specifically, certain intentional or, better, objectifying experiences (*objektivierende Erlebnisse*) that are also commonly subsumed under the ambiguous title of phantasy activity: so-called phantasy presentations, often called simply presentations (*Vorstellungen*). Examples include the experiences in which the artist contemplates his phantasy-formations — that peculiar inner seeing, or bringing-to-intuition, of centaurs, heroic figures, landscapes, etc., which we contrast with outer seeing, i.e., perception.

To this inner appearing-as-present in perception stands opposed the inner making-present-to-oneself (*sich innerlich Vergegenwärtigen*), the "hovering before one in phantasy." The faculty, the complex of original or acquired dispositions, is nothing phenomenological. The phenomenological sphere is that of what is truly given, of what is adequately findable, together with its real (*reell*) component parts. Disposition, by contrast, is a concept that objectifyingly reaches beyond the genuine immanent sphere. It is an important methodological concept of psychology, but it does not concern us. What does concern us is the phantasy experience, the so-called phantasy presentation, which is a phenomenological datum. It evidently belongs to the sphere of objectifying experiences: objectivities are brought to appearance in phantasizing, and they are potentially meant and believed. These objectivities themselves — e.g., appearing centaurs — are nothing phenomenological, just as the appearing objects of thing-perception are not. Nevertheless, they come into consideration for phenomenology insofar as the objectifying experience, here the phantasy experience, shows the immanent characteristic of bringing precisely this so-and-so appearing object to appearance and as being this. It is an immanent determination of the phantasy presentation, an essential peculiarity that can be found through evident analysis as a purely inner moment of such experiences. Thus the circumstance that the experience refers to something object-like, that it refers to it in this manner and form, and what the object-like presents itself as within it — all of this belongs to the phenomenological analysis of the experience.

Husserl notes that the popular concept of phantasy does not refer only to the sphere of artistic phantasy. At least closely related to it is a very common, narrower concept of phantasy, which psychology has taken up under the title of productive phantasy. Productive phantasy is voluntarily shaping phantasy, as the artist preeminently practices it. But two concepts, a broader and a narrower, must be further distinguished, depending on whether one understands the voluntariness of shaping simultaneously in the sense of free invention (*freies Erdichten, Fingieren*) or not. The historian also exercises productive, voluntarily shaping phantasy, but he does not invent: on the basis of secured data he seeks to draft, by means of shaping phantasy, coherent intuitions of personalities, fates, epochs — intuitions of actualities, not of fictions.

Ordinary usage extends the concept of phantasy beyond productive phantasy. Hallucinations, illusions, and dream appearances are often called phantasies, but not memory presentations or expectation presentations, in which non-present objects are valued as actualities — as having previously existed or being securely expected. Of hope it is said that phantasy enlivens it, but what counts as phantasy here are not definite expectations but merely fictions. The main role in the ordinary sense of phantasy is clearly played by a certain moment: phantasizing is opposed to perceiving and to the intuitive accepting-as-true of the past and future — in short, to all acts that posit individually concrete things as existing. Perception lets a present actuality appear as present and as actuality; memory places an absent actuality before our eyes, not indeed as itself present, but still as actuality. Phantasy, by contrast, lacks the consciousness of actuality directed at what is phantasized. Indeed, even more: commonly the word, especially the parallel word "imagination" (*Einbildung*), expresses un-actuality, pretense — what is phantasized is merely imagination, i.e., mere semblance. Yet not every semblance, not even every sensory-intuitive semblance, counts as a phantasy-semblance; the source of the semblance must lie in the subject. When semblance is attributed to physical causes — the broken stick in water, the impressively rising moon — one does not speak of a phantasy appearance.

These turns of the concept may hold some interest, Husserl says, but are not phenomenologically significant. Phenomenologically, only the immanent matters — inner characters of experiences grasped in pure adequation, their essential features, i.e., what gives occasion for essential generalizations and hence for concept-formations that admit of adequate realization, insofar as we can directly intuit the conceptual essence in evident generalization.

### §2. The Task of Obtaining an Essentially Unified Concept of the Phantasy Presentation as Phantasy Apprehension — Characterization of the Perceptual Apprehension

Whether a phantasy-presenting is artistic or non-artistic, voluntary or involuntary, inventive or non-inventive, we always find, alongside the varying empirical and psychological contexts that do not concern us and alongside varying consciousness-characters that are in themselves phenomenologically given, something common. And we find this same common element in the case of memories and expectations: what is designated as "presentation" (*Vorstellung*), standing in contrast to perception-presentation, sets itself apart in its self-contained peculiarity. But we do not find this common element in hallucinations, illusions, and dream appearances. Here the appearances or the underlying apprehensions are evidently perceptual apprehensions, and insofar as phantasy apprehension proves not to be identifiable with perceptual apprehension, we must — contra ordinary usage — exclude these phenomena.

If we abstract from the character of quality and the act of meaning in perceptions (in the ordinary sense), we obtain the perceptual apprehension (*Wahrnehmungsauffassung*). Holding to what is essential, this concept reaches as far as the distinctive phenomenon of appearing-as-self-present extends. This characteristic yields an essentially unified and phenomenologically realized concept. Various intentional characters may combine with this apprehension — believing, doubting, desiring, etc. — producing complex phenomena linked by the fact that one and the same presentation-type, the "perceptual presentation" or "perceptual apprehension," underlies them. Such presentations are found in hallucinations and illusions as well as in cases of physically natural semblance.

Likewise, the task now is to gain an essentially unified concept of the phantasy presentation as phantasy apprehension. Here too we notice, or can bring ourselves to insight, that under the popular title phantasy and under other titles such as memory and expectation, there stand intentional experiences that — alongside varying consciousness-characters — show an essentially common element as their underlying basis. These are objectifying acts presupposing objectifying apprehension, and this apprehension is in its specific essence the same whether we are dealing with freely rising phantasies, productive phantasies, intuitive expectation-presentations, or intuitive re-presentations of a past that we ourselves experienced.

The interest thus goes not toward differences among complex experiences that the now narrower, now broader concept of phantasy encompasses, but toward this unified and essentially unified apprehension-type that Husserl proposes to call phantasy presentation. Whether it is in fact an essentially peculiar type of presentation and one that is new relative to perception must still be investigated.

### §3. Failure of Contemporary Psychological Research on the Question of the Relationship between Perceptual and Phantasy Presentation. Absence of the Concept of Objectifying Apprehension

The question of the relationship between perceptual and phantasy presentations has been the object of many serious efforts. In the literature it has only exceptionally been treated in dedicated works, and then only in rather superficial fashion. But in various contexts, significant thinkers have touched on it in a way that shows they regarded it as no easy question. Lectures sometimes offer deeper treatment than publications: Husserl thinks of the exceedingly acute way the question was treated in Brentano's lectures, and the fine treatment in Stumpf's psychology lectures.

What made the problem appear so extraordinarily difficult, and what made a serious solution impossible, was in Husserl's view the absence of the concept of objectifying apprehension (*objektivierende Auffassung*) and the associated distinctions between apprehension-contents, apprehension-sense, and apprehension-form. Even the most significant researchers constantly confuse the sensory contents of perception with the object of perception. Confused by metaphysical prejudices, they posit an un-intuitable thing-in-itself as the object of perception, while the truly intuited object is overlooked in theoretical reflection and identified with the sensation-content.

The same happens with phantasy presentations. The sensory content experienced in the phantasy presentation, which functions as a representative in the phantasy apprehension, is confused with the object of the phantasy — the two are identified. Consequently, the phantasy apprehension as a mode of objectification is completely overlooked, just as with perception. What is specifically characteristic of perception — the present-apprehension (*Gegenwartsauffassung*) — is not recognized as a phenomenological characteristic. This also explains the dispute over the distinction between act and content in intuitive presentations. Many researchers hold that when we perceive or phantasize a color or tone, the tone is conscious but consciousness is nothing peculiar belonging specifically to this tone. Some even eliminate the pure I and say simply that content is all that is findable; finding is not a new content appended to contents. On this view, so-called psychical acts, understood (as Brentano understood them) as experiences distinguished from "physical phenomena" (color-phenomena, tone-phenomena, etc.), are fictions.

### §4. Brief Presentation and Critique of Brentano's Doctrine of "Presenting"

On the other side stands the Brentano school. For Brentano, "presenting" (*Vorstellen*) is the title of the first fundamental class of "psychical phenomena," i.e., of intentional experiences. He separates presentation and what-is-presented: the presentation is the act, the presented is the content. It is most remarkable that a researcher of such extraordinary acuity never separated the various concepts of the presented or of content, never carried out the relevant descriptive analyses, and never appreciated the fundamental significance of these distinctions. The content is usually for Brentano the sensation-content of perception. Not clearly distinguished from this — or really not distinguished at all — is what we call, following purely the sense of perception, the perception-object, the supposedly standing-over-against-us, supposedly self-intuited thing. Brentano occasionally speaks of "object" as distinguished from content, but this is the external object in the absolute, metaphysical sense, which he confuses with the object intended in perception — evidently overlooking that only in reflection, in natural-scientific and metaphysical reflection, do we come to set the phenomenal object, as merely appearing, in relation to something else — a complex of atoms, ether-vibrations, energies — that does not fall within the scope of perceptual apprehension but within that of scientific theories referring to perception only indirectly and conceptually.

For Brentano, since he on the one hand wants to maintain that presenting is an act, an intentional consciousness (partly on the grounds of inner experience, partly for theoretical reasons), and on the other hand does not grasp the essence of apprehension (of the perceptual presentation in the genuine sense as an objectifying interpretation), there are in the act-character of presenting itself no differentiations at all. The only differentiation comes from the "content": presenting is as manifoldly determined as there are contents to which it is directed. That such a view is unsatisfactory — that this presenting appears to many as a peculiar thing, as a purposeless form — is understandable. Brentano's presentation strengthens the conviction on the opposing side that presenting is an empty fiction, that there are only contents plus at most the pointed function of attention.

Brentano's incomplete phenomenological analysis naturally entangles him in the greatest difficulties. Presenting is supposed to be something without differentiation — it differentiates only by its contents. But what about the differences between perceptual presentation, phantasy presentation, symbolic presentation, between intuitive and non-intuitive, categorial and sensory, etc.? How are these to be reduced to mere differences in content? Brentano tried, and devoted all his admirable acuity to reinterpreting away all essential differences in the modes of presenting. Yet occasionally he almost ends up conceding that in a certain way modes of presenting must be assumed after all: he feels that something is missing in the analyses. What is missing is the distinction between meaning (*Meinung*), quality, apprehension-character, and apprehension-form. Certainly, mere presenting — grasped as a bare having-something-hover-before-one, a looking-at without any decision — is a unitary character admitting no further differentiation, a final difference in the genus "act." But if one understands by presentation the apprehension — what makes the appearing in intentional acts, independently of believing or not-believing, doubting or wishing, i.e., what remains identical when intellectual indecision passes into decision, affirmation into negation — then there are indeed manifold differences. The apprehension admits of very important analyses. Since these are lacking (in Brentano and equally in the other psychologists), the possibility of methodically approaching the dispute about the relationship between perceptual and phantasy presentation and of separating the sub-problems is also lacking.

### §5. The Question of the Difference between Perceptual and Phantasy Presentation, and the Special Problem of Distinguishing the Corresponding Apprehension-Contents: Sensation and Phantasm

Perceptual appearance and phantasy appearance are so closely related, so similar, that they immediately suggest the relationship of original and image. In both cases we have objectifying apprehensions; in both cases the same object can come to appearance, even with exactly the same determinacies falling within the appearance — from the same side, in short, the appearances are "the same," only that we have perception in one case and phantasy in the other. What can account for the difference? Evidently two things: the contents serving the apprehension, and the apprehension-characters themselves.

Regarding the contents functioning as apprehension-contents, the question is what kind of contents these are and whether in perceptions and phantasies the same contents function as apprehension-contents or not. Perceptions are grounded in sensations, phantasies in sensory phantasms. The question is whether sensory phantasms are, descriptively (not genetically) speaking, generically identical with sensory sensations, or whether they are different. Here a problem marks itself off that can ordinarily be separated from the problem of distinguishing perceptual and phantasy presentation. Whether sensations serve as apprehension-contents of perception or not is immaterial for now. The apprehension-content by itself is not yet a perceiving interpretation. And likewise the phantasm, apart from all apprehension as phantasy of a centaur, a house, etc., is a sensory content that is something totally different from the phantasy. To every sensory sensation-content — e.g., the sensed red — there corresponds a sensory phantasm: the red that actually hovers before me in the intuitive re-presentation of a red thing.

How do the two stand to each other — sensation-red and phantasm-red? Both are red-experiences. Genus and species may be the same. Are there still essential differences? Or is it a difference in a new dimension, such that a red is possible as sensation and specifically exactly the same red is possible as phantasm, and such that the designations "sensation" and "phantasm" do not point back to genetic differences (whether stemming from peripheral or central stimulation), nor to the apprehension-function (whether the same content founds two different apprehensions), but rather to an inner, essential difference? This would be a special problem. Does the apprehending of perception and phantasy have at its disposal two fundamentally different classes of apprehension-contents that nevertheless stand from the outset in the wonderful relation that one class and the other repeat the same genera and species?

Essentially different from this is the other problem: that of the clarification of the perceptual and phantasy apprehensions. Are we dealing in both cases with the same apprehensions, merely grounded in an alleged essential difference of sensory contents as sensations versus phantasms, or are we dealing with essentially different apprehensions? If the latter, what is peculiar about the phantasy apprehension, how does it stand to its apprehension-contents, what modifications can it assume, what commonalities are preserved amid the change of apprehension-contents, and how is the constitution of the whole phenomenon of a phantasy presentation to be understood, both in itself and in comparative regard to related phenomena?

Whoever, like very many psychologists, sees only the contents and shuts his eyes to objectification — to the difference between the content that is experienced and the object that appears — naturally falls into the worst predicaments, whether he posits essential differences between sensation and phantasm or not. If it is an essential difference, one may not ask why in perception an object stands there as present and in mere phantasy it does not — one cannot claim that "present" and "non-present" is a merely verbal expression for two genera of objects. And if the differences between sensation and phantasm are merely gradual, the question arises whether the difference between the present object of perception and the merely re-presented object of phantasy is also a gradual one — and whether gradual gradation here is not nonsensical.

### §6. Critical Discussion of the Differences between Perception and Phantasy Advanced by the Psychologists

The one-sided direction of interest toward genetic explanation, long before the beginning of a description not even appreciated for its importance and difficulty, conceals all problems for many psychologists and epistemologists with psychologistic tendencies. They are quickly done, pointing simply to the difference of origin: perceptual presentations arise from peripheral stimulation, phantasy presentations do not. When asked about descriptive differences, they point (following Aristotle, who was the first to do this) to the greater vividness (*Lebhaftigkeit*) of perceptual presentations. Hume makes do with this difference alone. In more recent times, new differences have been sought. Following Alexander Bain, the characteristic of fullness (*Fülle*) is named: phantasy presentations are more gappy, poorer in distinctions, determinacies, and characters in comparison with corresponding perceptual presentations. Further, the characteristic of durability versus fleetingness is noted: a perception endures in unchanged fullness and strength as long as the causative stimulus endures, while phantasms hover fleetingly, emerging and vanishing, changing even in their content. Finally, the voluntary variation that phantasies permit is adduced — variation not based on voluntary intervention in the external world.

With such differences one sought to make do, supplemented by genetic differences and differences in psychological effects (again causal, not phenomenological differences). It is easy to see that the core of the matter is not touched by such differences and that the problems of distinguishing sensation and phantasm and of analyzing the two kinds of apprehension are jumbled together. The characteristic of intensity or vividness belongs to the contents, not to the apprehensions — objectification cannot be sensibly characterized as strong or weak. The characteristics of fullness and fleetingness have essential relation to apprehension, but the differences in fullness and fleetingness within phantasy presentations of the same object are at least as great as those between all of them and perceptual presentations of the same object. This whole point is unclear because what the reference-point of varying fullness — namely, the same presented object — phenomenologically means has not been clarified. Regarding apprehension, one would need to ask what the presentifying (*Vorstelligmachung*) of the same object means in perception and phantasy, and whether this self-sameness does not permit a differentiation, a difference in the mode of objectification, which constitutes the true distinction between perception and phantasy — a sharp distinction, not a merely relative one like that of fullness and fleetingness, which also applies within each of the two presentation-types. The characteristic of voluntary variation is likewise useless for a descriptive distinction. We cannot get far with the usual presentations of the psychologists, however much valuable psychological material they have gathered. But the analyses of perception have laid the essential problems so clearly before us that we can immerse ourselves directly in the center of the matter.

## 2nd Chapter: Interpretation of the Phantasy Presentation as Image-Presentation (Imagination) Like the Physical Image-Presentation

### §7. Related Differences within the Perceptual and Phantasy Apprehension

It is evident, speaking in terms of ideal possibility, that to every possible perceptual presentation there belongs a possible phantasy presentation that refers to the same object and in a certain sense in exactly the same way. Almost all the distinctions made regarding perception also apply to phantasy. There are corresponding, mutually related differences on both sides (apart from the difference between apprehension and the characters of meaning and qualitative decision built upon it — characters that on both sides can evidently be exactly the same). Within the apprehension, we see on both sides related differences: e.g., between apprehension-contents and apprehension-characters, between object and content, the objective appearance that may bring the object to appearance from only one side, etc. The object can even hover in phantasy in exactly the appearance in which it was perceived: from the same side, in the same lighting, coloring, adumbration, etc. With all this, it is perceived in one case and phantasized in the other.

### §8. The Phantasy Presentation as Depiction. Beginning of the Essential Determination of Imaging Presentation

Perception was characterized as an act in which the object-like appears as in its own person, as self-present. In phantasy, the object also appears insofar as it is indeed the object that appears, but it does not appear as present — it is only re-presented (*vergegenwärtigt*), it is as though it were there, but only as though; it appears to us in an image. The Latin term is *imaginatio*. The phantasy presentation seems to claim or presuppose a new character of apprehension: it is depiction (*Verbildlichung*). That the mere objective similarity of a phantasy presentation with a corresponding perception — whether concerning the sensory substrate or anything else in the phenomenon — does not suffice to fix what matters, we need not still teach. Everyone knows what it means to re-present an object to oneself, to bring it before oneself in an inner image. But unfortunately only implicitly. For what matters here is to bring explicitly to consciousness that imagehood (*Bildlichkeit*) first has sense through a peculiar consciousness — that having a similar content does not mean apprehending an image; rather, similar things become images of similar things only through the peculiar and utterly primitive image-consciousness, a primitive and ultimate consciousness like the perception- or presence-consciousness.

In fixing a proper genus of presentation as image-presentation or imaging apprehension, one must extend this sphere as far as there truly is an apprehension that depicts its object in an imagistic way. Thus we must immediately see that we must include in our sphere of imagination not only the inner image-presentations normally meant by the expression "phantasy presentation" — i.e., presentations through mental images — but also image-presentations in the ordinary sense of the word: those remarkable presentations in which a perceived object is determined and enabled by similarity to make another object present in the familiar way that a physical image makes its original present.

Husserl holds both kinds of imagination together initially and seeks to bring to clarity what is common to imaging presenting. He proceeds with the utmost caution. For however easy the analysis initially seems, great are the difficulties that subsequently emerge and that stepwise demand modifications of what was earlier assumed. This is the characteristic of phenomenological analysis: every step forward gives new viewpoints from which what was already found appears in new lights, so that what could originally be taken as simple and undifferentiated often presents itself as manifold and differentiated.

What does re-presenting in an image mean — in short, what does imaging presenting mean? In every such presenting we distinguish image (*Bild*) and thing (*Sache*). The thing is the object intended by the presentation, and — by virtue of the interwoven qualitative characters (intellectual or emotional characters) — it is the object held to be existent (e.g., the remembered or expected), or held to be non-actual (as in conscious fiction), or the doubted, wished-for, inquired-about, hoped-for, feared object, etc. Setting aside these characters, we must hold fast to the meaning (*Meinen*). When the Berlin Palace hovers before us in a phantasy image, the Palace in Berlin is the intended, the presented thing. From this we distinguish the hovering image, which is of course not an actual thing and not in Berlin. The image makes the thing present but is not it: we already notice here that this image appears in quite a different sense than the thing, and that calling both "presented in phantasy" involves a troublesome equivocation.

### §9. Physical Imagination as a Parallel Case of the Phantasy Presentation

Before entering into closer analyses, Husserl looks at the parallel case of the physical image. The situation is somewhat more complicated here. When image and thing are distinguished, the concept of "image" is double. Opposed to the depicted thing stands a twofold: (1) the image as a physical thing — this painted and framed canvas, this printed paper — and (2) the image as the appearing image-object (*Bildobjekt*), i.e., the object appearing through the particular distribution of colors and forms. This is not the depicted object, the image-subject (*Bildsujet*), but the precise analogue of the phantasy image: the appearing object that serves as representative for the image-subject. For example, a photograph of a child works by primarily projecting an image that resembles the child overall but diverges markedly in apparent size, coloring, etc. The appearing miniature child in disagreeable gray-violet coloring is obviously not the intended, depicted child — it is its photographic image. When we say the image is unsuccessful or resembles the original only in this or that, we obviously do not mean the physical image (the thing lying on the table), but this image-object. The photograph as thing is an actual object accepted in perception; the image-object is something appearing that never existed and never will exist and that never for a moment counts as actuality.

Three objects are thus distinguished: (1) the physical image (the thing of canvas, marble, etc.); (2) the representing or depicting object (image-object); and (3) the represented or depicted object (image-subject). The image-object is not a part or side of the physical image-thing. The color-pigments distributed on the canvas, the drawing-strokes on the paper — these are not the representing image, the proper image of the imagination, the semblance-thing that appears on the basis of color- and form-sensations. What appears to us is a three-dimensional body with corporeal color-distribution — e.g., in an engraving, Emperor Maximilian on horseback, a three-dimensionally appearing figure built visually from gray nuances and outlines. This is obviously not identical with the gray shadings really found on the physical image, on the paper. The same color-sensations that we interpret as the objective color-distribution on the paper we interpret the other time as the image-rider, the image-child, etc. These image-objects are then further to be distinguished from the depicted objects.

The differences between representing image and image-subject — between the properly appearing and the thereby depicted and intended object — vary from case to case and above all according to the kinds of depiction, and are quite variable. But such differences are always present. Were the appearing image phenomenally absolutely identical with the intended object — were the image-appearance in no way different from the perceptual appearance of the object itself — an image-consciousness could hardly arise. Certainly a consciousness of difference must be present, even though the subject does not appear in the proper sense. The appearing object does not count for itself but as a representative for another, similar or alike object.

### §10. Essential Commonality of Physical Imagination and Ordinary Phantasy Presentation with Respect to "Mental Images"

The situation is more complicated in the case of physical imagination than in ordinary phantasy presentation, but in essence we find commonality: in the former, a physical object is presupposed that exercises the function of awakening a "mental image"; in ordinary phantasy, the mental image is there without being tied to such a physical stimulus. In both cases the mental image is precisely image — it represents a subject. In the simpler case of ordinary phantasy, we distinguished two objects under the titles "image" and "thing." To make two objects present requires two objectifications, two apprehensions; or rather, in the unity of the phantasy presentation, two directions or components of apprehension must be phenomenologically distinguishable. The naive interpretation is much simpler: the image is "in the mind" and the object is "outside." But if it is a mere fiction (e.g., when we phantasize a dragon), then only the mental image is present and there is nothing further to explain. The phenomenological response: the naive view errs in thinking of the mental image as a thing really immanent in the mind. It thinks of the image as present in the mind just as a thing is present in actuality. But in the mind — phenomenologically in consciousness — there is no image-thing present. The case is exactly the same with physical-image representation: the painted lion appears but does not exist.

The image-object truly does not exist — not only does it have no existence outside my consciousness, but it has none within my consciousness either; it has no existence at all. What truly exists (aside from the physical thing "painting") is a certain complex of sensations that the viewer experiences and the apprehension and meaning he builds upon it, so that the consciousness of an image arises for him. Likewise the phantasy image truly does not exist at all; the phantasy image has no psychological existence. Rather, a certain complex of sensory contents — the complex of phantasms — exists, and upon it rests a certain apprehending consciousness through which image-consciousness is completed. Just as color-sensations are not yet the image itself (they contain nothing of the full three-dimensional corporeality belonging to the appearing image), so too in the case of phantasy the phantasm is not yet the phantasy image. What is added in both cases is the objectifying consciousness, the apprehension that interprets the content, gives it objective reference, and makes from the blind existence of the content something that accomplishes objective apprehending. The appearing object of the presentation does not exist as a reell constituent of consciousness — only the apprehension-content, the corresponding mode of apprehension, and the meaning founded therein truly exist, linked perhaps with various higher intentional characters.

### §11. The Reference to the Image-Subject, or the Two Apprehensions Built upon One Another in the Phantasy Presentation — Reference to an Exact Analogue: the Word-Appearance as Bearer of a Second Apprehension as Sign

The apprehension of the experienced sensory contents yields the appearing image, the appearing representing image-object. But with the constitution of this appearance, the reference to the image-subject has not yet been constituted. With a single apprehension we would not yet have, in the proper sense, any image, but at most the object that subsequently functions as image. How does it come to so function? How is it to be made understandable that, while the image-object appears to us, we do not rest content with it but intend another object by its means? A mere meaning cannot help: there must be a presenting in the sense of an apprehending, an objectifying that intentionally constitutes the new object. Meaning presupposes something meant; where there is no objectifying apprehension, meaning can aim at no object.

Thus the phantasy presentation — more precisely, the phantasy apprehension — must be a more complex phenomenon than the perceptual presentation. In perception we have one apprehended object, which in complete perception is the intended one. In the phantasy presentation we have two apprehensions built upon one another, constituting two objects: the phantasy image, which appears, and the imagistically depicted object, the image-subject, which is depicted through the image. The meaning in the complete phantasy presentation is directed at the image-subject. The image-apprehension is a second apprehension founded in the first, which gives it a new character and a new objective reference. In the image, which is not itself the thing, we yet view the thing.

There is thus in the phantasy presentation a certain mediacy of presenting that is absent from the perceptual presentation. Perception presents its object directly: an object appears, and it is what is intended and taken for actual. In the phantasy presentation an object also appears, but this primary and properly appearing object is not the presented one. Phantasy presents an object by first bringing another, similar object to appearance and taking it as a stand-in — or better, the only word here is image — taking it as an image for the properly intended object. An exact analogue: it is like reading a word (e.g., "Integral"), where the word is seen but not intended. Alongside the word-appearance we have a second apprehension (which is not an appearance): the word counts as sign, it means. In normal use we intend not what we see, not what sensorily appears, but the symbolized. The appearing object appears but does not count for itself — it counts for another and thus as an analogical representative, as image.

### §12. Presupposition of the Whole Previous Discussion: the Double Objectivity in the Phantasy Presentation and in the Physical Image-Apprehension

For this entire analysis, the presupposition is that a double objectivity truly and rightfully comes into consideration in the phantasy presentation — that there is an immanent double objectivity, not merely a conceptual distinction introduced by reflection that relates the phantasy experience to actuality. It is not a distinction of the kind we sometimes hear regarding perception, between the appearing thing (in the ordinary empirical sense) and the thing-in-itself. In that case, the two things — the empirical thing and the thing-in-itself — do not both belong to the experience itself, to its apprehension-sense and meaning. The naive consciousness perceives and knows nothing of a thing-in-itself; the reference to it lies not in perception but in metaphysical reflections.

Quite otherwise with the two objects of the phantasy presentation. Everyone who phantasizes has an image-experience. Something object-like appears to them. But no one takes this appearance for a self-appearance of the object. This wavering, fleetingly emerging and vanishing appearance, changing manifoldly in content, so faint — no one takes it for the appearance of the object itself, but for a "presentation" of it, for a re-presentation, a depiction. And yet the appearance as it is actually given is not itself meant; one does not inspect it as it is and appears and then say, "That is an image." Rather, one lives entirely in the new apprehending that is grounded on the appearance: in the image one views the thing. The image-consciousness has a function that gives it a significance pointing beyond its primary object — the character of representation by similarity.

So it is also with the physical image-apprehension: the mere circumstance that sensory sensations underlie perception and phantasms underlie phantasy cannot exhaust the difference between the two. In the imaginative presentation enacted in the contemplation of a painting, we also have sensations as apprehension-contents. But the result of the apprehension is not a perception. The Raphael Madonna I view in a photograph is not the photographically appearing little image. The perceptual appearance depicts a non-perceived object — and this is not conceptual knowledge, not a distinguishing and relating, but the image immediately feels itself as image. The apprehension built on sensory sensation is not a mere perceptual apprehension; it has a changed character: the character of representation through similarity, the character of seeing-in-the-image.

### §13. The Two Apprehensions Essentially Belonging to the Constitution of the Imaginative Presentation

When we speak of two apprehensions essentially belonging to the constitution of the imaginative presentation, we do not mean two separate, same-level apprehension-experiences merely held together by some bond. If the depicted object were constituted by one act by itself and the image by a second separate act, we would have neither image nor depicted — we would merely have one object presented here and another there, at best supplemented by a comparison-consciousness of similarity. But the situation is not so: we do not have two separate presentations and above all not two separate appearances. Rather, two apprehensions are interwoven in one another. A primary apprehension yields an appearance (e.g., a castle-appearance); but with that we imagistically present the castle in Berlin itself, apprehending the appearing castle as a similarity-representative. The new apprehension gives no new presentation with its own separate apprehension-contents; all available sensory contents are already "used up" for the constitution of the image-object.

### §14. Repetition and New Presentation: The Interpenetration of the Two Apprehensions Constituting the Consciousness of Imagehood, and the Similarity-Covering or Separation of the Objects of These Apprehensions. The Givenness of the Conscious Reference to the Image-Subject through the Consciousness of Re-Presentation of a Non-Appearing in the Appearing

Husserl recapitulates: the previous lecture attempted to bring both phantasy presentations and physical-image presentations under the unified viewpoint of imagination and to bring to analytical clarity the peculiarities of all presentations falling under this viewpoint — i.e., imaging presentations in general — in contrast to the perceptual presentations considered before. The constitution of imaging presentations proved more complex than that of simple perceptual presentations. Several essentially different apprehensions showed themselves built upon or into one another, corresponding to the multiple objectivities that intersected and came to the fore for preferential meaning as attention shifted. In physical imaging, three objectivities were interwoven; in phantasy, two. The common element on both sides was that an appearing objectivity did not count for itself but imagistically represented another, non-appearing one.

The new apprehension is not something merely externally attached to the image-appearance, merely outwardly linked with it. The new apprehension permeates the old and has absorbed it. The appearing image-thing does not awaken a new presentation otherwise unrelated to it. It does not point beyond itself in the manner of a mere (analogical) symbol or arbitrary sign toward another object that has no inner relation to the sign. Rather, the image-object illustrates (*veranschaulicht*) what is not identical with it but more or less content-wise the same or similar. In the kindred features lives something of the consciousness of the intended object. Into the image we look the intended object, or out of it the object looks at us. Phenomenologically, the image-object bears a new apprehension-character that permeates and fuses with the original — one that points not simply away from the content of the appearing but through this content to the properly intended object. What functions representatively in the content of the image-object is distinctively marked out: it depicts, re-presents, illustrates (*verbildlicht*), makes intuitive. The subject gazes at us, as it were, through these features.

These features divide into: (a) the veridical, analogizing moments that carry the consciousness of inner representation; (b) the opposing, conflicting moments that carry the consciousness of difference from the subject; and (c) characterless features that neither analogize nor conflict. The conflict-consciousness — the consciousness that the image diverges from the subject — essentially presupposes the consciousness of matching, illustrating moments. Only the latter establish an image-consciousness at all: if the conscious reference to something depicted is not given with the image, we have no image. This conscious reference is given through the peculiar consciousness of re-presentation of a non-appearing in the appearing. How this is possible — since only the image and not at all the subject falls within the appearance — would be a miracle or nonsense if two objectifying apprehensions were not interlaced. The new presentation does not lie alongside the presentation of the image-object but covers itself with it, permeates it, and gives it thereby the character of image-object. The covering pertains to the moments of similarity.

If there were similarity in all respects, there would be all-sided covering, and we would have the consciousness that the depicted object is fully and completely re-presented. At the limit of perfect similarity, we feel as though the object itself were present — but only "as though." Even with full inner covering, sufficient moments for a doubling of consciousness as image- and subject-consciousness need not be absent: we are then pointed to external moments. With a perfect portrait, one feels as though the person were there — but the real person belongs to a different context than the image-object (the real person moves, speaks, etc.; the image-person is a rigid, mute figure). In phantasy too, a perfectly vivid phantasy or fresh memory approaches the point where the consciousness of "mere image" barely arises. But upon closer inspection this is always only re-presentation, never present-being. The phantasy image dissipates, does not long retain freshness, is interrupted by other phantasy images that do not constitute the unity of an objective presence. Nevertheless, there are, as is well known, very variously perfect images, hence very different grades and levels of image-consciousness. Only in the limiting case does the covering between the direct objective apprehension (corresponding to the image-object) and the indirect one (belonging to the subject) go so far that we fully view the image-subject in the image-object; in general the two objects step apart, identifying in some respects and diverging in others.

## 3rd Chapter: Image-Consciousness in Immanent Function and in Symbolic Function — On Aesthetic Image-Contemplation — Question of the Relationship of the Founding Apprehension in Phantasy and Image-Consciousness to the Perceptual Apprehension

### §15. Commonality and Difference of Imaging and Symbolic Apprehension

The preceding considerations make the interpenetration of the two apprehensions constituting the apprehension-consciousness of imagehood somewhat comprehensible, and let not only the difference from the perceptual apprehension but also that from the symbolic apprehension emerge clearly. Both imaging and symbolic apprehension have in common that they are not simple apprehensions — both point beyond themselves in a certain way. But the symbolic apprehension points out of itself, and the signitive even toward an object inwardly foreign to the appearing thing. The imaging apprehension also points to another object, but always to one of the same kind, one that presents itself in the image, and above all it points to the object through itself. In symbolic presenting, the meaning-gaze is directed away from the symbol; in imaging presenting, it is directed into the image. To make the object present, we are to look into the image; in what is the bearer of the image-function, we are to find the object depicted.

### §16. Introduction of the Distinction between Inner (Immanent) and Outer (Symbolic) Imagehood

Two cases of representation through analogy must be carefully distinguished. An image can function representatively inwardly, in the manner of immanent imagehood; or it can function representatively outwardly, in a manner that essentially amounts to the consciousness of symbolic representation. For example, an engraving of the Raphael Madonna can remind us of the original we saw in the Dresden Gallery. Images can function as analogical memory-signs. Recently published volumes with complete series of great artists' works in the smallest reproductions serve not primarily for the awakening of inner imagehood and the associated aesthetic enjoyment, but as illustrated catalogues — repertories of memory, "illustrative catchwords," aids of memory. They work imagistically but also associatively, intended to reproduce more complete image-presentations in memory. One who purely looks into an image lives in the imagehood, having in the image itself the re-presentation of the object. One who uses the image as a memory-aid seeks and perhaps finds a different re-presentation that may offer a richer re-presentation of the same object.

Two classes within symbolic presenting could thus be distinguished: (1) the symbolic in the original, old sense — the outward making-present through images, symbols, hieroglyphs (language and writing originally had symbolic/hieroglyphic character; through attrition and the formation of technical terms, algebraic signs, etc., signitive presenting arises — presenting through signs that have no inner relation to the things); and (2) most scientific images also belong to the first class.

### §17. The Interest in the How of the Depiction of the Image-Object in Aesthetic Image-Contemplation, in Contrast to the Exclusive Direction of Interest toward the Image-Subject in Ordinary Phantasy and Memory Presentation

From images functioning as symbols — and from the image-consciousness enacted in the symbolic function of the image — we must distinguish the intuitive image-consciousness, the consciousness of immanent imagehood. This alone plays its role in aesthetic image-contemplation. We look into the image; our interest belongs to it; in it we view the subject. The image does not have the mere function of awakening an outward presentation. In aesthetic image-contemplation, it is not that only the subject matters: where the image works aesthetically, the play of phantasy may be set in motion so that we live ourselves into the world of the subject, but the interest constantly returns to the image-object, finding enjoyment in the manner of its depiction.

Very essentially different from the stance toward the image-object in aesthetic contemplation is the behavior of ordinary phantasy and memory presentation, whose interest and meaning go exclusively to the image-subject. In phantasy too, the imagehood-consciousness is purely inward (at least in fully vivid phantasy). The image-object does not mean anything in the manner of a symbol; it does not point away from itself. Exceptionally one can also enjoy one's phantasies aesthetically: then we attend not merely to the subject through the image-consciousness but are interested in how the subject presents itself, what imagistic mode of appearance it shows. The artist will eavesdrop on his own phantasies to catch their aesthetically finest poses. But this is not the normal case. In phantasizing, we live in the phantasized events; the how of the inner-imagistic depiction falls outside the range of our natural interests.

### §18. Possibility of Change in the Direction of the Meaning Intention and Corresponding Change of Object. Description of the Mode of Appearance of the Image-Object, e.g. in Psychological Interest

On the same apprehension-ground, different presentative acts can be built up. It is a different presentative habitus: meaning the image-subject, meaning the image-object, and meaning the image-object as image of the subject. The change in the direction of the meaning-intention means also a change of object. Living in free phantasies or memories, the meaning goes to the image-subject. But we can also attend to the image-object and again to the manner of its appearance, to the constitutive components of the appearance, to the sensory phantasms, etc. We can describe the image-object of the phantasy: e.g., "I am now remembering the Botanical Garden as it was in summertime, full of rustling trees, blossoming flowers, shady slopes. But the colors won't come to me; I find more the plastic forms, instead of colors more a fleetingly changing gray." Here we attend to the appearance itself and compare its content with the intended subject. The phenomenon of the normal phantasy presentation and of the presentation directed at phantasy-objects is evidently different: the apprehension-ground can be exactly the same, the same image-objects appear, but in one case the image-objects, in the other case the distant lands (or whatever is the subject), are what is intended and interesting.

### §19. Self-Sufficiency and Non-Self-Sufficiency of the Two Interpenetrating Apprehensions, and the Question of the Relationship of the Founding Apprehension to the Perceptual Apprehension in the Case of Imagination Mediated by Physical Images. Falling Away of the Image-Consciousness in Illusions à la Panopticon, Panorama, etc., and Aesthetic Semblance

Of the two apprehensions interpenetrating in the consciousness of phantasy-imagehood, one is evidently non-self-sufficient and the other self-sufficient. The appearance that puts the image-object before our eyes could, as it occurs in the imagehood-consciousness, also be experienced without such an imaginative function. But the modifying apprehension through which the image first becomes image is evidently bound to a founding appearance. Where there is no appearance, nothing is present that could serve as an image for re-presenting another — an object must stand before our eyes so that we can make another present in it.

How does this founding apprehension relate to perceptual apprehension? We can study the situation in cases where the image-consciousness falls away. In the physical image-apprehension, presupposing the physical image is given in perception, even when the image-apprehension is present (from which we can easily abstract), it is clear that the founding image-object appearance taken by itself has the character of a perceptual appearance, an ordinary presentation. It is of course not a normal and full perception insofar as the appearing thing (e.g., the image-person of an oil painting) does not count as actually present — it appears as present but is not taken for actual. That the transformation of an image-phenomenon by the falling away of the imaginative function can produce an ordinary perceptual apprehension, even a full perception equipped with normal belief, is shown by the oft-mentioned illusions à la Panopticon, Panorama, etc. We may initially see the wax figure as a human being — we have an (afterward recognized as erroneous) normal perception. When we become aware of the deception, the image-consciousness enters. But in these cases it will not maintain itself for long: the wax figure resembles a natural human so closely that the perceptual consciousness momentarily reasserts itself. The accompanying conceptual judgment that it is "mere image" becomes ineffective against the perceptual semblance. The cleavage this places us in is a coarse and quite un-aesthetic effect. Wax figures give perceptual appearances of humans that so perfectly cover the depicted persons that the moments of difference cannot produce a clean, clear difference-consciousness, i.e., a secure image-consciousness. But this is the essential foundation for the possibility of aesthetic feeling in the visual arts. Without image, no visual art. And the image must clearly separate itself from actuality, purely intuitively, without any help from indirect thoughts. We are to be lifted out of empirical actuality and raised into the likewise intuitive world of imagehood. Aesthetic semblance is not sense-deception.

### §20. Whether the Founding Apprehension in Phantasy in the Ordinary Sense and in Memory Has the Character of a Perceptual Apprehension. Falling Away of the Image-Consciousness in Vision and Hallucination. Waking Dreaming and Consciousness of the Semblance of Phantasy Configurations

How does the situation stand in the phantasy not mediated by physical images — phantasy in the ordinary sense, including memory? Should we say, at least in cases where a clear and content-rich intuition of the phantasized objectivities is present, that the founding apprehension has the character of a perceptual apprehension? Is all proper appearing one and the same, everywhere having the same character of presentation (*Präsentation*)? Here too the image-consciousness sometimes falls away, and where it does, we must assume that the remainder has merely the character of perception. Husserl recalls the transitions from phantasy to vision. The phantasy-configurations no longer hover as images before the inner eye; the empirical perception in which the visionary bodily lives is switched off, and with it the opposition between this actuality and the phantasy-imagehood. The visionary is now in a trance-state; the world of phantasy is now his actual world, counting for him as actual — his intuitions are perceptions, also equipped with the character of belief.

The same holds for dreaming — not only in sleep but also in waking dreaming. Sometimes we yield to the pull of phantasy so much that we begin to react to the phantasy-appearances as if they were perceptions — our fists clench, we hold loud conversations with imagined persons. But with this, the dream usually ends; actual perception disperses the fiction. The more frequent case is that the actual world almost vanishes from our view while we follow the phantasies, but still lets its presence be felt a little, so that a faint consciousness of semblance constantly colors the phantasy configurations.

Such experiences speak for the view that the appearances of phantasy, apart from the imagehood-consciousness, are in principle not different from those of perception. But the question is whether this holds only for limit-cases — whether in those cases the phantasy appearance simply converts into hallucination, into a perceptual appearance, while in themselves the image-apprehension in phantasy appearance and perceptual appearance are essentially different. In particular, the question arises whether, even if we need not assume differences in the mode of apprehension constituting an image-object versus a perception-object, essential differences in the apprehension-contents must be conceded.

## 4th Chapter: Differences between Ordinary Image-Presentation and Phantasy Presentation

### §21. The Underlying Apprehensions in the Physical Image-Presentation; the Question of the Identity or Difference of the Apprehension-Contents

Before taking up the question of the relationship between sensation and phantasm, Husserl wants to carry out some interesting and important analyses. The differences between imaginations grounded in perception and imaginations of phantasy are now to be studied. A considerable difference seems to obtain regarding the underlying apprehension. On the side of the physical image-presentation, it seems more complex. In the phantasy presentation, the whole complex of sensory contents belonging to its experiential unity arranges itself into a single appearance — that of the phantasy image. In the physical image-presentation, by contrast, two objects come phenomenally into question: the physical image appears, and the mental image — the depicting image-object — also appears. Both of these objects can be attended to and intended. But a doubt arises when the question of the apprehension-contents of these two appearances is raised: the image-object and the physical image do not have separate and different apprehension-contents but identically the same ones. The same visual sensations are interpreted as points and lines on paper and are interpreted as an appearing plastic figure. The same sensations are interpreted as a thing of plaster and are interpreted as a white human figure. With the identity of the sensation-substrate, the two apprehensions cannot both obtain at once — they cannot simultaneously produce two appearances.

### §22. The Appearance of the Image-Object and Its Character of Non-Actuality, of Conflict with the Perceptual Visual Field Constituting the Present

Husserl examines the situation more closely. In an engraving, yielding to the intentions of artist and engraver, we do not apprehend the drawing as a system of strokes and shadings on a paper surface; rather, insofar as the drawing extends, we see not paper but plastic figures, and through them the reference to the subject is carried out. The engraving has a white paper border: there we see paper. The image has a frame; the frame stands out from the wall on which it hangs along with its paper; the wall belongs to the room. All of this is significant: while we live in the imagination of the subject, our perceptual visual field (*Blickfeld*) does not disappear. We have, even if not in the form of primary meaning, the perception of the surroundings. The image-object appears and bears the subject-consciousness. The apprehension-contents are used up for this appearance. A second apprehension — the paper-apprehension — is in a certain way also there, connected with the continuously unified visual-field apprehension, stimulated by it, but — while the rest of the visual field is appearance — it is not appearance, since its apprehension-contents have been taken from it. And yet it belongs to these apprehension-contents: in short, there is conflict (*Widerstreit*). The image-object prevails insofar as it comes to appearance; the apprehension-contents merge with the image-object apprehension into the unity of the appearance. But the other apprehension is still there, maintaining its normal, firm connection with the surrounding appearance. The perception gives the character of present actuality. The surroundings are actual surroundings; the image appears but conflicts with actual presence — it is merely "image," it is, however much it appears, a nothing.

Visually, corresponding to the continuity of the sensory contents in the visual sensation-field, the entire appearing objectivity — the imagistic and that of the image's surroundings — arranges itself into a single objective context. But this context splits, according to reality-value, into two. The ideal world of the image is a world for itself. Why? How is it phenomenologically characterized as such? The visual field extends beyond the image-field; the frame is there; we look through the frame as through a window into the image-space. The seen objects and the quasi-seen image-objects enter into relation. But why do they not genuinely form an objective context, a perceptual context, a single unified present? Because the surroundings up to the boundary of the image are perceived without any imagehood-consciousness, while the image-object is given in a perceptual apprehension modified by the character of imagination. But even this does not suffice: the appearance of the image-object differs from the normal perceptual appearance in an essential point — it bears in itself the character of non-actuality, of conflict with the actual present. The surrounding perception constitutes actual presence and continues through the frame: there it says "printed paper" or "painted canvas." The sensation-material that the image-object apprehension has claimed for its appearance cannot in the proper sense become the appearance-core a second time. But in an improper way, as a kind of "improper presentation," the paper-perception is an appendix of the surrounding perception. So we have a unity of perception filling the entire visual field, constituting "present, actually present actuality." And with part of this, a second perception — or rather only a perceptual apprehension — covers itself: appearance of a non-now in the now.

### §23. The Relation of Actually Present and Mere Fictum in the Conflict of Two Perceptual Apprehensions in Cases of Sense-Semblance

Wherever one perceptual apprehension enters into conflict with a second (presupposing they have wholly or partly the same sensation-substrate), the apprehension that fits together with the unity of the total actual perception into a comprehensive total-perception, and that participates in the strength of mutually founding belief-intentions, determines the actually present. The other apprehension, insofar as it seizes upon sensory content and yields an appearance, constitutes a mere fictum, a semblance-object, a mere "image," as one also says where no image-representation takes place. This is the case with every "sense-semblance": the broken stick in water is a fiction, a semblance-image.

### §24. Anticipatory View of the Situation in the Case of Phantasy: Complete Separation of Phantasy Field and Perceptual Field

The situation in the case of phantasy (including memory) is quite different. The apprehension-contents of phantasy are evidently not simultaneously bearers of actual or improper perceptual apprehensions. The phantasy image does not appear in the objective context of present actuality. The centaur that now hovers before me in phantasy does not seemingly cover a piece of my visual field, the way the centaur of a Böcklin painting that I actually see does. Actual perceptual space has no region that frames itself and makes room for my phantasies. The phantasy field is completely separated from the perceptual field. But if so, why do we separate them under the titles perception and phantasy? Perhaps because of the imagehood-apprehension? But could phantasy-apprehensions not function without any imagehood — and would they then not be perceptions? Could we then have two perceptual fields, merely two separate ones, thus multiple visual fields, multiple tactile fields, etc.?

### §25. Recapitulation: The Double Mode of Representation through Similarity — (1) Inner Imagehood as the Properly Imaginative Consciousness; the Illustrating Moments in the Image-Object Appearance as Bearers of the Consciousness of Inner Representation, and the Remaining Moments; the Double Conflict-Character of the Image-Object Appearance; (2) Outer Imagehood as a Mode of Symbolic Consciousness

Husserl provides a thorough recapitulation of the preceding analyses. Image-representation can take place in two essentially different ways: (1) It can have the character of inner representation. We view the subject in the image itself; the image-object brings the subject to intuitive presentation within itself, in greater or lesser measure, according to more or fewer illustrating moments. A notable difference emerges regarding the various moments composing the image-appearance. Some are the proper bearers of the consciousness of inner representation; others lack this function. In the former, the image-object presents the subject to us — looking upon them, we view the subject. These moments have a special validity as illustrating moments (e.g., in an engraving or plaster bust, the plastic form of the image-object, but not the black-and-white nuances). The complementary group of moments constituting the image-appearance lacks this distinction: they are in the image but do not count, do not illustrate, and have no symbolic function either. The illustrating moments, however, have their intentional connection with the subject-moments through association. When interest specifically turns to the non-illustrating moments, conflict-consciousness emerges.

The whole image-object bears a double conflict: (a) the conflict against the actual perceptual present (between image as image-object appearance and image as physical image-thing); (b) the conflict between the image-object appearance and the subject-presentation overlapping with it. The greater the range of conscious agreement between image-object and image-subject, the more perfectly the subject is illustrated in the image.

As for outer, transient imagehood — a second mode of representation through similarity, in a series with representation through signs — a photograph can remind us of a person in a way similar to how a sign reminds of the designated. When it does so, the image is phenomenologically characterized as the reminding thing, while the person is intentionally given in a second, separate (though linked) presentation. Images can also function like symbols insofar as they receive the determination, conventionally or by personal stipulation, to function as "memory-motors." In this case, images carry a "should" character: they not only bring along the presentation of the intended object but also point to it as what is meant to be intended.

## 5th Chapter: The Phantasy Appearance in Contrast to the Physically Imagistic Appearance and to the Perceptual Appearance

### §26. The Fictum and the Question of the Mode of Appearance of the "Phantasy Image"

In the physical case, the image-object is a fictum — a perception-object but a semblance-object. It appears in the manner of an actual physical thing but conflicts with the actual present constituting itself in unchallenged perception. This fictum or fiction-consciousness permeates the consciousness of representation, and thus the imaginative consciousness arises in the new conflict between fictum and *imaginatum*.

Looking at phantasy, the fictum is absent in this sense. The "phantasy image" is not an image that establishes itself in the midst of actual present-actuality. It does not appear in the form of a perceptual apprehension; it does not constitute itself as a quasi-actual thing between the phenomenal actualities of the visual field; it does not prove itself a fictum through its conflict against the unchallenged actuality of the present. How then does it appear? Does it really appear in the manner of an image? Does a phantasy really constitute an image-object through which an image-subject is viewed? Husserl confesses that here he was repeatedly seized by serious doubt. Part of the difficulty was resolved once the differences between outer and inner imagehood were separated. The phantasy appearance normally does not function in the manner of outer imagehood — but it can. Even where it does, inner imagehood is always primary. The question then is how these inner intentions are to be understood and whether they are really to be understood as image-intentions. If they are, the image-consciousness constitutes itself on a different foundation: the conflict of actual presence against the fictum inserting itself in between is absent — but must not another conflict be supposed? If nothing challenged the appearance, would it not have to count as perception?

### §27. The Phantasy Appearance: Grades and Levels of the Adequacy of the Presentation to its Object in the Case of Physical Imagehood and in Phantasy

Various grades and levels of the adequacy of the presentation to its object must be distinguished. Even in the sphere of physical images, the depiction of the image-subject by the image-object shows various levels of adequacy — regarding the extensity (the range of illustrating moments) and the intensity (the degree of primitive similarities). These gradual differences in physical imagehood do not alter the fact that the fictum appears in full force and fullness of perception. Whether the print, engraving, or drawing is excellent or poor, it brings an image-object to appearance with the same sensory force and fullness as any genuine perception. Abstracted from accompanying act-characters of validity or invalidity, there is between a painted and an actual thing in principle no difference any longer — or rather, between the thing-appearance in the one case and the other.

In phantasy, objects also stand before us in the same or very closely related sense as in perception or physical imagehood. A priori we can claim as evidence that any object that can appear in one form can appear in each of these forms. But there are in general great differences: the appearing phantasy-object does not in general appear so that we could say (setting aside the differing act-characters) that in principle there is no difference from the perceptual appearance. The phantasy-thing appears not just in a completely different world, separated from the world of actual presence, but also differs in itself: the phantasy-thing appears as something shaped, colored, etc., and yet we could not expect to find exactly such a thing among perception-objects. Strip away the characters of nothingness and imagehood from the image-object of physical imagehood, and you have a perception-object as good as any. Do the same with the phantasy-thing, and you do not.

### §28. The Protean Character of the Phantasy Appearance: the Change of Fullness, Force, and Vividness, and the Associated Change in the Adequacy of Representation

The foregoing was stated with some caution. We all distinguish between vivid, clear, firm phantasies and pale, unclear, unfathomably fleeting, airy-shadowy phantasies. Occasionally — for most persons only quite exceptionally — phantasy appearances arise in a way that approaches and even seems to approach to phenomenological equality with perceptual appearance. Whether it is really equality is hard to decide. Often phantasy objects appear like empty schemata, transparently pale, with quite unsaturated colors, deficient plasticity, often only vague and wavering contours, filled with a *je ne sais quoi*, or actually with nothing that would be interpreted as a bounding, so-and-so colored surface. In protean fashion the appearance changes: something like color and plastic form flashes up and is already gone again, and the color, even where it flashes, has something peculiarly empty, unsaturated, forceless; and similarly the form has something so vague, so shadowy, that it could not occur to us to place such things in the sphere of actual perception. These are differences that we describe with expressions from the perceptual domain and yet do not find there again; they are new differences.

Thus, while in physical imagehood the primary appearances (of the image-objects) fully have the fullness and force of perception, here with phantasy images a sphere of differences and gradual shadings opens up, concerning the fullness and vividness of the appearance, and evidently concerning corresponding differences in the apprehension-contents, the phantasms. Connected with this change of fullness and vividness is a change in the adequacy of representation in phantasy. A gradual change of adequacy in phantasy presentation occurs that is absent from physical imagehood. And at the same time we notice that in the latter the respective image is a fixed image with its level of adequacy once and for all, while here the image is something floating, wavering, changing — now increasing, now decreasing in fullness and force.

### §29. Continuity and Discontinuity in Perceptual Appearance, Physically Imagistic Appearance, and Phantasy Appearance

A second difference — absent from ordinary images — lies in the discontinuity of the sequence of appearances (not only regarding vividness), based on the same, identically maintained presentational intention, as opposed to the continuity found in the case of the physical image-appearance, which behaves in this respect exactly like perception. In short: the protean character of phantasy.

In the unity of a perception, changes in the appearance-ground are only those that change what belongs together into what belongs together. The same holds for the unity of the representative image in the physical image-presentation. All the changes that arise as our gaze glides over the image belong together; in them the relevant "side" of the object constitutes itself. Continuity and constancy of appearance.

Over against this stands the protean character of the phantasy appearance: in the unity of the phantasy presentation, the unity of the representative image is not maintained. The object appearing as image does not remain unchanged in the identical unity of the imagistic presentation directed at the same unaltered object, but changes constantly. And with it, wealth and poverty of representative moments change. The image is now a faithful representative of the object, then a less faithful one. Within a phantasy presentation, different representative objects may emerge that cannot count as alterations of one another (e.g., imagining Bismarck first in cuirassier uniform via a known image, then suddenly another image in civilian clothes, etc.). Furthermore, the intermittence of the image — its fleetingness, vanishing and returning. And the variability of individual images — the change of the image (normally occurring during any not-too-short phantasy presentation) is not to be confused with changes in the appearance moving within the synthesis of the appearance-context. In the latter case the depicting object is unchanged; in the former it is changed.

## 6th Chapter: Recapitulating Presentation of the View that Phantasy Presentation Can Be Interpreted as Imagehood-Presentation

### §30. Parallelism between Ordinary Imagination and Phantasy-Imagination

How does phantasy relate to the ordinary imaginative function? In physical imagination we distinguish the primary appearance, which is the bearer of the depiction, from the depiction itself. In the former the image-object appears; in the latter we refer to the image-subject. The parallel in phantasy is between the primary, direct appearance and the conscious reference to the phantasized object. We have here too a difference between appearance and thing, one that — both here and in ordinary imagination — is not to be confused with the difference between appearance and thing in perception (the latter referring to the object's appearing through its various sides; the former already pertaining to a single side). Furthermore, the phantasized object presents itself through the medium of the primary appearance at various levels of perfection, both in range and in degree of the primitive moments of analogization.

### §31. Strong and Flowing Differences between Ordinary Imagination and Phantasy

Differences found: (1) The phantasy appearance does not appear within the visual field of perception and is therefore not a fictum of perception. (2) In general the phantasy appearance cannot be inserted into the visual field — it has a strikingly different character from any perceptual appearance and thus also from any ordinary image-object appearance. In general there emerge: (a) inner differences regarding the presenting contents and parallelly regarding the moments of the primarily appearing object falling within the appearance — differences of forcefulness, vividness, fullness; (b) on the side of phantasy, the lack of fixity, fleetingness and constant varying of the presenting contents, not only regarding fullness but also quality and content-specific nature; (c) with this protean variability of the presenting contents, the objective appearances change in parallel, and they are typically not only variable but change abruptly — the appearances do not follow each other in the order of the synthesis of intuitional context. The object presents itself now from the front side, then suddenly from the back, now as it appeared at one time, then at a quite different, distant time. Added to this is the variability of the presenting material and the primary appearances themselves. In the protean change, a single primary object does not constantly constitute itself in the way that in physical imagehood we had a single fixed image-object.

These are strong differences, but they are flowing ones. There are also clear and firm phantasies (especially memories) that maintain empirical continuity of appearance over large stretches and that, regarding the presenting contents, approach the forcefulness, sensory freshness, or fullness of perception so closely that one can at least doubt whether any differences still remain. Why, in all cases of clear and firm memories or fictions, do their primary appearances not count as perceptions? They do not carry the consciousness of being, more particularly of being-present. On the contrary, as they stand, they count for us as not-being. Can a ground be given for this?

### §32. The Conflict-Relation of the Phantasy (or Memory) Field and the Perceptual Field, and the Fictum of Phantasy in Cases of Clear Phantasy

Would a clear and firm memory-appearance inserting itself into the visual field of perception and conflicting with its empirical demands explain the nothingness-consciousness? Then we would have a fictum of the same kind found in every ordinary image. But the clear and firm phantasy-appearance does not insert itself into the perceptual visual field — it has its own field, completely separated from that of perception. Yet a kind of conflict-relation still holds: the whole phantasy field conflicts with the whole perceptual field, without any interpenetration. When we are wholly immersed in phantasy, we do not attend to the perceptual objects, but they continuously appear and exercise their tension against the corresponding phantasy field. The tension holds between corresponding sense-fields of perception and phantasy and corresponding parts of these fields. Thus a kind of conflict determines the fictum of the phantasy. The phantasy image constitutes itself as an appearance that maintains itself for a time against the visual field of perception, but in this opposition receives the phenomenological characteristic that emerges when we return to perception and back to the image. Perception — unchallenged, uncontested from within or without — constitutes appearance of actual presence. What conflicts with it is not present. As a unity of coexistence with the present, the phantasy object is impossible, not merely objectively but phenomenologically characterized as incompatible with it. The phantasy-appearing is thus not-present. Precisely stated: the primary object of phantasy is a fictum, and thus nothing stands in the way of construing the mode of representation as normal imagination.

### §33. The Cases of Unclear Phantasies and the Question Whether Image-Object and Image-Subject May Be Distinguished at All Here. Reference to Analogous Phenomena in the Perceptual Sphere: Double Images and Rivalry of Visual Fields in Squinting

In cases of unclear phantasies, what prevents a perceptual consciousness from arising — what is the conflict with actual presence that characterizes the objective content of the immediate appearance as nothing existing for itself and thereby enables its use as image for something else? Even if, in principle, there were no difference in apprehension-material and apprehension-character relative to normal perception, differences against normal perceptual appearances would remain, and thus also against normal fictions within the perceptual domain. The fictum within actual presence is something as fixed and clearly bounded as an actual thing; in phantasy, something vague, wavering, so different in content and overall character from the normal perceptual appearance that the like could not occur within the perceptual sphere. But a doubt arises: may we distinguish image-object and image-subject at all in these quite vague appearances? Here a reference to analogous phenomena in the perceptual sphere helps: double images and rivalry of visual fields in squinting — transparent, vague, wavering appearances that count as semblance and simultaneously as analogical and symbolic pointers to certain perceptions. The image-object is here different from a normal one: it appears as a shadowy semblance, with a certain pointing toward being. Similarly with vague phantasies: something always appears — at least an outline of the object, apprehended in a manner similar to the vague, broken contours of a perceptual double-image. The interpretation goes beyond the sensed and intuitive; a certain, though imperfect, objectification takes place, and only upon it is the subject-apprehension built.

## 7th Chapter: Attempt to Establish an Essential Difference between Phantasy Presentation and Imagehood-Presentation

### §34. The Context of the Visual Field of Perceptual Consciousness and Its Foundation in the Contexts of Sensations in the Sensation Fields

Perception can pass over into fiction and physical imagehood without the underlying perceptual apprehension needing to change essentially; on the other hand, perception can often pass into phantasy-like fiction in a clear way. The starting point is the previously made observation that the phantasy-apprehension has no fictum at its foundation — no primary non-existent image-object constituting itself as it does in the physical image-apprehension within the perceptual visual field. Husserl now analyzes the visual-field context. The visual field of a temporal moment — phenomenologically, the visual field in which objectivities appear simultaneously — comprises all simultaneously coexisting appearances constituting a single context: an objective context appears in them. This context extends continuously in succession through time. The context obtaining between perceptions and constituting a unified objectivity-in-appearance has its foundation in the essential contexts of the apprehension-contents, the sensations in the sensation-fields. Sensations are not isolated in the visual field but continuously, unitarily connected, fused with one another. Similarly tactile contents in the tactile field, etc. Between sense-fields, it is the apperceptive context that establishes unity in the sensuously appearing object (simultaneously seen and touched). As far as simultaneous perception extends, so extends the intuitively-unified context of phenomenal objectivity, the unity of the intuitive, actual present.

### §35. The Relation of Phantasms and Phantasy Appearances to the Contexts of the Perceptual Field

How do phantasms and phantasy appearances relate to these contexts? Phantasms are also sensory contents, of the same genera and species as those found in sensation. We can undoubtedly experience sensations and phantasms simultaneously (e.g., reading musical notation with accompanying tone-phantasms). Do all visual contents form a single visual field, one part of which is perceptually apprehended and the other phantasy-apprehended? The answer is negative. The phantasms of the visual sense also appear in a visual field, but generally this field has no unity with the perceptual visual field. Husserl illustrates: a phantasy-appearance of the Roons (a local hill near Göttingen), as it would have presented itself perceptually from his window, belongs to a unified spread of visual contents, a phantasy-sense-field. But these sensory contents and this field lack phenomenologically all connection with his current perceptive sense-field. Correspondingly, the context of appearances and phenomenal objects is not unified between those built on sensations and those built on phantasms. The phantasy-appearing objectivity is one thing and the perceptually-appearing objectivity another; they may be linked by intentional bonds, but not by those bonds of mutual intentional belongingness that constitute a unity of intuition.

### §36. Deepened Discussion of the Question of Coexistence versus Conflict of Perceptual and Phantasy Fields, Using the Example of Individual Sense-Fields

Are the two fields — perceptual and phantasy — such peaceable coexistences as are, e.g., the perceptive visual field and tactile field? Could we have simultaneously multiple visual fields, essentially alike, distinguished merely by the fact that one bears a perceptual, the other a phantasy apprehension? Could the apprehension-type not switch? The answer reveals further differences. The perceptive visual field and tactile or auditory field coexist: the sensation-groups are separate but can be viewed together and fused in intuitive-apperceptive unities — objects appear that may contain the relevant sensations (merely interpreted) united. But the visual field of perception and the visual field of phantasy cannot be viewed together at all. Looking at the one pushes the other down, and vice versa. The situation resembles the rivalry of visual fields in stereoscopic conflict. Looking at the perceptive visual field, we have no intuition of the Roons; but if this intuition breaks through, the perceptive visual field is for the moment unoccupied.

This conflict also holds for the tactile fields in phantasy and perception, pertaining to corresponding parts of the fields. But phantasy fields of hearing do not disturb perceptual fields of touch and vice versa. The conflict apparently concerns only locality — the foundation of the objective-phenomenal spatial order. The perceptive and the imaginative visual fields have the same ordering-values, the same phenomenological place-arrangements. Simultaneously, however, two intuitions in which the local values repeat themselves cannot be brought to the unity of one intuition. There is no genuine phantasizing-into-perception as though a genuine mixture could arise. If I phantasize the white chalk as red chalk, I have for the moment a prevailing phantasy "red chalk," but immediately alternating with the perception "white chalk": both brought to the synthesis of conflict.

### §37. Whether Perception Must Not Have an Original Privilege, Since Sensations Alone Are Founders of Present-Actuality. The Difficulty Regarding the Irreal Phantasms as Present Sensory Contents. Attempt at an Answer: Imaginative Apprehension of the Phantasms Immediately Constituting a Re-Presentation Consciousness; Possibility of Subsequent Incorporation of the Phantasy Appearance and the Founding Phantasms into the Present

A further question arises. The corresponding spatial fields of perception and phantasy alternate and exclude the possibility of unity in one appearance. But why is one perception and the other phantasy? What gives the different apprehension its foothold? Must not perception have an original privilege that enables the back-reference of all objectivity to it? In the sensory contents on both sides, a phenomenological difference seems to obtain. Sensations alone have genuine actuality — present-actuality — and are the founders of genuine actuality in intentional contexts. Phantasms, by contrast, are like nothingnesses: they are irreal, counting for nothing by themselves, serving only as depicters of something else that, given, would itself be sensation.

But a great difficulty arises. The evidence of the *cogitatio* teaches that phantasies and hence phantasms are actual experiences — phantasms are in truth something present, present sensory contents, and as parts of actualities, themselves actual. One might answer: to sensations, perceptual apprehension essentially belongs. They are primarily apprehended as self-present, and upon this are built the extending empirical apprehensions constituting what is transcendently perceived. To phantasms, by contrast, imaginative apprehensions belong. These are not founded in direct perceptive apprehensions that first set the sensory content as something present and then take it as image of another. Rather, by virtue of their more or less remote similarity, phantasms immediately found an immanent re-presentation consciousness — a modified consciousness of seeing-the-intended-into-the-experienced — without the sensuously experienced first counting as something present for itself. Afterward, however, we can abstract from this character of imagination and, grasping the concrete phantasy appearance as simultaneous with a given perception, set it as a now. Only the indirect process yields here the incorporation into the present.

### §38. Characterization of the Difference of the Phantasy Apprehension from the Perceptive-Imaginative Apprehension through the Absence of the Consciousness of a Present Object That Would First Have to Function as Bearer of an Imagehood-Consciousness

In the ordinary image-apprehension (perceptive-imaginative apprehension), something appearing in the manner of perception — a phenomenally present object (even if characterized as a semblance-object) — serves as representative of another. In phantasy, we have no "present object" and in this sense no image-object. In clear phantasy, we experience phantasms and objectifying apprehensions that do not constitute anything standing there as present, which would first have to function as bearer of an imagehood-consciousness. The reference to the present is entirely absent from the appearance itself. Immediately, a seeing of the intended in the appearing takes place. In the simple phantasy experience, there is no apprehending of a "present appearance of the town hall" or of a presently self-presenting image-object.

As for unclear phantasies, they seem to demand a certain mediation: the wavering-unclear appearance points toward possible clear ones that would provide a kind of fulfillment. But true fulfillment would be the corresponding perception. In the experience itself, taken simply, the imaginative intention is carried out on the basis of the phantasms so that it has, in the similar, consciousness of the similar; where it has no analogization, that is, so to speak, a gap in the intention. Even here the possibility is given to take the phenomenon as the phenomenal appearing of a presently appearing image-object very different from the subject. But the consciousness of a present is entirely absent, and thus also the mediation. The illustrating moments carry the imagination; the rest are not determinate moments counting as determinate but "indeterminacies" that do not always conflict with the intention, and thus give no distinct image-object consciousness. Alternatively, a consciousness of an image-object may actually be enacted, only the image-object appears not as present but itself already as image — an imaginative image-object parallel to the perceptive image-object of perception.

### §39. Consequence of the Attempted View: No Direct Imaginative Consciousness within the Sphere of Perception, and Establishment of an Original Phenomenological Distinction between Sensations and Phantasms. Reference to the Belief-Character and the Division of Phantasy Presentations into Mere Presentations and Memories

In the consequence of the attempted view: within the sphere of perception, there would be no direct imaginative consciousness of the kind just described for phantasy. If an imaginative consciousness is enacted on the basis of sensations, this occurs by the mediation of perceptive apprehensions constituting a present, a presently standing image-object. Why? The sensation resists, as it were, the demand to count as mere image for something else. It is itself the stamp of actuality; all actuality measures itself against it; it is primary, actual presence. But while it lets a presence appear, it can simultaneously direct consciousness toward the analogous and permit a different, non-present thing to be seen in it. The phantasm, by contrast, the sensory content of phantasy, gives itself as non-present, resists the demand to be taken for present, carries from the outset the character of irreality, and has primarily the function of counting for something else. Only indirect reflection lends it an acquired presence.

We thus arrive at the establishment of an original phenomenological distinction between sensation and phantasm (impression and idea), and upon this distinction is originally built the distinction perception–phantasy presentation. To sensation, perception essentially belongs; all development of transcendent perceptions retains the common feature that it presupposes the core of sensation as what makes perception possible. Perception gives the actual present, the primary, the intuited. Unchallenged perception is belief — originally intuitive belief that phenomenally constitutes the truly present as such. Challenged presence, i.e., the conflict of a present-appearance against an unchallenged one, yields intuitive semblance.

That belief does not constitute the distinguishing mark of perception hardly needs saying. Phantasy presentations divide into mere presentations and memories. The latter are likewise distinguished by belief. In memory, an objectivity appears intuitively, but nothing of it is given in the primary sense. While in perception the appearing side is the actually present part of the thing, in memory it is only the actually remembered part. The rest of the object is in both cases co-apprehended.

## 8th Chapter: Results and Anticipatory View of the Analyses of Time-Consciousness

### §40. Determination of the Essential Difference between Imagination in the Proper Sense (Perceptive Imagination) and Imagination as Phantasy

Husserl recapitulates the results. (1) Imagination in the proper sense (e.g., physical imagehood) consists in an appearing object counting as a copy (*Abbild*) for another, similar or alike object. In the case of a physical image, the appearing object constitutes itself in a perception: an object appearing as present functions as image-representative for a non-present one. Here, multiple apprehensions interpenetrate — quite similarly to the signifying/symbolizing function: the symbol appears for itself but is the bearer of a reference to another. In the proper image-function, the "image" is constituted in its own objective apprehension and is the bearer of a reference to the depicted. Important differences emerged between the symbolizing function and the image-depicting function: the symbolizing function is an outwardly presenting one, the imaging function an inwardly depicting one — seeing the thing into the image. In every image-representation, we distinguish the bearers of the consciousness of depiction (which must always include the plastic form for a thingly depiction) from the moments that remain external to this consciousness.

(2) Imagination as phantasy is sharply separated from the proper image-function by the fact that it lacks a separately constituting image-object — and especially an image-object appearing as present. In phantasy there is no viewing of the subject into a presently appearing image-object, no outward depicting of the subject through such an object, no symbolizing by remote similarity. In the phantasy presentation we have an appearance of an object, but no appearance of a present thing by means of which an appearance of a non-present thing would come about.

### §41. Distinction of the Simple Phantasy Presentation and the Imagistically Mediated One; Simple Phantasy Presentation as Presupposition of the Genuine Imaginative Function in Phantasy

Two cases must now be distinguished: (1) the simple phantasy presentation and (2) the imagistically mediated one. In the latter, the presentation relates to the object mediately, through an image-presentation, so that an image-consciousness constitutes itself analogously to the physical image-function. In the simple phantasy presentation, this is not the case. In the imagistic phantasy, two presentational functions are built on one another and related through imagehood-reference: the founding one is a phantasy presentation, which phantasy-wise constitutes an object that is then equipped with an imaginative function (e.g., a geologist forming an intuitive conception of a prehistoric animal species on the basis of a few features suggested by fossils). The genuine imaginative function in phantasy presupposes a phantasy presentation that is not itself imaginative in the same sense — we are thus referred to simple phantasy presentations: just as perceptual imagehood is founded in perception, phantasy-imagehood is founded in phantasy that is not itself already imagehood.

### §42. Circumscription of the Concept of Simple Phantasy Presentation as Enactment of Pure Re-Presentation Consciousness; Immanent Image-Consciousness as Phantasy-Consciousness. Terminological Fixing of the Oppositions Perception–Phantasy, or Presentation (Presentation) – Re-Presentation (Representation)

How are simple phantasy presentations to be understood? When our phantasy busies itself playfully with angels and devils, dwarves and water-nymphs, or when our memory transports us into the past parading in intuitive configurations before our mind, the appearing objectivities do not count as image-objects, as mere representatives, analogues, images for others. While with genuine images a looking-outward, a being-pointed-to-something-else, is possible and takes place, here this has, strictly considered, no sense at all. The word "imagination," the talk of phantasy-images, etc. must no more deceive us here than, in perception, the talk of "perception-images." These expressions stem from a reflection that opposes the appearances of phantasy to the possible perceptions of the same objectivity, and again the perceptions to the "things-in-themselves" not given perceptually.

The phantasy-appearance, the simple one unburdened by any imagehood built upon it, refers just as simply (*einfältig*) to the object as does perception. Of clear phantasies, what holds is that on the basis of the phantasms and the objectifying apprehension, a pure re-presentation consciousness is enacted. The phantasms are objectified, and there constitutes itself not first a hovering and even presently-appearing image-object; rather, the appearing is immediately the non-present. The objective intention, directed at the phantasized object, has its fullness in the experienced phantasms, just as the objective intention of perception has its fullness in sensations. This does not exclude that phantasy has an essential relation to perception — that it confirms itself in the case of identification with a corresponding perception — and that the consciousness arises: the phantasized is merely a re-presentation of what is truly given in perception, and so in a certain sense phantasy gives a mere image of the perceptual objectivity. But in itself the phantasy presentation contains no manifold intention. Re-presentation (*Vergegenwärtigung*) is a final mode of intuitive presentation, just as perceptual presentation, as presentification (*Gegenwärtigung*), is.

What temporarily disturbed and misled us was the evident inner kinship of immanent, inward-turned image-consciousness with phantasy-consciousness. In fact, the consciousness is in essence the same on both sides: this immanent image-consciousness is phantasy-consciousness. But it interpenetrates here with a presentative consciousness. The same sensory contents, the same sensations, are simultaneously apprehended as the image-object and simultaneously serve, wholly or at least in their core, as bearers of a phantasy-consciousness. Perception has built upon it a phantasy-consciousness — but this is possible only by virtue of the discussed conflicts that cancel the presentifying function of the sensations. Where conflict is absent, sensation is always objectified and characterized as presence, which evidently excludes the "non-present" of phantasy. Although one speaks in a certain good sense of imagehood in phantasy, and although in ordinary imagehood phantasy constitutes the most essential moment, it seems most appropriate to speak of "imagehood," "imaging apprehension" only where an image appears that first functions as a representing object for something depicted. In simple phantasy, where that does not take place, it is best to use a different terminus. Either we use the word "phantasy" itself, or the word "re-presentation" (*Vergegenwärtigung*). Perception is thus opposed to phantasy, or presentification (*Gegenwärtigung*, presentation) to re-presentation (*Vergegenwärtigung*, representation). Where any confusion with imagistic and signitive presentation is possible, one must say precisely: genuine re-presentation, simple re-presentation, as opposed to imagistic, symbolic, signitive, improper re-presentation.

### §43. The Situation with Unclear Phantasies: the Simple Phantasy Presentation in Any Case Presupposed. Concluding Overview of the Presentational Modes Emerging from the Analyses

Husserl has so far spoken only of "clear phantasies." The unclear ones caused him considerable unease. Here the not merely wavering and fleeting but also content-wise very inadequate "image" deviates far from the phantasized object. But he has finally come to agree with himself that the construal of these phenomena as genuine imagehoods would not help. If the unclear phantasies are constituted on the basis of an imagehood, the primary image-object is already a phantasy-object. And so the pure and simple phantasy-function is in any case presupposed. Our analysis can thus gain nothing principially new; it is a mere question of fact.

Husserl states his view, based on manifold observation, that the presentation is normally not a mediated one. The intention directed at the object, when the phantasy is at least relatively and partially clear, has in the representing features (corresponding to the depicting moments in mediated image-consciousness) a hold, a fullness; the remaining features do not count, are a nothing. The difference between the intended object and what is given in the phantasm and receives objectification does not lead to conflict-consciousness or to the separation of the two objects. In the physical image-object, sensation prevails everywhere — wherever sensation is present, a closed objectification and hence a graspably and firmly constituted image-object. But in phantasy, the image-object mostly does not constitute itself, despite the difference. We then have no genuine intuition of the object — not a merely empty intention, but also no full intuition; rather, a beginning of intuition, a shadow of intuition in place of intuition itself. In very dark phantasies, re-presentation reduces to a quite meager remainder; if this wholly vanishes (as when phantasms intermit), the determinate but empty intention directed at the object remains. With the meager remnants that surface again, the intention strengthens and fills itself in this or that respect. But it becomes genuine intuition only when a rich image is given. The gaps, the dissolving colorings, the sinking into the light-dust of the phantasy-visual-field, etc. — such things are objectified only if we choose to interpret them by analogy with actual objectivity.

A double objectivity immediately sets in when a clear and firm phantasy-image emerges that partially covers the phantasy-intention but clearly deviates from it at certain points. The memory may retroactively modify the intention and produce the conflict (e.g., a clear memory-image gives an intention directed at friend X intuitiveness, but the image initially brings a black full beard, while the intention demands a brown one — normally the image will not stand firm and will modify itself accordingly).

Through our analyses, the following primitive presentational modes emerge: (1) two simple modes of genuine presentation — perception and re-presentation (*Repräsentation*); (2) a simple mode of improper presentation — empty intentions; (3) the founded presentational modes, built upon the simple intuitive or empty intentions. The various primitive forms of founding would still need study. Already distinguished: imagistic presentations, symbolic presentations through similarity, and symbolic presentations through mere signification (without analogizing relation). More closely analyzed were the imagistic ones, in which perception and phantasy, or phantasy and phantasy, interpenetrate and found a depicting intention.

### §44. Separation of a New Concept of Appearance with Regard to the Consciousness-Character of Presentification versus Re-Presentation as What Distinguishes Perception from Phantasy. Indication of the Transition to the Analyses of Time-Consciousness for the More Precise Distinction of the Differences in Perceptual and Phantasy Consciousness

So far, Husserl says, we may rejoice in clear results. But a continuation is needed, which will very soon lead into the sphere of the more precise distinction of the various differences in perceptual and phantasy consciousness — and that means above all into the forms of time-consciousness.

One thing must first be discussed. When we re-present (*vergegenwärtigen*) any object-like in phantasy, it presents itself in a determinate appearance that exactly corresponds to a determinate appearance of a possible perception. The synthesis of the possible perceptual context corresponds exactly to the synthesis of a possible phantasy-context, referred to the unity of the same object. The same object presents itself from the same side with the same phenomenal determinacies — the same colors, brightness-gradations, perspectival adumbrations, etc. — in short, with "the same appearance" in a presentative and re-presentative manner. This same appearance means, in the experiences, naturally something identical; likewise something identical corresponds to the intentional consciousness directed on both sides at the same object. But here and there the identical is not the same. The reference to the object is a matter of the apprehension-sense. But what was here taken as identical under the title "appearance" does not concern the bare apprehension-sense — that would be the same even if we were not dealing on both sides with the exactly corresponding members of the objective synthesis. Nor is appearance in the present sense quite the same as what was designated as the pure perceptual content in the *Logical Investigations*, nor as what was designated as appearance in yet another sense. Only in the identity that here emerges between perceptual presentation and phantasy presentation does the new concept of appearance separate itself out. Appearance is not perceptual presentation (i.e., perception under abstraction from the belief-moment); nor is it the pure perceptual presentation remaining after abstraction from the symbolic components. For what is at issue is something that occurs equally in the perceptual and in the phantasy presentation and is or can be identical on both sides. It is clear what must be abstracted from: on the one hand, from what in the apprehension characterizes the appearance precisely as presentification; on the other, from what characterizes it as re-presentation.

It seems, then, that we must think the constitution of simple genuine presentations as follows: the sensory contents that undergo apprehension do so in an apprehension-sense that gives them reference to the respective object. But this happens in such a way that with the same apprehension-sense manifold possibilities obtain. The apprehension-sense is an abstractum that particularizes itself in the form of an appearance. In the appearance, the object is not merely intuitive as so-and-so determined; it is and can only be this by virtue of the fact that this or that side of the object comes to appearance. Appearance here means the determinate particularization of the apprehension-sense in unity with the apprehension-contents. The appearance finally still bears a certain consciousness-character that first constitutes what distinguishes perception from phantasy: the appearance is either a presentifying or a re-presentifying appearance — i.e., it receives yet a character that marks it as the one or the other.

We can also say: an objectivation is enacted in the simple intuitive acts, one that initially contains nothing of the characterization as present or non-present (phantasized, past, future, etc.); rather, this characterization first comes in addition. Admittedly, this first objectivation is not something that can be for itself, since evidently what appears is either phenomenally present or not present.

The relationship of presentification and re-presentation, and the question whether these are co-equal characters — so to speak, two mere colorings only specifically distinguished — still poses very remarkable difficulties, as we shall hear. But in any case, with our distinctions a first approximation has been accomplished that gives the truth a first and provisionally acceptable expression. The resolution of the difficulties here only indicated will have to form a main piece of the analysis of time-consciousness.

## 9th Chapter: The Question of the Phenomenological Difference between Sensation and Phantasm, and the Question of the Relationship between Perception and Phantasy

### §45. Connection to Brentano's Position: No Essential Differences between the Apprehension-Contents — Sensation and Phantasm

Before proceeding, Husserl says he must add something more closely pertaining to the sphere of the problems so far treated: a gap has remained in the presentation, for the question of the phenomenological difference between sensation and phantasm has not been thoroughly entered into. The investigations carried out by various researchers always treated this question mixed together with the question of the relationship of perception and phantasy. But however intimately the two hang together, clear separation is the precondition for successful treatment. The most detailed discussion of the first question known to Husserl was given by Brentano in his lectures. It ends with the rejection of essential differences between sensations and phantasms: on both sides they are in essence the same sensory contents, separated by no abysses, not divided by any moment of fundamentally different genus. All differences occurring here are rather continuously mediated. Primarily they are intensity-differences: phantasms are sensory contents of extraordinarily low intensity compared with normal sensations. Besides intensity, other relative differences come into consideration — especially fleetingness, voluntary variability, etc. These differences in their complex provide sufficient empirical grounds for the attachment of the different apprehensions and prevent us from switching at will between perceptual and phantasy apprehension. As for the difference of these apprehensions themselves, Brentano locates it in the claim that perceptions are genuine (*eigentliche*) presentations while phantasy presentations are improper (*uneigentliche*) — i.e., indirect, conceptually mediated presentations. But Brentano did not carry out a deeper-penetrating phenomenology of the apprehensions on either side.

Brentano's main grounds: the vividness of phantasms intensifies until they pass into sensations and lead to perceptual illusions; conversely, sensations can become so weak that we waver over whether we still sense or merely phantasize (e.g., listening in late evening for the clock-stroke and, deceived by anticipatory expectation, believing we hear it yet doubting). Methodically, one may assert: as long as we can manage without invoking a fundamentally essential difference between sensation and phantasm, we should refrain from positing one — for the direct comparison between the two, though open to us at every moment, fails. Given the fleetingness and variability of phantasms and the difficulty of abstracting from the apperceptions that give them and sensations their significance, one arrives at no firm result.

### §46. Locating the Difference between Sensation and Phantasm in the Modes of Apprehension. Discussion of an Inadequacy of This Theory in Brentano and Others: the Interpretation of Humean Vivacity as Intensity

In the different apperception-mode — in the phenomenological characters studied by Husserl that build upon the sensory contents — we do have a means of distinction. The real difference would then lie in the modes of apprehension, not in the apprehension-contents. Depending on circumstances, the same content could be called sensation in one case and phantasm in another. Empirical-psychological grounds would have to account for why a determinate mode of apprehension comes about. One might concede that sensory contents fall by and large into two groups — the one relatively very vivid and intense, the other widely separated in intensity — with mediating contents not entirely absent but with interwoven moments mostly sufficing to fix a determinate apperception-mode.

Husserl himself had long preferred this view. But recently he has become more uncertain. What displeases him in Brentano and others is the interpretation of Humean vivacity (*vivacity*) as intensity. Certainly, very soft and weak sensations often surrogate for loud, intensive ones — a determinate melody can be an imaging presentation of a loud one while actually having very soft tones as its foundation. But whether phantasizing a melody can be understood on this schema, especially where we have clear and fully vivid phantasies, is questionable. And in many sense-domains — already in the domain of color — we would need special theories of intensity. What is intensity in the color-domain? Not brightness. We would have to define the peculiarly changing force and vividness of phantasms precisely as intensity.

The case of phantasy presentations of psychical phenomena raises the difficulty even more sharply. Just as we can perceive and phantasize physical things, so too we can inwardly perceive and genuinely experience psychical states — judgments, doubts, volitions — and at other times merely phantasize them. What makes the difference here? We must distinguish apprehension-contents from apprehension-acts, and the same inner appearance must present itself as present in one case, as non-present in the other. The general difference is evidently the same on both sides. An actually rendered judgment "feels" more vivid than a merely imagined one. But for many of these phenomena, talk of intensity in the same sense as with sensations makes no sense. What is a more intensive judgment? Surely not a more vivid conviction — for then a phantasized judgment would be a weakly vivid conviction. Conviction-grades evidently have nothing to do with the difference between actual experience and mere phantasy. Perhaps a different kind of distinction comes into consideration, one that is an analogue of intensity.

### §47. The Difficulty of Understanding How the Difference between Phantasy of a Psychical Act and Actual Enactment of That Act Is Possible. The Moment of Belief and the Improperness of Presenting

The persistent search for abysmal differences between sensation and phantasm stems from the feeling that a thoroughgoing difference between perception and phantasy must be produced, for which a thoroughgoing difference between the apprehension-contents recommends itself most readily. But one does not escape the difficulties at the level of intentional phenomena by positing differences in the apprehension-contents.

The difficulty is this: when I phantasize a color, the color-phantasm and the experienced color show generic kinship — both are color, and like is represented by like. Now take psychical acts: I present a judgment, a volition. If I can do so intuitively, I have a judgment-phantasm that corresponds to the judgment-sensation (the actually enacted judging) as something generically alike. Thus the qualitative, what characterizes a judgment as judgment, as well as the entire really experienced judgment-content, I find in the phantasm. So do we actually judge when we present a judgment? Do we actually will when we merely imagine a willing?

The great difficulty is to understand how the difference between phantasy of a judgment and actual enactment of a judgment is possible — a difference that is yet so palpable and evident that no one could ever doubt its existence. This evidence tells us with absolute clarity: actually to judge, actually to will, really to wish, really to be angry, etc., is something other than presenting an anger, a wish, a willing, a judging. And these latter experiences are not complexes that include the former within themselves. To present a judgment is not to judge and something besides.

Neither gradual differences nor abysmal ones can help. When we re-present in phantasy a long-corrected error, we do not now err, not even in the slightest degree. If one says it is not a matter of magnitude of error but a phenomenological gradation representing an analogue of intensity, the reply is: whether the judgment is vivid or less vivid, rich or poor in fullness — if it is a judgment at all, then we believe, and we would actually err every time we imagined an error in phantasy. In truth, when we remember a former error, we err only if we not merely remember but still believe the relevant state of affairs, while — having meanwhile been taught better — we still enact the re-presentation and memory but no longer the belief. The example brings the distinction out with special clarity. If the memory of the former belief contained this belief itself — if the belief-phantasm, as content-wise generically alike the belief-sensation, were to be equated with an actual belief — then the distinction between memory of a belief with actual belief and memory without sharing the belief would have no sense.

Nor is the difficulty removed by assuming an abysmal difference between the apprehension-contents of perception and phantasy. This could only mean that some moment of the experience on the one side flips over into a totally different one on the other side. But the content-wise kinship must persist, for otherwise the phantasy-image would not be an image of this judgment. The judgment in all its essential components — above all its belief-moment and its reference to the relevant state of affairs — must recur in the phantasy-image. Only if one were to posit improperness (*Uneigentlichkeit*) of presenting with respect to precisely this moment could one get around the difficulty — but then the re-presentation would be no clear and genuinely intuitive one, and we would have to deny that a judgment can be fully re-presented in the sense in which a thing, a color, a tone, a melody can be genuinely re-presented.

Of course, improperness does play a large role. It does so already in perception: I can perceive someone else's anger without being angry in the least. All psychical moments belonging to personality as such are indirectly inserted, mostly through empty intentions melted into the unity of perception. But we will not say that acts can only be re-presented through empty intentions and that there is no genuine intuition of them.

### §48. Resolution of the Difficulty: Grounding the Difference between Perceptual and Phantasy Apprehension through the Addition of the Consciousness-Characterization as "Present" versus "Re-Presented"

There is, according to Husserl's analyses, only one way out, and it is probably the only thinkable one. The difference between perceptual and phantasy apprehension is not a mere difference of two genera or classes of contents and cannot be. For everything generic and differentiating down to the last difference can be present in the mode of perception and phantasy alike. They are differences of consciousness. The difference lies not in the objectivation common to both sides, in which the "appearance" of the object is accomplished, but in that characterization that constitutes the difference between present and re-presented.

Two cases are thinkable: (1) The difference of the characterized apprehension has no essential relation to the contents per se — i.e., in principle exactly the same experiential content could undergo the one and the other apprehension, and the factual apprehension would be determined not by phenomenological features but only by psychological grounds. (2) In the phenomenological essence of an experience, its characterized apprehension as a present or presentant of a present, and as a non-present or presentant of a non-present, is prefigured.

Under the first supposition: the actual judgment that we inwardly perceive and the same judgment that we remember or merely imagine are distinguished by the characterization. In general, other differences will also obtain, but in the limiting case of a really full and clear re-presentation, such differences (at least relevant ones) need not obtain. Nonetheless we do not say, in the case of phantasy, that we actually judge. The mere being-there of an experience with all the determinacies belonging to the peculiar essence of a judgment does not yet fully constitute what we call actual judging. For actual presence, the relevant psychical content must be experienced in presence-consciousness, not quasi-depicted in phantasy-consciousness. The one consciousness gives the content the credit of the actual; the other robs it of this credit and gives it the character of the merely re-presented. This characterization does not belong to the conceptual essence of an object, nor to that of a judgment. The objecthood constitutes itself in the appearance independently of the characterization. This essential core is common to perception and clear phantasy. But it is important to note that not every experience containing all moments of this essential core is already a judgment. For judgment, simply speaking, means actual judgment — enacted, present in the relevant consciousness. Thus the full concretion of a consciousness that gives reality requires more than the conceptual essence: it requires also the characterization of consciousness that establishes real present-being, or else the modifying characterization as non-present must obtain, and the appearing is then irreal.

### §49. New Difficulties Regarding the Actually Present Acts and the Question of Inner Perceivedness, or the Modification of the Discrediting Phantasy Re-Presentation

But now a severe difficulty arises. Is the characterization as present not itself a consciousness-moment, and is the concretum produced by it not itself again a present? Thus we would arrive at a second-order characterization and an infinite regress. Closely related is the difficulty that we set actual judging over against phantasizing of a judgment, but then treated the difference between a perception of the judgment and a phantasizing of the judgment — tacitly treating the actually present psychical acts as inwardly perceived. But perceptions themselves are acts, and present acts. Are they present only on the ground of a second-order perceiving? And so *in infinitum*.

The difficulties would vanish if we resolved to say: the present constitutes itself intuitively in perceiving, as primarily and actually given present. The ideal possibility of the perceptual apprehension extends far beyond actual perceptual apprehension — it reaches as far as consciousness. Every concrete experience is *eo ipso* present, i.e., it can ideally be perceived. But ideally every concrete experience can also undergo a modification — in an apprehending that grasps it as re-presentation. Thereby it is as it were discredited: it no longer counts for itself as present but as re-presentation of something other. The re-presentation itself is then again a present. When we judge, a judgment-consciousness refers to a state of affairs. Of this judgment-consciousness we do not by virtue of a directed act have an inner perception, but we can have one — in "reflection." We actually judge as long as nothing further takes place than that simple belief-consciousness. We still judge when we perceivingly glance at this consciousness — perceiving does not modify; on the contrary, in it the actual "being-there" constitutes itself intuitively. But as soon as we behave in a phantasizing manner, taking the judgment-consciousness as representative for an equal judgment-consciousness, the judgment is no longer an "actual" judgment but a judgment-representative.

### §50. Cases Where Remembered and Actual Psychical Acts Are Referred to the Same Presentational Basis

What about cases where we remember a past joy and simultaneously actually rejoice over the same thing, or remember a past conviction and still share it? These are double-phenomena that cover each other but must nevertheless be understood as double. The opposite cases make this clear: we remember our past joy over a party's victory while now rather regretting it; we remember a past conviction we no longer share. Then the modified consciousness of joy is linked with the actual consciousness of displeasure, the modified consciousness of belief with actual unbelief — always referred to the same presentational basis. Were all these unmodified experiences, such combinations could not be produced (presumably on the basis of essential laws): a joy over A and a displeasure over A, over the same object in the same respect, exclude each other. But the modification of an act does not at all disturb the actual enactment of the unmodified opposite — both lie, so to speak, in different dimensions. The conceptually identical phenomena give no intuitive beside-each-other, but they give, by virtue of the difference of apprehensions, a duality in covering overlap.

### §51. Toward the Clarification of the Overall View of Perception versus Phantasy: Either the Positing of Representation as Modifying Character and Presentation as the Correspondingly Unmodified

Nothing would be changed in the overall view. Presentification and re-presentation, we said, is the fundamental difference. We now clarify it by seeing in re-presentation a modifying character and in presentation the correspondingly unmodified. Perception takes the appearing as self-being — it does not modify, does not imagine. The same appearance can underlie a re-presentation consciousness — that is the modification. But this must not be understood as though the appearing were first given un-modified and then the modification enters, imagistically reinterpreting the presently given into a non-given. That would not work — it is excluded by our analyses of phantasy. The phantasm is an experience, but not one first taken as present, as self, and then taken for another. We take the phantasm for a present only because it is a component of the phantasy presentation, which for its part is a present. "Unmodified" here means everything that does not function as a phantasm in a phantasy.

### §52. Or: the Positing of Two Co-Equal Apprehensions — Presentification and Re-Presentation — and Correspondingly of Two Intrinsically Different Apprehension-Contents: Sensation and Phantasm

The other way of arranging the matter consists in positing two co-equal apprehensions or characterizing modes as presentification and re-presentation, and correspondingly also two ways in which generically equal contents can be realized in consciousness. Sensation and the corresponding phantasm are, apart from the different modes of apprehension, intrinsically already differently characterized, notwithstanding their content-wise commonalities. It then belongs to the essence of the phantasm that it can function only representatively.

When we judge, the judging is generally not perceived. But it is sensed (*empfunden*). When we imagine a judgment, the judgment-experience is not sensation but phantasm. Judgment-sensation and judgment-phantasm differ in the same essential moment that separates sensation and phantasm in general. Likewise, when we perceive, we do not again perceive the perceiving. But perceiving is an experience, an experience of the sensation-type. If, however, we present a perceiving to ourselves in phantasy, the present "image" of it is a phantasm of a perceiving, not a sensation-experience of a perceiving. Whether this view can be carried through remains to be seen.
