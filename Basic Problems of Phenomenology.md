# **Husserl's *Grundprobleme der Phänomenologie* (1910/11)**

## **Lectures from the Winter Semester 1910/11**

---

## **CHAPTER I**

### **Die natürliche Einstellung und der „natürliche Weltbegriff"**

*(The Natural Attitude and the "Natural Concept of the World")*

**§1. Das Ich in der natürlichen Einstellung (The I in the Natural Attitude)**

**1\. Opening: The task of the lectures.** Husserl announces the subject-matter: fundamental problems of a general phenomenology of consciousness. The aim is to study the basic constitution (*Grundverfassung*) of consciousness as such.

**2\. Phenomenology requires a new dimension.** The investigations demand a totally different attitude (*Einstellung*) from the natural one within which natural-scientific and psychological knowledge is won. Phenomenology is by no means psychology; it lies in a new dimension and requires a fundamentally different attitude from that of any science of spatiotemporal existence. To make this clear, an introduction is needed, beginning with a description of the various attitudes in which experience and knowledge can occur — first of all the natural attitude in which we all live and from which we set out when we perform the philosophical change of regard.

**Key distinction: natural attitude vs. phenomenological attitude.** The natural attitude is the one in which we ordinarily live; the phenomenological attitude requires a fundamentally different orientation from every science of spatiotemporal existence.

**3\. The I as centre of an environment.** Each of us says "I" and knows himself thereby as an I. The I finds itself as the centre of an environment (*Umgebung*). "I" means for each of us something different — the particular person with a particular name, who has perceptions, memories, expectations, feelings, wishes, volitions, dispositions, abilities, etc. Each I has its own, and the very finding-oneself (*Vorfinden*) in which the I finds all this belongs itself to this circle. On the basis of experience and convictions the I predicates of itself that it is such-and-such a person with such-and-such properties, experiences, aims, etc.

**4\. The I is not identical with its experiences.** While the I finds itself as the one who "has" all that is predicable of it, it finds itself on the other hand as not something homogeneous with what is had. The I itself is not an experience (*Erlebnis*) but the one who experiences, not an act but the one who performs the act, not a character-trait but the one who has the character-trait as a property. Furthermore, the I finds itself and its experiences in time, not only as now existing and having this and that, but also through memory as the same I who previously had other experiences. Every experience has its time-position, and the I itself is an identical something in time.

**Key distinction: the I vs. its experiences.** The I is the subject that has and performs experiences; it is not itself an experience or act.

**§2. Der Leib und die raumzeitliche Umgebung (The Body and the Spatiotemporal Environment)**

**5\. The body as thing among things.** Each I finds itself as having an organic body (*Leib*). The body is not an I; it is a spatiotemporal "thing" around which an unlimited thing-environment groups itself. Each I has a limited spatiotemporal environment that it immediately perceives or retains in retentional memory.

**6\. The world beyond immediate perception.** Each I "knows" — is certain — that the immediately intuited environment is only a perceived piece of a total environment and that things in infinite (Euclidean) space continue further. The I knows that things are not only when they are perceived; things that are, are in themselves, even when not present in the actual field of experience.

**7\. Description of general certainties.** Husserl pauses to clarify the method of the description: what is being described is what every I finds — what it directly sees or indirectly holds in certainty — and this certainty is such that every I can transform it into absolute evidence. Each I knows it can err in singular cases, yet the general content stated is evident. The describing itself involves no commitment to the ultimate truth of the matters described, nor does it express doubt.

**8\. The body as Eigenleib.** The thing that each I finds as "its body" stands out from all other things as the body proper (*Eigenleib*). It is always and unavoidably present in the perceptual sphere, perceived in a peculiar way, and is the constant central member of the surrounding-thing-apprehension. Everything that is not the body appears oriented with respect to the body — as right and left, front and back, near and far — and temporally as now, just-before, and after.

**§3. Die Lokalisation der Erlebnisse im Leibe (The Localisation of Experiences in the Body)**

**9\. Psychic localisation differs from physical localisation.** Everyone relates their experiences and their specific I-possessions to the body: they localise them in the body. But this localisation is of a quite peculiar kind, entirely different from the way physical parts and moments are localised in a thing. Joy and sorrow are not in the heart as blood is in the heart; tactile sensations are not in the skin as pieces of its organic tissue are. Experiences are acknowledged, on the basis of experience, as in a certain not precisely determined extent dependent on the body.

**Key distinction: psychic localisation vs. physical localisation.** Experiences are related to the body, but in a fundamentally different way from how physical parts belong to things.

**§4. Die Einfühlung und das fremde Ich (Empathy and the Foreign I)**

**10\. The foreign I through empathy.** Each I finds in its environment things it regards as foreign bodies, each belonging to a foreign I. It does not "see" the foreign I in the same way it sees itself by self-experience; rather, it posits them in the mode of "empathy" (*Einfühlung*). The foreign I is posited as likewise having its soul, its actual consciousness, its dispositional character-traits, its body-environment — and crucially, the environment that the foreign I finds is by and large the same as our own.

**11\. All I's as relative centres of one world.** All I's apprehend themselves as relative centres (*Mittelpunkte*) of one and the same spatiotemporal world, which in its indeterminate infinity is the total environment of every I. For each I, the other I's are not centres but environment-points (*Umgebungspunkte*), having a different spatial and temporal position in the one universal space and the one world-time.

**Key distinction: Mittelpunkt vs. Umgebungspunkt.** Each I is originarily given to itself as the centre of orientation, while other I's are given as points within its environment.

**§5. Das Raumphänomen und die Entsprechung der Erscheinungen (The Spatial Phenomenon and the Correspondence of Appearances)**

**12\. Objective space vs. the spatial phenomenon.** Each I finds itself as the zero-point (*Nullpunkt*) of a coordinate system from which it regards all things. Each distinguishes the objective space as a system of objective positions from the spatial phenomenon — the way space appears with "here and there," "front and back," "right and left." The same applies to time.

**13\. Normality and the correlation of appearances.** Each I has a different appearance of the same thing depending on its spatial position. When two bodies exchange places, the appearances belonging to the respective I's ideally exchange as well. This holds under a certain ideal normality: under ideal conditions, two normal individuals at the same position would have exactly the same appearances. Deviations fall under the title "normal and anomalous perceiving" and are traced back to different functioning of the body.

**14\. Verständigung (mutual understanding).** The I's communicate with one another. Each makes experiences of things, judges on the basis of these experiences, and exchanges these judgements in mutual understanding. When one is directed straightforwardly toward the experienced object, one judges not about the appearances but about the things, describing the same unchanged thing even while one constantly has different appearances — near-appearances, far-appearances, front-views, rear-views, etc.

**§6. Rekapitulation der vorangegangenen Ausführungen (Recapitulation)**

**15–16. Recapitulation.** Husserl recapitulates the previous lectures. Each I posits itself as existing and as experiencing such-and-such, as the subject of personal properties. The I finds itself in time and through memory as the same I that was. It has a body as a thing among things, an environment partly immediate and partly mediate (co-posited as an infinite, indeterminate thingly environment). He then recapitulates the descriptions of the body as Eigenleib, of foreign bodies as bearers of foreign I's known through empathy rather than self-perception, and of the differences in orientation and the ideal correspondence of appearances under normality.

**§7. Die natürliche Einstellung als die Einstellung der Erfahrung (The Natural Attitude as the Attitude of Experience)**

**17\. The natural attitude is the attitude of experience.** What was designated under the title "finding" (*Vorfinden*), prior to all inferring, is nothing other than what is called "experience" in the pregnant sense. The natural attitude is therefore the attitude of experience. It is the exclusive attitude of the animal and of pre-scientific humanity.

**18\. Evidenz of descriptive judgements vs. Evidenz of the experience-thesis.** The purely descriptive judgements that express what is found in experience have a certain absolute evidence — namely, evidence as mere expressions of the experienced. But what is experienced is posited as *being*, and the judgement carries the evidence of the *experience-thesis* (*Erfahrungsthesis*), which is, generally speaking, an imperfect evidence. Everyone knows that "experience can deceive." Yet we also have an absolutely evident certainty that I am and that a world is, and that general types of findings belong to the I — even if error is possible in the particular. The character of this evidence is left undecided.

**Key distinction: evidence of description vs. evidence of the experience-thesis.** Descriptive evidence (the cogitatio faithfully expressed) is absolute; but the existential thesis of experience is imperfect, open to revision.

**19\. The right of experience.** Experience has its right; judging on the basis of experience has its self-evident right: first the simply descriptive judging, then the inductive scientific judging of descriptive sciences, and finally the judgement of the exact objective sciences, which goes beyond what is immediately experienced and infers what is not experienced, but always remains referred back to its ultimate source of right: immediate experience-givenness.

**§8. Die Erfahrungswissenschaften (The Sciences of Experience)**

**20\. Physical natural science and psychology.** (a) The scientific investigation of things is the business of physical natural science. Things are given as existing in themselves in objective space and objective time. Crucially, things are not appearances but the identical something that appears in manifold appearances. (b) Human beings also gain psychological knowledge through self-perception, self-memory, and empathic experience. Psychology describes and causally explains human personalities with their changing states, acts, and dispositions — not the appearances in which they appear to themselves and others.

**21\. All appearances fall within psychology.** Though the description of how someone appears is different from the description of the person itself, the consciousness in which I am object to myself is an I-experience, and likewise the consciousness in which another stands before me. Things are not thing-appearances. Yet the having of the appearance and the thinking founded upon it fall within psychology. All this belongs to psychology in the sense in which it is apprehended in the described natural attitude: the psychological I belongs to objective time, the same time to which the spatial world belongs.

**22\. Natur as the one world.** Every psychic being is spatiotemporal. Husserl argues against the idea of two separate worlds — things and souls. Experience knows only one world, insofar as souls are souls of bodies, and the world is the experiential world that refers back to I's who themselves have experiential placement in the world. The world or all-nature (*Allnatur*) is the lawfully unified whole of all spatiotemporal existence.

**Key distinction: Natur as unified totality.** There are not two worlds (things and souls) but one world — nature — as the unified totality of spatiotemporal existence.

**§9. Empirische oder natürliche Einstellung und apriorische Einstellung (Empirical or Natural Attitude and A Priori Attitude)**

**23\. A new attitude: the a priori.** What new attitude is possible over against the natural? Husserl observes that while nature encompasses all actual being (understood as spatiotemporal existence), correct thinking deals also with ideas that are not things and not facts of nature — e.g., numbers.

**24\. Ideal worlds.** Over against the empirical world there are ideal worlds — worlds of ideas that are non-spatial, non-temporal, non-real, yet are, just as numbers in the number-series are. One must distinguish between the natural or empirical attitude and the a priori attitude. In the one, existential objectivities; in the other, essential objectivities (*Wesensgegenständlichkeiten*). In the one, nature; in the other, ideas.

**Key distinction: empirical attitude vs. a priori (eidetic) attitude.** The empirical attitude is directed toward factual, spatiotemporal existence; the eidetic attitude grasps ideas and essences that are free of existence-positing.

**25\. Examples of ideation.** It is one thing to perceive a colour as a moment of a thing and another to grasp the idea of this colour-species. It is one thing to see four strokes and another, on the basis of seeing them, to be turned toward the one idea, the number 4\. Such ideas function as objects and enable assertions of unconditional universality.

**26\. The purity of the a priori.** The pure a priori — its freedom from existence-positing — requires a step or resolution to see and hold fast. Naturalists and mathematicians tend to give mathematical propositions an empirical interpretation. But the person of full intellectual honesty, who has learned to see what is given in the eidetic attitude, will maintain the daseinsfrei character of the a priori against all confusions.

**27\. Ontology of nature vs. formal ontology.** The eidetic attitude yields two groups of a priori disciplines. First, those belonging to the idea of nature: geometry, pure chronometry, pure kinematics, Kant's "pure natural science" — together forming the *Ontology of Nature*. Second, a group of essentially different disciplines: pure formal logic, pure probability theory, pure arithmetic, pure theory of manifolds — which concern not nature specifically but "something in general" (*Etwas überhaupt*). These can be united under the idea of a *formal, unconditionally universal ontology*. The ontology of nature is far more restricted because it is materially determined.

**Key distinction: Ontologie der Natur vs. formale Ontologie.** The ontology of nature unfolds what belongs to the idea of nature as such (space, time, thing, causality); formal ontology concerns "something in general" and applies to any objectivity whatsoever.

**§10. Das Apriori der Natur, der natürliche Weltbegriff und die Naturwissenschaft (The A Priori of Nature, the Natural Concept of the World, and Natural Science)**

**28\. The natural concept of the world and Avenarius.** Husserl engages with the positivism of Avenarius' school, which sought to eliminate all "metaphysical" intrusions from the concept of the world and to restore the "natural" concept of pure experience. Our description of the natural attitude was general and in a certain way evident, yet it was a description that posited the existence of what was described. Husserl asks what evidences are available.

**29\. The a priori binding character of the natural thesis.** Descriptive evidence is evident insofar as it faithfully expresses the sense of the relevant cogitationes. Further, if what is posited in the natural attitude is real, then what such a sense demands a priori must hold. All natural science, insofar as it presupposes the thesis of the natural worldview, is a priori bound to the real ontology.

**30\. Critique of pure experience.** If the positivists are right that natural science is pervaded by distorting interpretations violating the natural worldview, then it is an important task to exercise "critique" — a "critique of pure experience." The ontology of nature unfolds in its disciplines the pure formal-general sense of the natural thesis. But one must not confuse the natural concept of the world with a concept humans have empirically formed as a product of biological evolution.

**31\. The a priori character of the natural concept of the world is absolute.** As long as the talk of a world in which humans have consciousness retains its sense, the natural concept of the world holds absolutely and a priori. This a priority does not mean that another thesis than that of the natural world is impossible in every sense, but rather: if we start from the fact of the natural attitude and from the fact that its thesis has its undeniable right, then every natural-scientific assertion is senseless if it violates the sense of this thesis. It is therefore senseless to say that humans could, through experience, have reasonably worked out a different concept of the world — for in speaking of humans and nature, we have already presupposed the natural concept of the world. In the world there cannot be something that cancels the sense of speaking of the world, since it presupposes this sense as essence.

**32\. The possibility of other worlds.** While it is nonsense to claim that within the world a human could rightly find that *another* world is the actual world, it is not nonsense to assert that perhaps another world altogether could be — totally differently constituted, without Euclidean space, etc. — so long as we do not claim that beings constituted like humans could find and scientifically know such a world. The great problems attached to this possibility of other worlds lead us back to the question of attitudes deviating from the natural one.

## **CHAPTER II**

### **Fundamentalbetrachtung: die phänomenologische Reduktion als Gewinnung der Einstellung auf das reine Erlebnis**

*(Fundamental Consideration: the Phenomenological Reduction as the Gaining of the Attitude toward Pure Experience)*

**§11. Die Sphäre der Erkenntnis im subjektiven Sinne (The Sphere of Knowledge in the Subjective Sense)**

**33\. The completeness of the a priori.** Husserl asks whether the a priori disciplines identified so far exhaust the a priori, or whether new directions of regard remain. The a priori found so far was determined by starting from the natural attitude and looking at the natural world. But what about reflection on thinking itself and on the experiences connected with normative evaluation — e.g., the manifold changing perceptions underlying an experiential judgement?

**34\. Rational psychology.** The subjective sphere seemingly belongs to psychology as natural science. But Husserl insists there must also be a psychological a priori — one that unfolds what belongs to the essence of "soul"-positing, the positing of persons, of experiences as experiences of persons. Despite the crass empiricism reigning among psychologists, the idea of a rational psychology is evident once seen. Just as we clarify what a thing as such essentially is, so too must the essence of an I-character, or of experiences as belonging to persons, admit of eidetic elucidation.

**Key distinction: empirical psychology vs. rational (a priori) psychology.** Alongside empirical psychology there must be a rational psychology that unfolds what belongs to the essence of the psychic.

**§12. Das Problem der Ausschaltung des Empirischen (The Problem of Excluding the Empirical)**

**35\. The question of a new attitude.** Husserl poses the crucial question: Can we gain an attitude in which the empirical — the distinctive character of the natural attitude's givenness — is completely excluded, including even its *essence as essence of nature*, while certain components that enter into the essence of nature are retained?

**36\. The I's attachment to the body is empirical, not essential.** In the natural attitude, the I is experienced as bound to a body. The I's experiences are experienced as related to the body in a certain hierarchical order. Sensory experiences — perceptions with their sensation-components — have a peculiar relation to the body as Eigenleib; kinaesthetic sensations are localised in body-parts; sensory feelings have a similar attachment. Higher psychic experiences are interwoven with these. The body as thing has the primary ordering in objective time and objective space; everything correlated to the body receives its temporal and spatial placement secondarily.

**§13. Die Auflösbarkeit der empirischen Verknüpfung von "res cogitans" und "res extensa" (The Dissolvability of the Empirical Link between res cogitans and res extensa)**

**37\. The distinctio phaenomenologica.** All attachment of experience to the body can be severed from the body. It does not belong to the essence of a thing that it is a sensing thing; it is a facticity that a body is linked with the psychic. An empirical connection, but not an essential one. Conversely, nothing in the essence of pain, pleasure, sensation, perceiving, judging, etc., requires attachment to a thing. Since the connection is empirical (contingent in the ideal sense, though necessary in the empirical sense), we can "sever" it — performing a *distinctio phaenomenologica*. We can consider experiences in and for themselves, without considering their empirical connections. We can exclude every natural positing and conduct investigations in which we make absolutely no use of any positing of nature — investigations that retain their validity whether a nature exists or not.

**Key distinction: essential connection (Wesensnotwendigkeit) vs. empirical-causal connection.** The link between cogitatio and res extensa is empirical, not essential; the essences of cogitatio and extensio have nothing to do with one another.

**§14. Der Seinsvorzug des Erlebnisses (The Ontological Privilege of Experience)**

**38\. Experience has its being independently of nature.** An experience has its being in itself — it is what it is even if the talk of a spatiotemporal nature were a meaningless fiction. The being of experience has a tremendous advantage over the being of a natural object.

**39\. Empirical being vs. phenomenological being.** Husserl brings out the contrast. Things claim an in-itself-being (*An-sich-sein*) relative to knowledge. A thing exists whether experienced or not. But the thing's in-itself-being vis-à-vis knowledge is always merely *pretension* (*Prätention*): the experience-thesis can never be definitively validated. Experience by its sense posits *transcendence* — the thing is given through "presentations" (*Darstellungen*), through "appearances" (*Erscheinungen*). Every perception gives its object in principally incomplete appearance — one-sidedly, never all-sidedly. Complete experience of a thing is something infinite; to demand a finite, conclusive perception is a contradiction excluded by the essence of experience.

**40\. The perception of pure experience is radically different.** The perception of pure experiences, which cuts off all empirical apperception and takes them purely in themselves, is fundamentally different. We grasp, say, a feeling purely in itself, without drawing in the "empirical apperception" — without apprehending the feeling as a state of this empirical person under these psychophysical circumstances. The feeling, taken in itself, remains what it is even if all nature were annulled. Even though the empirical apperception is always present, we can direct our regard to the feeling in itself on the one hand, and to the intertwined empirical apperception in itself on the other. It is one thing to live in the empirical apperception; it is another to grasp and posit the feeling in itself. The empirical apperception can be made into an object in and for itself without our making it our own — i.e., without our continuing to posit what it posits.

**Key distinction: empirical perception vs. phenomenological perception.** Empirical perception always gives its object through appearances and carries the weight of an experience-thesis that is never definitively validated; phenomenological perception gives pure experience absolutely, without Darstellung, without remainder.

**§15. Die phänomenologische Einstellung (The Phenomenological Attitude)**

**41\. Definition of the phenomenological attitude.** In this new attitude, every empirical, transcendent attitude is excluded. We no longer accept as reality any object posited in the empirical attitude. We no longer "perform" any empirical attitude. Every empirical act is put in brackets (*Klammern*); what it offers us as being, we do not accept. Instead of living in the performance of such acts and naively holding fast to their positing, we look at the acts themselves, making them and what they offer us in themselves our objects — objects that are no nature and contain no natural positing. The term for the giving acts of the phenomenological attitude is *phenomenological intuition* or *Erschauung*.

**42\. Phenomenological Erschauung vs. innere Wahrnehmung.** The phenomenological perceiving of pure experiences must not be confused with Lockean reflection or "inner perception" (*innere Wahrnehmung*). Inner perception is empirical perception and remains so as long as every empirical positing has not been excluded. Not only must the positing of external nature, the body, and the psychophysical relation be suspended, but also the positing of the empirical I as person. Only when the phenomenological reduction is carried through consistently and completely — when the experience is no longer apprehended as a state of the experiencing I in objective time — does one gain the pure experience as object of genuine phenomenological perception.

**Key distinction: phänomenologische Wahrnehmung vs. innere Wahrnehmung (Lockean reflection).** Inner perception is still empirical; phenomenological perception requires the complete exclusion of the empirical I and all natural positing.

**§16. Descartes' fundamentale Betrachtung (Descartes' Fundamental Consideration)**

**43\. Descartes and the phenomenological reduction.** The first philosopher to have performed a phenomenological reduction — only to abandon it immediately — was Descartes. The fundamental consideration opening the entire development of modern philosophy was nothing other than the staging of a phenomenological reduction. The correlate of phenomenological perception is the cogitatio in the Cartesian sense — pure as opposed to empirical consciousness. Everything the empirical psychologist claims as psychic experience becomes cogitatio in the absolute sense only through phenomenological reduction — and only then is the givenness pure and absolute, so that every doubt loses its sense.

**44\. Husserl's difference from Descartes.** For Descartes, the decisive point was the absolute dubitability of the cogitatio; for Husserl, this is not the main thing. The question of whether and how phenomenological knowledge can ground absolute knowledge cannot be settled in advance. Descartes' procedure failed because he attempted to ground absolute science without investigating the sense of absolute science and without establishing systematic phenomenology.

**§17. Unabhängigkeit des phänomenologischen Urteils vom natürlichen Urteil (Independence of Phenomenological from Natural Judgement)**

**45\. The performance of the new attitude.** Husserl illustrates the reduction concretely. In the natural attitude I perceive this room with the people in it and pass perceptual judgements. Now I turn my regard: I perform a new attitude. The things "still stand there," but I do not ground the judgement that they are there; I do not assert anything about these things. Instead, the judgement itself, with its intentional properties — that it judges about benches in "this room," about things in nature — belongs to my phenomenological sphere. But I only *describe* what the judgement judges, what it posits as true, without myself taking up what is posited as true.

**46\. Analogy with critical reflection.** This is analogous to the critical attitude of unprejudiced examination. Just as in critical reflection we "bracket" a judgement while examining it without presupposing its truth, so in the phenomenological attitude we bracket all empirical judgements. It is evident that judgements from which I make no use as premises cannot influence the truth of my phenomenological findings. The phenomenological statements remain untouched whether one then returns to making empirical judgements, or whether, as a sceptic, one doubts all empirical judgements. Everything that sets the existence of the phenomenologist himself as a member of nature is likewise excluded.

## **CHAPTER III**

### **Vorläufige Erörterung einiger Einwände gegen die Absicht der phänomenologischen Reduktion**

*(Preliminary Discussion of Some Objections to the Aim of the Phenomenological Reduction)*

**§18. Der Einwand des Solipsismus (The Objection of Solipsism)**

**47\. Phenomenological research is not solipsistic.** Is phenomenological research solipsistic — restricted to the individual I and its psychic phenomena? Nothing less\! Solus ipse would mean: I alone am, excluding all the rest of the world but not myself and my psychic states. The phenomenologist, on the contrary, excludes himself just as much as all the world, and his psychic states no less, since as *his* they are nature. The absurd epistemological theory of solipsism arises from confusing psychological, psychologistic immanence with genuine phenomenological immanence — from a misunderstanding of the proper sense of transcendence and its exclusion.

**Key distinction: psychologische (solipsistische) Immanenz vs. phänomenologische Immanenz.** Solipsism confuses the psychological I's enclosure within its own states with the phenomenological field, which excludes the empirical I along with everything else natural.

**§19. Der Einwand gegen die phänomenologische Ausschaltbarkeit des Ich (The Objection against the Excludability of the I)**

**48\. Can the I be excluded?** The objection: the reduction that would exclude the I is unthinkable. Cogitatio requires reference to an I. Husserl answers: the possibility of bracketing all empirical transcendence, including the empirical I, is undeniable. The objection can only mean that perhaps, over against the empirical I, a *pure I* must be posited as something inseparable from the cogitationes. Husserl leaves this question undecided here, noting only that phenomenological research must speak of everything it finds within its attitude. If, while the natural world stands in brackets, something like a pure I or a pure time is found to be given and positable, then it is something phenomenological.

**§20. Einwände gegen den absoluten Charakter des phänomenologisch Gegebenen (Objections against the Absolute Character of the Phenomenologically Given)**

**49\. Doubts about phenomenological knowledge.** Objectors will say: experiential givenness is excluded because experience is in principle never a definitively giving act. Phenomenological intuition is supposed to be free of this deficiency — what it gives is not mere appearance but being itself. But can this be maintained? Is absolute givenness ever achievable? Even if it were, what could we do with it? Descartes could do nothing further with the indubitable being of the cogitatio. It is not to be seen how a science — let alone natural science — could be established here.

**50\. The counter: nature is knowable only through experience.** Nature is by its essence knowable only through experience. Any inference ending with assertions about natural objects requires premises ultimately grounded in experience.

**§21. Die Motivationslosigkeit der phänomenologischen Reduktion (The Motivelessness of the Phenomenological Reduction)**

**51\. Phenomenology needs no external motive.** One need not impute motives to phenomenology for why it excludes experience-positing. As phenomenology, it has no such motives; these the phenomenologist may have as private affairs. Phenomenology simply excludes empirical positing and confines itself to what remains. The only question is whether there is then room for a science. One may not say "our only interest is nature" — that is the naturalist's private view.

**§22. Vorüberlegung zur Diskussion der Einwände (Preliminary Reflection on Objections)**

**52\. The right of phenomenological knowledge in analogy with natural science.** Before considering the objections to the absoluteness of phenomenological knowledge, Husserl makes a preliminary point. Perhaps phenomenological knowledge, like natural-scientific knowledge, has its undeniable right, even if it is not absolute. Perhaps there is a sphere of rich scientific insights here. If so, no further proof is needed for establishing phenomenology. Even if absolute certainty is an idea not fully realised in any actual science, phenomenology retains its value, provided it is evident that phenomenological givenness is genuine givenness and phenomenological method genuine method. Perhaps within the reduction itself distinctions between different modes of givenness — some absolutely certain, some not — must be drawn. Perhaps "phenomenology" is as much the title of a method as of a discipline.

## **CHAPTER IV**

### **Das Hinausgehen der Phänomenologie über den Bereich des absolut Gegebenen**

*(Phenomenology's Going Beyond the Sphere of the Absolutely Given)*

**§23. Das Problem des absoluten Charakters phänomenologischer Gegebenheit (The Problem of the Absolute Character of Phenomenological Givenness)**

**53\. Varieties of givenness.** What objections arise against the absolute character of phenomenological givenness? Pursuing them will show that the phenomenological reduction first leads to absolute givenness — phenomenological perception — whose absolute, undeniable character can be defended. But interwoven with it, other modes of givenness emerge within the phenomenological attitude whose absolute character cannot be maintained in the same sense (as certainty). The concept of phenomenological intuition must be broadened to run parallel to empirical experience: phenomenological presencing (*Gegenwärtigung*) and representification (*Vergegenwärtigung*).

**Key distinction: phänomenologische Wahrnehmung (absolute givenness) vs. phänomenologische Vergegenwärtigung (non-absolute givenness within the phenomenological attitude).** Within the reduced sphere, not all givenness has the same character of absolute certainty.

**§24. Die absolute Gegebenheit des phänomenologisch Wahrgenommenen (The Absolute Givenness of the Phenomenologically Perceived)**

**54\. The enduring perception and its "was."** I perceive, exclude the existence of perceived things, and hold fast to the perception itself as a *this*. But the perception is an enduring being — it lasts, with a now that passes into a "just-past" and a new now that arises. How does it stand with absolute givenness here? The past of the perception is no longer given. Does the "was" itself perhaps involve deception?

**55\. The senselessness of radical exclusion.** If we try to exclude even the just-past and confine ourselves to the pure now, the now itself is the ever-fleeing boundary-point, and even asserting this much already requires retention. If we try to exclude the retention, the entire enterprise of exclusion loses its sense: the exclusion is so radical that we find nothing whatsoever to judge about. But we must not let ourselves be led astray. The perception, taken as a "this," as a unity of duration, with its positing as this enduring thing, excludes doubt. Doubt presupposes the givenness of the "opinion" (*Meinung*) that is placed in doubt; the perception as phenomenon of enduring empirical givenness is absolutely given.

**§25. Die in der phänomenologischen Wahrnehmung implizierte Retention (The Retention Implied in Phenomenological Perception)**

**56\. Retention as a transcendence within the phenomenological attitude.** From the givenness of the enduring perception, one can also find the right stance regarding the just-past of the perception. The "having-been" is a given, a *this*, but as a just-past phase of the given enduring. A further question concerns its description and analysis — but the phenomenological intuition opens a ground on which thinking can establish itself. We have admitted a "transcendence" within the phenomenological attitude, since we allow not only the retention itself but also that of which it is retention. We could show that distrusting retentional evidence would amount to absolute scepticism, and that the natural scientist who builds on empirical perception, which itself presupposes retentional evidence, has no right to play the hyper-critic when it comes to phenomenology.

**Key distinction: Transzendenz in der Immanenz.** Within phenomenological immanence itself, transcendence arises — the just-past transcends the now — but this transcendence is of an entirely different kind from the transcendence of nature.

**§26. Die phänomenologische Wiedererinnerung (Phenomenological Recollection)**

**57\. Recollection and the possibility of deception.** Phenomenologically reduced recollection is not absolutely certain. Empirical deceptions in memory can be shown to produce parallel deceptions in phenomenologically reduced memory. E.g., if empirical memory first says two events were simultaneous and later says they were separated, the phenomenological reduction of each yields contradictory phenomenological data. This suffices to show the possibility of deception in phenomenological recollection.

**58\. Every empirical recollection yields a phenomenological one.** Every unitary empirical recollection, through phenomenological reduction, yields a unitary phenomenological recollection. Recollection says: such-and-such was. But it also enables a reflection that says: such-and-such was *perceived*. The deception-consciousness of subjective intuition produces a corresponding deception-consciousness for the phenomenologically reduced recollection.

**§27. Die Erwartung (Expectation)**

**59\. Expectation.** The entire domain of the empirical can be appropriated through phenomenological reduction in the same sense. Each empirical expectation has a corresponding phenomenological one. E.g., watching birds — the male following the female from tree to tree — I expect the male to follow. Phenomenological reduction brackets the natural content, but the expectation of a certain seeing with such-and-such content remains. However, the purity of the reduction does nothing to give this motivational expectation absolute givenness-value: a mosquito flies into my nose, and the expected seeing comes to nothing.

**§28. Die phänomenologische Erfahrung (Phenomenological Experience)**

**60\. Transzendenz in der Immanenz and the richness of phenomenological motivations.** The stock of phenomenological motivations is infinitely richer than the few vague titles — perception, retention, recollection, expectation — suggest. One must attend also to the empirically objective content that is perceived. The phenomenological reduction always yields a surprising abundance of intuitive connections given not in the mode of phenomenological perception but in various other modes of phenomenological experience. Everywhere there is "transcendence within phenomenological immanence" and everywhere the possibility of deception. E.g., any perception of a thing yields, through reduction, motivational connections of sensation-contents, apprehensions, kinaesthetic series, etc. Empathy and self-experience likewise yield motivational connections, but the knowledge of these is not knowledge of phenomenological perception — it is not the absolute self-givenness to which the evidence of the cogitatio initially led.

**§29. Das Hinausgehen über den Bereich absoluter Gegebenheit (Going Beyond the Sphere of Absolute Givenness)**

**61\. The necessity of going beyond absolute givenness.** If we confined ourselves to the givenness of the cogitatio as absolutely certain perception-givenness, we could only ever say "this" — no scientific knowledge would be conceivable. But retention, recollection, expectation, and phenomenological reduction of all natural experience yield an infinite abundance of phenomenological givenness. The psychologist will claim all this as psychic phenomena, and rightly so when apperceived naturalistically. But we insist on also excluding the empirical subject; then the phenomenologically-singular being is not a psychological experience but, as it were, a *transcendental-psychic* being as opposed to the *empirical-psychic*.

**62\. The legitimacy of non-absolute phenomenological experience.** Should phenomenological experience that is not absolutely certain be admitted? Nobody demands that the modes of givenness on which natural science builds be absolute. It would be absurd and unnecessary for strict science. Thus nothing stands in the way of attempting a transcendental psychology — a science of experiences in phenomenological reduction. Phenomenological experience may often be no better than empirical experience, but it is also no worse. Why then should there not be a phenomenological science of experience alongside the naturalistic one?

**Key distinction: transcendental-psychic vs. empirical-psychic.** The reduced experience is not a psychological experience of an empirical subject but a transcendental-psychic datum.

**§30. Immanenz und Transzendenz (Immanence and Transcendence)**

**63\. The multiple senses of immanence and transcendence.** Such a science would deal with objects that are occasionally "transcendent" (when remembered or expected) but in another and more important sense *immanent* — they are by their own nature capable of being given absolutely and are not objectivities given through appearance or presentation (*Darstellung*). Nature, by contrast, is necessarily given through presentations, through *Abschattungen* — and these presentations can in principle never be a reduplication of what is presented.

Husserl distinguishes three senses of transcendence:

1. **General intentional transcendence:** The object of consciousness is not itself the act. This applies even in phenomenological intuition. But no one calls this transcendence, because its opposite "immanence" would have no sense.

2. **Transcendence as non-self-givenness:** Being meant without being bodily self-present (*leibhaft gegenwärtig*). The strictest sense: consciousness is an intuition that has or touches what it intuits, as when a present living cogitatio is intuited and grasped in living presence. On the side of immanence stands only the presently intuited; on the side of transcendence stands everything else, including remembered or retained data.

3. **Transcendence as appearing through Darstellungen:** An ontological classification of individual objects. Immanent objects can in principle be given in absolute self-presence; transcendent objects can only appear through presentations. All phenomenological consciousness refers to immanence; nature falls on the side of transcendence, since nature is a title for the totality of objectivities that present themselves through appearances.

**Key distinction: three senses of Transzendenz and Immanenz.** (1) The general intentional relation; (2) bodily self-presence vs. non-presence; (3) objects given absolutely vs. objects given only through Darstellungen/Abschattungen. Phenomenology does not exclude all transcendence — only transcendence in sense (3), i.e., nature as that which appears through presentations.

## **CHAPTER V**

### **Die phänomenologische Gewinnung des ganzen, einheitlich zusammenhängenden Bewusstseinsstromes**

*(The Phenomenological Recovery of the Entire, Unitarily Connected Stream of Consciousness)*

**§31. Der Hintergrund des phänomenologischen Gegenstandes (The Background of the Phenomenological Object)**

**64\. The field of phenomenological experience.** Husserl reviews the situation. Phenomenological experience, like empirical experience, has its various modes: phenomenological perception (absolute self-givenness), retention, recollection, expectation, empathy — each with its right and evidence. The field of knowledge is infinite on both sides: on the natural side, the totality of objects we call nature; on the phenomenological side, the totality of objects we call consciousness.

**65\. The background of every phenomenological object.** Every phenomenological object has its objectual background — for perception, a background of co-conscious but not co-intended present data. This background can be brought to intendedness through subsequent reflection and memory. The same holds for every experience.

**66\. The identity of the phenomenological datum across different acts.** The same phenomenological datum may be first expected, then perceived, then retained, then recollected. Empathy has no exceptional status here either. The identity of the phenomenological datum in different acts of consciousness is not an extra-phenomenological fact but is itself something phenomenologically given — a fact of phenomenological experience, given in an intuiting identity-consciousness.

**67\. Phenomenological time-consciousness.** Various bindings relate to phenomenological time-consciousness (not to be confused with empirical time-consciousness). Expectation necessarily precedes perception, perception precedes recollection, first recollection precedes second — and other bindings hold for varying content.

**§32. Wiederholung und neue Darstellung (Repetition and New Presentation)**

**68\. The phenomenological reduction reviewed.** Husserl repeats the main thoughts for his students. We begin in the natural attitude and then perform an all-encompassing phenomenological reduction, excluding every natural existence-positing. We want to make no use of any naturally given existence in our judgements — without doubting it or suspecting it. Every empirically founded judgement is excluded as premise and theoretical assertion. This does not mean judging a priori in the usual sense, for the genuine a priori deals with universals; we, on the other hand, retain through the reduction a world of individual being — the world of phenomenological data, of pure consciousness.

**69\. Consciousness admits of phenomenological reduction.** A part of the natural world — called consciousness — has the peculiarity of being phenomenologically reducible. When all natural existence-positings are put out of action, consciousness remains as purely immanent being — as something that in this positing is not nature, not self-presenting being that merely displays itself, nor anything that participates in such being through indirect co-positing.

**§33. Die Erstreckung der phänomenologischen Erfahrung über den ganzen Bewusstseinsstrom (The Extension of Phenomenological Experience over the Entire Stream of Consciousness)**

**70\. Modes of givenness of the reduced sphere.** Phenomenological perception (also called phenomenological intuition) gives every reduced phenomenon as a self-present enduring being. This phenomenological self-presence is not appearing presence (*erscheinende Gegenwart*) but self-presence in the absolute sense. The duration of the immanent being involves a flowing now-point and a continuum of flowing past-points. Corresponding to every phenomenological perception is a point of now-perception and a continuum of retentional memory, in constant flux.

**71\. Recollection and the grouping of cogitationes.** When successive phenomenological perceptions are performed, each is self-present while it lasts. Once elapsed, a stretch remains in living retention. Recollections may arise of particular cogitationes and of whole series. In recollection we can attend to the time-background of the recollected cogitatio and form group-consciousnesses and series-consciousnesses not formed in the original perception.

**72\. The entire stream of consciousness as an experiential field.** Phenomenological experience is not confined to the singular cogitationes that happen to be currently present. It extends over the entire stream of consciousness as a single temporal connection, even though not the whole extent falls into the light of intuition at any one time. In the phenomenological reduction, an infinite unity of consciousness stands before us — an endlessly unified stream. The stream of consciousness, in phenomenological purity, becomes its own field of experience, its own domain of knowledge.

**§34. Aufhebung einer künstlichen Einschränkung. Die doppelte phänomenologische Reduktion (Removal of an Artificial Restriction. The Double Phenomenological Reduction)**

**73\. The double phenomenological reduction.** The previous discussion operated under an artificial restriction. What was said gains its proper value only if we start from the stream of consciousness as it presents itself in natural reflection and only then exercise phenomenological reduction. Every experience admits of a double phenomenological reduction: first, the one that brings the experience itself to pure immanent intuition; second, the one exercised on its intentional content and object. E.g., in recollection we can perform a phenomenological reduction on the intentional content of the recollection — attending to past sensations, past appearances, past co-intentions — that was not performed in the original perception.

**Key distinction: the double phenomenological reduction.** Every experience can be reduced both (1) as an act in itself and (2) with respect to its intentional content, yielding phenomenological data from within the intentional horizon.

**§35. Die transzendenten Einheiten als Indizes (Transcendent Unities as Indices)**

**74\. Transzendente Einheiten as indices of pure consciousness-connections.** Where natural experience posits a transcendent unity — a thing, a constellation, an event — this existence is bracketed but serves as an *index* for determinate pure consciousness-connections, discoverable through phenomenological reduction.

**75\. The phenomenological "conversion" of all natural experience.** Starting from the natural attitude, we survey the intuitively given manifold of nature. Performing the reduction, each perception of, say, this table, with its particular appearance, corresponds to a determinate manifold of experience-possibilities — motivated, real possibilities that can become actual. The exclusion of nature means that we no longer make the experienced thing the object of our assertions, but rather the experiences of the thing, the actual and possible ones, taken in pure immanence. It is a cognition of enormous importance that every natural experience, taken as immanent being, motivates a manifold of other natural experiences and real experience-possibilities, and that these motivational connections — connections of pure consciousness — can be unravelled and inspected.

**76\. Hume and Mill.** The first germ of this peculiar reduction is found in Hume and more specifically in Mill's doctrine of permanent possibilities of sensation. In essence the same is said by Mach's sensation-monism. But Husserl sets aside metaphysical-epistemological constructions and focuses on the phenomenological content.

**77\. Sensation-contents, appearances, and motivational connections.** Performing phenomenological reduction on any thing-perception yields sensation-contents, apprehensions, co-apprehensions, kinaesthetic series — both actual and motivationally possible. Each specific spatial form corresponds to a specific syntax of motivations. The same can be done within recollection. Thus all natural experience can be converted into phenomenological experience: instead of investigating nature, we investigate the thing-experiences in their immanence, what lies within them, how they connect and motivate one another, including their connection with judgements, feelings, desires, etc. — all under consistent exclusion of every judgement about the being of nature. Every experienced thing as such is an *index* for a certain lawfulness of consciousness as pure consciousness.

**78\. True existence and non-existence as indices.** Specifically, if we attribute validity to the experience of a thing, this true existence of the thing is an index for quite determinate appearance-connections and thought-processes. Conversely, non-existence is an index for differently structured consciousness-connections in which the existence-positing evidently cancels itself. All content of the sciences can be phenomenologically converted — serving as index for phenomenological connections. We do not posit the theories or nature but go back to the judgement- and grounding-connections whose significance-content and validity-content these theories are, and perform phenomenological reflection within the acts of such theorising.

## **CHAPTER VI**

### **Die Gewinnung der phänomenologischen Monadenvielheit**

*(The Recovery of the Phenomenological Plurality of Monads)*

**§36. Der intersubjektive Zusammenhang des Bewusstseins (The Intersubjective Connection of Consciousness)**

**79\. The question of intersubjectivity.** An important supplement is needed. The theoretical content of a science and nature are intersubjective unities. But the previous lectures have not yet addressed the intersubjective connection of consciousness — the experience running from one I-consciousness to another. Does the phenomenological reduction restrict us to the connections of pure consciousness belonging, in empirical-psychological apperception, to a single empirical I — namely, the phenomenologist's own?

**§37. Das Prinzip der Konstruktion eines einheitlichen Bewusstseinsstromes (The Principle of the Construction of a Unified Stream of Consciousness)**

**80\. The unity of a single stream.** How is a single phenomenological stream characterised? The empirical I has a body and, in a quite different sense, has consciousness. Does the latter give a unity under phenomenological reduction? Yes: the unity of the phenomenological stream and the unity of the consciousness that is exclusively the consciousness of a single I in empirical apperception (and that emerges from this through reduction) are one and the same.

**81\. The temporal background and the Zeitlicher Hof.** Every cogitatio has its temporally ordered background. None is isolated; each is something intended out of an environment of more or less intimately connected phenomenological data (or better, *Dabilien* — data-candidates that require the turn of the intending regard to become actual data). Every phenomenological I includes all that this background contains in the direction of past, present, and future. This background may be clear or dark, but it is always determinable.

**82\. Can two memories be disconnected?** Husserl raises the question: could two memories posit two disconnected streams of consciousness? An essential law answers: any two memories belonging to the unity of a present consciousness unite into a single memory — i.e., a (perhaps not intuitively filled) time-consciousness in which what is remembered in each belongs to one time. It may be that the time-order is indeterminately conscious — which is earlier, which later, or whether they are simultaneous — but this indeterminacy is determinable in the sense of one of the three cases. There must then be a possible chain of clear memories connecting them and establishing the continuous temporal connection of the stream.

**83\. The principle of the unity of the stream.** More generally: any two experiences that fit together into the unity of a synthetic consciousness encompassing them fit together into the unity of one experience, and to this unity there belongs a time-unity of the experienced. This holds for phenomenological experience in particular. Thereby the principle is found — and the only decisive one — that constructs the unity of the stream of consciousness: i.e., the principle deciding whether multiple cogitationes belong to the unity of a single phenomenological I.

**Key distinction: the principle of the unity of the Bewusstseinsstrom.** The unity of the stream of consciousness is constituted through the essential law that any experiences belonging to a single present consciousness must belong to a single temporal unity.

**§38. Die Einfühlung (Empathy)**

**84\. Empathy is not image-consciousness.** Husserl asks: do we ever reach another phenomenological I? The path so far leads only to one stream. But we have not yet considered empathy. Empathy is a special form of empirical experience: in it, the empathising I experiences the consciousness of another I. But it does not experience it as it does its own — not in inner perception or Lockean reflection, not in memory or expectation. Is it image-consciousness (*Bildbewusstsein*), an analogising of the other's consciousness through one's own similar consciousness? Husserl denies this. In empathising anger, one is not oneself angry. Empathy is as little a consciousness of actual pictorial representation as are recollection or anticipation. Husserl regards empathy as a consciousness most closely related to these acts — an act of the broadest class of representifications (*Vergegenwärtigungen*).

**85\. Nor is empathy analogising through phantasy-images.** One might think of another kind of analogising — as when we form a phantasy-image of a described situation. But to interpret every empathy thus has its problems: we intuit the other's experience directly and immediately, without awareness of any impressional or imaginative representification. When we merely form a picture of the other's experience, we feel this as something different.

**Key distinction: Einfühlung as Vergegenwärtigung, not Verbildlichung.** Empathy is a unique mode of representification, neither image-consciousness nor analogising phantasy, but a direct Vergegenwärtigung of the other's experience.

**§39. Die Gewinnung anderer phänomenologischer Ich (Recovering Other Phenomenological I's)**

**86\. The double reduction applied to empathy.** Empathy, like every other experience, can be phenomenologically reduced — and again with the double reduction. First, the empathy-act itself is given in phenomenological perception and belongs, with its temporal background, to one stream. Second, the empathy is experience *of* an empathised consciousness, in which we can also perform phenomenological reduction. The datum so gained also has its temporal background and is thus a datum of a phenomenological I.

**87\. The law of two separate streams.** A crucial law holds: the empathised datum and the empathising experience itself can in principle *never* belong to the same stream of consciousness, to the same phenomenological I. From the empathised stream, no channel leads to the stream to which the empathising belongs. The simultaneity of the empathising act and the empathised act is not a self-intuited (*selbsterschaute*) simultaneity but a *representified* (*vergegenwärtigte*) one. The empathised "now" is a representified now, identified with the actual now. The identification of the time of the empathised and empathising consciousness is mediated through the relation to the objective time of the body and the thing-world — which is subject to phenomenological reduction.

**88\. The plurality of monads.** After reduction, all phenomenological being reduces to one (to "my") phenomenological I — distinguished as perceiving and remembering, empathising, and phenomenologically reducing — and to other I's posited in empathy. The empirically experienced natural objects, through exclusion of their existence, become *indices* for determinate actual consciousness-connections and motivated consciousness-possibilities.

**89\. Nature as index of the coordination of Ichmonaden.** Through natural empathy, the empathised I's are posited as belonging to their bodies, as centres of thing-environments that extend to the one all-nature. In the phenomenological reduction, every thing is also, for the empathised I, an index of experience-connections attributed to it. Nature is thus an index for an all-encompassing lawfulness (*Regelung*) spanning all streams of consciousness standing in experiential relation through empathy. Every objective time-point is an index for a quite determinate lawful coordination that brings every Ichmonade into relation with every other, with respect to determinate correlative consciousness-motivations.

**Key distinction: Natur als Index für die Koordination der Monadenvielheit.** Nature, after the reduction, functions as the index for the lawful coordination binding the plurality of phenomenological monads to one another.

## **CHAPTER VII**

### **Schlussbetrachtungen über die Tragweite phänomenologischer Erkenntnis**

*(Concluding Reflections on the Scope of Phenomenological Knowledge)*

**§40. Die Enthaltung von jedem Urteil über das Sein der Natur (Abstention from Every Judgement about the Being of Nature)**

**90\. No judgement about nature has been made.** All of the foregoing holds if we perform what we called the phenomenological reduction — if we do not judge about the being of nature but about the being of purely phenomenological connections. Notably, we have *not* said that nature "is in truth nothing other than" this consciousness-to-consciousness coordination; we have not said that consciousness is the only true being and nature merely an imaginary picture that consciousness projects in itself. Such theories make express assertive claims about nature and therefore have nothing to do with us here, since our entire investigation proceeded within the phenomenological reduction, which by definition means the omission of every assertion about nature.

**Key distinction: phenomenological abstention (Enthaltung) vs. metaphysical thesis.** The phenomenological reduction does not make a metaphysical claim (idealism, subjective idealism, etc.) about nature; it simply abstains from all judgement about nature's being.

**§41. Das Problem der Möglichkeit phänomenologischer Wissenschaft (The Problem of the Possibility of Phenomenological Science)**

**91\. Phenomenology as parallel to natural science.** We have thought of phenomenology as a parallel to natural science — both dealing with individual objectivities. But we have not considered the role of a priori knowledge within the phenomenological sphere: whether, on the basis of phenomenological experience, ideations can be performed and ideal scientific knowledge won. For nature, there is pure natural science (the a priori of nature) alongside empirical natural science; the a priori serves as methodological instrument of the empirical but the latter yields something completely new. Have we confirmed that the insights gained into the phenomenological sphere are at bottom pure eidetic knowledge, and that something like an experiential phenomenology is still questionable or perhaps impossible?

**92\. The question of matter-of-fact science of consciousness.** If we hold to experience genuinely as experience — as the positing of individual being — we may be sure that the domain of such positing is very wide, but not quite so sure that an experiential science (*Erfahrungswissenschaft*), a genuine *matter-of-fact* science, can be grounded upon it.

**§42. Die Äquivalenz von Naturerkenntnis und Erkenntnis der korrelativen Bewusstseinszusammenhänge (The Equivalence of Natural Knowledge and Knowledge of Correlative Consciousness-Connections)**

**93\. The equivalence thesis.** What also strikes us is that all the consciousness-connections in which the being of nature "expresses itself" in consciousness come to our knowledge without our asserting a single judgement about nature and without even tacitly using nature's existence as a premise — and yet the knowledge of these consciousness-connections is in a certain way *equivalent* to the knowledge of nature, and vice versa. The validity of experience and empirical knowledge has its correlate in certain actual and possible connections of experiential consciousness; conversely, if these connections are assumed to hold, then experiential knowledge has validity.

**94\. Does phenomenology yield anything beyond a "conversion" of natural knowledge?** In this domain of phenomenology, we seemingly have nothing other than a "conversion" (*Umwendung*) of natural knowledge into the phenomenological. Could phenomenology achieve this knowledge independently, without prior natural knowledge? Perhaps instead it is a priori knowledge belonging to the essence of consciousness — won in purely immanent research — that finds application to empirically developed natural knowledge in its phenomenological connections, yielding a knowledge of existence-connections of phenomenological data that could not have been won directly from the individual data. This doubt certainly applies to the entire domain of physics.

**95\. The special case of psychophysics.** The matter is more difficult for psychophysical knowledge, which is properly natural knowledge only through its entanglement with physical natural knowledge (transcendent knowledge). Psychophysical knowledge is, at bottom, an intermediate link between natural knowledge and purely phenomenological knowledge.

## **Summary of Key Distinctions**

1. **Natural attitude (*natürliche Einstellung*) vs. phenomenological attitude (*phänomenologische Einstellung*)** (Chapters I–II): The natural attitude is the attitude of experience in which we posit the existence of the spatiotemporal world; the phenomenological attitude brackets every such positing.

2. **The I vs. its experiences** (Chapter I, §1): The I is the subject who has, performs, and lives through experiences; it is not itself an experience, act, or character-trait.

3. **Psychic localisation vs. physical localisation** (Chapter I, §3): Experiences are related to the body, but in a fundamentally different manner from physical parts within a physical thing.

4. **Mittelpunkt vs. Umgebungspunkt** (Chapter I, §4): Each I is given to itself as the zero-point of orientation; other I's are given as points in the environment.

5. **Empirical attitude vs. a priori (eidetic) attitude** (Chapter I, §9): The former is directed toward factual spatiotemporal existence; the latter grasps ideas and essences free of existence-positing.

6. **Ontologie der Natur vs. formale Ontologie** (Chapter I, §9): The ontology of nature unfolds the a priori of nature (space, time, thing, causality); formal ontology concerns "something in general" and applies to any objectivity whatsoever.

7. **Essential connection (Wesensnotwendigkeit) vs. empirical-causal connection** (Chapter II, §13): The link between cogitatio and res extensa is empirical (contingent in the ideal sense), not essential — the *distinctio phaenomenologica*.

8. **Empirical perception vs. phenomenological perception (Erschauung)** (Chapter II, §§14–15): Empirical perception gives its object through appearances and involves an ever-revisable thesis; phenomenological perception gives pure experience without Darstellung, absolutely.

9. **Phänomenologische Wahrnehmung vs. innere Wahrnehmung (Lockean reflection)** (Chapter II, §15): Inner perception is still empirical and retains the empirical I; phenomenological perception requires the complete exclusion of the empirical I.

10. **Psychologische (solipsistische) Immanenz vs. phänomenologische Immanenz** (Chapter III, §18): Solipsism arises from confusing the psychological I's enclosure within its own states with the phenomenological field, which excludes the empirical I along with nature.

11. **Phänomenologische Wahrnehmung (absolute givenness) vs. phänomenologische Vergegenwärtigung (non-absolute givenness)** (Chapter IV, §23): Within the reduced sphere, perception gives absolute self-presence, while retention, recollection, expectation, and empathy yield non-absolute but legitimate givenness.

12. **Three senses of Immanenz and Transzendenz** (Chapter IV, §30): (1) General intentional transcendence; (2) bodily self-presence vs. non-presence; (3) objects given absolutely vs. objects given only through Darstellungen. Phenomenology excludes only transcendence in sense (3).

13. **The double phenomenological reduction** (Chapter V, §34): Every experience can be reduced both (a) as act in itself and (b) with respect to its intentional content, yielding phenomenological data from within the intentional horizon.

14. **Transzendente Einheiten als Indizes** (Chapter V, §35): Transcendent unities of natural experience, once bracketed, serve as indices for determinate connections of pure consciousness.

15. **The principle of the unity of the Bewusstseinsstrom** (Chapter VI, §37): Any experiences belonging to a single present consciousness must belong to a single temporal unity — this is the sole principle constructing the unity of the stream.

16. **Einfühlung as Vergegenwärtigung, not Verbildlichung** (Chapter VI, §38): Empathy is a unique mode of representification, neither image-consciousness nor analogising phantasy, but a direct, immediate grasping of the other's experience.

17. **Natur als Index für die Koordination der Monadenvielheit** (Chapter VI, §39): After the reduction, nature functions as the index for the lawful coordination binding the plurality of phenomenological monads to one another.

18. **Phenomenological abstention (Enthaltung) vs. metaphysical thesis** (Chapter VII, §40): The reduction does not assert idealism or deny nature; it abstains from all judgement about nature's being.

19. **Äquivalenz of Naturerkenntnis and correlative Bewusstseinserkenntnis** (Chapter VII, §42): Knowledge of nature and knowledge of the corresponding phenomenological consciousness-connections are correlatively equivalent, but it remains an open question whether phenomenology yields anything beyond a conversion of natural knowledge.

# **APPENDIX XII (XVII of No. 5\)**

## ***Reflection on the Relation Between the Second, Psychological, and the Third, Humanities-Based, Path to Pure Consciousness. The Understanding of the Motivation of the Mind and the Contexts of Motivation of Individual Minds*** **(ca. 1910\)**

### **Section 1: The Human Being as Subject of Motivation (p. 171, lines 1–6)**

The surviving text opens in the middle of an argument. Husserl is articulating a methodological viewpoint from which the human being is considered "purely" as a **subject of motivation** — that is, not as a psychophysical organism causally embedded in nature, but as someone who is *moved* by what appears in consciousness. Husserl identifies three distinct sources of motivation:

1. **Nature** — the human being is motivated by the natural world as it is "apprehended" (aufgefasst) in consciousness, i.e., not by nature-in-itself but by nature as it *presents itself* to the experiencing subject.  
2. **Fellow human beings** — the subject is motivated by others as they are apprehended "in such and such a way," meaning that the motivating force of another person depends on how I interpret and understand that person.  
3. **Social institutions** — the subject is also motivated by supra-individual social formations.

This threefold schema establishes the scope of the humanities (Geisteswissenschaften): they study persons not as natural objects but as motivated subjects existing in a meaningful world of other subjects and social structures.

---

### **Section 2: Motivation vs. Causality — The Noetic–Noematic Structure of Motivation (pp. 171–172, lines 7–15 and footnotes 8–9)**

Husserl draws a sharp distinction between **motivation** and **natural causality**. In everyday language, we say things like "I let myself be influenced by my friend" or "I did that because he did that." These expressions have the surface grammar of causal, objective judgments. But Husserl insists that this is misleading: what truly motivates is not the real, natural friend-in-the-flesh but rather **my presencing (Vergegenwärtigung) of my friend** — my "thinking" of him and my "thinking about" what he has done. The motivational nexus is thus always between acts of consciousness: "One instance of 'I think' has been motivated through another 'I think.'"

This is a signature Husserlian move: redirecting analysis from the objective-causal register to the intentional-correlational register. In footnote 9, Husserl himself corrects and deepens the point: motivation has **two sides, the noetic and the noematic**. My *position-taking* (Stellungnahme) — the noetic side — is motivated not by the friend in the natural attitude but by my friend "perceived, presenced, so or so thought about, interpreted in such and such a way" — which constitutes the **noematic correlate** of my experience. This aligns the analysis of motivation with the broader framework of noesis/noema from the *Ideas*.

---

### **Section 3: The Legitimacy of the Objective Expression (pp. 171–172, lines 16–3)**

Husserl then concedes that the everyday, "objective" way of speaking about influence is not simply wrong. When I say "my friend influenced me," I am operating in the **attitude of motivation**, in which I am turned toward the *content* of my judgments (the state of affairs, "S is p") and toward the other person's deeds. In this attitude, I attend to things, persons, and situations rather than to acts of consciousness as such. Husserl illustrates this with vivid examples: "The insufferable person is coming downstairs; I will quickly get out of the way\!" and "The mailman is coming; I will go to meet him." In the natural motivational attitude, I respond to *what I encounter* — not to "consciousness of encountering."

However, Husserl adds that a **complete description** will name acts of consciousness together with their correlates. What is "real" (Reales) enters motivational relationships only insofar as it is an "object apprehended by consciousness." People affect each other not merely through their physical nature but through **motivations brought about through reciprocal understanding** (Verständigung). This means that the "having an effect" that concerns the humanities is fundamentally different from physical causation: it passes through the medium of intentional, interpretive consciousness.

---

### **Section 4: The Concept of Motivation — Broader Extensions (p. 172, lines 4–13)**

Husserl widens the concept. The effect (Wirkung) persons have on each other by means of motivation belongs to psychology as well, but the **sociologist and historian** who investigate how persons affect each other in practical and social life are not seeking causal explanations in the natural-scientific sense. They aim instead to reconstruct the **motivational contexts** (Motivationszusammenhänge) that obtained. In a parenthetical remark, Husserl notes that his concept of motivation is taken so broadly that it encompasses "all the spheres of the phansic" (das Phansische) — a rare Husserlian technical term referring to the immanent phenomenality of the stream of consciousness, as distinct from the phenomenality of the world. The breadth of the concept signals Husserl's ambition: motivation is the universal lawfulness of subjective and intersubjective life.

---

### **Section 5: Motivation by Social Objectivities (p. 172, lines 14–27)**

Husserl extends the analysis beyond person-to-person motivation to include motivation by **social objectivities** (soziale Gegenständlichkeiten). The individual is not merely influenced by particular other persons (actual or imagined) but also by supra-individual formations that are experienced as "effective objectivities in their own right" — as "influencing powers" (Mächte). Husserl's examples are pointed: "He is afraid of 'the government' and carries out what it commands"; the police officer is feared not as an individual but as a "representative of the government"; the customs, the church, etc. are "felt as powers."

Seen from the objective perspective of the historian and sociologist, the task is to describe these interconnected social relations in general, concrete, and comparative terms — to delineate "universal class-concepts and rules, etc., just as in any morphology." This is a conception of sociology as a morphological or descriptive science, not a nomological-causal one.

---

### **Section 6: The Method of Historical Understanding — Nacherleben (pp. 172–173, lines 28–10)**

Husserl articulates the methodological demand of the historical humanities: to understand a community's development and its dependence on other communities, one must **immerse oneself (einleben) in the consciousness of the respective individuals** so deeply as to be able to "exactly relive their motivations." This is the method of *Nacherleben* — empathic re-living. The historian must bring to "givenness" (Gegebenheit) the interpretations, supposed experiences, even the "superstitious fantasies" of historical agents, in order to understand how those agents let themselves be influenced, guided, attracted, or repelled. The "real connections" (reale Zusammenhänge) of history consist precisely in the fact that under given circumstances, certain notions were "understandably" evoked in persons, motivating certain reactions, which in turn determined the course of their development.

This is a powerful formulation of the hermeneutic-phenomenological approach to history: historical explanation is not causal explanation of physical events but the reconstruction of chains of motivation as they were lived by the agents themselves.

---

### **Section 7: Motivation, Psychology, and the Autonomy of the Humanities (pp. 173–174, lines 11–33)**

This section, set off by asterisks as a new thematic unit, addresses the relationship between psychology as a natural science and the humanities as sciences of motivation.

Husserl concedes that everything that can be said about motivation "in terms of lawful generality" belongs to psychology as a **nomological natural science**, and that a general doctrine of the **essence of mind** (Wesen des Geistes) must serve as its foundation. Every psychic being that appears in history is, in principle, subject to concrete natural-scientific explanation; from the perspective of natural science, history furnishes data for psychological explanation.

However — and this is the crucial point — Husserl argues that a different kind of "psychology" is possible that takes no interest in natural-scientific findings, and this **for essential reasons**, not out of laziness or ignorance. This alternative science interests itself in **consciousness in itself**, in mind, individual minds, and their interconnections. In the sphere of what belongs properly to "humanity," **psychophysical causality is completely irrelevant**. The humanities study the unique "causality" of **intersubjective motivation** — the system of "motivating and being motivated" that constitutes a self-contained realm of intersubjective relations and "effects" in an historical and psychological sense. The context of effecting and being effected in the domain of the humanities is not governed by the spatial-temporal laws that govern natural factual beings.

---

### **Section 8: The Distinctive Temporality of Motivation (pp. 174–175, lines 1–28)**

Husserl develops a remarkable thesis about **time and motivation**. In the system of motivational effects, no "objective time" is involved — not the time of psychophysical nature as measured by clocks and sundials. Rather, the only relevant time is the one that **immanently belongs to minds and their conscious processes**, communicated by way of empathy. What is motivating *precedes* what is motivated (as in the case of an intellectual "result"), but this precedence does not mean determination at an objectively fixed spatial-temporal site. If someone acts because his master commanded it, then the act has the command as its "ground" and takes place "in consequence of" the command — but this is a motivational, not a causal-physical, connection.

Husserl grants that if everything in the world is exactly determined in spatial-temporal factual being, then every motivational context is "a piece of the causal nexus of necessity." But this does not collapse the distinction. Even in the case of logical motivation — where a premise motivates a conclusion — it is never "intuitively evident" that the factual occurrence of the thought of the premise could *causally dictate* the temporal occurrence of the conclusion in the sense of genuine natural causality. In a self-correcting footnote (15), Husserl states: "There is just no definiteness in the single monadic being."

The upshot is that the researcher of culture and mind — the historian, the sociologist — who pursues motivations and studies intersubjective contexts of reciprocal effect is **not a bad natural scientist making do with incomplete constructions**. Rather, the humanities have their own proper domain and method.

---

### **Section 9: The Humanities and the Surrounding World (pp. 175–176, lines 1–26)**

The humanities consider **mind in relation to the surrounding world of the mind** (Umwelt des Geistes). Nature and the spatial-temporal world remain posited — they are not bracketed phenomenologically. But the scientific interest of the humanities aims at investigating the different ways the **I-subject** relates himself to the world: the world of his presencing, judging, valuing, willing, and acting. How does the subject comport himself to things, to fellow human beings with whom he shares a unified **environing world** (Umwelt)?

Each person belongs to the surrounding world of every other. The things of my surrounding world are also things of the other's surrounding world. These "environing relations" are also "sociological relations." Here, things and persons are not investigated according to what they "simply are" in their empirical nature. Rather, persons come into view as **subjects who find themselves "in" a world** that is simultaneously "over against" them. They relate to this world through position-taking (judging, valuing, willing) and through abstaining from position-taking. In relation to fellow subjects, they **interact in communication**: in directed acts of love, trust, consolation, command, etc. They enter into "intercourse" (Verkehr), living together in the unity of a social life with active or reactive reciprocity.

---

### **Section 10: Communicative Acts as Constitutive of Sociality (pp. 175–176, lines 27–end)**

The final section of Appendix XII, set off by a sub-heading ("The acts constitutive for sociality: The 'communicative' acts"), develops Husserl's theory of **social or communicative acts** — a concept that anticipates later speech-act theory (Austin, Searle) and the pragmatics of Paul Grice.

Husserl distinguishes communicative acts from other acts that rest on empathy. Many acts involve understanding the other — for example, I may understand someone's behavior as love or as doing something for another person, without that other person even noticing. But **communicative acts** are something more: they are acts that **turn toward the other**, in which the other is taken as the one *toward whom I turn*. They are acts that contain within themselves the **idea that the other understands this turning-toward** and may modify his behavior accordingly — for instance, by responding with an act of a similar kind.

These communicative acts bring about a **"higher unity of consciousness" between person and person**. They integrate the objective world as a *common world* of judging, willing, and valuing. To the extent that the world incorporates this communicative relation, it acquires the character of a **social world** — a world that has "taken on significance for the mind" (geistige Bedeutsamkeit).

The translators add an apt modern illustration in footnote 16: using a turn signal while driving is a communicative act — it addresses other drivers, expresses an intention, and generates mutual expectations and beliefs.

# **APPENDIX XIII (IV of No. 1\)**

## ***Empathy of the Other Consciousness and Divine All-Consciousness*** **(1908)**

---

### **Section 1: The Problem of Empathy and Fulfillment (p. 177, lines 5–24)**

Appendix XIII opens with a compact statement of the **epistemological problem of empathy**. Husserl begins with the standard claim: we acknowledge other consciousness on the basis of empathy, which functions as a kind of **analogizing**. But he immediately presses on the problem: any analogizing, *according to its own essence*, seems to require the **possibility of adequate perception as its ultimate fulfilling intention**. That is, just as a belief about a picture is ultimately fulfilled when I see the pictured object itself and recognize it as what was meant, so empathy — if it is a legitimate form of analogizing — should in principle be fulfillable by a direct *perceiving* of the other's lived experiences.

Husserl tests this with the example of **touch**: if I see my own hand and imagine it being touched, the touched spot "comes with" a contact-sensation. If the other person's hand is touched, I grasp the corresponding contact-sensation only "pictorially" — through a kind of presentational belief, not through actual sensation. But can this presentational belief be **confirmed through perception**? Husserl's answer is decisive: **No, it cannot.** Empathy is not keyed to confirmation through perception, "for that would entail a contradiction." The lived experiences of the other are, in principle, inaccessible to my direct perceptual fulfillment. This is not a contingent limitation but an **essential** one: to perceive the other's consciousness directly would be to collapse the distinction between self and other, which is constitutive of empathy as such.

This passage crystallizes a problem that runs throughout Husserl's phenomenology of intersubjectivity and that later thinkers (Heidegger, Levinas, Merleau-Ponty) would develop in different directions: the other's consciousness is given to me as an irreducible **absence within presence** — appresented but never directly presented.

---

### **Section 2: The Divine All-Consciousness as a Thought-Experiment (pp. 177–178, lines 25–end of p. 177\)**

Having established that finite consciousness cannot, in principle, directly perceive the consciousness of another, Husserl turns to a remarkable thought-experiment: **divine all-consciousness** (Allbewusstsein).

If we ascribe to God the "capacity" to "peer into" the consciousness of others, this is conceivable only under the condition that **God's being holds within itself all other absolute being**. For God — and only for God — there would be no contradiction in possessing multiple visual fields simultaneously, as many as there are finite consciousnesses. God would not *need* empathy in the way finite subjects do, because God's all-consciousness would directly encompass what each finite consciousness experiences.

Yet Husserl immediately qualifies this with a self-interrogating footnote (1): "But is that conceivable?" This is not dogmatic theology but a phenomenological thought-experiment that tests the limits of the concept of consciousness by imagining an impossible-possible extension of it.

---

### **Section 3: Perspectival Givenness, Visual Fields, and the Coordination of Finite Consciousnesses (p. 178, lines 1–22)**

Husserl develops the thought-experiment further by considering the **perspectival constitution of things**. Even if an absolute all-consciousness existed, the structure of empathy and the **impossibility of one finite consciousness perceiving on the basis of another's visual field** would remain in effect. The essence of a thing requires that it manifest only **one actual profile** (Abschattung) in one Now; to another I, coordinated with the same thing, the thing discloses a *different* profile, filling up that I's visual field accordingly. The visual fields of distinct finite consciousnesses are **essentially different**. Yet in the empathizing apperception, one and the same thing is constituted as being within one and the same space for all individuals. This is the intersubjective constitution of objective reality.

God, however, sees the thing **from one side with my consciousness** and "at the same time" **from the other side with the consciousness of the other**. God identifies both apperceptions — but not in the way that I, as a finite subject, apperceive different appearances in a sequential flow. Rather, the identification is compared (tentatively) to the way I identify a thing and its mirror image — except that it is not a mirror-image relation but a unique **coordination relation**, to which there corresponds a "definite lawfulness in the coordination of the course of appearances in the diverse finite consciousnesses."

The translators' footnote (2) directs the reader to *Ideas I*, §43, for Husserl's treatment of divine perception, and to the immediately preceding text in manuscript folder B II 2 (1907–1908), *The Possibility of an All-Consciousness*, where Husserl argues that the All-Consciousness "encompasses all the finite ones, even in their contradictions and conflicts" yet, because it is an "excess of consciousness" not absorbed by any single one, it produces a **teleological unity** for them all.

---

## **Summary and Philosophical Significance**

### **Appendix XII**

Appendix XII is a rich, compressed sketch of Husserl's **phenomenological foundation for the humanities** (Geisteswissenschaften). Its key contributions include:

* A rigorous distinction between **motivation** (the lawfulness of conscious life) and **natural causality**, grounded in the noetic-noematic structure of intentional consciousness.  
* The claim that the humanities have their own **autonomous domain** — the intersubjective nexus of motivation — irreducible to natural science.  
* A distinctive account of **historical-hermeneutic method** as empathic re-living (Nacherleben) of motivational chains.  
* A theory of **communicative acts** that anticipates speech-act theory, locating the constitution of the social world in acts that are inherently addressed to the other and that carry the idea of mutual understanding within themselves.

### **Appendix XIII**

Appendix XIII is a brief but philosophically dense meditation on the **limits of intersubjective knowledge** and the concept of **divine consciousness**:

* It establishes that empathy is **essentially unfulfillable** by direct perception — the other's consciousness is constitutively absent from my perceptual field.  
* It uses the thought-experiment of divine all-consciousness to illuminate the **perspectival structure** of thing-constitution and the coordination of finite visual fields.  
* It raises, without resolving, the profound question of whether an all-encompassing consciousness is even *conceivable* — a question that points toward the deepest tensions in Husserl's transcendental idealism and his later reflections on teleology, monadic community, and the facticity of absolute consciousness.
