# Nature and Spirit: Lectures, Summer Semester 1919

## Introduction

In the typical forms that human culture has assumed among various peoples and in various epochs, scientific culture plays a very different role. An independently developed science — a science in our strict and proper sense — is not a necessary component of a highly developed culture, as the Mongolian culture plainly demonstrates.

Husserl opens the lecture course by drawing a distinction between culture in general and scientific culture in particular. His point is that the possession of genuine science, understood in its rigorous theoretical sense, is not a universal feature of advanced civilizations. This is not a claim about the inferiority of non-scientific cultures, but rather a claim about the contingency of scientific culture: it is a specific, historically situated achievement, not an inevitable accompaniment of civilizational sophistication. The reference to Mongolian culture serves as an empirical counterexample to the assumption that high culture necessarily entails science.

---

It is the distinguishing fundamental character of Greco-European culture that, paradoxically, it has scientific culture, that it is through and through scientific culture and wills to be such. It has scientific culture insofar as it first brought the idea of science to development within itself and concretely realized it in an endlessly growing sequence of disciplines. But science is not merely the title for a distinct realm of the highest cultural values within this culture; rather, we observe that from science a tendency issues forth to reshape the entire life of humanity, to permeate all domains of culture, to scientificize them, and thereby to impress upon humanity and its culture a new developmental form. Science in the pregnant sense has sprung from *philosophia* — that is, it is the product of a purely theoretical interest that detaches itself from all practical ramifications and becomes independent. But matters do not rest there. The product of theoretical *eros* soon takes on practical — indeed, practically all-encompassing — functions.

This long paragraph articulates the unique position of science within the Greco-European tradition. Several key claims are made. First, that European culture is distinguished not merely by possessing science but by being constitutively shaped by it — it is "through and through" scientific culture and understands itself as such. Second, that science arose from *philosophia*, from a purely theoretical interest that separates itself from practical concerns and becomes autonomous. Third, and crucially, that this theoretical product does not remain confined to theory: the "theoretical eros" generates practical consequences that ramify across the whole of human life. Husserl thus establishes a dialectical structure: science begins in pure theory, but its effects are totalizing — it transforms the practical orientation of the culture that produces it. The OCR renders the Greek *philosophia* imperfectly ("piAoaocpia"), but Husserl's point is clear: science originates in the Greek invention of disinterested theoretical inquiry.

---

Where science has entered the scene, where it has spoken its theoretical judgment, only that person acts in the highest sense rationally who lets himself be guided by it in practice. Every extension of science also extends the realm of this practical reason in the higher sense. This intensifies into an ideal as the highest practical ideal of humanity: namely, to found rigorous science in all spheres of possible knowledge, thus to create a true universitas of the sciences and thereby to set in motion a universal practical reason and a human life guided purely by scientific insights. The human being must raise himself to the transcendent height of a pure rational being, and communal humanity to the transcendent height of a rational humanity — a humanity that in all life and striving would be guided exclusively by motives whose legitimacy could be demonstrated from rational principles, according to scientific concepts and laws.

This paragraph develops the connection between theoretical science and practical reason into an explicit ideal. Husserl describes the emergence of what amounts to the Enlightenment vision: the aspiration to found rigorous science across every domain of possible knowledge and thereby to create a universitas scientiarum — a totality of the sciences — that would undergird a wholly rational form of human life. The language of "transcendent height" ("überschwängliche Höhe") carries Kantian overtones; Husserl speaks of humanity elevating itself to the status of a "pure rational being" and of a communal "rational humanity" whose entire practical life would be governed by scientifically legitimated motives. This is presented not as Husserl's own aspiration but as the inner telos of the European scientific tradition — an ideal that unfolded historically as a consequence of the Greek discovery of theory.

---

Thus the breakthrough of the idea of science entails, as a comprehensible consequence, the breakthrough of a universal practical rational ideal, a new idea of humanity as rational humanity. Already with the name of Plato is connected the conception of this ideal or principle — one that quite essentially determined the further fate of European humanity — of founding the entire life of humanity upon rational insight, and thus ultimately upon rigorous science. If this ideal already proved effective in stretches of antiquity, it became all the more a consciously guiding and finally an almost all-ruling motive in European culture since the Renaissance.

Husserl traces the practical-rational ideal back to Plato, making Plato the originating figure for the vision of a humanity whose entire life would be grounded in rational insight. He marks a historical progression: what was partially operative in antiquity became a consciously guiding and ultimately nearly all-dominant motive in modern European culture from the Renaissance onward. The phrase "quite essentially determined the further fate of European humanity" is strong — Husserl is asserting that this ideal is not merely one cultural factor among others but a constitutive force shaping the trajectory of European civilization.

---

One can see in this directly the most general sense of the Enlightenment epoch, indeed of the entire modern age, whose life-pulses still course through us. The repercussion of this practical ideal of humanity upon the development of the sciences themselves (from which it, as we saw, originally sprang) is tremendous: it shows itself in the fact that right at the entrance of the modern age, Bacon, the passionate herald of this ideal, designs in his *Instauratio magna* the great thought of a universal classification of all possible sciences and attaches to it the demand that all those still unworked should now be brought to actual execution. But knowledge is power. It requires an all-encompassing universitas scientiarum so that the idea of the new humanity and of a universal rational world-culture can be realized.

Husserl connects the Enlightenment and modernity as a whole to this Platonic ideal. The key structural point is a feedback loop: the ideal of rational humanity, which originally emerged from the sciences, in turn spurred the development of ever more sciences. Bacon's *Instauratio magna* is cited as emblematic: the vision of a universal classification and completion of all possible sciences. The famous Baconian dictum "knowledge is power" is woven into Husserl's narrative not as mere rhetoric but as the practical corollary of the ideal — the universitas scientiarum is needed not just for theoretical completeness but for the realization of a new form of human life.

---

Thus the unparalleled energy and passion with which the modern age progressed toward the founding of ever-new sciences and, within those already founded, advanced from theoretical discoveries to discoveries is understandable. Not only did the mathematical disciplines already firmly established in antiquity come into an extraordinarily rich flowering; natural sciences — sciences of nature in a completely new sense and style — were founded and filled the cultural world with the glory of their discoveries and their practical successes. And the same tremendous development was taken, though somewhat later, by the human sciences. On both sides the number of disciplines has already grown so large that we could hardly still enumerate their names.

This paragraph chronicles the explosive proliferation of the sciences in modernity as the working-out of the Platonic-Baconian ideal. Husserl marks two great branches: the natural sciences (Naturwissenschaften), which he emphasizes are sciences of nature "in a completely new sense and style" — a reference to the mathematized, experimental natural science inaugurated by Galileo and Newton — and the human sciences (Geisteswissenschaften), which followed somewhat later. The sheer number of disciplines on both sides has become overwhelming. This sets the stage for the problem the lectures will address: the lack of principled clarity about what distinguishes and connects these two great regions of science.

---

One would now expect — precisely given the tremendous intellectual energies that dominate our modern scientific culture, furthermore in view of the high level of modern sciences and the highly intensified demands placed on method and strictest scientificity, and in view of the proud self-confidence that fills their representatives — one would, I say, expect that principled clarity would prevail regarding the radical divisions that separate the main regions of scientific disciplines from a principled standpoint, regarding the essential character of their domains, and regarding the methodological character dependent upon it. But this is remarkably not the case at all. Naivety and reflection designate the development toward all higher rational achievement. (At the level of passive sensibility, apperceptions and passively arisen drives lead over into action at a naive level. Success and failure and their modifications provoke reflection upon the why and because of success or failure. And this takes place in various gradations at ever-higher levels of clarity and insight.)

Husserl states the central problematic: despite the immense achievements of modern science, there is no principled clarity about the fundamental regional divisions among scientific disciplines. The proud self-confidence of modern scientists is not matched by genuine insight into the essential character of their domains and methods. This is a striking diagnosis, and Husserl explains it through a general principle: all rational development proceeds through the dialectic of naivety and reflection. First there is naive activity, then reflection on its conditions of success and failure, then higher-order activity informed by reflection, and so on. The parenthetical elaboration sketches a phenomenological genealogy of reflection from the most basic level of passive sensibility upward.

---

So too science has arisen naively in imperfect, groping beginnings. But the level of genuine science emerges only through a radical philosophical reflection upon the sense, cognitive value, and achieved goals of such intellectual work. Every decisive advance in the high stage of the sciences, every new goal-setting by which sciences of a principally new type come to definitive foundation, grows in the same way. Naively or relatively naively won cognitions captivate the interest of a great genius who sees in them something principally new, the idea of a new goal dimly appearing, and who now directs his intellectual energy toward the clarification of this idea, toward the norm of the methodology demanded by it. Only then does a horizon of definite tasks to be accomplished open up, and in their execution the development of special methods of a typical, learnable, and habitually practicable form. This is progress, but also a danger: ever greater intellectual labor is saved, but ever greater stretches of thought become unintelligible through the mechanization of method; to external rationality — the verification through mutually confirming results — there does not correspond internal rationality, the understanding of the innermost sense and intellectual goal and of the fundamental methodological elements. The continued work in this direction (and such continued work is constantly necessary) is no longer felt as a need.

This is a pivotal paragraph. Husserl argues that science begins naively and achieves its proper form only through radical philosophical reflection on its own sense and method. He describes a recurring pattern in the history of science: a genius grasps something principally new, clarifies the idea, establishes the methodology, and opens a horizon of determinate research. But this very success breeds a danger — the mechanization of method. Once methods are codified and made routinely practicable, they can be applied without genuine understanding of their inner sense. Husserl distinguishes "external rationality" (the mutual confirmation of results) from "internal rationality" (comprehension of the deepest sense and goals of the method). Mechanized science achieves the former but loses the latter. The need for philosophical reflection ceases to be felt, precisely because the technical apparatus continues to produce results.

---

With the division of labor and the development of far-reaching special disciplines that claim the entire life-energy of particular researchers, the danger of the mechanization of method grows. So we find, together with the tremendous successes of modern science, also great and ever-more-keenly-felt deficiencies. The sciences flourish on the basis of a technicized methodology that can be successfully handled without being truly inwardly understood, and countless individual disciplines shoot up side by side without being accompanied by a clear consciousness of the research goals prescribed by the essential character of their radically distinguished regions and of the opposition of these goals to those of other scientific regions. But this ultimately hinders the progress of scientific culture; it binds the sciences to a lower level that indeed makes possible an infinite abundance of results but blinds one to the ultimate cognitive goals and to work in depth rather than in breadth.

Husserl deepens his diagnosis. The specialization and technicization of the sciences — which are themselves consequences of the modern scientific ideal — have created a situation in which no individual researcher grasps the principled foundations of even their own discipline, let alone the relations among regions. The proliferation of results in breadth is mistaken for genuine scientific progress, while the deeper questions about the sense of the regions, their interrelations, and their grounding are neglected. This is not merely an organizational complaint but a philosophical one: without insight into the essential character of the regions, science remains at a "lower level."

---

What I understand here by the expression "region," in order to grasp principially closed groups of disciplines, you can make clear to yourselves by considering mathematics or physics. Even before all scientific-theoretical discussion it will be palpable to you that all the disciplines of pure analysis — arithmetic, number theory, function theory, and so on — belong together and that botany has nothing to do within their circle. Likewise it is clear that mechanics, acoustics, optics, and so on belong together internally, that is, for some principled reasons to be demonstrated, but not mechanics and psychology. Likewise, that literary science, art science, religious science, and so on as cultural sciences essentially — regionally, as I say — belong together, and so on. Yet it is a difficult task to bring what determines the unity of such regions to principled insight. Hence there is wavering and unclarity when, for example, the question of the separation or regional connection of analysis and geometry, or of mathematics and formal logic, is considered, or especially — something that will occupy us at length — when the relation of psychology to the natural sciences or to the human sciences is considered. A definitive decision can only be given by principled insight, which however is a most difficult matter and does not lie in the line of technicized science. Thus it is also understandable why all attempts at a classification of the sciences since Bacon's time have failed.

Husserl introduces the concept of "region" (Region) as a principled grouping of scientific disciplines that belong together not by convention but by essential connection. He offers intuitive examples — pure analysis hangs together internally, as do the physical sciences and the cultural sciences — but insists that articulating the principle of regional unity is genuinely difficult. The wavering that persists about the regional placement of certain disciplines (geometry relative to analysis, psychology relative to natural science and human science) testifies to this difficulty. The failure of all post-Baconian attempts at a classification of the sciences is a symptom of the deeper failure to achieve principled insight into regional essences.

---

A classification of the sciences, if it is to be carried out in a grand sense, is obviously nothing other than a systematic clarification of the scientific regions that are separated on principled grounds. The regions of possible scientific domains are to be apportioned, and in each region what essentially belongs together is to be connected — what no arbitrary act can separate — according to principles that a priori forbid the separation. (Were all domains of possible knowledge and science ordered side by side and were they to divide the unity of possible objectivities in general the way sea and continents divide the surface of the earth, then the division would not be a difficult matter. But the situation is at best like this only within a region, with respect to the division of a radically unified science into special disciplines, and not with respect to the unity of all cognitive objectivity according to regions. The difficulty here is an extraordinarily deep-lying one, rooted in the deepest essence of cognition. In a confusing manner — as for example with respect to the psychical — the same concretely given objects seem, depending on standpoint and attitude, to fall into sciences of quite different character and methodological style, while it is not clear what the principled change of sense actually consists in that conditions this reordering.)

This paragraph elaborates the idea of a classification of the sciences as a philosophical task. Husserl contrasts a naive picture — regions of objectivity neatly divided like continents and oceans — with the actual situation, which is far more difficult. The difficulty is that the same objects (e.g., the psychical) can appear to belong to radically different sciences depending on the standpoint and attitude adopted. The concrete given object does not simply declare its regional identity; rather, the same object can be taken up naturalistically or in a human-scientific orientation, and the principled sense-change that makes this possible is precisely what needs to be clarified. This is a direct anticipation of Husserl's later analysis of the difference between the naturalistic and the personalistic attitude.

---

It does not however require a classification carried out from the ultimate principles in order to principially delimit individual regions — such as the mathematical and, again, the natural-scientific — and to gradually penetrate into the innermost sense of the being of this delimited object-sphere. Yet even this task already involves tremendous difficulties, and quite especially this concerns the regions of nature and spirit, which seem mutually to encompass one another and then again to be radically separate. The remarkable thing, however, is that a deepening into the problems that press themselves upon us in the attempt to clarify these regions and their separation very soon leads to the recognition that they are closely connected with the great worldview problems, that what is at stake here are not merely interesting special problems of theory of science, but problems whose solution also decides the "standpoint" of philosophical world-cognition.

Husserl focuses the lectures' specific theme: even without a complete classification of all the sciences, one can work on the principled delimitation of specific regions — and the regions of nature and spirit are both the most pressing and the most difficult cases. They seem at times to encompass one another and at times to be radically separate. Crucially, Husserl claims that these apparently technical questions of Wissenschaftstheorie (theory of science) are in fact connected with the deepest worldview problems. To clarify the sense of "nature" and "spirit" is not merely to tidy up the organization of the sciences; it is to decide fundamental questions about the character of reality and our relation to it.

---

For example: what seems more familiar and clearer to us — accustomed as we are from school to the basic elements of modern natural science — than the concept of nature that determines the regional idea of natural science and thereby sharply closes off a most comprehensive complex of special sciences? But not only was this concept of nature by no means a self-evident pregiven; rather, it had to be worked out only after long philosophical struggle in order to found modern natural science. It is also today by no means already a sufficiently clarified concept. As its creators had worked it out, it sufficed to provide a framework for theoretical researches, for determinate goals of natural-scientific work, and for the formation of a determinate methodology. On the other hand, it was precisely the unclarities with which this concept of nature remained afflicted in certain directions — directions not keenly felt in practical scientific work — that allowed that naturalism to take root and flourish ever more luxuriantly, a naturalism that in various forms of materialism, sensualism, and positivism buried the development of a more fruitful philosophy and brought to dominance in the widest circles a worldview that made a truly free and great spiritual life, one turned toward the eternal goals of humanity, impossible.

This paragraph develops the example of the concept of nature. Husserl makes a historical and philosophical point simultaneously. Historically, the concept of nature that grounds modern natural science was not self-evident but required long philosophical labor (the work of Galileo, Descartes, and others). Philosophically, this concept, though adequate as a methodological framework for natural-scientific research, harbored unclarities that bore philosophical fruit of a poisonous kind: naturalism. The unclarities in the concept of nature — its failure to specify its own limits and its relation to the spiritual — allowed materialism, sensualism, and positivism to claim that nature (in the natural-scientific sense) exhausts reality. This naturalistic worldview, Husserl charges, stifled the development of genuine philosophy and rendered impossible a truly free spiritual existence oriented toward humanity's highest goals.

---

The natural scientists trade with an inherited philosophical capital, so to speak, as technicians; they have become, as it were, engineers of science. They are tremendous practitioners; they understand how to put the philosophically developed methodological norms into masterful operation and to apply them, to apply them to ever-new domains of experience and to adapt them. But as thinkers they have fallen back to the stage of naivety. Fundamentally no differently from the prescientific human being, they take nature as absolute being. They do not see the irrevocable relativity of nature to spirit, for which as cognizing spirit nature is there and in whose experiential phenomena it constitutes itself. They do not suspect the depths of the transcendental correlations according to which nature and spirit constitute themselves transcendentally and in mutual determination. Habitually living in the attitude of external experience that gives them their research-horizon, they find the physical and the psychical, the bodily and the spiritual, empirically-externally connected, and now, since they already accept the physical as absolute reality, they fall into a perverted naturalization of the spiritual, which becomes for them an accidental — namely empirically-factual — annex to the physical, to physical, spatial-thingly connections. Thus everything spiritual in the higher sense — free subjects and subject-communities and therein free spiritual formations — becomes accidental occurrences in the spatial world, regulated there at bodies according to contingent natural laws. And the same holds for all ideas and ideals. They, which from the standpoint of an absolute consideration of reality are the forces that are effective in the ultimate and truest sense, become meaningless facts within a meaningless world-machinery.

This is one of the sharpest passages in the Introduction. Husserl characterizes natural scientists as philosophical naifs who, despite their technical mastery, have regressed to a prescientific ontological attitude: they take nature as absolute being and fail to see its constitutive relativity to spirit. The key philosophical point is the concept of transcendental correlation: nature and spirit are not simply two separate domains, one of which could be absolute and the other derivative. Rather, they constitute themselves "transcendentally and in mutual determination." The natural scientist, habituated to the external attitude, observes an empirical connection between the physical and the psychical (at bodies) and erroneously naturalizes the spiritual, reducing it to a factual annex of physical processes. The consequence is a worldview in which subjects, communities, and all ideas and ideals become "meaningless facts within a meaningless world-machinery" — a devastating phrase that anticipates the Crisis.

---

So dominant has this naturalism become that it even paralyzes the human sciences and constantly imputes to them the phantom of a research into spirit according to natural-scientific method as the supposedly alone valid one, and even where the practitioners of the human sciences protest against this, it can be observed that they are sure of their ground only so long as they pursue concrete special research, while they prove unable to understand the proper sense of their own sciences and to make it fruitful for a general worldview. Indeed, even more: the naturalism of the worldview also hinders the further development of the human sciences themselves, which, like the factual sciences of all regions, cannot always remain at the stage of descriptive and concrete sciences.

Husserl extends the diagnosis to the human sciences (Geisteswissenschaften). Naturalism does not merely distort philosophy — it actively paralyzes the human sciences by imposing upon them the phantom-image of natural-scientific method as the sole legitimate mode of inquiry. Even those Geisteswissenschaftler who resist this imposition lack the philosophical resources to articulate and defend the distinctive sense of their own work. Their concrete special research proceeds well enough, but they cannot translate it into a general understanding of the nature of spirit or a worldview adequate to their own findings. Moreover, naturalism hinders the very development of the human sciences by preventing them from advancing beyond the descriptive to the theoretical-explanatory and the a priori.

---

Just as descriptive natural science has above it a theoretically explanatory natural science, and this again stands in necessary connection with purely a priori disciplines that work out the a priori of nature, so too for the human sciences there exists as a postulate the founding of a priori disciplines of spirit and, by means of these, theoretically explanatory human sciences. This task, however, is either not seen or completely perverted in its sense. One demands, for example, together with us a theoretical explanation of the concretely spiritual, but believes it must be accomplished through a natural-scientific psychology. One is caught up in a complete unclarity about the radically different sense of natural-scientific and human-scientific research directions. One does not see the innermost unity of the natural and the spiritual and the two-sidedness of the questions that spring from it. Ultimately the path leads here into philosophy. But all this is far from being acknowledged, precisely because the receptivity for philosophically clarifying work is still lacking — work that must be directed at the deepest sense of nature and spirit, at the deepest sense of natural-scientific and human-scientific goal-setting and method. Of course there has also been lacking until now a philosophy that would have done justice to the greatness of this task.

Husserl articulates a positive programme: just as natural science rests upon a priori disciplines (geometry, kinematics, etc.), so too the human sciences require their own a priori disciplines of spirit that would ground genuinely explanatory Geisteswissenschaften. This is the postulate of an "ontology of spirit" parallel to the ontology of nature. The reason this postulate has not been fulfilled is twofold: on the one hand, the demand for theoretical explanation of the spiritual is perverted into a demand for natural-scientific psychology (the naturalistic misunderstanding); on the other hand, the philosophical work required to clarify the deepest sense of nature and spirit has not yet been carried out. Husserl acknowledges that philosophy itself has so far been unequal to the task.

---

In any case, the practitioners of the human sciences too have remained, so to speak, at the level of the philosophically naive technique of human-scientific method. Just like the natural scientists, they too, proud of the undeniable force and abundance of their achievements, want nothing to do with philosophy, want to take nothing from its clarifying work. But that is simply the curse of the modern specialization and technicization of the sciences and, in consequence, their detachment from the original sources of intuition from which all science has sprung — sprung with respect to the originary concept-formations that determine goal and method.

Husserl generalizes the charge: not only natural scientists but also human scientists have succumbed to the technization of their methods and have lost contact with the intuitive sources from which scientific concept-formation originally drew its sense. The word "curse" (Fluch) signals the depth of Husserl's concern. The detachment from "original sources of intuition" (Urquellen der Intuition) is not merely an intellectual deficiency but a distortion of the entire scientific enterprise, which draws its legitimacy from intuitive evidence.

---

There manifests in the modern further-development of scientific culture a deep tragedy of that Platonic ideal of humanity: namely, that it had to be acknowledged in its rational necessity and moreover had to determine the development, but on the other hand, in its working-out (again by immanent necessity), it assumed the form of a mechanized and mechanizing scientific culture that robbed the ideal of its power to awaken and promote free spirituality. Science is supposed to make us free — first theoretically free and then free in our entire activity and creation. But the specializing and thereby technicizing science does not make us even theoretically free. Only one who at every point has access to the deepest spiritual sources of method, only one who can understand the deepest and ultimate sense of cognitive achievement and can give account of the absolute sense of the being logically constituted by science in all its regions, has true theoretical freedom — he judges from the autonomy of true and ultimate insight. But nobody has this insight! The insight that we thinkers of the constantly dogmatic, because merely technical, science have is like the insight of the art-understanding and often enough productive and genial craftsman, who practically very well understands why he proceeds this way and not otherwise, chooses his tools and means this way and not otherwise, and yet has no theory and lacks theoretical insight.

This paragraph presents the "tragedy" at the heart of the modern scientific ideal. The Platonic vision of science as liberation — theoretical first, then practical — has been betrayed by the very success of the ideal's realization. The mechanization and specialization of science, which are immanent consequences of the ideal itself, have stripped science of its liberating power. True theoretical freedom would require insight into the deepest sources of method and the absolute sense of being as constituted by science — but, Husserl confesses, "nobody has this insight." The comparison of the modern scientist to a skilled craftsman who operates effectively without theoretical understanding is pointed: the scientist is a master of technique but not a master of meaning.

---

With this example the problem has already been carried over into the practical sphere of reason. How shall the acting human being attain the dignity of a practical rational being in the sense of the Enlightenment ideals, even if all possible sciences were set to work and all domains of natural and spiritual happening theoretically scientificized, when the mechanized scientific enterprise with its technicized methods indeed provides a secure confidence in its achievements, in the truth of the worked-out propositions and theories, but provides nothing less than insight into the sense of being itself with all its scientifically worked-out predicates, to which the truths pertain?

Husserl extends the diagnosis to the practical domain. Even a complete system of sciences, if mechanically operated, would fail to yield the kind of understanding needed for genuine practical rationality. Confidence in scientific results is not the same as insight into the meaning of being. Without the latter, the Enlightenment ideal of a rational practical life cannot be realized, because rational action in the deepest sense requires not merely correct information but understanding of the sense of the reality to which action is directed.

---

It is clear that the misery of these times (contrary to all the transcendent expectations of the Enlightenment epoch in the eighteenth century) has arisen together with the progress of the sciences, and has arisen although this progress itself has fulfilled the most transcendent expectations — precisely because this fulfillment is a fulfillment in the form of methodically technicized special sciences. And it is further clear that this tragedy of scientific culture can be overcome only in a new and in a new sense philosophical age — an age that does not abandon the Platonic idea of scientific reason but rescues it, and rescues it through the philosophical work that goes hand in hand with special-scientific work, work that aims everywhere at ultimate clarifications, at a constant enlivening and deepening of insight, everywhere going back to the original sources of knowledge, to the ultimate sense-bestowals, to the ultimate sense-acquisitions and sense-syntheses that lift everything researched in isolation — in the isolation of a scientific region and discipline, and thus one-sidedly researched — out of its isolation and one-sided consideration. For all sources of truth spring from a single source and have their inner unity, from which the unity of the true and ultimate sense of absolute reality itself springs. Only thus — not with a single blow, but steadily progressing and in the sense of a demand that is step by step fulfilled and must be ever newly posed — shall we attain that genuine theoretical and then also practical freedom: a completely insightful, that is, truly philosophical science and a truly philosophical life — a life that is not merely regulated from the outside by a methodical but soulless science, but is illuminated from within by the light of theoretical and practical insight.

This climactic paragraph of the Introduction offers Husserl's positive vision: the overcoming of the tragedy of scientific culture through a new philosophical age. Philosophy must not replace science but accompany it, providing the perpetual work of clarification and return to sources that keeps science alive to its own meaning. Husserl insists that all sources of truth spring from a single source — a thesis of the fundamental unity of being and knowledge — and that the one-sided abstractions of specialized research must be reintegrated through philosophical insight. The goal is not merely a better science but a better life: a "truly philosophical life" illuminated from within by genuine understanding, not merely governed from without by soulless technique. The echoes of Plato, of Descartes's aspiration to universal science, and of the Crisis (yet to be written) are unmistakable.

---

These lectures want to serve this high goal in all natural modesty and limitation. As philosophical lectures, they do not want to interfere in the active work of the sciences — to teach the researcher and master of his field a better way — but rather they want to clarify and to seek principles of clarification. As philosophical, they are directed toward absolute being, i.e., toward a world-cognition that goes back through the medium of the sciences to the ultimate origins, but that also, in the cognition of the essential unity that connects prescientific and scientific consciousness, already makes the givens of the former — without which science can never be made intelligible — into a theme. Nature, as it objectifies itself in spirit at ever-higher levels and finally natural-scientifically; spirit, as individual and social, shaping for itself a spiritual environment as a cultural world and developing itself therein, but also as it externalizes itself as spirit in nature and becomes a natural-scientific theme — these shall be our study. And as philosophers we want to keep our gaze constantly directed toward the ultimate unity that connects nature and spirit at their source, and toward the world-position that is thereby theoretically and practically prescribed for us.

This final paragraph of the Introduction states the programme of the lectures. They aim not to intervene in specialized research but to pursue philosophical clarification. Their theme is "absolute being" — not a metaphysical absolute in the pre-critical sense, but the world understood through the medium of the sciences and traced back to its ultimate constitutive origins. The lecture course will examine both nature's progressive objectification in and through spirit, and spirit's self-development through culture and its externalization in nature. The ultimate aim is the "ultimate unity" connecting nature and spirit at their source — a unity that carries both theoretical and practical implications. This programme effectively outlines the entire trajectory of the lectures to follow.

## Part I. General Introduction to Phenomenology

### The Return from Science to Prescientific Consciousness

After this introduction we go to work. We seek clarity about nature and spirit, and yet we have sciences of nature and spirit. It is self-evident to us that every question about what an object is can be answered not from naive experience and knowledge, but only through the theoretical achievement of science. It is self-evident to us that the lowest thinking and asserting, oriented immediately by experience and by apperceptions that have grown up in the naive course of living, does indeed lead to assertions that are not without value for the purposes of practical life, but that these are unclear, fluid, full of vague indeterminacy. Only through the logical work of science are they converted into a fixed truth, acknowledged by every rational judge, built from fixed, strictly identifiable concepts. It is thus self-evident that only science can say what an objectivity truly is; in other words: genuine being is throughout the correlate of science. On the other hand, we make clear to ourselves that all the work of science ultimately presupposes a substrate that lies before science. An objectivity must first be given to us in intuition, and originally in perceptions, as existing reality, before thinking can be brought into play, before the question "what is this existing thing?" can be posed, before the truth or genuine being can be worked out in the graded logical elaboration of the vague concepts and propositions of experience.

Husserl begins Part I by articulating a tension. On the one hand, only science can tell us what an object truly is — "genuine being" is the correlate of science. On the other hand, science presupposes a prescientific substrate: objects must first be given in intuition and perception before any theoretical determination can begin. This double acknowledgment sets the agenda: rather than diving into the developed sciences, the philosopher must return to the prescientific experience that underlies and makes possible all scientific work. The vague, fluid, practically useful but theoretically indeterminate understanding of objects in naive experience is the starting point from which science elaborates its fixed concepts — and therefore it is to this starting point that philosophical clarification must return.

---

We further note that, in addition, the givens of immediate perception — the perceptual things given to us in sensory embodiment through seeing, hearing, and so on — in the natural course of living set manifold spiritual activities into play and accordingly undergo manifold apprehensions that vary from subject to subject but also spread through tradition within subject-groups and are then relatively firmly attached to the things of the intuitive surrounding world, belonging thus to the vague experiential stock to which scientific work is referred back. All the noematic apperceptions of the natural human being belong here, as do all the practical and traditional apperceptions by virtue of which the human being outside scientific thought-circles overlays his surrounding world with countless empirically vague predicates drawn from his life-experience or from inherited experiences of others.

This paragraph extends the analysis of the prescientific substrate. It is not just raw sensory perception that precedes science; rather, the surrounding world as it is lived in carries layers of apperception — interpretive overlays that vary from individual to individual and from culture to culture. These include "noematic apperceptions" (ways of taking objects as being such-and-such) and practical-traditional apperceptions (culturally transmitted ways of understanding and valuing things). All of this constitutes the "vague experiential stock" upon which scientific work operates. The key philosophical point is that what the scientist encounters as a given is already multiply interpreted through pre-theoretical activities of consciousness.

---

(Finally, it is to be noted from the outset that when scientific work with its logically determining activities enters into action, it can take different directions. The empirically-intuitively given and prescientifically multiply apperceived can have different dimensions of logical indeterminacy and determinability. If one dimension awakens the theoretical interest, then logical determination, bound to the unity of logical interconnection (which alone makes systematic development possible), is held fast in one and the same dimension. Thus it can come about that in the general investigation of an object-sphere an endlessly progressing science grows up that never in its course encounters the problems related to the other regions that continually belong to its objects. We must keep this possibility in view from the outset for our theme "nature and spirit." Perhaps, for example, "nature" is a title for questions that the experienced surrounding world poses to theoretical research — questions that go exclusively in a single direction of regard, one in which the spiritual, though perhaps inextricably interwoven with it, is abstracted from as a correlative theme of inquiry and must be properly abstracted from.)

This parenthetical paragraph introduces a crucial methodological point. The prescientific lifeworld has multiple "dimensions" of indeterminacy and determinability, and scientific inquiry, by its very nature, follows one dimension at a time. This means that a science can develop enormously within its chosen dimension without ever confronting the problems belonging to other dimensions of the same objects. Applied to the theme of nature and spirit: "nature" may simply be the title for one direction of theoretical inquiry into the experienced world — a direction that systematically abstracts from the spiritual dimension, which is nonetheless always co-present. This explains how natural science can flourish without any awareness of its own abstractive character, and why the natural scientist can mistake the abstraction for the whole.

---

In every way we are thus called upon to pursue a path of clarification opposite to the one we would initially expect. Although it is quite correct that only science can tell us what nature and spirit and every objectivity truly are, it is nevertheless not our task to work our way into the endless series of truths, justifications, and theories of the natural and human sciences. We would thereby become very learned, but clarity about the ultimate sense of nature and of spirit — a sense that runs through all these truths and theories — we would not obtain. To be sure, it is in some way logically elaborated and determined through them, but all the logical determination draws its sense from a prelogical source, from an original source in which what, by our conjecture, is inseparably connected shows itself — what the necessary abstraction of logical-scientific work separates. At this original source an original sense-bestowal for the world considered under the titles "nature" and "spirit" takes place. Only when we have understood this primitive sense-bestowal can we hope to understand the higher achievement of science; only then can we pose the higher-order problem of clarifying this achievement and, in connection with it, the sense of the scientifically true being of nature and spirit.

This paragraph states the methodological thesis of the lectures: the path to clarity runs not through the developed sciences (which would yield erudition but not insight) but back to the prescientific sources of sense. All scientific determination derives its meaning from a "prelogical" source — the original intuitive encounter with the world. At this source, nature and spirit appear in their original, unseparated connection; it is only the "necessary abstraction" of scientific work that splits them apart. The philosopher's task is to recover this original sense-bestowal and thereby to understand both the unity and the separation of nature and spirit. Only on this basis can the higher-order question — what does science achieve, and what does scientifically determined being mean? — be properly posed.

---

Only on this path do we solve the problem that is not already solved but rather first posed by however rich a development of rigorous sciences: the problem of what the world in truth and in its ultimate source-sense is, and thereby also the problem of making good the theoretical violation of the world, indeed of making it useful in the highest sense by transforming it into an understanding of the world.

This brief paragraph sharpens the point: the sciences do not answer the philosophical question about the world but rather pose it. The "theoretical violation of the world" — the abstraction and one-sidedness inherent in all specialized research — needs to be redeemed through a philosophical comprehension that would integrate what science separates. The phrasing is strong: science "violates" the world by treating its abstractions as the whole truth.

---

We begin, then, by going back from science to prescientific consciousness. Not as if it were now a matter of a historical-anthropological investigation into how humanity represented the world before the advent of science, or how various peoples who even today lack science represent the world to themselves. The prescientific consciousness that we mean here, and that alone can come into question for us, is also for us human beings of a scientific culture constantly demonstrable, or at the very least evidently producible through a methodical exclusion of all apperceptions stemming from former theoretical acts. It is after all evident in advance that we constantly, whether or not we are engaged in theoretical thinking, find ourselves surrounded by an intuitive world.

Husserl clarifies a potential misunderstanding: the return to prescientific consciousness is not an exercise in historical anthropology. It is not about how primitive peoples thought before science existed. Rather, prescientific consciousness is a permanent structural feature of our experience as conscious beings — even within scientific culture. We always already find ourselves surrounded by an intuitive world that precedes and underlies any theoretical activity. This intuitive world can be recovered at any time through the methodical exclusion (Ausschaltung) of the theoretical apperceptions that have been deposited upon it. The phenomenological method of reduction is here anticipated in its basic form.

---

This world is ceaselessly there for us, with a stock of sense that is only occasionally given over to theoretical work, and necessarily given over, for how could thinking ever come into play without objects already being given to it in consciousness with a certain endowment of sense — objects that, so to speak, address questions to us, calling upon us to determine them conceptually in this or that respect, to find the laws of their behavior, and so on? A pregiven object can, as at higher stages of scientific thinking, be one far from intuition — a theoretical product stemming from earlier thinking and perhaps only symbolically and cognitively made present — as when the astronomer reflects on perturbations in the orbit of Mars and seeks to fix theoretically the unknown member of a mathematical expression determining them. But it is clear that it belongs to the sense of every such theoretical formation that it can be step by step unwound, and, following the mediations, finally led back to intuition.

This paragraph establishes a fundamental thesis: all theoretical products, however abstract, are ultimately traceable back to intuitive givens. Even the astronomer's symbolic-mathematical reasoning about orbital perturbations is meaningful only because it can, in principle, be "unwound" step by step back to perceptual experience. This is the principle of the ultimate intuitive grounding of all scientific meaning. The prescientific world is not merely the historical antecedent of science; it is the permanent foundation from which all scientific sense draws its life.

---

We are thus referred to objects of the immediately intuitive world, which lie before all theoretical thinking and which already had, as objects in prescientific consciousness, a sense-content that served as substrate for scientific work. Moreover, even the researcher who theorizes in the most intuition-remote mathematical spheres is, during this thinking, surrounded by an intuitive world; the intuition-remote thinking is only one layer in the total stream of his waking conscious life, and only an occasional one; a constant layer, however, is the experience of world-intuition in the most literal sense. This world constantly stands before us with changing things and changing modes of appearance, whether we attend to it and reflect upon it or not. Into this world we live, we think, feel, value, act, and create into it, and in the alternation of all such activities it is pregiven. It is not merely given in general; it is a world presenting itself in manifold phenomena within our subjectivity itself, a world creating an original sense from itself, from these "phenomena."

Husserl emphasizes the permanence of the intuitive world as a layer of experience that is always there, even during the most abstract theoretical work. The world is not only an object of occasional attention; it is the constant background of all conscious life, the horizon within which thinking, feeling, valuing, and acting take place. The closing phrase is significant: the world creates "an original sense from itself, from these 'phenomena.'" This is the first explicit use of the concept of "phenomena" in the text, and it signals Husserl's phenomenological commitment: the world is not a thing behind appearances but something that constitutes its sense through and within the appearances themselves.

---

But the following is now to be noted: although this world is designated as a pregiven, intuitive, experienced world, it is so only in the determinate sense of our consideration. When we interrupt the naturally naive flow of our consciousness, so to speak, with the question of how the world was conscious in this flow, a reductive process is needed to work out the intuitive world, and in the process various hidden theoretical components must be excluded even from what is already intuitive. The temperature that we read from the thermometer, the air pressure that we read from the barometer, the weight, the refraction of light, and the like, which under given intuitive circumstances we loosely say we "see" — we of course do not see them; the actual seeing, the intuiting of the surrounding world, has for our scientific products, so to speak, many colorings that are precipitates of theoretical achievements. But we can always also reflect upon this, we can ask about the source of the colorings given to things in consciousness, and in asking what temperature-degrees, air-pressure-degrees, weight, and so on actually mean, we are led by the proper sense of the consciousness of these matters back to physical concept-formation, to theoretical processes, and then back to the pre-theoretical intuitive stock as a necessarily demanded one.

Husserl introduces the idea that even our "intuitive" experience is already permeated by sedimented theoretical determinations. When we "see" temperature or weight, we are not perceiving in a pure sense but apprehending through concepts deposited by prior scientific activity. The phenomenological return to prescientific consciousness therefore requires a reductive process — a methodical stripping-away of these sedimented theoretical layers to reach the genuinely pre-theoretical intuitive content. This is a preliminary articulation of what will become the method of phenomenological reduction.

---

We further note that the elaboration of an intuitively given or, if you will, experiential world before all encroachments of theoretical achievement gives the concept of experience a determinate sense through this relation to theorizing. Insofar as the human being is active not only as theoretically achieving but in many other directions — for example, as aesthetically, ethically, technically achieving — the prospect opens of the possibility and perhaps necessity of otherwise-directed reductions and of the exposition of a pregiven world as a world of "experience" that lies not only before all theory but before all human accomplishing. And in fact this will matter very much for grasping the original source of the idea of "nature" — specifically as the idea of nature that constitutes the theme of modern natural science. And not only that: this kind of step-by-step separating and excluding of apperceptive layers in the sense of the world given in consciousness and entwined with apperceptions of different sources are in general the indispensable methodological prerequisites for understanding the sense of the original goals that the radically distinguished sciences set for themselves. The original structure of the world of prescientific consciousness and the demarcations prefigured in its own sense fundamentally determine essentially different directions of possible theoretical research and thereby the principially possible sciences.

This paragraph draws a crucial methodological consequence. The reduction cannot stop at the removal of theoretical overlays; there are also aesthetic, ethical, and practical apperceptions that must be stripped away to reach the most fundamental level of experience — a world given before all human "accomplishing" whatsoever. The step-by-step peeling-off of apperceptive layers is itself the method for understanding how the various sciences get their distinct orientations: the original structure of prescientific experience, with its own internal demarcations, prefigures the possible directions of theoretical inquiry. This is the phenomenological foundation for the classification of the sciences.

### Ego-Life as Constant Perceiving: Impression and Reproduction

If we try to penetrate the typical structure of the pregiven world, we encounter a first division that we intelligibly describe as that between subjects and things. But in order to penetrate to complete clarity, we pose the most radical question: concerning what can be pregiven to the knowing I as pre-theoretical. Perhaps it will turn out that what we intuit pre-theoretically as "world" constitutes only one portion of all pregivenness, and it is very important to characterize this portion and to understand the function of the rest. (What we now investigate bears the title "Transcendental Aesthetic.") We can begin the necessary description in the following way, wherein you must constantly convince yourselves that nothing is said here, and nothing may be said, that is not actually demonstrated in indubitable evidence, and that all expressions we use have the mere character of purely descriptive expressions.

Husserl now begins the phenomenological analysis proper. The most radical question concerns what is given to the knowing I before any theoretical activity. He signals that the intuitive "world" may be only one portion of the total field of pregivenness — a claim whose implications will unfold later. The parenthetical reference to "Transcendental Aesthetic" deliberately invokes Kant's term but gives it a phenomenological reinterpretation: this will be an investigation of the most basic structures of givenness, but conducted through pure description of what is evident in consciousness rather than through a deduction of conditions of possibility.

---

Each of us says and can say: "I am." He thereby expresses the most absolute of all conceivable evidences. I am, and I am in that I live. My life is an unceasing stream of subjective experiencing, and enclosed within it a stream of ceaseless "consciousness" that is in itself consciousness of something — consciousness in which I have something conscious, and that in various forms: I live in the form of "I perceive," and perceiving I have conscious a perceptual object; I live in the form of remembering, expecting, fantasizing, thinking, collecting, relating, universal and particular predicating, and so on, wherein something, an objectivity, becomes conscious in the mode of the past as remembered, or future and expected, or fantasized, or a subject of predicates, or a particular of a universality, a set, a law, and so on. Again we find in the stream of "I live" conscious events of the form "I take pleasure in something" (and therein I am conscious of something pleasant), "I am grieved about something" (and therein something sad is conscious), "I wish this or that" (and therein something as desirable, as good, is conscious), "I resolve" (and therein a resolve is conscious), "I act" (and therein an action or deed is conscious).

This paragraph offers a phenomenological survey of the forms of conscious life. Starting from the Cartesian "I am," Husserl unfolds the essential structure of ego-life: it is a continuous stream of experiences, each of which is "consciousness of something" — i.e., is intentional. The list of intentional forms is broad, encompassing perception, memory, expectation, fantasy, thinking, predicating, as well as affective and volitional acts (pleasure, grief, wishing, resolving, acting). Each form has its own characteristic way of presenting its object: the remembered object is given as past, the expected as future, the fantasized as not-real, and so on. This is a preliminary phenomenological taxonomy of intentional life.

---

I, my consciousness in such countless forms, and the conscious-therein as such in the correlative forms — all this is obviously a quite indubitable pre-theoretically given, indeed in a certain way the title of the most original pregivennesses. In another way again we say: it is what makes pregivennesses possible at all. For it is entirely evident: for me something is given and to be given, to be given only through some modes of consciousness (in intentional experiences). But as being-for-me, there is given not only consciousness itself and its immanent contents, but also what is foreign to it, external to it.

This paragraph draws a fundamental conclusion from the preceding survey. The I and its conscious life constitute the most original level of pregivenness — the condition without which nothing else could be given at all. Everything that is given is given through modes of consciousness. But the givenness extends beyond what is immanent to consciousness: what is "foreign" to the I, "external" to it, is also given through consciousness. This is the intentional thesis in its most basic form: consciousness gives both itself and what is other than itself.

---

If we remained in the I-attitude, we would have to continue roughly as follows: through the conscious experiences in which my life flows as ego-life, there runs an unceasing, according to an essential necessity never-to-be-interrupted stream of experiences of a distinguished type — the type of perception. Ego-life is constant perceiving. By perception we understand here every consciousness in which an individually singular object is intuitively — that is, in its selfhood, in features that make up its own essence — conscious to us as actually existing: for example, these objects that now stand before my eyes in consciousness, or the view of the lovely Günterstal that emerges in memory in relatively clear intuitiveness. But, contrary to the original word-sense of *perceptio*, what matters is not whether the self-given is actually grasped by me or not — whether in the special mode of attending-to, closer-inspecting, or otherwise being actively occupied with it I make the self-given into a theme of my I, or not. For it may be that a wealth of objects is intuitively there and conscious for me without my being mentally with them. This usage of the term is established by the philosophical tradition (I recall Leibniz's distinction between *perceptio sine* and *cum apperceptione*).

Husserl introduces the concept of "perception" (Perzeption) in a broad, technical sense borrowed from Leibniz. Ego-life is "constant perceiving" — there is always a stream of perceptual consciousness running through experience. The key distinction is between perception in the broad sense (mere consciousness of something as present in its selfhood) and perception in the narrow sense (active attending, grasping, thematizing). The Leibnizian distinction between perception with and without apperception maps onto this: much of what is perceptually present for me is not actively attended to. The use of the German "Perzeption" rather than "Wahrnehmung" signals the broader, more inclusive concept.

---

The perceptions in their constant unified stream then fall into two classes: impressions and reproductions. Named first are experiences that we also call "perceptions" [Wahrnehmungen], a word we could well use if we allowed the same extension of sense as with "perception" [Perzeption]. Ordinarily we understand by perceiving a being-aware-of; the I is directed toward an object in awareness, is active as grasping, inspecting a perceived. But our concept should also encompass the countergroup of cases in which objects are perceptually conscious but not conscious in the mode of being-aware-of. The I is then not with them, not attentively, graspingly occupied with them. A special terminus is unavoidable here, at least in cases where this extension must be marked and emphasized, and for this the Humean word offers itself, though we do not here adopt Hume's actual views. But what is the essentially distinctive feature of impression — this perception in the extended sense — as against reproduction? The answer is: every perceptive experience in which the individually singular is not merely intuitively conscious at all, not merely conscious in its selfhood, but conscious in the awareness that it is present in the original — is an experience of perception (impression). Every experience in which the perceived itself is conscious as not present in the original, in other words in the mode of mere re-presentation — is a reproductive, that is, a re-presenting experience (in the widest sense). Every memory belongs here. However vividly and clearly a past may hover before us in its selfhood, it does not give itself in the memory-experience as present reality, not as bodily there, so to speak there in its own person, but as merely hovering, merely re-presented. In every perception, by contrast, the perceived is conscious as, so to speak, bodily reality, as bodily presence. It is not the consciousness of temporal present that makes the difference. For when we now imagine the Schlossberg, we take it as a presently existing thing, and yet it is perceived in the mode of mere re-presentation. Note well that we have here purely described what lies in the proper essence of the conscious experiences in question, that the differences between bodily presence and mere re-presentation of the perceived object are not imported from outside but express immanent modalities — modes of the way in which the object is here and there conscious and characterized.

This is a central passage in which Husserl establishes the fundamental distinction between impression and reproduction. Impression (or perception in the extended sense) is consciousness of something as present "in the original" — bodily, in its own person. Reproduction (memory, fantasy, expectation) gives the object as "merely re-presented," hovering before consciousness but not given as bodily present. Crucially, the distinction is not one of temporal consciousness (thinking of the Schlossberg as presently existing is still re-presentation if the Schlossberg is not before my eyes); rather, it is an immanent modality of the experience itself — a difference in how the object is characterized within consciousness.

---

It is further a basic property of I-consciousness, an evidently irrevocable necessity, that through the stream of experiencing to which the I necessarily belongs, an unceasing stream of perception, of impression, runs — indeed, that it is in a certain way such a stream itself.

This brief but important statement asserts that impression is not an occasional feature of conscious life but a continuous, essential structure. There is never a phase of ego-life without impressional consciousness. The stream of experience just is, in a certain sense, a stream of impressions — a continuous flow of original presentation.

---

No phase of ego-life is thinkable without a sphere of bodily presence being real for the I; whether noting it or not, the I is referred to it. If we articulate this impressional sphere, we find — and always according to an essential necessity — two groups of possible perceptually present particulars: "I-foreign" and "I-own" (or "I-ish," subjective in the determinate sense). On the one side, for each of us, the objects of this room, so far as one's current visual field embraces them. On the other side, each naturally finds himself, or can find himself.

Husserl draws the fundamental division within the impressional sphere: between what belongs to the I (the "I-own" or subjective) and what is foreign to the I (the "I-foreign"). The objects of the room belong to the second group; the I itself and its acts belong to the first. This is not a metaphysical dualism imposed from outside but a distinction found within the immanent articulation of impressional consciousness itself.

---

To the latter belongs evidently and necessarily that the I not merely has experiences in general — say, mere background experiences, dozing in dull sleep — but that it carries out some "I-act": an "I attend to this and that," "I run through its features inspecting them," "I judge," "I value," and so on — experiences in which the I is directed toward some something and is somehow active with this objectual element. Whenever this is the case, there can attach to it a new consciousness, an I-reflection, in which the I directs an aware gaze upon itself as subject of the relevant acts, and, passing from acts to acts, reflexively grasps itself as the one absolutely identical I of these acts, whereby of course these acts themselves have come to awareness. I-acts and all experiences belonging to the stream of ego-life not only are, but are also conscious — and indeed conscious in the mode of impression. Wondrously, consciousness is only as itself also conscious — indeed, as said, impressionally conscious — and it can, where it is not currently noted in awareness, be brought to awareness by way of a turning-back of the gaze, a reflection. This reflection is then itself impressionally conscious, and a reflection upon the reflection can set in, and so on. In any case it is evident that for me my ego-life, and I myself with all my essential components, is a constant domain of pre-theoretical facts. This entire sphere of what is own to the I, accessible to its grasping reflection, is a sphere of absolute impression, absolute self-givenness — that is, here any doubt whether what is grasped really or only apparently exists is senseless; why it is senseless, the sequel will make intelligible.

This rich paragraph develops several interrelated points. First, the I-own sphere requires active ego-life — not mere passive background experience but genuine I-acts (attending, judging, valuing). Second, reflection is the means by which the I gains access to itself and its acts: the I turns back upon its own activity and grasps itself as the identical subject of diverse acts. Third, Husserl notes the remarkable reflexive structure of consciousness: consciousness is always itself conscious (impressionally), and any consciousness can be made the object of a further reflective consciousness, in an iteration that is in principle infinite. Fourth, the sphere of I-own experience is characterized as "absolute impression" and "absolute self-givenness": here doubt about the reality of what is given is senseless. This last point is the Cartesian thesis in phenomenological dress — the absolute indubitability of immanent self-experience.

---

Secondly, we would have a realm of I-foreign facts: at all times what is I-foreign is given to the I in the character of bodily presence — for example, these things round about, these people. The example immediately brings out two groups: things and subjects, specifically as foreign subjects. Both are given to me perceptually, so far related to me, but given precisely as not-I, as not I-ish.

This brief paragraph establishes the I-foreign sphere as divided into things and other subjects. Both are given impressionally (in bodily presence), but their sense includes the character of being not-I. The appearance of other subjects alongside things signals that the prescientific world is not merely a world of material objects but also an intersubjective world.

### Inner and Outer Perception. Object and Appearance

What is I-ish is grasped, we saw, in acts of reflection (on the basis of "immanent perception"): in one the I refers to itself, in the other to its acts, its experiences, and their individual moments, merely inspecting and grasping. But in the stream of consciousness there also constantly appear perceptions of a quite different kind: "outer perceptions," whose objects characterize themselves therein by having something "outside" the I and its I-ish as existing reality conscious. Hereby the relation of perception and perceptual object shows itself to be fundamentally different on the two sides. What is I-ish or immanently perceived is not only perceived in general but is inseparably one with the perceiving itself. Both belong to the same single stream of experience of the I and are really one. But what is externally perceived or I-foreign is conscious as being "in itself" over against the perceiving and its I. It is not inseparably united with the perceiving; its enduring *esse* is not exhausted by its *percipi*.

Husserl now draws the fundamental contrast between inner (immanent) and outer (transcendent) perception. In immanent perception, perceiving and perceived are inseparably one — they belong to the same stream of experience. The Berkeleyan formula *esse = percipi* holds for the immanent sphere. In outer perception, by contrast, the perceived presents itself as "in itself" — as having a being that exceeds and is not exhausted by its being perceived. The perceived thing endures beyond the perceptual act; it was before and will be after the perception. This ontological difference between the I-own and the I-foreign is not a theoretical construction but a description of how these objects present themselves in experience.

---

While what is I-ish is, in that it is conscious, and has passed, is over, and simply is no more when consciousness is over, what is I-foreign — what is conscious as outer — is, in accord with the proper sense of outer perception, an objectively "real" thing that endures even when it is no longer perceived, that may well have been in real being even if it was never perceived at all. It thus gives itself as something for which being-perceived, having-been, and going-to-be are contingent — as in-itself over against the factual perceiving of the perceiving subject, as transcending the perception and with the perception the whole sphere of the I-ish. We therefore also say "transcendent perception." So every outer perception in itself intends the reality of the perceived as I-foreign; it is by its own essence perception of something existing "in itself" in the designated sense, be it the perception of things or of foreign subjects, human beings or animals. Of course the perception can occasionally be a false one; it then falsely posits this I-foreign in-itself.

This paragraph elaborates the transcendence of the outer-perceived. The transcendent object presents itself as ontologically independent of the perception: it exists "in itself," before and after and apart from being perceived. This is not a metaphysical thesis but a phenomenological description of the sense of outer perception — what it means to perceive something as an external thing. The possibility of error (false perception, illusion) is structurally connected to this transcendence: because the object claims to be "in itself," the perception can be wrong about it.

---

(It is generically evident that it belongs to the essence of this type "outer perception" — e.g., thing-perception — that for any perception there always remain two open possibilities: that it is a valid or an invalid perception. This says: every perception of this type stands within a further, never-to-be-concluded experiential context; it refers to further contexts of already past and still future perceptions, the latter being at every moment open possibilities. But so that either the experiential course confirms the original positing of reality with its sense-content — at most determining and supplementing it more closely — or that it brings dissonances and leads to the rejection of the originally given and for a time maintained perceptual reality. The one was the process of reality-confirmation, the other the process of reality-rejection, which indicates that we say, for example: it was only an "illusion," it was a deceptive appearance. All this is connected with the fact that an externally perceived thing, although given in its bodily presence, is actually always given only presumptively — its content or sense, with which it is given, points beyond itself to what is not given, and if this was co-intended, then the intention must agree with the actual further experience.)

This parenthetical paragraph develops the theme of the presumptive character of outer perception. Every outer perception stands within an open-ended experiential context: it can be confirmed or disconfirmed by further experience. This is the structure of "presumption" (Präsumtion): the perception intends more than it actually gives, and the excess of intention over givenness means that the perception can always turn out to be wrong. The process of reality-confirmation (concordance of further experience with the original perception) and reality-rejection (the discovery of illusion) are both essential possibilities belonging to the very essence of outer perception.

---

We were in the last lecture contrasting immanent perception and transcendent perception — the perception that the I has of itself and all its I-ish, and the quite different perception that it has of everything I-foreign. On the one side, its experiences of the unceasing stream of experiencing are constantly impressionally conscious — conscious in the mode of bodily and not merely reproductive presence — and they come to the I's aware grasping through mere reflection, a mere grasping I-gaze that directs itself reflexively upon them. This act of reflection is then a new experience, itself again impressionally conscious, itself again ready for perception: a new grasping gaze can direct itself upon the gaze just carried out, and so in infinitum.

Husserl recapitulates the distinction between immanent and transcendent perception, emphasizing the key features: immanent experiences are always impressionally conscious and accessible through reflection, and the reflective process can iterate indefinitely. This recap serves as a transition to deeper analysis.

---

It is evident that experiences of the stream must already be there in order to be able to be graspingly apprehended, and it is further evident that specifically I-acts must already have been carried out before they can be noted — further, it is evident that the I can note not only its I-acts but also itself as I of these acts, but only in a subsequent manner through reflection. Only after the I has been active as carrying out acts of some kind can it become aware of itself through reflection. This happens in a new I-act, whereby a third is needed for grasping the identity of the I in all these successive acts and reflections. And we fix further the evidence that it is unthinkable — unthinkable in the hardest sense of an absurdity — that an aware perceiving could encompass the totality of all experiences that are impressionally conscious at a moment. For suppose we were to assume such an all-encompassing immanent perceiving: it would be an experience that, having entered the scene, would at once belong to the present stream of experience, but could not be aware of itself, for which a new reflecting would be required first.

This paragraph develops the phenomenology of reflection with rigorous precision. Several eidetic necessities are established: (1) experiences must pre-exist to be reflectively grasped — reflection is always subsequent; (2) the I can reflect upon itself, but only after having been active in pre-reflective acts; (3) the identity of the I across acts requires a further synthetic act; (4) an all-encompassing reflective grasp of the totality of current experiences is absurd, because the reflective act itself would be an additional experience requiring yet further reflection. These are structural features of ego-life that no amount of theoretical ingenuity can circumvent.

---

Likewise it is evident that while a self-reflecting I is a priori possible — without which we would have no possibility of saying "I" — there belongs to it a wondrous splitting of the I, and belongs to it a priori: the I that lives in the mode of self-grasping, that exercises the act of reflection, is to be distinguished from the I that, at a prior I-act, is an I noted in awareness, perceived in the mode of the grasped. Despite the necessary identification (which is carried out in a further reflection and synthesis between the one and the other I — a reflection in which, to be sure, the just-active I is again transformed into a reflected, objectified one), we must distinguish, must speak of different modes — speak of the so to speak primordialy living I, which in its primordial livingness, namely in its being in actual activity, is in principle ungrasped, and of "the same" I that in the mode of reflection is grasped as having just been primordialy living but, precisely thereby, as no longer the living-active one. These were first glimpses that we were permitted to take into the wonder of all wonders: into I-being as I-activity and into "self-consciousness."

This is one of the philosophically richest passages in the text. Husserl describes the "wondrous splitting" (wundersame Spaltung) of the I in reflection: the reflecting I and the reflected-upon I are in a sense the same I, but they occupy different modal positions. The "primordialy living I" (urlebendiges Ich) — the I in the act of living activity — is in principle ungrasped; the moment it is grasped in reflection, it is no longer the actively living I but the I-as-just-having-been-active. Self-consciousness is thus never a simple coincidence of knower and known; it involves an irreducible temporal displacement. Husserl calls this the "wonder of all wonders" — a phrase that signals the depth and inexhaustibility of the problem of self-consciousness.

---

In further analyses we then studied the relation of the real being of the pregivennesses of the I-ish sphere to immanent impression, or immanent perception. We used the expression: the *esse* of the I-ish components is exhausted in their *percipi*. It is of course meant to recall Berkeley; for the rest, it is, naturally, to be understood *cum grano salis*. The being of the currently real experiences of the experience-stream of my I is exhausted in their impressional consciousness, which can here and there have the mode of aware perceiving. Perception and perceived are here inseparably one and form a single individual being. As the experience in question is struck by the aware gaze, this gaze necessarily encompasses it entirely in its absolute selfhood. Herein lies that the realm of immanent impression is a realm of absolutely evident being, whose real being for the I — the subject of the experiences — cannot be a theme of possible doubt or possible negation. For me, my experiences — namely those that are impressional presences at the given moment — and I myself, are so given that I simply cannot do otherwise than to posit myself and all this mine as being. This being is an absolutely necessary being for me. Doubt and negation are here absurd.

Husserl now states the Berkeleyan thesis for the immanent sphere in its phenomenological reformulation. The being of immanent experiences is exhausted in their being-conscious (*esse = percipi*), but this must be understood carefully: it is not a metaphysical idealism but a phenomenological description. When the reflective gaze strikes an experience, it encompasses it "entirely in its absolute selfhood" — there is no gap between the experience and its being known. Consequently, immanent experience is a domain of absolute evidence: doubt and negation are here not just unlikely but absurd (widersinnig). This is the Cartesian *cogito* in its phenomenological redescription.

---

In contrast, in experiences of outer perception, I-foreign objectivities — things and animals — are indeed likewise impressionally given as bodily presences and realities, and yet again so that confirmation but also relinquishment of this reality is possible, indeed an always-open possibility. A manifold of I-foreign objects stands before us, characterized as "in itself" existing, as objectively real realities; we are entirely certain of this existence, and yet it can be that these objects, these things before us, these people or animals, are not at all as they bodily stand there, indeed, that in the end they do not exist at all. It is characteristic of the perception itself — which makes them conscious as bodily reality — that it branches out into a series of modal modifications that are completely excluded in the case of immanent perception, of reflection upon what is I-ish. Such modalities are to be expressed by "it could be," "it is merely perhaps so," "it is doubtful, probable," "it is not so." An outer perception can become uncertain, can become doubtful: the object still stands in bodily selfhood before our eyes, and yet we become uncertain about the reality; we then speak of its being perhaps an appearance, an appearance that the thing there, which we see as a sphere, really has the spherical shape and not rather a more or less similar other shape; we see it as pure white, but become doubtful whether it is not rather yellowish, and so on. So we eventually test the colors, the shapes, weigh, doubt, and not infrequently end in negation. What matters is that negation — whether directed at the reality of perceived features or at the reality of the whole object — is possible and always possible, even while the object remains impressionally conscious. The words "appearance," "illusion," "hallucination" express the modal modification of consciousness in negation, as compared with what we take as the normal starting point: normal perception with its so to speak unbroken belief in reality.

This long paragraph spells out the contrast with immanent perception. While immanent perception excludes all modal modification (doubt, negation, etc.), outer perception is essentially susceptible to them. The outer object presents itself as bodily real, but this reality-positing can be modalized: it can become uncertain, doubtful, probable, or negated. Illusion and hallucination are not anomalies but essential possibilities belonging to the structure of outer perception. The "normal" perception with its unbroken reality-belief is simply one mode among others — the starting point from which modalization proceeds. This analysis establishes the fundamental distinction between the absolute evidence of the immanent and the merely presumptive evidence of the transcendent.

---

The impression of an I-external thus has two sides: on the one hand, it is bodily-presence-consciousness; on the other, it is reality-consciousness, a taking-as-real, a belief in the existence of what is bodily conscious. This second side can and according to principled possibility does modalize itself in any outer perception.

This brief paragraph neatly summarizes the twofold structure of outer impressional consciousness: (1) the bodily-presence component (the object appears as "there in person") and (2) the doxic component (the belief that the bodily-present object really exists). While the first component remains constant (the thing still looks the way it looks), the second can undergo modal modification. Distinguishing these two sides is essential for understanding how perception can be illusory: the bodily-presence aspect can remain intact even as the reality-belief collapses.

---

Why this is always an open possibility, while for immanent perceptions it is unthinkable, must be clarified. "Ego cogito." That an experience of my own — an "I think," "feel," "will," "I affirm," "I deny," "I disapprove," and so on — at the moment when I have it in the grip of reflective grasping, should not be, is likewise unthinkable; that I should be uncertain about it, in doubt, and so on, is impossible. Thus immanent perception does not admit those modal modifications at all. But this is connected with the fundamentally different structure of the two kinds of perception, or impression, and with the fact that the one in itself bestows upon the perceived the sense of an objectively real, an in-itself-existing, the other the sense of a merely subjective experience and its I.

Husserl now connects the modal asymmetry to the different senses that the two kinds of perception bestow upon their objects. Outer perception gives its object the sense "objectively real, in-itself-existing" — and it is precisely this sense that opens the space for doubt, illusion, and negation. Immanent perception gives its object the sense "subjective experience" — and this sense excludes modalization: my own experience, as reflexively grasped, simply cannot not-be. The *ego cogito* is invoked as the formula for this apodictic certainty.

---

Now it is to be seen: a perception can have a real, something characterized as an in-itself over against it, conscious only in the manner of an intending, a meaning-beyond that intends beyond itself and constantly aims at fulfillment, and even in fulfillment again aims beyond itself at something new. And so in infinitum. So constituted is outer perception that the bodily existence of the perceived is constantly and by irrevocable necessity a mere presumption. How does this come about? How is it that immanent perception is not and can never be mere presumption? The answer is: all outer perception is — and not contingently but in eternal necessity — perception through appearance. In outer perception, one must distinguish, and unavoidably distinguish according to its own sense-content, between appearance and what appears. And this means: the classes of objects of possible perception that we called "I-foreign" or "transcendent" or "outer" are only perceivable in the way that they present themselves to us, "appear." We can straightforwardly say: they are what they are only as something appearing in appearances; for were they otherwise, perceptions would have to be thinkable in which they would bodily give themselves in their otherness. If an individual being is real, a perception must be possible that shows the object as it itself is and that presents this self-being bodily. We see, then, that for outer objects, for objectively real ones, it is not even thinkable that they would be perceived without appearance.

This is one of the most important paragraphs in the entire lecture course. Husserl establishes the thesis that all outer perception is necessarily perception "through appearance" (durch Erscheinung). Appearance is not a deficiency or distortion; it is the essential mode in which transcendent objects can be given at all. The outer object is "what it is only as something appearing in appearances." Because outer perception always gives its object through one-sided, perspectival appearances that necessarily leave further sides and aspects undetermined, the reality of the perceived is always a "presumption" — a well-motivated positing that extends beyond what is actually given. This is why outer perception is essentially susceptible to doubt and revision. The contrast with immanent perception, where there is no appearance-structure and the perceived is given "absolutely," explains the apodictic character of inner experience.

---

Conversely, we say, it belongs to the essence of immanent perception that its object — that what is I-ish, and thus the experiences of my I at the moment of their actual being-experienced, and likewise the I itself — in principle cannot appear. For immanent perception, the distinction between the object itself and the appearance of the object is necessarily absent. Precisely thereby it characterizes itself as a perception that has its perceived as absolute being within itself, while outer perception always has only an appearance within itself and its object only as a something that appears.

Husserl states the converse: immanent perception is structurally without appearance. There is no gap between the experience and how it presents itself — no "appearance" through which the experience would be mediately given. The immanent is given absolutely, not through perspectives or aspects. This is the ground of the absolute indubitability of the immanent sphere and of the essentially presumptive character of the transcendent.

---

But now a detailed clarification of these propositions is needed. We lay out here what belongs to the irrevocable proper essence of the two types of perception and, correlatively, to the irrevocable essence of the two types of objects that are perceived on each side and that receive their sense as inner and outer, immanent and transcendent, I-ish and extra-I-ish exclusively through the perceiving consciousness. Let us therefore question the perception itself, let us see how objects on each side present themselves and what therein is contingent and what necessary. We proceed from clear examples, which however we take exclusively as representatives of their type.

This transitional paragraph announces a shift to concrete descriptive analysis. The theoretical points about the structure of inner and outer perception are now to be confirmed through detailed examination of actual examples. The objects' sense as immanent or transcendent is received from the perceiving consciousness — a key phenomenological thesis: the distinction is not metaphysical but constitutive.

---

Let us take as an example an object that we all have before our eyes — this lectern here — and let us attend to what necessarily remains preserved in the way the perceptual object presents itself, however we may think this object freely modified in imagination or replaced by other perceptual objects of the same highest generic type of outer perception. The lectern, and so every outer object, is, let us say, "given through appearances" in its bodily real existence, and otherwise it cannot be given. What is this supposed to mean? The lectern is given to us bodily with a certain stock of features, in a certain extension and shape over which a certain coloring is distributed, likewise certain tactile qualities such as roughness and smoothness, and so on. Let us assume for simplicity's sake that we initially move neither head nor eyes, that the perceptual phenomenon does not change at all, just as it was already a simple case that we favored a resting and during the perception unchanged object.

Husserl launches into a concrete phenomenological analysis of thing-perception using the example of a lectern. The move to a specific example is characteristic of Husserl's method: eidetic insights are won not through abstract reasoning but through the careful examination of particular cases taken as exemplars of their type. The simplification (no movement, no change) isolates the essential structure of perspectival givenness.

---

How is the perceived thing, its perceived spatial shape, color, and so on, given? Obviously only from one side is the thing visible, and it is given only in one contingent view, aspect. Likewise every quality belonging to the visible side is given only in one contingent aspect; and just as the object is not its side and not itself the view it happens to offer us, so too its own color is not the view we have of it, and the same for every feature perceived in the mode of bodily reality. We customarily speak of perspective in this regard, though usually only in the visual domain. Every thing is in every case seen only in a certain perspectival adumbration; the shape itself is thereby distinguished from the given shape-adumbration. But the other features are also affected, so that we speak directly of a color-perspective. The matter becomes still clearer when we lift the restriction to the case of an unchanging perception and bring in the obviously always-possible change — the manifold of perceptions and perceptual continua in which the same thing can be given. Not only can we have many perceptual experiences that are perceptions of the same thing, but this same thing presents itself again and again from new sides, in ever-new aspects and views, its shape in ever-new shape-aspects or adumbrations, but also its color in ever-new color-aspects, and so for every feature belonging to the bodily-presence stock. We constantly observe: a distinction must be made between object and object-aspect, between color itself and color-aspect, and so in every respect. What is here called aspect, view, way of appearing of the object and is distinguished from the object itself — that is obviously nothing invented, but something to which we can attend at any time through a turning of the mental gaze and which we can grasp; it is an indubitable givenness.

This paragraph develops the Abschattungslehre — Husserl's doctrine of adumbration (perspectival presentation). The key insight is that every outer object is given only in one-sided, perspectival "adumbrations" (Abschattungen): a shape-adumbration, a color-adumbration, and so on. The object itself is not identical with any of its aspects but is the identical unity that shows itself through the changing manifold of aspects. This is not a theoretical postulate but a directly describable feature of perceptual experience. The distinction between object and aspect is not invented but found — a "turning of the mental gaze" suffices to make it evident.

---

Two attitudes are distinguished, to which the distinction between object and object-aspect correlatively corresponds. In the one we are naively turned toward the object itself; the perceptions run in their continuous sequence, the manifold aspects flow off within them, they lie constantly within the experience, but we do not attend to them — we attend to the object, which in this attitude is conscious as the one and the same, as this identically unchanged lectern. At any time, however, we can enter into the new attitude, the one directed toward the aspects, the appearances: the object also now stands there as the one and the same, but we do not attend to it; rather, we attend precisely to its "appearances," to the constantly changing way in which the shape appears, to the shape-adumbrations, likewise to the appearance-mode of the color, and so on. In the one attitude we have something identical and — by assumption — unchanged, experienced and posited as unchanged, numerically one: this lectern here. In the other we have a flow of changes, a constantly other, though in relation to the pertinent identical — in other words, a constant change of appearances, but as appearances of an identical.

This paragraph makes explicit the correlation between two attitudes and the object/aspect distinction. In the naive attitude, we attend to the object as identical through changing appearances; in the reflective-phenomenological attitude, we attend to the appearances themselves as a flowing manifold. Both attitudes reveal the same structure from different sides: the identity of the object and the multiplicity of its appearances are correlative. This dual-aspect structure is the heart of the phenomenology of perception.

---

Now we have a possible limit-case: a perception that persists without changing from moment to moment. But even then we distinguish between appearance and object and speak of the contingent appearance in which the object offers itself in this perception — because we are aware that we can at any time bring the perception into flow and correlatively convert the appearance into a manifold of appearances of the same object. Obviously, what we have here intuited is not a mere fact of experience — something that is so by chance, is usually so, but could also be otherwise, could be thought otherwise. It is no more contingent than that a number given as > a and < b cannot also be given as < a and > b, or, going further, than that any arithmetical or purely geometrical or purely logical truth is contingent. Rather, what we intuit in the examples of outer perception, we recognize in suitable generalization as a priori in that strictest sense in which everything purely mathematical is a priori. We see — and in the most perfect clarity conceivable — that it necessarily and in unconditional universality must be so: that no object of the type of an outer, I-foreign can be thinkable that would not be given through appearances, and that it could be given otherwise only with absurdity.

This paragraph elevates the Abschattung-thesis to the status of an a priori insight. The perspectival givenness of outer objects is not a contingent fact about our perceptual apparatus; it is an essential, necessary truth of the same rank as mathematical truths. No outer object is thinkable without adumbration. To suppose otherwise would be to commit an absurdity — a violation of essential necessity. This is a paradigm case of what Husserl means by phenomenological evidence: an insight into an essential structure that holds with unconditional universality.

---

It is of a truly incomparable importance for the young philosopher to fully appropriate this evidence, which has never been attended to by philosophy, and which is not only in itself highly important but, once grasped, also makes the mind's eye see an infinity of related, so-called phenomenological evidences. Let us bring it still closer. Outer perception is, by irrevocable necessity, simultaneously impressional consciousness of its outer object and of an appearance or continuous appearance-series of its object, in such a way that every appearance is characterized in itself as an appearance of this object, and the object correlatively as object of this appearance. It belongs to the essence of the appearance that it is never thinkable as the sole possible appearance of this object — that it leaves open other, indeed in principle infinitely many other appearances of the same object. If the lectern is now given from this side and accordingly presents itself in a certain appearance, a certain shape-perspective, color-perspective, and so on, it could also have been given in others, even though it was perhaps now given only in this one. To this belongs indeed the evidence that the actually present perception, and so any possible actual perception, is to be converted into an infinity — that is, an endlessly open manifold — of new perceptions that are all characterized in themselves as perceptions of the same object, namely characterized by virtue of a unity-consciousness, derivable from the continuous transition from perception to perception, as consciousness of this one and the same. Every outer perception thus belongs a priori to an infinite perceptual system, and to a determinate one.

This paragraph is an extended phenomenological meditation on the infinite system of possible perceptions belonging to any outer object. Every actual perception is one member of an open-ended, in principle infinite system of possible perceptions that would present the same object from different sides, in different aspects. The unity-consciousness — the awareness that all these perceptions are of the "same" object — arises from the continuous transitions between them. This infinite system is not merely factual but a priori: it belongs to the essential structure of outer perception as such. Husserl emphasizes that this insight, though fundamental, has been overlooked by the entire history of philosophy.

---

To this perceptual system there corresponds evidently a system of appearances: actually, an outer object may be given in whatever appearance; it necessarily fits into an infinite system of manifold possible appearances. The general type is fixed, as are all possible appearance-courses that can be and have been characterized in themselves as appearances of the same object. On the other hand, it is not fixed which of these appearances belong to the reality of the object — which will present themselves from the actual running-through in experience. For so constituted is perception by its essence that, presenting us the object as bodily reality, it presents it to us only from one side, and in such a way that the other sides are not fully determined in advance according to the perception's own sense-bestowal. The lectern is given to us through perception as an object with a sense determining it, to which the designation "lectern" points — thus given as a spatial object, spatially self-enclosed, with extensive features. This sense circumscribes a norm of possible perceptions, possible appearance-modes. Certainly the thing has a back side, has there a determinate shape, a determinate coloring, and so on. But for the perceiving consciousness this shape is indeterminate in another sense: not only is it not properly intuitively given, not properly seen, not actually touched, and so on — it is indeterminate even if we have already perceived the object from all sides.

This paragraph drives home the essential indeterminacy of outer perception. Even when all sides of an object have been perceived, the perception does not achieve full determinacy: there is always more to be determined, always further possible experiences that would further specify the object. The "norm" circumscribed by the perceptual sense (e.g., "lectern" = spatial, self-enclosed, with extensive features) constrains the range of possible appearances but does not fix them completely. This essential incompleteness of outer-perceptual givenness is the ground of the presumptive character of all transcendent positing.


---

What we thereby learn to understand is that every outer perception, insofar as it is perception through mere appearance, is necessarily perception through mere presumption. With respect to its object, it is perception, is consciousness of its bodily reality. And yet: it is only with respect to one "side" of the object, with respect to a limited stock of its essentially own features, that it bodily gives genuine sense; with respect to another stock it merely co-intends, has it as co-present, while this stock in reality is not bodily there, is not properly perceived. Constantly and necessarily there obtains this two-layeredness of outer perception: in one layer it is properly presenting, in the other merely appresenting. The visible side of the thing is not something self-sufficient — not a piece of the thing but something thinkable only as a "side." And the invisible aspect of the object is conscious only as a more or less indeterminate horizon of appresented thingly moments that, proceeding from the given side and as inseparable from it, would become actually visible.

Husserl introduces the crucial distinction between presentation (Präsentation) and appresentation (Appräsentation) as the two inseparable layers of outer perception. Every outer perception properly presents some features of its object (the visible side) while appresenting others (the invisible sides, the inner structure). Appresentation is not representation by a sign — it is not like a pointer directing attention elsewhere — but a structural moment of the perception itself in which what is not directly given is nonetheless co-intended as belonging to the same object. The visible front of the lectern is not a self-standing piece; it makes sense only as a side, implying the co-presence of unseen sides. This two-layered structure is essential, not accidental.

---

Further, even what is properly seen of the thing, besides the outer horizon of unknownness and incomplete determinacy regarding the invisible features, also has an inner horizon of such indeterminacy and unknownness, and necessarily has it. We really see the brown color of the side of the lectern facing us, we see it as uniformly brown, but it is open that upon closer approach — in new perceptions — it might appear non-uniform in color, that still-invisible structures of the wood, unevennesses, and so on, would come to appearance. The uniform brown is thus, although seen in a more proper sense, still merely anticipation, and even if this anticipation is confirmed, every new confirming perception remains again anticipation, presumption. No perception is, and with respect to nothing however clearly falling within the perception, final perception. In all and every respect it is affected by horizons of indeterminate determinability; constantly the object is given through appearances, and constantly appearances are anticipating, presuming. Appearance is through and through appearance; the idea of an "adequate appearance" — one that would present the object perfectly clearly, perfectly determinately, perfectly exhaustively, in the manner of a pure presentation without any appresentation — is an "idea" lying in infinity.

This paragraph deepens the analysis of perceptual indeterminacy. Even the properly seen features carry an "inner horizon" of further determinability: the uniformly brown surface might, on closer inspection, reveal color variations, textures, structures. Every perception is anticipatory — it projects beyond what is given — and every confirmation of the anticipation is itself a new anticipation. The regress is infinite. Husserl draws the radical conclusion that an "adequate perception" of an outer object — one that would give the object exhaustively, with no remainder of indeterminacy — is an idea lying at infinity, never actually achievable. This is a structural feature of outer perception as such, not a limitation of human cognitive equipment.

---

With all of this — above all with this essential character of perception through appearances, constantly anticipating and having indeterminate horizons — it is connected that for the reality-consciousness of perception the possibility not only of confirmation but also of cancellation lies ready. The actual progress from perception to perception can run so that the new appearances confirm the presumption, or it can be that it was a decoy, an appearance, an illusion. All the modalities — doubt, possibility-consciousness, probability-consciousness — are constantly ready possibilities, precisely because outer perception as perception through appearances posits reality only through a medium of anticipation, of fore-meaning.

Husserl connects the appearance-structure to the modal possibilities. Because outer perception always operates through anticipatory, presumptive appearances, the doxic modalities — confirmation, doubt, possibility, probability, negation — are structural possibilities of every outer perception. This is not an empirical observation about how perception sometimes goes wrong; it is an essential truth about the structure of transcendent givenness.

---

You see how peculiar the structure of outer perception is, how much and how significant it is that it is perception through appearance. You must bring yourselves to full evidence that if this tension constantly obtains between the perceptual object itself and its appearances, this "constantly" expresses an unconditional necessity. An outer object that, so to speak, coincided with its appearance, that thus fell into perception in such a way that what is seen of it no longer pointed beyond itself to new perceptions that would still need to determine more closely, to bring to sight what is unseen, to determine what is indeterminate — such an outer object is simply unthinkable. As soon as we posit this case as an ideal and view it here as an attainable ideal, we pretend thereby that there exists an "adequate perception" of the outer object. But then the object would simply be no outer object; it would be absolutely enclosed within perception, immanent to it. And a thing, an I-foreign something of the sense that outer perception bodily makes conscious, to want to have as the object of an adequate perception — that is as absurd as wanting to obtain by adding units to a number a smaller number. A thing, an outer, is a priori thinkable only as appearing, only as inadequately, only as presumptively, only as constantly determinable and never finally determined, only as remaining in suspension between being and non-being — and to ascribe to God, say, an adequate perception of things means to ascribe to him absurdities.

This is a climactic statement. Husserl argues with full apodictic force that the idea of an outer object given without appearance, without indeterminacy, without presumption, is an absurdity comparable to a mathematical contradiction. The outer object is essentially transcendent, essentially given through one-sided appearances, essentially determinable but never finally determined. Even God could not perceive a thing "adequately" in the sense of a complete, exhaustive, non-perspectival givenness — because that would destroy the very sense of "thing." The concluding formulation — the thing remains "in suspension between being and non-being" — captures the essential precariousness of all transcendent positing.

---

The sense of externality and of the in-itself-being belonging to it thus explicates itself directly through the proposition: to be external is a being that in principle gives itself only through appearances, hence a being that is in principle always presumptive. A proposition that is reversible. On the other hand, immanent perceptions characterize themselves to us by contrast with the transcendent as perceptions whose objects, so to speak, are their own appearances. But better we say: whose objects do not appear but are absolutely given, adequately perceived. They do not appear — that says: we reckon to the concept of appearance that the "appearing" is something different from the "appearance" according to all its determinacies.

This paragraph crystallizes the fundamental ontological distinction in a pair of reversible propositions: externality = givenness through appearance = presumptive being; immanence = givenness without appearance = absolute being. The immanent is not given through appearances at all — there is no gap between how it presents itself and what it is. This asymmetry is the deepest ground of the phenomenological distinction between consciousness and world.

Outer perception makes its object conscious by bringing it to presentation in immanent data that belong to its own absolute stock of being — bringing it to presentation "through appearance." Can we not say, in metaphor, "through depiction"? Here at once the dangerous reef of the naive picture-theory must be noted. How did we begin and in what direction did our questions go? Let us reflect. We began with "I am," and we asked: what is pregiven to me, who find myself in reflection as actual subject and take myself exclusively as I reflexively find myself as act-subject, and how in particular is the perception that gives the pregiven outer world configured? In this we came upon the wondrous connections between immanent components of perception, which we called "appearances," and the outer thing appearing through them or in them. If one leaves this attitude and, in the external consideration of I-subjects, compares perception and perceived natural object — as the naive thinker readily does — then the temptation is very great indeed.

This paragraph marks a pivotal transition. Having established the appearance-structure of outer perception, Husserl now confronts the "picture-theory" (Bildertheorie) — the naive philosophical interpretation that treats perceptual appearances as "images" or "pictures" of external things, analogous to the way a painting depicts its subject. This theory arises naturally when one leaves the pure ego-attitude and adopts the external standpoint: from outside, it seems as though a person has "images" of things in their soul. Husserl warns that this temptation can only be resisted by maintaining the pure ego-attitude in which the question is always: how does the object present itself in and through the immanent data of consciousness?

---

Here let only this much be said: when I find people before me, I apprehend them as external I-subjects related to bodies, in the context of external nature as a whole. I naturally also ascribe to them perceptions — perceptions of the same nature into which I too look. If I have not yet gained clarity, through inner analysis, about the intentional connection between perception and perceived — for which I would have had to remain purely within the immanence of my perceiving, attending to intention and intended — then it is certainly tempting to compare the relation between perception and perceived things with the relation between image and imaged, and then, taking the comparison seriously, to fall into gross errors, indeed into absurdity. The person over there has in himself, in his soul, perceptual images of the things external to him, which are indeed in themselves outside, as I myself also see them outside. These images are, like images generally, more or less good images, in accord or not in accord depending on the constitution of the subjects. As with images in general, it can happen here that the original does not exist at all while the image exists; and so everything seems to fit nicely.

Husserl reconstructs the picture-theory sympathetically before dismantling it. When I observe another person perceiving a tree, it is tempting to think that the person has a "tree-image" in their soul — a mental picture that may or may not accurately represent the external tree. This model seems to explain illusion (the image exists but its original does not) and subjective variation (different subjects have different images of the same thing). But, as Husserl will show, the model is fundamentally misconceived.

---

But now it is time to reflect and to recognize the absurdity of interpreting the perceptual appearance as an image of the appearing. A statue we call an image of its original — say, a general. One real thing is here called the image of another, separate, similar real thing. Is this the situation in the relation between perception and perceived? The perception of the perceiving I is an experience in its life-stream; immanent to it is the appearance as appearance of the appearing; and even the fact that the appearance brings just this and nothing else — this thing, this person, or whatever it may be — to appearance is something that plays out purely within the I. The I perceives — that is, it lives through an appearance and finds itself in it related to the appearing. Even if the I then comes to the conviction that the object is illusory, it still appears, only no reality, no possibility of verification, belongs to it. Now if we approach subjectivity from outside and perceive a person and are aware that he is inwardly carrying out a perception — say, of the tree standing before both of us — we certainly attribute to his soul, more precisely to his stream of experience, a certain tree-appearance. But do we attribute to him something like a tree-image, an analogue of a statue? Is that not utter nonsense? Can we attribute to his inner life, to his stream, anything other than immanent elements — specifically, elements analogous to those we find in our own inner attitude?

Husserl now delivers the critique. The key move is to show that the relation between appearance and appearing thing is nothing like the relation between a statue and its original. A statue is a real thing that is separate from and merely similar to another real thing; the similarity relation is external and contingent. But an appearance is not a separate real thing at all — it is an immanent component of the perceptual experience itself, inseparable from the intentional relation to the appearing object. The appearance does not "resemble" the object the way a painting resembles its model; rather, the appearance is the medium through which the object presents itself to consciousness. To treat the appearance as a thing-like image that could exist independently of its object — the way a painting can exist when its model has been destroyed — is to misunderstand the entire intentional structure of perception.

---

Besides, let us ask ourselves how the statue comes to become an image, to be able to become one. Between image and original there is similarity, which can come more or less close to equality. In a pine forest we have thousands of pines that are more or less equal or similar to one another. Is each one thereby already an image of the others? Does each have, besides its color, its form, and so on — besides the inner features of the pine that ground the similarity — also a feature "image"? Of course that is nonsense. Without entering into deeper analyses, it is clear that an object is an image only as the object of an image-apprehension that some I carries out. But is not a statue in itself an image, whether we see it as such or not? We answer: objective imagehood belongs to it only insofar as it is a thing that, by all I-subjects of a certain normal and ordinary developmental level, when perceived or intuitively represented, is at the same time apprehended in a certain peculiar consciousness as image of another object. This means: imagehood does not adhere to the thing itself as a feature, but to the perceived, intuited thing as such — or to certain preferred appearances within the appearance-series of the thing "statue" there adheres a new intentional character, insofar as the normally developed subject accompanies what appears in these appearances with an image-apprehension as a higher-order consciousness.

This paragraph deepens the critique by analyzing the concept of "image" itself. Similarity alone does not make something an image — pines in a forest resemble each other without any of them being an image of the others. Imagehood requires a specific act of consciousness: an "image-apprehension" (Bildauffassung) that takes one thing as representing another. A statue is an image only for a subject that apprehends it as such — and this apprehension is a higher-order intentional act that presupposes the perceptual consciousness of the statue as a thing. The crucial point: image-consciousness is founded upon and presupposes perceptual consciousness. Appearances cannot themselves be images, because image-consciousness already requires appearances as its basis.

---

I speak of preferred appearances. Indeed, if for example we enter into a giant statue like the Kassel Hercules and have the perceptual appearances of its inner formations, the image-consciousness naturally drops away. We must see the Hercules from outside and at a suitable distance and position; we must have certain selected appearance-series for the image-consciousness, motivated by them, to be able to set in. But the main point here is: it is clear that the image is only image for an image-consciousness, and this presupposes, as an apprehension of a higher level, already a perceptual consciousness, hence the living-through of appearances. To interpret these — which as appearances inseparably harbor in themselves the consciousness of the appearing — themselves in turn as images, hence to make out of appearances themselves again things that would be perceptually apprehended as images, is manifold absurdity.

This clinches the argument. Image-consciousness is a higher-order act that presupposes perceptual consciousness (the experience of appearances). To treat the appearances themselves as images would require a further perceptual consciousness of those image-appearances, which would itself involve new appearances, and so on in a vicious regress. The picture-theory thus generates an infinite regress and is self-contradictory. Appearances are not images; they are the original medium of intentional reference to objects. The intentional relation between appearance and object is sui generis and cannot be modeled on the relation between two independently existing things.

### The Pure Ego-Attitude and the Idea of a Transcendental Phenomenology

We are in fact preserved from such fundamental errors if we consider in advance what eo ipso belongs to the kind of descriptions of a priori connections that we carry out in pure ego-attitude, or, seen from another side, if we bring ourselves to the most perfect clarity about what kind of attitude it is that we can carry out at any time under the title "pure ego-attitude" and what distinguishes and delimits its pregivennesses and their possible descriptions.

This transitional paragraph introduces the next phase of the investigation: the explicit thematization and clarification of the pure ego-attitude that has implicitly guided all the preceding analyses. Husserl signals that a methodological elevation is needed — not new content, but reflective clarity about the attitude in which the content was obtained. This will lead to the ideas of the pure I and of transcendental phenomenology.

---

Let us connect here in order to raise ourselves to a higher methodological standpoint. Our ego-attitude was none other than that which Descartes first carried out in the first of his famous Meditations and which led him to the much-cited *ego cogito, ego sum*. So we too began: *ego cogito, cogito cogitata*; more specifically: I have perceptually given, or can have perceptually given — what can I have so given? But note well: our procedure was that we took the respectively perceived as perceived of the respective perception, exactly as what is bodily given in it, exactly as the something that was perceived in such-and-such a sense, with the absoluteness or with the appearance-mediated indeterminacy and determinability with which it was perceived. No other descriptions did we carry out, and we now establish this retrospectively and bring it into the focus of attention: no others than those that kept within this framework. We could move within it without conceptually carrying out its delimitation. I simply led you through the descriptions; you followed without difficulty; you had throughout an immanent path without reflecting on it. Now, however, bring to your consciousness that everything remained, in a certain way, enclosed within the framework of immanence.

Husserl makes explicit what was implicit throughout: all the preceding analyses operated within the framework of the *ego cogito*. The Cartesian starting-point was not merely a rhetorical opening but the actual methodological stance: everything described — I-own and I-foreign, immanent and transcendent, appearance and object — was described purely as it presented itself within consciousness, as the "perceived of the perception." No claim about the independent existence of external objects was ever needed or used as a premise. The descriptions stayed within the immanent sense-content of the giving consciousness. Husserl now asks his listeners to recognize this retroactively.

---

To be sure, we also reflected at length about genuine being or non-being with respect to perceptual objectivities, and brought to consciousness in general a priori evidences the absurdity of any doubt and non-being with respect to what is immanently given, and in contrast the open possibility of non-being for everything given in the mode of transcendence. But how was even this demonstration carried out? By considering all I-external purely as what it was in possible outer perceptions as perceptually intended, as appearing in appearances, and by purely following the a priori prefigured by the immanent content and sense of the perception in terms of appearance and appearing. In the perception of a thing there lies the perceived thing as such, as appearing thus-and-so, as determinable and determined, whether the thing in true actuality exists or not. Perception in general has its sense, its fullness and intentional horizons, has its changeable appearances, adumbrations, and so on, in absolute evidence as something belonging to its immanent content — and only upon this did we draw. Purely following the sense, there resulted in immanence the principled possibility of the non-being of all outer objects, and it resulted that being and non-being are indices for certain structures of perceptual courses — concordant fulfillment and confirmation, or discordance and cancellation.

This paragraph is a retrospective methodological clarification. Even the thesis that external objects might not exist — which sounds like a metaphysical claim — was established purely by analyzing the immanent sense-content of perception. The perception of a thing has its sense (its intended object with all its determinations) regardless of whether the thing actually exists. Being and non-being, for outer objects, are not metaphysical absolutes but "indices" for types of experiential course: concordant fulfillment means the object is confirmed as existing; discordance and cancellation mean it is not. This entire analysis was conducted within immanence.

---

But what interests us now is no longer the picture-theory, but the fundamental methodological advance that results from a further continuation of the reflection on the sense of the pure descriptions of the ego-attitude. We are now already far enough to carry out a principled delimitation of an idea of the pure I and pure ego-consciousness and to characterize a pure method of I-related source-research that is of the greatest philosophical significance — indeed, of such great significance that all radical philosophy depends on it. Only a last and easy purification of our previous ego-attitude needs to be carried out, which eliminates a moment that, while it does not enter into the descriptive content of the assertions, still carries with it in an undesirable way a dispensable prejudice.

Husserl announces the decisive methodological step: the purification of the ego-attitude by the explicit elimination of a residual prejudice — the tacit reality-positing that accompanied the analyses even though it played no role in their content. This purification will yield the idea of the "pure I" and the method of phenomenological research, upon which "all radical philosophy depends."

---

Let us consider the following. When each of us says "I" for himself and freely resolves to state what he finds given in perceptual givennesses (and in theoretical reduction, i.e., under exclusion of all theoretical and in general all opinions gained through justifying thinking), and further, what he could find in possible perceptions because types of givenness present and could present themselves to him, then he takes as reality what presents itself to him simply as reality — both his I-ish and the outer objects, things, surrounding persons, people, and also animals. But when he concentrates his interest, as was always the case in our reflections, on the types of possible givenness in general and the general evidences that hold unconditionally for such givennesses (as an a priori without which they could not even be thought and could not be), then obviously the reality of the perceptual objects standing before him individually and as fact, or of the intuitive memory-objects through which he perhaps makes present objects of past perception, loses its function for his descriptive and generally ascertaining behavior.

This paragraph begins the argument for the irrelevance of reality-positing. When the philosopher concentrates on types of givenness and a priori evidences — as we have been doing throughout — the factual existence of the particular objects used as examples becomes irrelevant. The lectern was used as an example, but any thing of the same type would have served equally well. The a priori insights gained do not depend on whether this particular lectern exists.

---

The much-discussed lectern here before our eyes was for us — was for each in the attitude "I and what I find" — a found individual reality; as really existing it stood there and was so taken, posited by the I. But it was merely an example for a whole type of possible ego-givennesses, in the purest sense possible outer perceptual objects — an example on which we indeed freely undertook arbitrary changes in free fantasy. The freely refashioned thing served us just as well as the one posited with the character "real"; and in addition we freely operated with possible objects that we were allowed to draw from pure fantasy without any connection to actual experience. We did not for a moment relinquish the reality of the experience-objects as they were in fact experienced by us; on the other hand, this reality-positing that we constantly carried out did not hinder us from running through the pure possibilities such that we gained the most perfect evidence that every object of possible outer perception could also not be, even though it is perceived, thus given in bodily presence as present — while of course for the immanent objects, those of immanent perception, the contradictory opposite was no less evident.

Husserl shows that the analyses operated in a dual mode: reality was accepted (the lectern was taken as existing) but was simultaneously treated as a mere example that could be freely varied in fantasy. The eidetic insights — such as the principled possibility of non-being for any outer object — were gained by running through pure possibilities, not by relying on the existence of any particular object. The factual reality of the lectern made no contribution to the evidences obtained.

---

With this, however, it is absolutely certain that the reality-positing of the outer experience-objects — although we constantly carried it out, although we did not for a moment think of changing anything about it — harbors no prejudice whose validity could affect our results. In other words, the existence of the outer things we happen to see and in no way question, and thus the existence of outer being in general, can be no presupposition on which our findings depend. For we would otherwise come into a manifest absurdity. If our finding is that a priori no possible outer perception excludes the non-being of the perceived, then the consequence is not far off that no outer object, no outer world at all, must be.

The argument reaches its conclusion. If the a priori truth that outer objects could always not-be were dependent on the presupposition that some outer object exists, then this presupposition would have to be necessary — which would contradict the very truth established. The reality-positing is therefore completely irrelevant to the eidetic findings. It is a "fellow-traveler" (Mitläufer) that accompanies the investigation but does not enter into its premises.

---

Can the evident possibility that no thing is depend on the presupposition that a thing that is now experienced as actual is? Or can the possibility that no perception has irrefutable validity depend on the presupposition that some factual perception has irrefutable validity? If there were a logical necessity for me to hold fast to the reality-positing of my perceptions and to build all my other findings upon them, then they would be in principle non-negatable; hence it would be a contradiction to assert that every outer experiential positing is possibly invalid. But we must assert this; it is an absolutely indubitable evidence. Accordingly it is absolutely certain that the ongoing reality-positings with which, during the descriptions in the ego-attitude, we accompany our ongoing actual experiential givennesses — or rather, in which we leave them — do not make the slightest contribution to the assertions we pronounce. Therefore we can — and for reasons of purity of method we must — expressly suspend these reality-positings, set them out of play.

This paragraph formulates the decisive step: the explicit suspension (Ausschaltung) of all reality-positings. Since these positings make no contribution to the a priori evidences, they can be removed without loss — and methodological purity demands that they be removed, to prevent any possible contamination of the eidetic results by unstated existential presuppositions. This is the germ of the phenomenological epoché.

---

It is in itself certain and can be made clear with absolute evidence that there are findings of pure possibilities and of a priori laws (which by their essence are nothing other than laws of pure possibility), and that it belongs to the sense of all such findings that they are afflicted with no prejudice whatsoever regarding any actuality. Pure geometry, for example, or pure mathematics, is through and through a science of pure possibilities; it judges in such a way that not the slightest element of actuality is presupposed by it as a premise. And for just this reason it is called an a priori science.

Husserl illustrates the point with the analogy of pure mathematics. Geometry makes no existential claims about actual spatial figures; it deals exclusively with pure possibilities and their a priori laws. The geometer draws figures on paper and uses them as examples, but the existence of these particular figures is completely irrelevant to the geometrical truths derived from them. The same holds for phenomenological research: it is an a priori science of pure possibilities of consciousness.

---

A radical investigation such as ours cannot proceed as naively as mathematics; it must make clear to itself in good time this absolute independence of possibility and actuality — specifically, of what we just called "pure" possibility — and bring it step by step to ultimate reflective evidence. But above all at the primal source of all philosophical assertions, at the pure I of the ego-attitude. To be sure, I begin with assertions like "I am and find such-and-such," but all the reality-positings can be set entirely out of play as completely irrelevant where I speak of the typology of possible pregivennesses — for example, of I-own and I-foreign — and, more sharply put, speak of their pure possibilities and pronounce general a priori propositions about the necessary existence of the immanent, about the principled possibility of the non-being of the transcendent, and the like.

Husserl now applies the principle to the ego-attitude itself. Even the "I am" — though it is absolutely evident as a fact of the *cogito* — functions in phenomenological research merely as an example for the type "pure I." The phenomenologist speaks not about this factual I with this factual stream of consciousness, but about the pure possibilities of any I whatsoever. All reality-positings, including the positing of one's own existence, can be treated as irrelevant.

---

Phenomenology names such connections of pure, generically evident, most clearly graspable possibilities "eidetic connections," and the consciousness that originally gives them "eidetic intuition."

This brief but important sentence introduces the technical terminology: "eidetic connections" for the a priori connections among pure possibilities, and "eidetic intuition" for the mode of consciousness in which these connections are grasped. This is Husserl's technical name for the method of insight into essential structures through the examination of pure possibilities.

---

In this way, the I in the ego-attitude grasps in eidetic intuition possibilities of the I-ish and I-foreign sphere that prescribe unconditionally valid laws to all possibly occurring actuality.

This sentence summarizes the upshot: from the ego-attitude, using eidetic intuition, the philosopher gains insights into the a priori laws governing all possible experience — laws that hold for any actual experience but are not derived from any particular actual experience.

---

Now, however, something else of great importance for philosophical method remains. Until now we had our gaze directed at the absolutely evident essential laws that we had gained in the attitude of ego-reflection. There is no reason to suppose that those we demonstrated are the only evidences that generically hold for possible pregivennesses of the I. It is rather to be foreseen that in the transition — which the I can freely carry out — from its respective actual pregivennesses to its possible pregivennesses, it can find in free fantasy-operating and -generalizing a perhaps endless abundance of evidences, of a priori valid laws.

Husserl opens the horizon: the few a priori truths obtained so far are merely samples from what must be an inexhaustible field. Pure consciousness, considered as a realm of pure possibilities, must harbor infinitely many essential laws awaiting discovery.

---

If we take the realm of pure possibilities that can belong to the sphere of what is I-ish in the manner of its immanent components — hence the realm that Descartes first designated under the title *cogito*, and of which he recognized that to its most general essence belongs the irrevocability of the positing of being — then the expectation opens up that manifold and unknown a priori laws hold in this realm. Just as phenomenal space, as a realm of pure possibilities of geometrical formations, is the field of an endless a priori science — namely, pure geometry — so we expect that the realm of pure ego-consciousness, as a title for ideally possible consciousness-formations in general — perceptions, representations, judgments, feelings, and so on — is the field of a new endless a priori science. And in fact it is the field of phenomenology.

This is the foundational analogy: just as space is the field of geometry (an infinite a priori science of spatial possibilities), so pure consciousness is the field of phenomenology (an infinite a priori science of consciousness-possibilities). The analogy is not merely illustrative but structural: both sciences investigate the essential laws governing a domain of pure possibilities, without presupposing any particular actualities.

---

Husserl then develops a series of methodological reflections on the relationship between immanent and transcendent a priori sciences, arguing that while sciences like geometry deal with the a priori of external (transcendent) objects, phenomenology deals exclusively with the a priori of pure consciousness. The two are complementary but distinct: the a priori laws of space govern what is possible for objects in space, while the a priori laws of consciousness govern what is possible for the experiences through which objects (including spatial objects) are given.

He introduces the concept of "region" in its full technical sense: a highest material genus of possible objects, corresponding to a closed system of pure possibilities that can be traversed through free fantasy-variation while maintaining the identity of the essential type. Each region has its own a priori — its own system of essential laws — and these regional a priori are what the various eidetic sciences (geometry for space, phenomenology for consciousness, ontology of nature for physical things, etc.) investigate.

Phenomenology, however, occupies a unique position among the eidetic sciences. It is not merely one regional a priori science alongside others; it is the philosophical foundational science upon which all other philosophical concepts and truths are essentially dependent. This is because all other regions are regions of objects that are given through consciousness, and the essential laws of that givenness — the laws governing how objects are constituted in and through conscious experience — are precisely what phenomenology investigates.

The transition to the distinction between the pure I and the human I is now made. In the natural attitude, anyone who reflects upon themselves naturally apprehends themselves as a human being — a psychophysical subject in the world. But this "I-person" apprehension is itself a transcendent positing: the human being is an object given through appearances (through the body), subject to the presumptive character of all transcendent givenness. The pure I of the *ego cogito* is not this human person but the subject for which the human person (along with everything else in the world) is an appearing object.

Husserl argues that the body is an external object like any other: given through spatial appearances, susceptible to doubt and negation. "Perhaps I am only dreaming that I have a body." The I-as-person — the embodied, worldly subject — could in principle not exist, just as any external object could not exist. What remains after the suspension of all external being is the pure I and its pure stream of consciousness, freed from all psychophysical and worldly determinations.

This leads directly into the phenomenological reduction.

### The Method of Phenomenological Reduction. Transcendental Phenomenology as A Priori Science of Pure Consciousness

We now understand that from the beginning, describing in the pure ego-attitude, we in fact described ourselves not as human-I but as pure I. For it follows from the fact that within these descriptions, reflecting upon the I that was describing, we took it for the ego in the sense of the absolute Cartesian evidence *ego cogito, ego sum*. We further understand that in the group of descriptions where we spoke of ego-acts, experiences, and the a priori laws belonging to them, we already meant the entire sphere of pure ego-consciousness, of pure ego-experiences and ego-acts that make up the pure stream of experience — insofar as we here too invoked the Cartesian evidence.

This paragraph confirms retrospectively that the entire preceding investigation was, without fully recognizing it, already operating within the domain of pure (transcendental) consciousness. The descriptions given were descriptions of the pure I, not the human I — because they invoked the Cartesian evidence of the *cogito*, which pertains to the pure subject, not to the empirical person.

---

On the other hand, our entire procedure was naive; our evidence was indeed genuine evidence, but naively carried out, just as the direction of gaze upon the purely I-ish was there but was naive — that is, it lacked the very necessary reflective clarification and the theoretical distinction that alone could prevent the conflation of the givennesses of the two counter-attitudes: the attitude directed toward the transcendental and the attitude directed toward the objective. For once the "I-person" apprehension has developed in consciousness, anyone reflecting upon himself will normally apprehend himself as a person. The I does not remain in purity but receives an objectification. It is only this — quite natural — sliding between the attitudes that has prevented the transcendental sphere, that of the pure I and its pure ego-experiences, from being recognized as a possible and completely self-enclosed field of research.

Husserl diagnoses the source of the historical blindness to the transcendental domain: the natural tendency to slide from the pure ego-attitude into the personalistic self-apprehension. Once we habitually apprehend ourselves as human persons in the world, the pure I is obscured by this objectifying overlay. The transcendental sphere can only be recognized as a self-enclosed research field if this sliding is explicitly blocked.

---

This method is that of the phenomenological reduction — precisely the reduction to the pure phenomena. The first and most important piece of this reduction is the suspension, the phenomenological epoché regarding the totality of outer realities, hence of the whole universe. With absolute evidence we have recognized and can at any time recognize that no outer perception absolutely guarantees the existence of the perceived, or also recognize that every outer being, however bodily, clearly, completely given and thereupon known, exists only provisionally, that it need not exist. With absolute evidence we recognize that the positing of possible non-existence does nothing to the being of the perceiving and other experiencing in which the outer was perceived, pregiven, thought.

This paragraph gives the formal statement of the phenomenological reduction. It has two components: (1) the epoché — the suspension of all position-taking regarding the existence of external realities; and (2) the recognition that this suspension leaves the being of consciousness itself intact. The world may not exist; but even if it does not, the stream of conscious experience continues unaffected in its immanent being.

---

If we hold to this sphere of absolute, principally indubitable, non-negatable, in its thesis non-modalizable being — we gain it in its absolute ownness and purity by seeing to it that nothing of all that we experience under the title of an outer, or judgmentally posit as reality, intrudes into this sphere. This happens by our formulating the principle and letting it become practically and unconditionally determinative for our research: to inhibit every position-taking that expressly or implicitly prejudices the existence of an outer. Then heaven and earth, things and animals and people, but also our own body and we ourselves as human subjects, fall away. And what then remains? — An I, this pure I, and my "I perceive," "I think," "I feel," "I experience this streaming absolute life."

This dramatic paragraph describes the result of the reduction. Everything external falls away — the entire world, including one's own body and one's own personhood. What remains is the pure I and its streaming life of consciousness: perceiving, thinking, feeling, experiencing. This is not a loss but a gain: the disclosure of the field of pure consciousness in its absolute self-givenness, freed from all admixture of the presumptive and the doubtful.

---

It is not in order to carry out a reconstruction of all the sciences and to gain for such a reconstruction an absolutely firm, absolutely indubitable foundation, as Descartes wanted, that we adopt this method; it is not indubitability as absolute security that attracts us. Rather, what we want is the making-visible of an infinite, self-enclosed domain of purely intuitive givennesses and the working-out of the same as a domain of possible science. Indubitability, non-negatability, is for us only an essential characteristic belonging to the peculiarity of this domain. In general it is not a matter for us of a restitution of the Cartesian fundamental meditation, but of an essentially new one that can arise through a certain transformation, purification of the Cartesian one.

Husserl distinguishes his project from Descartes's. Descartes sought an absolutely certain foundation for rebuilding the edifice of the sciences. Husserl's aim is different: to disclose pure consciousness as an infinite field of intuitive givenness that can become the subject matter of a new science. The indubitability of the immanent is not the goal but merely a characteristic of the domain. The phenomenological reduction is not a repetition of Cartesian doubt but an essentially new methodological achievement.

---

It is to be kept sharply in view that the suspension, the bracketing of all objective existences, does not harbor any skepticism regarding them or indeed a devaluation of what we call "objective existence." In truth, nothing is done to the objective world, and even our conviction of the existence of this world and the validity of the sciences relating to it and already developed is not in the least relinquished. It is only a matter of our carrying through, in the framework of the investigations called "purely phenomenological" or "transcendental," the methodological principle of making absolutely no use of any and all convictions, of any and all position-takings regarding outer realities — of abstaining in phenomenology from all judging about the objective.

This is a crucial clarification. The epoché is not skepticism; it does not deny the world or cast doubt upon it. The phenomenologist as a person continues to believe in the world. But within the phenomenological investigation, this belief is methodically set aside — not used as a premise, not relied upon, not appealed to. The distinction between having a conviction and making use of it within a given domain of inquiry is fundamental to the method.

---

But we turn our investigating gaze toward what remains for us and what always and necessarily is there — indeed, is our entire streaming life: the I and ego-consciousness with all its formations, with all the emerging, streaming intentions, appearances, thoughts, feelings, and so on, all taken in the full abundance of their essentially own components. The transcendental is thus not some concept, some construction of an extravagant philosophy, but streaming life itself as it actually is ego-life, under exclusion of everything that in this life, in the form of intentions and appearances, is merely intended, appearing, thought, and the like, but is not itself in the stream, not occurring streaming.

Husserl insists that the "transcendental" is not a philosophical construction or a metaphysical abstraction — it is simply conscious life itself, taken purely as it is lived, without the overlay of objectifying interpretations. The transcendental is the streaming life of consciousness in its full concreteness. What is excluded is not anything real but only the interpretive layers that claim the intended objects are "out there" independently of consciousness. The transcendental is the nearest and most intimate reality, not the farthest and most abstract.

---

On the other hand it is also clear, though still standing in unanalyzed distance, that the exclusion of objectivity yields pure consciousness for itself and as a self-enclosed field of being for the respective I of the ego-attitude, but that the outer world, which for the I is a constant surrounding world, has a wondrous relation to the field of pure consciousness — on account of which it makes no sense, or seems to make no sense, to separate it entirely from consciousness and to regard world and consciousness as two side-by-side spheres of being. For the I in its own ego-life is what, so to speak, creates for itself its surrounding world; it is the achievement of certain of its experiences, the play of immanent appearances, peculiar syntheses of these appearances under the titles "concordant flowing-together in consciousness of selfsameness," and the like, that makes it the case for the I that it has the outer as bodily pregivenness and that it can have one and the same outer given in ever-new appearances.

This anticipatory paragraph points toward the deepest insight of transcendental phenomenology: the world is not simply excluded by the reduction but rediscovered within consciousness as its intentional correlate. The outer world has a "wondrous relation" to pure consciousness — it is constituted through the synthetic achievements of consciousness. The I "creates" its surrounding world, not in the sense of fabricating it, but in the sense that the sense of "world," "thing," "reality" is constituted through the intentional performances of consciousness. This is the theme of constitutional phenomenology, which the reduction opens up as a field of research.

---

Husserl then emphasizes the importance of the methodological reflections, noting that unlike in the natural sciences — where methodology is a technical matter that the beginner can do without — in phenomenology the method is constitutive of the domain. Without the reduction, the field of pure consciousness cannot be accessed at all. "There is no royal road into phenomenology and thereby into philosophy. The phenomenological reduction is the one and only gateway that leads into the philosophical realm."

The reduction is then extended to cover not only external realities but also external possibilities and the a priori sciences (geometry, mechanics, etc.) that deal with them. Even a fantasized centaur, though it does not belong to the actual world, is transcendent in the relevant sense: it is given through one-sided appearances and could be subject to further determination. The exclusion must therefore encompass all transcendent positings — whether of actualities or of pure possibilities — to secure the purity of the phenomenological field.

What remains after this comprehensive reduction is transcendental phenomenology: an a priori science of the essential structures of pure consciousness. It is explicitly distinguished from empirical psychology (which studies the factual mental life of human persons in the natural world) and from the a priori sciences of external objects (geometry, mechanics). Phenomenology studies pure consciousness in its ownmost essential structures, using eidetic intuition and free fantasy-variation as its method.

The remainder of Part I (approximately pp. 82–117 of the book) develops the programme of transcendental phenomenology in further detail. The key developments are as follows.

Husserl addresses the problem of the transcendence of the a priori itself. A priori truths — mathematical laws, logical principles — are not temporal events in the stream of consciousness. The proposition 2 × 2 = 4 is not located in any particular experience as a "real" (reell) component. In that sense, it is "transcendent." But Husserl resolves this apparent difficulty by distinguishing between the a priori truth as thematic content of the experience and the experience of insight in which that truth is grasped. The phenomenologist does not posit the a priori truth as a transcendent entity; rather, the phenomenologist studies the act of evident insight itself, as a type of consciousness with its own essential structure. Geometry cannot serve as a premise for a theory of geometrical knowledge; but the acts in which geometrical truths are grasped are themselves phenomena to be studied.

---

A crucial supplementary point follows: all that has been excluded by the reduction — all transcendent realities and possibilities — nonetheless remains within the phenomenological field in a modified way. This is the doctrine of the "phenomenological bracket" (Einklammerung). The bracketed content does not vanish; it remains as the intentional correlate of consciousness — the "perceived as such," the "remembered as such," the "thought as such." The outer world, suspended as to its reality, reappears within phenomenology as the noematic correlate of the acts in which it is intended.

This is a decisive insight. The phenomenological reduction does not impoverish the field of investigation but infinitely enriches it. Every act of consciousness has its noematic correlate — the object as intended, with all the determinations, modes of givenness, degrees of clarity and indeterminacy that belong to it. Phenomenology studies not only the acts (the noetic side) but also these correlates (the noematic side). The entire world — physical things, persons, cultural objects, scientific theories — becomes accessible to phenomenological investigation, not as posited reality but as intentional sense.

---

Husserl introduces the fundamental distinction between "real" (reell) and "intentional" components of consciousness. The real components are the actually occurring data of the experience-stream: sensation-data (Empfindungsdaten), apprehensions (Auffassungen), the characters of bodily presence or mere imaging, temporal phases. The intentional components are the noematic correlates: the intended object with its features, the mode of its givenness, the horizons of indeterminacy belonging to it. The intended object is "irreell" — it is never a real piece of the experience-stream but is constituted through the synthetic unity of manifold real components.

The distinction between noesis and noema now receives its full formulation. "Noesis" designates consciousness itself, the stream of experience in its full concreteness, considered according to everything that a real analysis — a division into parts, pieces, and abstract moments — can yield. "Noema" designates everything that can be found on the side of the intended, of what is conscious as such — the object as it is meant, with the sense through which it is meant, with the modes of clarity or obscurity, determinacy or indeterminacy, in which it is given.

Every noetic moment has noematic function: nothing in the real content of a consciousness fails to contribute to the sense-giving for the noematic x. Consciousness is through and through consciousness-of-something; analyzing it in any direction, one always finds the "of something."

---

Husserl then takes up the question of the relationship between the noematic object ("the object in quotation marks," the intentional correlate) and the object simpliciter (the object as posited in the natural attitude as really existing). In the natural attitude, the object is simply taken as real — the perception reaches through to the thing itself. In the phenomenological attitude, this reality-claim is suspended, and what remains is the noematic object: the thing as perceived, with the sense and the horizons that belong to it as perceived.

Existence, Husserl argues, is not a property of the object but an index for a certain structure of experiential course: the idea of an identical x as the theme of a consistently self-confirming positing, an open infinite horizon of concordant experience. To say that an object exists is to say that there is an open-ended process of experience that would consistently confirm and further determine the object without ever requiring its abandonment. Existence is thus "an idea" — an ideal limit-concept pointing to an infinite experiential process.

---

Husserl then develops the concept of "transcendental guidelines" (transzendentale Leitfäden) — the idea that the ontological categories of each region of objects (thing, person, cultural artifact, number, etc.) serve as guiding threads for the phenomenological investigation of the constitutive consciousness in which objects of that type are given. The formal categories (object, property, relation, whole, part, number) provide the most general guidelines; the material-regional categories (physical thing, animate body, psyche, person) provide more specific ones.

This leads to a systematic overview of the programme of phenomenological research. In the pre-theoretical sphere, each region of objects calls for: (1) a formal-general study of how objects in general are given in consciousness — what modes of symbolic and intuitive givenness exist, how they relate to each other, how all symbolic consciousness refers back to intuition and ultimately to perception; and (2) a region-specific study of how objects of each particular type are given — through what appearance-structures, through what sensory and kinaesthetic systems, through what modes of evidence and verification.

When the theoretical sciences are drawn in, a parallel structure emerges: (1) a formal-general theory of scientific knowledge as such, correlated with the formal mathesis universalis (formal logic, arithmetic, set theory, analysis); and (2) region-specific theories of scientific knowledge for each material domain (physics, biology, psychology, the human sciences). The formal mathesis provides the most general transcendental guidelines; the material ontologies of each region provide more specific ones.

---

Husserl emphasizes that phenomenology is not merely an abstract philosophical exercise but the "great school of clarification" for all the sciences. Every science operates with concepts that have become sedimented and mechanized through habitual use; the original evidences in which these concepts were born have been lost. Phenomenological reflection recovers these evidences, distinguishes the genuine sense-content of concepts from the extraneous associations that have accumulated around them, and thereby purifies and deepens the foundations of the sciences.

The final paragraphs of Part I draw the threads together. Transcendental phenomenology is the foundational science upon which all genuine philosophy depends. Its relationship to the established sciences is one of mutual enrichment: the sciences provide the ontological material (concepts, distinctions, basic principles) that serve as transcendental guidelines for phenomenological investigation, while phenomenology provides the ultimate clarification of the sense and foundations of the sciences. The goal is a philosophical science in which the naivety of the natural attitude is overcome — not by abandoning the sciences but by supplementing them with the transcendental reflection that alone can provide ultimate understanding of what they achieve.

The natural-dogmatic sciences, Husserl concludes, are what we have before philosophy. But perfectly objective science is possible only within the framework of philosophy and through the methodical work of philosophy. The last dogmatic science is the one at which all clarifying work has already been done and which has alongside it a corresponding phenomenology. With this, the human being is at any time in a position to answer reflective sense-questions and transcendental-philosophical questions without confusion and to be safe from all misconceived metaphysics.

## Part II. Reflections toward a Transcendental Theory of Nature and Spirit

### The Articulation into Things and Subjects according to the Distinction of Nature and Spirit

After the indispensable general introduction to phenomenology and transcendental epistemology, and in particular into the transcendental theory of cognition directed at outer realities and possibilities — that is, cognition of nature and spirit — we take up again the themes touched on earlier.

Husserl marks the transition from the general phenomenological methodology of Part I to the specific thematic work of the lectures. The "indispensable" general introduction was not a digression; the entire framework of the reduction, eidetic method, and noetic-noematic analysis was needed before the question of nature and spirit could be posed with philosophical precision.

---

We had already, but in a quite naive way, begun to form first thoughts about the separation of nature and spirit. We had already grasped the world as the world of our knowing consciousness, had already resolved to exclude all impacts of scientific knowledge from the world-phenomenon and to consider the world as the world of pre-theoretical consciousness. As the first, roughest relevant division for nature and spirit, the distinction between subjects and things had emerged. All this we retain, only that in our present phenomenological attitude it receives a new valuation, its determinate eidetic-phenomenological significance.

The naive distinction between things and subjects — which opened the lectures — is now taken up again within the purified phenomenological framework. What was a rough, pre-reflective observation now becomes a theme for eidetic-phenomenological investigation. The naive sense of "nature" and "spirit" must be traced back to its origin in prescientific experience, specifically in perception and memory.

---

Husserl acknowledges that a full transcendental theory of nature and spirit would require a general phenomenology of consciousness-structures and a formal epistemology that cannot be presupposed here. But a connected series of investigations can be undertaken that will open new perspectives for those coming from either the natural or the human sciences. The phenomenological reduction is to be maintained throughout, even where not explicitly mentioned.

The fundamental question is then posed: how is transcendent objectivity (for which we have the still quite unclear titles "nature" and "spirit") given a priori as the correlate of outer intuitions? The basic types of outer objectivities are to be taken not as basic types of outer realities or possibilities taken at face value, but as guiding threads for noematic-noetic phenomenological investigations. All talk of "things" and "subjects" is in quotation marks — noematic, not ontological.

---

Husserl begins the systematic articulation with the most universal ontological structures. Every possible external concrete individual is a time-object with temporal predicates. Multiple individuals can coexist, and the totality of coexisting individuals forms a world ordered in a single, universal time. Time is not a "pure form of intuiting" (against Kant) but a form of the intuited as such — a structural feature of objects as they present themselves in experience.

All concrete external individuals also stand in spatial form. Things, distinguished from subjects, are *res extensae*: they have spatial extension, a shape, and a position in space at every moment. Subjects, insofar as they have bodies, have a mediated relation to space through the body.

---

A priori we can think things without their being bodies for subjects, and even when we have bodies before our eyes, we can think that these bodies are in truth not bodies but merely things, without being connected with subjects. If we restrict ourselves, within the universal temporal form, to those concrete objects that by their essentially own content fit into the spatial form — that is, if we restrict ourselves to the *res extensae* — we have a clear separation between nature and spirit. Nature in the specific sense, the theme of natural science, consists of mere things, things as mere nature, i.e., the *res extensae*, abstracting from the possible interweaving with subjects that gives certain things the character of bodies.

This is the first precise formulation of the concept of "nature" (Natur) in the phenomenological sense: nature is the totality of *res extensae* considered purely as spatial-temporal-causal things, abstracted from all connection with subjectivity. This abstraction is legitimate because things are thinkable without being bodies for subjects — a stone can be a complete concrete thing without being anyone's body.

---

However, the concept of mere nature is not so quickly won. The same things that are objects of natural science — tables, hammers, flags, artworks, churches — are in most cases also something more than mere physical objects. They carry "meaning-predicates" (Bedeutungsprädikate) — predicates that do not belong to the thing's real constitution but accrue to it through the meaning-bestowing acts of subjects.

Husserl develops this through a series of vivid examples. When I select a stone as a projectile, a piece of wood as a club, I do not change the thing physically, but it acquires a new predicate — "weapon," "tool" — that henceforth adheres to it and in terms of which I subsequently apprehend it. A carpenter shapes wood into a table: here there is both physical alteration and meaning-bestowal. A distinctive tree serves as a trail marker: no physical change, but a new meaning-predicate that can be transmitted through tradition to other subjects.

---

In general: spiritual meaning, consisting in certain predicates belonging to the object, is originally the achievement-correlate of certain subject-acts that bestow meaning upon pregiven objects. Such predicates can be fully intuitively understood only if one goes back to the active subjectivity in whose acts they originally constitute themselves. The meaning bestowed by subjects is not merely understood but actively taken over when other subjects carry out the practical positings contained in the comprehending acts and thereby let the meaning hold good.

This paragraph establishes the phenomenological concept of "meaning" (Bedeutung) in the broad sense: meaning-predicates are not intrinsic features of things but constitutive achievements of subjects — products of evaluating, willing, purposing, and acting. They are "founded" — they presuppose pre-given objects upon which they are built up. To understand what "hammer" means, one must trace the meaning back to the purposive acts in which something was first determined as a hammer.

---

What clearly separates itself is the contrast between every real change — which alters the pre-given object as a real object — and the creation or alteration of those quite different predicates that are truly ascribed to the real but are not real predicates and whose appearance and change need not go hand in hand with real change. Our everyday surrounding world is almost entirely filled with objects that are apperceived with manifold meaning-predicates — so immediately apperceived that these predicates are designated as "perceived," as "seen" or "heard," just as readily as the real predicates given in the most immediate sensory experience. How common is the expression: "I see that this is a hammer," "I hear that a violin sounds."

Husserl draws the fundamental distinction that grounds the entire analysis: real predicates (Realprädikate) versus meaning-predicates (Bedeutungsprädikate). Real predicates belong to the thing as a physical object and change only through physical alteration. Meaning-predicates are bestowed by subjects and can arise, change, or vanish without any physical change in the object. Yet in everyday experience, the two types of predicates are given together, fused in a single apprehension.

---

Through the contrast of "real predicate" and "meaning-predicate" we also gain a determinate concept of reality. If we carry out the reductive process — strip from an individual object all meaning-predicates — we obtain the pure real, and if we do so with a spatial thing, we obtain the mere natural object, the mere spatial thing as real. To consider given objects as mere realities, things as mere nature, thus means to consider them in abstraction from all meaning, from all predicates whose apperceptive source lies in subject-acts.

This is the phenomenological derivation of the concept of "nature": nature is what remains when all meaning-predicates are stripped away — the pure real substrate upon which all spiritual meaning is built. A flag is a physical thing (fabric, dye, fibers) and also a symbol; considered as mere nature, it has only its physical predicates. The meaning-predicates ("flag," "symbol of the nation") are irreell — they have no intrinsic spatial character of their own.

---

As with things, so with the external objects we call subjects: meaning-predicates are extrinsic to them. They are predicates whose sense includes a reference to another subjectivity that relates itself to these objects in acts, to a subjectivity that bestows meaning upon them. According to ideal possibility, ever-new subject-acts can relate to other subjects as external objects, and with respect to this merely ideal possibility, predicates with validity in themselves — beauty, ethical value, and the like — may belong to them. But it is not necessary that they actually become themes of such acts. In any case: completely apart from all possible and actual meaning, they have as subjects their own concrete being; in the sense of the concept of reality now guiding us, they have in and for themselves their real being, which can be found and verified intuitively without regard to subjects that relate themselves to this real (which is here itself a subject) in their acts.

Husserl establishes that subjects, like things, have a real core that is independent of whatever meaning-predicates may be bestowed upon them by other subjects. A person has their own concrete real being — their psychic life, their character, their consciousness — regardless of how others evaluate, classify, or regard them. The meaning-predicates (this person is a "professor," a "good person," an "enemy") are extrinsic overlays upon a real substrate.

---

The peculiarity of these realities — the psychic, the subjective — is however totally different from that of the *res extensae*. They belong to totally different regions, provided we take the mere subjects and not their bodies along with them. To their real kind as subjects belongs being in the mode of consciousness, which further implies being able to have consciousness and to be conscious of oneself, to be or to be able to be an actively functioning I, and thus to be able to be actively directed toward other things and oneself in the mode of grasping, taking a stance, and so on. Having consciousness includes the possibility of being able to have consciousness of external being — indeed, in ideal possibility, of being able to have consciousness of any external being whatsoever. The thingly real is, but knows nothing of itself and of other things; its essence excludes experiencing, having consciousness, having consciousness of other things and of having consciousness itself.

This paragraph establishes the radical ontological difference between the two fundamental regions. Things (res extensae) are spatial, extended, causally interconnected — but they do not experience, do not know, are not conscious of anything. Subjects are conscious, self-aware, capable of acts directed toward objects — but their being is not primarily spatial. Even if (as Bruno or Leibniz believed) every thing were ensouled, the physical thing as mere res extensa would still exclude the psychic from its own real essence. The psychic would belong to a soul "built upon" the body, not to the body itself considered as a physical object.

---

Our regional divisions in the pre-theoretically intuitive world now gain an essential supplement and further articulation when we introduce a fundamental distinction with regard to the subject-sphere: namely, between singular subjects and subject-associations, specifically between individual persons and personalities of a higher order — social communities. By subject-associations we understand not mere sets, mere pluralities of subjects, but unities of a higher order formed from subjects — articulated subject-wholes, in which the physical bodies of the subjects are indeed involved, but so that not they as physical objects determine the connection and articulation, but exclusively the subjects, who in their singularity are the ultimate members. The "connection" is a "spiritual" one: it is constituted through the mutual relating of subjects to one another in subject-acts and is a possibly self-propagating and self-sustaining achievement.

Husserl introduces the concept of social communities as "personalities of a higher order" — a concept that will be central to his later social philosophy. A community is not a mere aggregate of individuals but a structured unity constituted through the spiritual acts by which subjects relate to one another. The connection is specifically spiritual — it arises through understanding, communication, shared volition, and mutual meaning-bestowal, not through physical contiguity or causal connection.

---

If we call subjects, as subjects of performing ego-acts, "persons" or "spiritual subjects," then a subject-association is an association of subjects as persons, as spiritual subjects, and is constituted through their ongoing spiritual performance as a personal unity of a higher order. In a generalized and fundamentally essential sense, the personal associations themselves have the character of a subject and possibly even of a person. In near or distant analogy, one can speak of them as subjects of acts. Already what we call in ordinary social life a "party" — say, a tea party — is more than a plurality of subjects and more even than a plurality of persons; an overarching "communal spirit," a different level of social subjectivity, is thereby — albeit only temporarily — constituted. Likewise when we consider a marriage or any association as a purposive fellowship of manifold kinds, further a municipality, a state, a people.

Husserl develops the concept of social personality through a graduated series of examples: a tea party (a temporary, loose social unity), a marriage, an association, a municipality, a state, a people. Each constitutes a social subjectivity of a distinct type and level. The claim is strong: these are not mere metaphors — social communities genuinely possess a form of subjectivity, constituted through the spiritual acts of their members.

---

Within such a person-association, every individual person has their own individual life, and the association itself its associative life, a communal life, which is not a mere sum of the individual lives. Every individual person functions therein as a member of the association and as such has its functional predicates alongside its own real predicates. These are obviously a special kind of meaning-predicates; for everything in the communal association that, beyond the individual realities of its personal members, creates unity — thus constitutes the association as association — is, according to what has already been said, a performance of personal acts in which individual persons relate to one another and thus bestow meaning upon one another.

The communal life of a social association is irreducible to the sum of individual lives — this is a strong anti-reductionist thesis about social reality. The unity of the community is a unity of meaning, constituted through the mutual meaning-bestowal by which members determine one another's roles, functions, and significance within the whole. The functional predicates (citizen, servant, general) are meaning-predicates of a specifically social kind.

---

We now form a widest possible concept of culture by thereby taking together all real objectivities that are endowed with meaning, singly or in groups, possibly synthetically connected through meaning-performance — all real objectivities that are not merely realities or are not merely considered as such, but that, or insofar as they, are endowed with meaning by meaning-creating subjectivities. Culture would thus be in general the correlate of performing subjectivity. Specifically, all sociality is the correlate of performing subjectivity. What unites it, makes it a sociality, is the performance of subject-acts; the unity-form is a meaning-form.

This is the phenomenological definition of "culture" in its widest sense: the totality of objects endowed with meaning through the performances of subjectivity. Culture is not a thing or a collection of things but the correlate of meaning-bestowing activity. This definition encompasses both individual and social culture, both material objects (tools, artworks, buildings) and immaterial formations (laws, customs, institutions, languages).

---

In the contrast we have so far drawn, we have on the one side nature as the world of mere realities, as the world considered without meaning, as the world considered without regard to any meaning. On the other side, the meaning-rich world, the world in its meaning bestowed by individual subjectivity or communal subjectivity or even by a merely possible subjectivity, and considered in this meaning, for the sake of its kind. Nothing other than this is expressed by the juxtaposition of nature and spirit, especially if we think of the "human sciences" (Geisteswissenschaften) and the sense that the word "spirit" alone has and can have there.

This is the culminating formulation of the nature/spirit distinction. "Nature" = the world of mere realities, considered apart from all meaning-bestowal. "Spirit" = the same world considered in its meaning, as the correlate of performing subjectivity. The two are not separate worlds but two ways of regarding the same world — and this is precisely what the traditional contrast between Naturwissenschaften and Geisteswissenschaften expresses at the level of scientific methodology.

### The Basic Types of Possible Sciences according to the Articulation of Nature and Spirit

Husserl now asks what types of science are prefigured by the regional articulation of the world. The question can be posed ontologically (what sciences correspond to the essential structure of a possible world?) or phenomenologically (what constitutive structures of consciousness underlie the different types of scientific knowledge?).

On the ontological side, each region calls for both a priori (eidetic) sciences and empirical (factual) sciences. A priori sciences investigate the essential structures of possible objects of a given type; empirical sciences investigate the actual facts of the given world within the framework established by the a priori sciences. Among the empirical sciences, Husserl distinguishes descriptive sciences (which classify concrete types) from explanatory sciences (which seek causal laws and theoretical explanations).

The fundamental point is that the distinction between natural-scientific and human-scientific method is not arbitrary but rooted in the essential difference between the naturalistic and personalistic attitudes and the corresponding regional ontologies.

### Outlines of an Ontology and Phenomenology of Physical Nature

Husserl undertakes a detailed analysis of the a priori structures of physical nature. The investigation proceeds in two complementary directions: ontological (what belongs essentially to the idea of a physical world?) and phenomenological (what are the constitutive structures of consciousness in which a physical world comes to givenness?).

The ontological analysis begins with the most fundamental structures: spatiality, temporality, and material quality. Every physical thing has spatial extension, temporal duration, and qualitative filling. These are essentially interconnected. The spatial form has an a priori structure described by geometry. Husserl raises the question of whether Euclidean space is the only possible spatial form or whether other geometries could ground alternative possible physical worlds — a question connected to the mathematical concept of "definite manifold."

The analysis of material quality introduces the distinction between "primary" and "secondary" qualities, which Husserl subjects to phenomenological revision. The sensory qualities constitute the original intuitive content of physical things — the "filling" of spatial forms. Causality is treated as an essential feature of physical nature: every physical thing stands in causal relations, and a world of things without causal interconnection is inconceivable.

The phenomenological analysis examines the modes of consciousness in which these structures come to givenness: spatial form through kinaesthetic systems; quality through sensory data; causality through the experience of functional dependencies. Throughout, Husserl maintains the distinction between the ontological and phenomenological levels as complementary.

### Physical Nature as a Domain of Sciences and Truths

This section examines the types of scientific knowledge possible with respect to physical nature. On the a priori side, Husserl identifies the ontological disciplines (geometry, kinematics, pure mechanics) and notes that natural scientists have neglected the qualitative a priori. On the empirical side, he distinguishes descriptive sciences (biology, taxonomy) from exact-mathematical sciences (physics, chemistry).

Husserl analyzes the process of idealization and mathematization by which the intuitive world is transformed into the "world of physics." The philosophical question — whether the idealized world is the "true" world — is answered in favor of the intuitive: the mathematical-physical world is a theoretical achievement whose meaning derives from and refers back to intuitive experience.

### The Problems concerning the Psychophysical and the Psychic Sphere and the Corresponding Sciences

The final section of the main text turns to the psychophysical and the purely psychic. Nature as a physical world of genuine being is thinkable only as psychophysical nature — including animate bodies and conscious subjects. Husserl distinguishes three levels of the animate: (1) the physical organism; (2) the body as Leib (enriched by sensory fields and kinaesthetic functions — the domain of "aesthesiology"); and (3) the psyche or soul.

Psychology, Husserl argues, is in a state of fundamental confusion about its own nature. The influence of phenomenology on contemporary psychology has remained superficial. A properly founded psychology would proceed from the essential structures uncovered by phenomenology, apply them to the empirical psyche, and develop theories of mental life that understand the psychic from within — in terms of intentional structures and motivational connections — rather than merely applying natural-scientific methods to mental phenomena.

The lectures break off with these problems still under development, pointing forward to investigations Husserl will continue in later works.

## Supplement I

Supplement I is a methodological reflection on the distinctive character of philosophical-phenomenological inquiry as compared with mathematical inquiry. In mathematics, the axioms are established once and for all, each step is final, and the science progresses linearly from foundations to consequences. In phenomenology, by contrast, the investigation necessarily proceeds in a "zigzag" (Zickzack): a first stretch of evident thinking leads to provisional results, but these results, when combined with further insights, cast new light on the beginning, requiring a return to the starting points to rework and deepen them. This zigzag structure is not a deficiency but an essential feature of philosophical thinking, which deals with matters that reveal their full sense only progressively.

Husserl illustrates this with the methodological situation of the lectures: the inquiry began with an apparently simple "I-attitude" and the question "What do I find pregiven?" But only through the development of the analyses did the full import of this starting-point become clear. The return to the beginning — now equipped with the distinction between the pure I and the empirical I, with the method of the reduction, and with the basic phenomenological insights — transforms the initial question and deepens it.

This supplement also emphasizes that philosophical inquiry demands that each person think for themselves, starting from their own evidence and refusing to accept anything from tradition, authority, or established science that has not been personally verified. The student must "look within" and confirm each claim through their own intuition.

## Supplement II

Supplement II is a brief text offering further reflections on the general structure of the prescientific world and its layering. It develops the idea that the world as given before all science has an essential structure — a layering of constitution from the most basic physical stratum through the animate and the psychic to the cultural and personal — and that this structure provides the "transcendental guideline" for all phenomenological investigation.

## Supplement III

Supplement III develops further reflections on the nature/spirit distinction, particularly the idea that physical nature constitutes a self-enclosed region that can be investigated independently, while spirit and culture presuppose nature as their foundation. The relationship between the two is not one of simple parallelism but of foundational dependence: there can be no spirit without nature (specifically, without embodied animate nature), but nature can be thought without spirit.

This supplements also contains reflections on the status of causal explanation in the natural and human sciences. In natural science, causal explanation takes the form of subsumption under mathematical laws. In the human sciences, "explanation" takes a fundamentally different form: it proceeds through understanding of meaning, motivation, and rational connection. These are not two species of a single genus "explanation" but two essentially different types of cognitive achievement, rooted in the essential difference between the naturalistic and personalistic attitudes.

## Supplement IV

Supplement IV discusses the problem of the a priori and its relationship to the phenomenological reduction. It addresses the question of whether a priori truths (mathematical laws, logical principles) are themselves "transcendent" to consciousness and therefore subject to the phenomenological epoché. Husserl's answer is nuanced: the a priori truth itself (e.g., 2 × 2 = 4) is not a temporal event in the stream of consciousness and is in that sense "transcendent." But the conscious act in which the truth is grasped — the evidence, the insight, the judgmental experience — is immanent. Phenomenology does not claim the a priori truths as its own content but studies the conscious acts in which they are constituted, grasped, and verified.

This supplement also reflects on the relationship between phenomenology and formal logic, arguing that formal-logical structures (propositions, inferences, theories) have their own type of "ideality" that must be distinguished from both empirical reality and the pure possibilities of phenomenological investigation.
