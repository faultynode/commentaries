# Georg Picht, *Aristoteles' "De anima"* — Commentary on Part I

## First Part: Aristotle and Hegel — Introduction to Aristotle as Introduction to Metaphysics as Such

### I. Methodological Preliminary Remark

Picht opens the First Part by explaining a change in the structure of his lecture course. He had originally planned a second section of his introduction dealing with the relationship between Hegel and Aristotle, intended to show why the study of Aristotle is important for the problems of the present day. But in the process of working this out, he realized that anyone who speaks adequately about Aristotle and Hegel is speaking about metaphysics as such — about its central themes and its structure. He is speaking about the spiritual unity and the fundamental law of the epoch of European metaphysics, which stretches from the sixth century before Christ to the beginning of the twentieth century. Only under this historical perspective does it become clear why the founding of metaphysics must be understood by us in this turning of the ages, when metaphysics has entered its great crisis, if we wish to master the crisis of today. The second part of the introduction has therefore expanded into the first main part of the lecture: an introduction to Aristotle that becomes an introduction to metaphysics as such, carried out in the form of a comparison between Aristotle and Hegel.

Picht then addresses a fundamental methodological question about how historical comparison in the humanities (Geisteswissenschaften) is typically conducted. The standard procedure treats the two works being compared as isolated phenomena, things that are simply available in the mass of tradition, that one can pick up and compare at will. One establishes correspondences and differences; the correspondences are filed under the heading "influence," the differences confirm the trivial conviction that everything always changes in history. The catalogue of uncovered "relations" between Aristotle and Hegel then produces a quantitative increase in the total sum of positive historical knowledge, warranting an additional page in the next edition of Ueberweg's history of philosophy.

Picht insists this kind of research must not be undervalued, but one must not be deceived about its function and the nature of its results. The external fact that a young Privatdozent in Jena in 1805 was strongly impressed by Aristotle and changed his philosophical convictions is interesting enough in itself, but it has little to do with the great course of history, even if the Privatdozent bears the name "Hegel." For the understanding of Aristotle himself and the content of the books *On the Soul*, this historical accident is external. This is why the history of reception (Wirkungsgeschichte) is typically relegated to an appendix.

The situation looks entirely different, Picht argues, once one immerses oneself in the Wirkungsgeschichte and discovers that Aristotle prescribed the paths in which human beings actually thought for two millennia and still think today. When a philosophy achieves this — when it causes real history, insofar as it is influenced by human thought, to run differently than it would have run without this philosophy — then the Wirkungsgeschichte can no longer be treated as an external appendix. It says something essential about the philosophy itself. We can then no longer view the writings of Aristotle as a given object isolable from its reception history. The Wirkungsgeschichte is then the philosophy's own future, just as the prehistory that entered it is its own past. The historical power that these thoughts have exercised belongs to the inner truth of these thoughts. But this historical power cannot be discovered through merely positivist historical-philological exegesis. The findings of such exegesis stand to the true content of the thoughts as the chemical analysis of a painting's colors stands to the painting as a work of art.

Picht draws from this the consequence that we do not carry out the comparison between Aristotle and Hegel in order to convince ourselves externally that Aristotle was studied in Jena; we carry it out in order to gain a standpoint from which we can understand and appreciate Aristotle himself as the historical power he was. What we understand in this way is twofold. On one side, it is the only path by which one grasps what Aristotle is actually talking about. The concepts of his philosophy and the lines of thought that we retrace in studying the texts are at first just as external and abstract to us as the fictive objectivity we have before our eyes when we take note of the textual record. Only when we see how these thoughts intervene with irresistible power in the course of real history does it gradually become clear that it is reality itself that we encounter in these texts under a particular perspective. Only when we discover Aristotle in ourselves — in those representations that determine our behavior — and are able to identify him there, do we notice what it is in truth that the texts are about. Through Hegel's philosophy, Aristotle became one of the great driving forces of the history of the nineteenth and twentieth centuries, and to the extent that we become conscious of this, we discover the substantive content of those concepts that at first appear so abstract.

The second thing we recognize on this path is one of the great fundamental laws of European history. The age from Parmenides to Nietzsche is the age of metaphysics. Metaphysics has given its stamp not only to thinking but also to the political and social structures produced by human thought. Hegel could only receive Aristotle as he did because he recognized in Aristotle the fundamental law of the historical world of Europe. Hegel's great theme was history; he could not have carried out his philosophy of history within the framework of Aristotelian ontology if that ontology had not actually had a determining effect on the course of history. Since Nietzsche, philosophy has moved within the experience of the crisis of metaphysics. As long as our thought does not get to the bottom of this crisis, we will also not be able to solve the real problems of our future history. The comparison of Hegel and Aristotle is not the only but is an indispensable step toward recognizing the causes of the crisis of metaphysics. It is a step of the self-knowledge of our thinking.

For the construction of the lecture, Picht explains, this first part cannot be dispensed with. Here the question is marked under which we wish to examine the books *On the Soul*. We gain a first intimation of what took place in the thinking of Aristotle, and because we have the whole of the enterprise of the next two semesters before our eyes, he does not shy from laying out this first part broadly, thereby relieving the later work.

### II. Perspectives of the Comparison of Hegel with Aristotle: The Essence of Actuality — The Essence of Truth — The Essence of God

Picht begins this major section by noting that Hegel absorbed the philosophy of Aristotle so broadly that relations to Aristotle could be shown at any arbitrary point in his work. But the concern here is not an external historical collecting of data that one can tally up to quantitatively establish the so-called "influence" of Aristotle on Hegel. What matters is the moving principle of Hegel's philosophy and the question whether this principle has something to do with Aristotle. Hegel's philosophy since the *Phenomenology of Spirit* is in its totality a presentation of absolute spirit. Absolute spirit is for Hegel not some isolated phenomenon that could be considered separately from the phenomena of nature, history, law, and morality. Hegel rather teaches that everything that is at all, insofar as it is grasped in its actuality, is a shape of absolute spirit in which it comprehends itself in a mode of its self-externalization. Everything that is at all is, in some way, if only one grasps it in its truth, spirit.

Picht then turns to a pivotal passage in Aristotle's *De anima*: the statement that "the soul is in some way all beings" (III, 8, 431b21). The essence of the soul appears in its highest form, which Aristotle calls *nous*. Hegel translates *nous* as "spirit" (*Geist*). Thus it follows that Aristotle, interpreted in Hegel's sense, also taught that spirit is in some way the totality of beings. Picht cautions, however, that this sentence means something entirely different in Aristotle than Hegel's apparently corresponding doctrine. Nevertheless the centrality of the connection between the two teachings is visible at first glance. Picht adds that he has simultaneously indicated why we should seek access to understanding Aristotle precisely from the neglected work *On the Soul*. No one today knows what one should actually think at the word "soul." Psychology is one of the most popular fashionable sciences, but one will search in vain for a psychologist who can explain the name "psychology." From Plato to Hegel, the word *psyche* — the soul — designates the domain in which the actual shows itself in its truth. In Kant's language, *psyche* is the transcendental subject. Therefore, in the age of European metaphysics, the question of the essence of truth cannot be separated from the question of the essence of the soul. Only because the work *On the Soul*, like Hegel's philosophy of absolute spirit, is an investigation into the essence of truth could Hegel say that the essential purpose of a philosophy of spirit is to unlock again the sense of the Aristotelian books *On the Soul*.

The highest form of the soul — Hegel replaces the concept "soul" with the modern concept "consciousness" — is for Hegel spirit, for Aristotle *nous*. In its pure and perfect actuality, spirit for Hegel and *nous* for Aristotle is not the finite spirit of human beings but God. Therefore the books *On the Soul* are the preparation for understanding the theology of Aristotle in Book XII of the *Metaphysics*. The systematic connection between *Peri psyches* and *Metaphysics* XII is so stringent that the outlook on theology must already determine the interpretation of the books *On the Soul*. For Hegel, the theology of Aristotle corresponds to the doctrine of absolute spirit. Both philosophers thus share the feature that the question of the essence of truth is identical with the question of the essence of God. The concept "soul" or "consciousness" designates the horizon within which the essence of truth shows itself as the essence of God and the essence of God as the essence of truth. This shape of thinking is called "metaphysics." Metaphysics is experiencing its great historical crisis today. It is no longer possible for us, as for earlier generations, to take over its presuppositions as though they were self-evident. But what are these presuppositions? As long as we cannot recognize and identify them, it is also not possible for us to emancipate ourselves from them. A study of the books *On the Soul* is an investigation of the presuppositions of metaphysics, intended to put us in a position to understand what it is that is shaken by the crisis of metaphysics today.

Picht then identifies the axis around which the thought of both Hegel and Aristotle revolves: the connection between truth, soul, and God. The highest concept of Hegel's philosophy is the concept of the Idea. "Idea" does not mean for Hegel a merely subjective representation; Idea is rather the unity of the concept and objectivity. The agreement of concept and objectivity is called truth. Through the name "Idea," Hegel designates the true as such, the essence of truth. We use the word "truth" in a double sense: on one hand we call our representations and thoughts "true" when they agree with objects; on the other hand we also speak of the truth of the objects themselves, the objects as they are "in themselves," and distinguish this their truth from mere appearance. In Hegel, these two meanings of the word "truth" are brought together and unified. Therefore Hegel can say in the *Logic* that "everything actual is only insofar as it has the Idea in itself and expresses it." What Hegel here calls "Idea," Aristotle as a student of Plato designates with the name *eidos*. The *eidos* is for Aristotle not something supersensible outside the actual; rather, it is contained in the actual itself as the goal toward which it strives. "Goal" in Greek is *telos*, "contained" is *echein*. Therefore Aristotle designated the way in which the actual contains the Idea in itself as *en-tel-echeia*. Hegel's sentence that "everything actual is only insofar as it has the Idea in itself and expresses it" is a paraphrase of the Aristotelian concept of *entelecheia*. The form in which Hegel grasps the essence of truth — the Idea — is taken over from the philosophy of Aristotle.

#### 1. The Concept of Actuality

##### a. Poiesis and Praxis

Picht explains that the absolute Idea is for Hegel the unity of the objective world and its inner ground. This unity is the truth of the actual: actuality (*Wirklichkeit*). We do not usually think much about the word "Wirklichkeit," and few know that German mysticism in the fourteenth century coined this German word to translate the Aristotelian concept *energeia*. But Hegel consciously and with strict consistency used the concept "Wirklichkeit" in this original sense. For him, "Wirklichkeit" means *energeia*. We must therefore try to gain a preliminary concept of what the word *energeia* means in Aristotle, which is of great importance for understanding his doctrine of the soul.

Picht proceeds from a passage in Book IX of the *Metaphysics*: "The work is the goal; the *energeia* is the work (which is why the word *energeia* is formed with reference to the work), and it extends toward the *entelecheia*" (1050a21ff.). He acknowledges this is as difficult on first reading as a sentence of Hegel, but it expresses a simple thought that can be made clear step by step.

"The work is the goal" — in an indeterminate way everyone understands that the work is always the goal of the action that accomplishes it. But Aristotle has grasped this thought in a very specific way: he distinguishes two basic forms in which an action can relate to its work and thereby to its goal. There are actions in which a work results that, when completed, stands as something independent and other alongside the action — as when a carpenter makes a table, the work is accomplished the moment the table stands as a finished whole and no longer requires the carpenter's activity in order to be a table. The *energeia* — the being-at-work — lies in what has been produced. But there is another basic form of actions in which no other and independent work comes out alongside the action itself. As an example Aristotle names seeing. Seeing is the work of the eye, but it does not stand independently alongside the eye's activity; it is contained within that activity. A second example is intellectual contemplation, *theoria*. For the Greeks, theory was not the abstract result of research, not what stands in books; theory was the action of knowing as such, whose actuality — whose being-at-work — lies in the knowing itself. A third example, which will become very important, is life. Life is likewise not a product that could be detached from the carrying-out of living. In life too the work is contained in the carrying-out itself. What is the organ of life, as the organ of seeing was the eye? Aristotle names it *psyche*. One can already see that it is wrong to translate *psyche* as "soul." The books *De anima* treat of the organ of life, and thereby of life as such. That is why, in Hegel too, the concept of actuality is always connected with the concept of life.

Those actions in which the work stands as something other and independent outside the action Aristotle designates with the generic term *poiesis* (production). Those actions in which the work is contained in the action itself he designates with the generic term *praxis*. Even *theoria* is for Aristotle a form of *praxis* — indeed the highest form of *praxis*. Both theory and praxis are forms of life. The sentence "the work is the goal" applies to both forms. Aristotle shows that in all organic life, what we can call the actual can be traced back to these two basic forms: *poiesis* and *praxis* are the two basic forms of actuality in the domain of organic life in general.

##### b. Energeia and Entelecheia

Picht explains that *energeia* extends toward *entelecheia*. Both words are coinages of Aristotle, technical terms standing in a strict connection. Contrary to the etymology given in Frisk's dictionary, which derives *entelecheia* from *enteles* and *echein*, Picht shows that just as *energeia* is formed from *ergon* (work), so *entelecheia* must be formed from *telos* (goal). *En-tel-echeia* is a state that contains the goal within itself, just as *en-erg-eia*, grasped as *praxis*, is a state that contains the work within itself. The work is the goal, but in those activities that contain the work within themselves, the goal can be achieved better or worse. One can see dimly or clearly. The difference is even more obvious in the case of life, for Aristotle says that the state in which life contains its goal within itself is *eudaimonia*, "happiness" — showing how the concept of happiness was later misinterpreted and uprooted in later philosophy, including Kant.

The concept of *entelecheia* has an ontological significance for Aristotle. Every living being reaches its goal in the state in which its intended form purely emerges through the carrying-out of life. Only then does it arrive, as we still say, at its actuality. Only then does it show itself as it truly is. This pure essential form Aristotle calls, like Plato, *eidos*. But Aristotle always criticized Plato for not making clear in what form the sensible participates in the pure *eidos*. The concepts *energeia* and *entelecheia* designate his attempt to solve this problem. The *eidos* is the state of having the goal within itself, toward which the life-process strives because it is *praxis*, that is, *energeia*. Even imperfect praxis always already has the structure of *energeia*: it carries the work it accomplishes in itself. But to every form of praxis belongs also the striving to accomplish this work ever better, to work it out more distinctly. Praxis is therefore, simply by being praxis, directed toward a goal inscribed in its own structure and strives to reach it. This goal is its own *eidos*. Therefore every carrying-out, purely by being a carrying-out, contains within itself its *eidos*. This is the Aristotelian solution to the relationship between the sensible and the Idea.

Picht adds a parenthetical remark: this teaching of Aristotle is contained in all its parts in the Platonic teaching. What is new is the words, not the thought. But the domain that Plato primarily had in view with his doctrine of Ideas drops out of the Aristotelian teaching: mathematics. The concept of *entelecheia* cannot explain what was Plato's fundamental problem — why nature obeys mathematical laws. Kant explained mathematical knowledge not from the concept in which we grasp the *eidos* but from construction in pure intuition. Schelling's concepts of construction and intellectual intuition continue Kant's explanation and raise construction to the basic form of the knowledge of truth as such. That Hegel eliminated intellectual intuition and construction and became an Aristotelian in his doctrine of the concept had, among other consequences, the result that the mathematical structure of physics escaped the dialectic of the concept. The next step then yielded, with Marx, that his materialism remains "historical" and does not reach matter, that is, mathematics.

##### c. Eidos

Picht develops the connection between Hegel and Aristotle further through the concept of *eidos*. A concept (*Begriff*) is, for example, the word "book." When I say "this here is a book," I point through the pronoun to the object before me, while the word "book" designates the totality of characteristics that constitute this object as a book. The totality of characteristics that characterize the book as a book is nothing other than its *eidos*. In formal logic, the concept is part of the statement, a sign. The *eidos*, however, is what is designated by this sign. The logical concept is supposed to agree with the designated object, but with *eidos* the relationship is reversed: the object is supposed to agree with the *eidos*. When a rose unfolds and blooms, it arrives at agreement with the *eidos* "rose," but not at agreement with the logical concept for which we insert the word "rose" as a sign. Translating *eidos* as "concept" and inserting this into Hegel's statement yields: "The Idea is the adequate concept, the objective true, or the true as such. If anything has truth, it has it through its Idea, or something has truth only insofar as it is Idea." Only from the concept of *energeia* can Hegel's cited sentence be explained, that "everything actual is only insofar as it has the Idea in itself and expresses it."

##### d. Noesis noeseos — The Aristotelian Concept of God

*Energeia* is, as we saw, a praxis — an acting that carries its work within itself. This acting is perfect when it reaches its goal and attains the state of *entelecheia*. All praxis is a carrying-out of life. Everything that lives has, according to Aristotle, *psyche*. *Psyche* encounters us in nature at various levels. The highest level of life and thereby the highest level of *energeia* in general is, for Aristotle, *noesis* — that carrying-out in which the highest faculty of knowledge, *nous*, carries its work within itself and thereby fulfills its *nous*-being. The *entelecheia* of *noesis* is reached when it knows what is perfect. If *noesis* itself is the highest form of actuality, then the pure knowing of pure knowing — *noesis noeseos* — is the actuality of the actual simply as such. This is the Aristotelian concept of God.

To understand Hegel from here, we must recapitulate the relationship between *entelecheia* and *eidos*. If *noesis* is the highest level at which what life can be at all comes to unfolding, then *noesis* is the *eidos* of life as such, life in itself, pure unmixed life. This highest level is reached only where *noesis* grasps its own *eidos*, where it becomes *noesis noeseos*. Hegel translates *eidos* as "concept"; thus *noesis noeseos* is the concept's self-grasping of itself. On closer inspection, in the doubly occurring word *noesis*, neither the one nor the other can be designated as the *eidos* of *noesis*. The *eidos* is rather the identity that makes both one and the same. This identity Hegel calls the "absolute Idea." God is the *eidos* of *noesis* — he is the truth of actuality and life. The form in which concept, actuality, life, and Idea are here set as one is not derivable from the prehistory of these concepts in modern philosophy; it is purely Aristotelian.

One must also go back to Aristotle to understand the sentence that characterizes the truth of the absolute Idea: "The found actuality is at the same time determined as the executed absolute purpose." That "actuality" and "executed absolute purpose" are supposed to be identical can only be grasped when one discovers that actuality is thought as *energeia* and the executed absolute purpose as *entelecheia*. In God, according to Aristotle, *energeia* is always *entelecheia*. That is the absolute Idea. What is un-Aristotelian in this sentence is only one thing: that Hegel speaks of the *found* actuality. Here there is a break that points to a hidden deception in the synthesis between Aristotelian ontology and Kant's transcendental logic.

##### e. The Absolute Idea

The absolute Idea is determined by Hegel as "the identity of the theoretical and the practical" Idea. This determination contains Hegel's solution to the problem posed by Kant and not mastered by Fichte: the synthesis of theoretical and practical reason. Kant investigated the theoretical faculty of reason in the *Critique of Pure Reason*, the practical in the *Critique of Practical Reason*. He showed that theoretical and practical reason, causality and freedom, can be thought alongside one another without contradiction, but he did not resolve the antinomies between them; he made them transparent. Only in the *Critique of Judgment* does he pose the question of "the ground of the unity of the supersensible which underlies nature with that which the concept of freedom practically contains."

It initially appears that this determination of the absolute Idea has nothing to do with Aristotle and can be developed from Kant alone. But Hegel's solution becomes possible only by taking up into the absolute Idea determinations that cannot be derived from Kant but are gained from Aristotle: actuality, life, executed absolute purpose, the self-grasping of absolute spirit in its truth. Only because the absolute Idea is all of this, and is it as a unity, does it contain, for Hegel, the solution to the problem of transcendental philosophy. The absolute Idea is actuality, life, executed absolute purpose, self-grasping of absolute spirit in its truth. If these determinations are not to be an external addition, they must all be developed as various dimensions of a single thought.

No new thoughts need be introduced to solve this problem. *Energeia* designates for Aristotle the fundamental constitution of *praxis* as such, insofar as *praxis* is a carrying-out that contains its work within itself. *Theoria* is interpreted by Aristotle as a form of *praxis*, and the highest form of *theoria*, namely *noesis*, is as the *energeia* of God — in Hegel's language: the actuality of absolute spirit. The adoption of Aristotelian ontology and theology thus gives Hegel the possibility of developing his concept of God so that the perfect identity of the highest praxis and the highest theory is achieved in it. Picht expresses his personal conviction that the Aristotelian turn in Hegel was triggered by the re-encounter with the concept of God in Aristotle's theology. But Hegel deceived himself about the fact that the concept of freedom as thought by Kant leads into dimensions that were closed to Greek thought, whose horizon is Christian eschatology.

#### 2. The Concept of the Absolute

##### a. The Absolute as Ground

Picht opens this section by noting that so far only one side of the absolute Idea has been examined: the meaning of the actuality of the absolute Idea as identity of theoretical and practical Idea. Another dimension of the same actuality is indicated by the determination that in the self-knowing truth of the absolute Idea, actuality shows itself "as an objective world whose inner ground and actual existence is the concept." The question now is: what is the connection between the concept of actuality and the concept of "inner ground"? In Greek terms: what does *energeia* have to do with *aitia* or *arche*? How does thinking come to ask for something like a ground when it seeks to grasp actuality?

Picht introduces this question through Heidegger's 1929 Freiburg inaugural lecture "What is Metaphysics?", which closes with the question "Why is there anything at all and not rather nothing?" In the introduction Heidegger added later, he begins from Descartes's image of philosophy as a tree whose roots are metaphysics, and asks: in what soil do these roots find their hold? From what ground do they receive nourishing sustenance? What element pervades, hidden in the ground and soil, the sustaining and nourishing roots of the tree? What is metaphysics from its ground?

Heidegger was not the first to formulate this question. In Leibniz's *Principes de la nature et de la grâce*: "The first question that one has a right to ask will be: why is there rather something than nothing?" And in Schelling's first lecture of the *Philosophy of Revelation*: "Why is there anything at all? Why is there not nothing?" For Leibniz this question follows from the highest principle of his philosophy. He says: "There is reason in nature why something rather exists than nothing." This follows from his *magnum principium*, the principle of sufficient reason: nothing is without reason (*nihil est sine ratione*). Leibniz adds that this ground must be in some real being or cause, and such a being must be necessary — "and it is usually called by one word: GOD." The argument thus runs: the axiom that nothing is without reason compels us to seek the ground of every being; the ground of everything that is cannot itself be nothing; therefore everything has its ground in a real being; this real ground must be necessary; its name is God. This recapitulates a fundamental thought of all European metaphysics.

##### b. Plato: Philosophy as "Seafaring in Search of the Ground"

Picht explains that while this question can be traced back through Leibniz, it is Plato who is the philosopher with whom it was decided that all later philosophy would always be compelled to ask after the ground. Plato wrote a dialogue with the same theme as the books *On the Soul*: the *Phaedo*. There it is said that philosophy is "a seafaring in search of the ground" (99D). The so-called "principle of sufficient reason," supposedly first formulated by Leibniz, is already found in Plato's *Timaeus* as an axiom of his natural philosophy: "Everything that comes into appearance comes necessarily into appearance under the working of some cause" (28A). But what Plato means by "coming into appearance" (*gignesthai*) differs fundamentally from Leibniz's "fiat" (coming to being); at Plato it stands in opposition to being, designating appearance in time as mere appearance.

The seafaring in search of the ground leads, according to the *Republic* VI, "up to the unconditioned, to the origin of the all" (511A). This "origin of the all" bears in Plato the name "the Idea of the Good" — the Platonic name for God. When we ask who the seafarer is who is compelled to such an extraordinary course, Plato gives the answer: *he psyche* — the soul (511A). The domain we have called "soul" or *psyche* in Europe since Plato was first discovered through Plato's question about the origin of the all. Before Plato, the Greeks too did not know what was later called "soul"; and with the crisis of metaphysics, if the signs do not deceive, the soul will disappear again from the world of human beings and their historical experience. *Psyche*, soul, consciousness: these are core concepts of metaphysics, inseparable from it. Therefore the books of Aristotle *On the Soul* are a treatise on the inner possibility of metaphysics.

###### α. What Does Archē Mean?

Picht develops the Platonic concept of the Idea of the Good through its fourfold character as *arche*. In the *Republic* VI, the Idea of the Good is compared with the sun: as the sun radiates light, so from the Idea of the Good there shines down truth and being. The light of the sun grants nature not only visibility but also growth. What visibility is in the sun-comparison, truth is in the noetic domain; what growth and coming-to-be are in the sun-comparison, being is in the noetic domain. Everything that in nature comes to be and grows also becomes visible; everything that in the noetic domain "is" is at the same time true. The word *aletheia* here has the meaning uncovered by Heidegger: "unconcealment." Beyond this, it is said that the Idea of the Good bestows on the knower the faculty of knowledge.

The Idea of the Good is therefore *arche* in three respects: it is origin of being, origin of truth, and origin of knowledge. But there is a fourth dimension: the Good is defined by both Plato and Aristotle as that toward which everything strives. It is a relational concept — wherever good is, there is always at the same time something other, in various degrees of distance. Therefore, the Good necessarily has something set against it beneath it. This is not dualism; the bad and imperfect is posited by the essence of the Good itself. Since the opposed strives toward the Good, the fourth meaning of "origin" emerges: the Idea of the Good is origin of movement. Of everything that can appear in the world at all times, we can say that it is, that it shows itself, that it can be known, and that it is in movement. Therefore the Idea of the Good is origin of the all.

But something further is said: the Good is not being, but lies beyond being and surpasses it in age and in power. The origin surpasses being in age because as origin of being it precedes it; it surpasses it in power because it grants to beings their being itself.

Picht then explains Glaucon's famous outburst of laughter ("By Apollo! What a daemonic overthrow!") by relating the term *hyperbolē* to Sophocles' *Oedipus Tyrannus*, where Oedipus is said to have "shot his arrow in overthrow" — meaning he broke through the measure set for mortals and reached what belongs only to gods. The adjective *daimonios* is explained through Plato's *Symposium*, where the *daimonion* designates the intermediate realm between gods and humans — the realm of philosophy. *Psyche* is not a substance enclosed in the mortal body; it has its dwelling not in the human being but in the intermediate realm between humans and gods. Only because it is a priori "outside" the human being can the human being, in the movement of the soul, know truth.

###### β. What Does To Anypotheton Mean?

The second question — why Plato calls the origin of the all *to anypotheton*, the unconditioned — is developed through an extended interpretation of the *Phaedo*'s "autobiographical excursus" (96Aff.) and the *Sophist* (234Bff.), supplemented by passages from Pindar and Aeschylus.

In the *Phaedo*, Sokrates reports that he turned to the *logoi* — the statements — as a second-best approach after being unable to bear the overwhelming brightness of truth itself, just as one observes a solar eclipse by looking at the sun's reflection in water. This "second seafaring" is the approach through *hypotheseis* — propositions laid down as foundations. The dialectical method alone "takes its way by lifting up the hypotheses" and going to the origin itself.

Picht explains through the Aristotelian passage on Sokrates' discovery of the definition (*horismos*) what kind of statements are meant: they are definitions that answer the question "what is this?" and that delimit what something is by isolating its constitutive features. What is so delimited, Plato and after him Aristotle call, with a substantive derived from *ontōs*, the *ousia*. This concept was later translated by the Latin word *substantia*.

The Platonic concept of the *anypotheton* designates the origin of all fundamental propositions, which therefore can no longer be grasped in the form of a statement but only in intellectual intuition. It is a misinterpretation when *to anypotheton* is later translated as "the unconditioned," for this expresses only the character of the *arche* but not the negation of the *logos*.

###### c. Aristotle

####### α. Archē in Aristotle

Aristotle gives the Platonic Ideas their ontological dwelling in the structure of the categories rather than in mathematics. The categorical structure is held together by the fact that all categories are referred back to the first category, *ousia*. The first category has the formal structure of the *logos tēs ousias* — the statement that displays the *ousia*. This is the Socratic *horismos*. Because Aristotle grounds the "First Philosophy" on the categorical structure, he cannot follow Plato's "daemonic overthrow." In Aristotle's theology, God does not stand beyond *ousia*; he is rather himself the highest *ousia* and is known in the *logos tēs ousias*: the concept. The philosophy of Aristotle thus rests on the decision to make the *deuteros plous* — the second seafaring — of Sokrates from the *Phaedo* into the first and authoritative sailing, into the First Philosophy. Hegel's turn from Schelling to Aristotle repeats this decision. Therefore for Hegel the Absolute is the concept; therefore Hegel's dialectic unfolds as the concept's self-grasping of itself.

Aristotle defines *arche* in the first chapter of *Metaphysics* V: "The common element in all origins is that they are the first thing from which something either is, comes to be, or is known" (1013a17ff.). This matches Plato's dimensions of the *archē anypothetos* almost completely, but the most important determination is missing: for Plato the Idea of the Good is *arche* of truth; in the Aristotelian definition truth does not appear. Being is separated from truth and thereby changes its meaning; it becomes independent as mere *ousia*. The concept of truth simultaneously changes, and its possibility can no longer be inquired after. One sees, as Picht says quoting Pindar, how "Lethe's cloud approaches without landmark from the side."

Aristotle also distinguishes four forms of *aitia* (ground/cause) and declares that every *aitia* is *arche*. In his theology, the essence of God is determined as *arche*: "On this origin the heaven and nature are suspended." God is thus origin in all three senses: origin of being, of becoming, and of knowing. But *noesis* as knowing is the highest possible form of *psyche*. God is *nous*; in Hegel's language: God is spirit. One therefore understands not only the essence of the soul but also the essence of God, of nature, and of truth only when one grasps how in the *psyche* the three meanings of *arche* interlock into a unity.

Picht then shows how *energeia* is *arche* in all three senses, drawing on the eighth chapter of *Metaphysics* IX. *Energeia* has priority in the *logos* (1049b12ff.), priority in time as origin of becoming (1049b17ff.), and priority in *ousia* — in being (1050a3ff.). These three forms of the *arche*-being of *energeia* do not stand externally alongside one another; *energeia* has priority in logos and time only because it has priority in *ousia*. Aristotle then states: "It is evident that *ousia* and *eidos* is *energeia*" (1050b2). In this sense, *energeia* is origin of *ousia*, although it does not lie beyond *ousia*. Everything that has *ousia* at all lies within *physis*. It is misleading to translate this Greek word as "nature," for "nature" means for modern thinking the totality of objects of knowledge, while knowledge itself lies in the opposed sphere of freedom. The Greek concept of *physis* encompasses both the sphere of nature and the sphere of freedom. *Physis* itself has its origin in the *energeia* of God. This origin does not lie beyond *physis*; it is rather its *arche enyparxousa* — the origin contained within it as its ground and pervading it for all eternity. This is the concept of God in metaphysics since Aristotle.

####### β. The Principle of Contradiction as Archē Anypothetos

Picht now develops the extraordinary claim that Aristotle's principle of contradiction occupies the position that the Idea of the Good held in Plato. Aristotle formulates the principle: "The most secure *arche* of all is that concerning which it is impossible to be deceived. Such an *arche* must have the highest degree of knowability and it is *anypothetos* — unconditioned" (*Metaphysics* IV, 1005b11-23). The term *archē anypothetos* is, as we have seen, introduced by Plato to characterize the Idea of the Good. Aristotle uses the same term for the principle of contradiction.

How is this possible? Had Aristotle conceived the principle of contradiction only as an axiom of logic, it would have its proper place not in the "First Philosophy" but in the *Analytica Posteriora*. But Aristotle speaks here not of the *logos* but of the being itself and its composition or separation. The principle speaks of the *on hē on* — being as being. The temporal determination "at the same time" (*hama*), which Kant wanted to eliminate from the principle, shows that it speaks of the relation of a self-identical being to its non-self-identical properties — of the relationship between being (in the sense of being-identical-with-oneself) and time.

Picht provides an extended analysis showing that the principle has two readings. The first, more obvious reading relates it to the relation of a particular being to the temporal change of its properties. The second, deeper reading relates it to the *hyparchonta kath' hauto* — the determinations that belong to being as such as its constitutive grounds. In this second reading, the principle says: "It is impossible that the same thing, in the same respect, at the same time, underlies as ground and does not underlie as ground." It then speaks not of the relation of a determinate thing to its changing properties but of the relation of being as such to its origins. Both readings are different but do not exclude each other; the more obvious interpretation presupposes the deeper one.

The principle is *arche* in all three senses: origin of knowledge, origin of being, origin of becoming. It cannot be questioned beyond, because every statement and every possible knowledge presupposes it. Therefore Aristotle claims for it what Plato said of the Idea of the Good: it is *anypotheton*. We express it as a sentence, but it does not have the character of the *thesis* of an axiom in the usual sense; rather, it formulates the conditions of the possibility of axioms.

Once this is grasped, the difference between the principle of contradiction and the Idea of the Good is not as great as it first appeared. The Platonic concept of the *anypotheton* arose from Plato's necessity of distinguishing between the One and being. The principle of contradiction establishes how identity must be grasped if the Platonic difference between unity and being is not to be admitted. It is therefore in truth the fundamental principle of Aristotelian philosophy.

##### Excursus: The Stoic Origin of the Idea of the Absolute System

Picht inserts an excursus on the Stoic contribution to the formation of the concept of the Absolute. The Ciceronian phrase *absolutus et perfectus* corresponds to the Stoic concept of *autarkeia*. The cosmos alone is autarkic because it contains in itself everything it needs, nourishes itself and grows from itself. Because the cosmos contains everything and nourishes itself from itself, its being depends on no condition outside itself; it is unconditioned and absolute. Hegel's concept of the absolute system as a self-completing totality rests on this Stoic concept. Aristotle, for all his coherence and consistency, has no "system" in this sense. The system-concept of metaphysics is of Stoic origin. But only from Aristotle can one derive the "inner principle" of Hegel's philosophy: the actuality and the life of the Absolute, its substance that is subject by grasping itself. Hegel filled the Stoic blueprint of the total system with Aristotelian life.

### III. Summary

Picht provides an extensive recapitulation of the investigation's course. We are in the First Part of the lecture, which attempts under the guiding question of the relationship of Aristotle and Hegel to give an introduction to metaphysics as such. The methodological preliminary remark clarified why an introduction to Aristotle must take the form of an introduction to metaphysics: because Aristotle, as the comparison with Hegel is to show, founded European metaphysics in the form that has remained authoritative since. Starting from the center — Hegel's absolute Idea — the investigation examined: (1) the actuality of the absolute Idea as *energeia*, including the identity of theoretical and practical Idea; (2) the concept of the Absolute as unconditioned ground, which required investigating the concepts *arche* and *anypotheton* both in Plato and in Aristotle; (3) the fundamental difference between Aristotle and Plato, which consists in Aristotle thinking the origin as *ousia* while Plato thinks it as unity beyond *ousia*. It was shown that when Hegel lets Aristotelian ontology prescribe the inner principle of his philosophy, he takes over this decision with all its implications, and that Hegel's turn away from Schelling stands in precise analogy to Aristotle's turn away from Plato.

### IV. The Dialectic of Plato and the Dialectic of Hegel

#### 1. The Dialectic of Plato

Picht restricts himself to a single passage from the *Republic* (533Bff.) that, as will be shown, decisively influenced Hegel's conception of dialectic. Plato distinguishes, carefully avoiding the word *epistēmē*, two kinds of *technai*. One kind pertains to the sphere of the sensible world; the other comprises the "pure" sciences — arithmetic, geometry, astronomy, harmonics. Of these it is said that they "in some sense touch on being," but this is immediately qualified: "We see that they only dream in the vicinity of being and cannot look upon it in waking, so long as they use 'hypotheses' and leave them unmoved, because they are not in a position to give an account of them."

Picht prepares the interpretation of this passage through an extended discussion of Aristotle's *Analytica Posteriora*. The first sentence — "All teaching and all learning in the domain of *dianoia* proceeds from a prior underlying knowledge" (71a1f.) — is fundamental for all metaphysics. It contradicts the everyday opinion that all knowledge is acquired from outside. Rather, there must be a form of knowledge already contained in the soul that makes learning and teaching possible at all. *Psyche* is in its essence nothing other than the domain of knowledge *a priori*. The doctrine of the foundations of knowledge is therefore identical with the doctrine of the essence of the soul.

Aristotle distinguishes the *archai* that underlie every scientific demonstration: (1) All *archai* share the feature that they cannot themselves be proved; they are *amesoi* — unmediated, immediate. Because they cannot be proved, they must be assumed. An unprovable assumption is a *thesis* — a "positing." (2) The first kind of *theseis* are definitions (*horismoi*). (3) The second kind are *hypotheseis* — assumptions that something is, i.e., assumptions of one side of the contradictory opposition "it is — it is not." (4) Axioms: fundamental assumptions that everyone must have who is to gain any knowledge at all, such as the principle of contradiction.

Returning to Plato, Picht shows that Plato says of the mathematical sciences that they dream in the vicinity of being because they take their *hypotheseis* as something given and are unable to give an account of them. "The dialectical method alone takes its way by lifting up the hypotheses" — going to the origin itself. This refers to the procedure of Zenon's eleatic dialectic, in which it is shown that in the domain of *doxa* contradictory claims can be proved with equal rigor. The sphere of *doxa* comprises everything that is in time, i.e., in motion. If every assertion can be countered by an equally grounded opposite assertion, all assertions cancel each other out, proving that all motion is mere appearance. Plato carried this through in the *Parmenides*, concluding: "If unity is not, nothing at all is" and "whether unity is or is not, it itself and the other, in relation to themselves and to each other, all in every way both are and are not and both appear and do not appear."

#### 2. The Dialectic of Hegel

Picht traces the precise parallels between Plato's and Hegel's dialectic. In both, dialectic is not a method but the way of the soul (or consciousness) itself. Hegel says in the introduction to the *Phenomenology of Spirit*: "the way of the soul, which wanders through the series of its shapes, as stations set out for it by its nature, so that it may purify itself to spirit." As in Plato the journey of the soul requires "a turning around of the whole soul," so in Hegel "through a reversal of consciousness itself the series of experiences of consciousness raises itself to the scientific course." In both, the dialectical ascent constitutes a *paideia* — a formation. Plato calls it *paideia*; Hegel speaks of "the detailed history of the formation of consciousness itself to science."

The correspondences are so striking that it cannot be doubted that Hegel gained the idea of dialectic directly from Plato. Hegel himself confirms this in the last chapter of the *Logic*: "Dialectic is one of those ancient sciences which has been most misunderstood... Plato, as Diogenes Laertius says, was the originator of the third philosophical science pertaining to philosophy: dialectic — a merit reckoned to him by antiquity as the highest."

But Picht then identifies the decisive point of divergence: Hegel determines the goal of dialectic anti-Platonically. He says: "The goal is set for knowledge just as necessarily as the series of progress; it is there where it no longer needs to go beyond itself, where it finds itself, and the concept corresponds to the object, the object to the concept." This is anti-Platonic because in Plato the soul is always compelled to go beyond itself, since the Idea of the Good lies beyond being. In the preface to the *Phenomenology*, Hegel declares his purpose is that philosophy should "shed its name of love of knowledge and be actual knowledge." In Plato, dialectic is the way of the soul because the soul's essence is self-movement, the eternal striving after the Idea of the Good — the *eros* that constitutes the essence of *philo-sophia*. When Hegel opposes "actual knowledge" to the love of knowledge, he replaces, like Aristotle, the Idea of the Good with the *energeia* of pure *nous*. Then knowledge no longer needs to go beyond itself. The turn of negativity into the positivity of affirmation thus denies the previously enacted lifting of *ousia*. Where Aristotle clearly recognized that his concept of God compelled him to give up the dialectical method, Hegel fell into the deception that it was possible to unite Platonic dialectic with the Aristotelian concept of God.

Picht then analyzes the three moments of Hegel's dialectic from the *Encyclopaedia* (§§79-82): (α) the abstract or intellectual (*verständig*) — corresponding exactly to Platonic *dianoia*; (β) the dialectical or negative-rational — corresponding to Plato's method of "lifting the hypotheses" as in the *Parmenides*; (γ) the speculative or positive-rational, which "grasps the unity of the determinations in their opposition, the affirmative that is contained in their dissolution and passing-over." It is with this third moment that the Platonic and Hegelian paths diverge. The *ousia* that is lifted on the path of Platonic dialectic re-establishes itself on the path of Hegelian dialectic. The substantiality of substance is consumed through the negative movement of subjectivity only so that subjectivity can then be identified with the substantiality that has entered into it. All these thoughts are pure Aristotelianism.

#### 3. The Polemic of Aristotle Against the Platonic Dialectic

Picht demonstrates that and why the philosophy of Aristotle is not dialectical, through an analysis of *Eudemian Ethics* II, 6 (1222b15ff.). Aristotle here distinguishes two kinds of *archai*: those that are necessary, like the *archai* of mathematics, and those that can be otherwise — as the human being is *arche* of actions. What follows from necessary *archai* is itself necessary; what follows from changeable *archai* is itself changeable. Everything governed by mathematical laws is strictly determined; everything for which the human being is *arche* is not determined.

This passage contains a direct polemic against Platonic dialectic. Plato had said that deductive sciences "only dream in the vicinity of being and cannot look upon it in waking, so long as they use *hypotheseis* and leave them unmoved." The dialectical method lifts the *hypotheseis*. Against this, Aristotle insists that the *hypotheseis* are unmoved and therefore necessary; and precisely this claim is the presupposition for the distinction between necessity and freedom. Thus Kant's antinomy between theoretical and practical reason arises with compelling consequence from Aristotle's polemic against Platonic dialectic.

God also appears in this passage. God, like the human being, is origin of movement, but God cannot act otherwise than he acts. God's being as *arche* is identified with the same necessity as the *archai* of mathematics. This concept of God is a synthesis of the two forms of *arche*. But the synthesis is not dialectically achieved; it has the form of the in-itself-persisting identity defined by the principle of contradiction. Between this text and Plato there is no mediation. From this concept of God the monotonously self-rotating identity enters into Hegel's concept of the Absolute.

### V. The Concept of the Subject

#### 1. The Hypokeimenon in Aristotle

Picht now reviews the results: actuality, the Absolute (as origin and as the unconditioned), and the soul are all thought by Aristotle as *ousia*. Understanding all these concepts and thereby the Aristotelian concept of God depends on how *ousia* is to be thought. This is the theme of the First Philosophy.

Aristotle says: "In the highest degree, *ousia* seems to be the first *hypokeimenon*" (*Metaphysics* VII, 3, 1029a1). *Ousia* was later translated as "substance"; *hypokeimenon* was translated as *subiectum* in the sense still used in grammar. Aristotle defines the *hypokeimenon*: "The *hypokeimenon* is that of which the other is predicated; it itself is not predicated of another." If all other concepts are predicated of the *ousia*, then the *ousia* underlies them; without it they would have neither hold nor meaning. If the *ousia* itself can no longer be predicated of anything else, then nothing further underlies it; it is the first underlying, and because it is the first, it is *arche*.

This definition presupposes that the figures of the statement — the categories — display the structure of being as it is from itself: the knowledge of being as being is necessarily onto-logy. Categorical doctrine, principle of contradiction, theory of science, and priority of *ousia* are identical with the interpretation of *ousia* as *hypokeimenon*. Whoever understands *ousia* as the first underlying is caught in a circle of necessity that compels him to accept the categorical doctrine and the principle of contradiction, to think the *arche* as *ousia*. From this circle of necessity European metaphysics never freed itself. It is the fundamental law of metaphysics.

However, Aristotle also could not escape the compulsion of the phenomenality of phenomena that led Plato to place the unity of the Good beyond being. The identity of unity and being assumed as *hypothesis* in the concept of the *hypokeimenon* immediately dissolves again. Aristotle shows in the same chapter that the *hypokeimenon* is in itself ambiguous: it is on the one hand *hylē* (matter), on the other *eidos* (form). From this ambiguity the later opposition of materialism and idealism developed. The polarity of *eidos* and *hylē* — of structure and continuum — is what Plato in the *Parmenides* determines as the difference of unity and otherness. The art of thinking this difference is called "dialectic." Aristotle explained the polarity as that of *dynamis* and *energeia*. The concept of *energeia* is thus the Aristotelian solution to the problem that Plato tried to solve dialectically, giving him the possibility of forcing the polarity of *hylē* and *eidos* under the bracket of *ousia*.

#### 2. The Transformation of the Understanding of Being

Picht describes how, when thinking is tasked with seeking the first underlying and finding its halt therein, the meaning of the concepts *arche* and *aitia* imperceptibly transforms. In Plato, the gaze is directed upward toward the origin; the origin is not fundament but light-source from which being and truth shine down. The light-metaphorics that illuminated thinking from Parmenides to Plato are replaced by a ground-and-soil metaphorics. Ontology becomes "fundamental ontology." Being is no longer transparency but solidity, stability, security, massiveness. All this enters imperceptibly into the understanding of *ousia* from the concept of *hypokeimenon*. This understanding solidifies further when, under Roman influence, the *on* is understood not as the truly being but as *res* — a thing over which one can dispose — and the concept becomes a mere word, a mere name, an abstraction. The medieval dispute between realism and nominalism presupposes this reinterpretation of the central concepts of Greek philosophy. Actuality becomes "reality"; the pure *energeia* of the Aristotelian *nous* transforms into the *ens realissimum* of late-medieval and modern philosophy.

#### 3. The Modern Concept of the Subject in Wolff and Kant

Picht traces the Aristotelian *hypokeimenon* through Wolff's *Philosophia Prima sive Ontologia* (§§711-712) to Kant. Wolff defines *subiectum* as "the being insofar as it is considered as having an essence and capable of receiving other determinations." This is still the Aristotelian-scholastic sense. In Kant's *Prolegomena* §46, the same definition is adopted: the "proper subject" is understood as the "first underlying" of which the other is predicated — the "substantial itself." But Kant then draws the revolutionary consequence from Descartes: if the self-certainty of the thinking I is the foundation of all possible certainty, then nothing outside thinking can be considered as independently underlying. What we call "the being" no longer has its halt in itself; it becomes certain for us only by our assuring ourselves that we must represent it thus and not otherwise according to the laws of our own thinking. Being becomes an object (*obiectum*) — something set over against our thinking and acting.

If being has become an object, it no longer has halt in itself and can no longer be considered as *hypokeimenon*. Kant therefore concludes: "From this it follows that we should regard nothing we can reach as a last subject, and that the substantial itself can never be thought by our understanding even if the whole of nature were uncovered to it." At this point Kant introduces the modern concept of the subject: "It seems as though we have in the consciousness of ourselves (the thinking subject) this substantial... thus the absolute subject itself seems to be given in experience." The Aristotelian question after the first underlying is thus driven by Kant with strict consequence until the I emerges as the subject of everything that can be thought at all. The modern concept of the subject does not therefore represent a departure from Greek ontology; it becomes possible only as the most extreme consequence of that ontology. The I grasps itself as *hypokeimenon* in the Greek sense. Precisely thereby it appears to itself, in Kant's words, as "the absolute subject."

But Kant then shows this is a transcendental illusion. "The I is no concept at all"; in Greek, there is no *eidos* of the I; it is not possible to determine the I as *ousia*, as substance. By blasting apart the identity of subject and substance, Kant opens the possibility of recognizing what constitutes the Aristotelian concept of *ousia*. In the Schematism, Kant shows that what is unwavering and abiding in time is its pure form, its structure — the unity of time. This is represented by substance. But substance is also "the substrate of all that is real, i.e., belonging to the existence of things." Thus the Aristotelian coupling of unity and being in the concept of *ousia* breaks apart in Kant's transcendental dialectic. This holds both for Kant's destruction of the soul-doctrine of traditional metaphysics and for his refutation of the ontological proof of God.

Picht concludes Part I by showing that Hegel's fundamental position — "It is essential to grasp and express the true not as substance but equally as subject" (*Phenomenology*, Preface) — aims at restoring the Kantian disruption. The circle closes in the last chapter of the *Phenomenology*: "This subject is equally the substance." The whole philosophy of Hegel is a single colossal attempt to re-establish, in the absolute knowledge of absolute spirit, the identity of subject and substance that Kant had blown apart. Here the *hypokeimenon* is once again, as in the ontology of Aristotle, thought as *ousia*. The identity of subject and substance is for Hegel as for Aristotle the quintessence of the entire philosophy. Having investigated the concept of actuality (II.1), the concept of the Absolute (II.2), and the concept of the subject (V), and having found in all three that this triad of concepts and their peculiar coupling was founded by Aristotle, the introduction to metaphysics reaches its conclusion. As philosophy of absolute spirit, Hegel's system has the same thematic as the books *On the Soul*. This confirms the sentences from the *Encyclopaedia* §378 with which the lecture began: "The books of Aristotle on the soul... are still the most excellent or only work of speculative interest on this subject. The essential purpose of a philosophy of spirit can only be to reintroduce the concept into the knowledge of spirit, thereby also to unlock again the sense of those Aristotelian books."

## Second Part: Interpretation of *De Anima* I and the Structure of the Work

### I. The Position of the Doctrine of the Soul Within the Aristotelian Design of Philosophy (402a 1-7)

#### 1. On Method

Picht opens Part II by recalling the thesis that will guide the entire interpretation: the doctrine of the soul, which cannot be separated from Aristotle's theology, contains the center of his metaphysics. This is not self-evident; contemporary research, he notes, places the center of Aristotle's philosophy variously in the ontology of *Metaphysics* VII-IX, the *Physics*, the logical and scientific-theoretical works, or the ethics. The question of the inner unity of Aristotle's philosophy does not receive the attention it deserves because twentieth-century philosophical thought does not recognize such a unity and does not believe it is possible. Consequently, the theology of Aristotle is treated as an embarrassment — a mythological remnant, a foreign body, an external addition that has nothing to do with the matter of thinking itself. But if one holds the doctrine of *nous* as the unmoved mover, in accordance with Aristotle's own explicit statements, as the keystone and summit of his entire philosophy, then the doctrine of the soul necessarily moves to the center, because *nous* is the highest faculty of the soul.

Picht then addresses his method. He cannot simply assert claims about the systematic position of the soul-doctrine; he must examine what Aristotle himself says about it. Aristotle determines this position in the first two sentences of Book I, and their adequate understanding determines the interpretation not only of the three books but of his philosophy as a whole. Picht defends the intensive, slow method of interpretation — working through a few sentences or even individual terms in great detail — over against survey-style coverage, appealing to the example of the ancient Greek commentators whose works on *De anima* run to hundreds of pages.

#### 2. The Carrying-Out of Knowing and Questioning in the Horizon of Soul

Picht provides his translation of the first sentence (402a 1-7): "If we proceed from the basic assumption that knowledge belongs to what is beautiful and worthy of honor, the one [form of knowledge] being of higher rank than the other either with respect to its exactness or because it is directed toward what is better and more wondrous, then we must rightly place the investigation concerning the soul among the first of all. Its knowledge seems to contribute greatly both with respect to truth as a whole and especially with respect to nature; for it is, in a sense still to be more precisely determined, the origin of living beings."

Picht begins with the word *hypolambánein* — "to take as underlying" — noting its kinship with *hypothesis*. Unlike a *hypothesis*, which is necessarily true, a *hypolēpsis* is a basic assumption that we presuppose to be true but that may turn out to be false, whose truth must therefore be demonstrated. Aristotle's method is to begin from such commonly held opinions, because this is the real path of knowledge: we always begin from what we already think.

The assumption that knowledge belongs to what is beautiful and honorable is grounded, Picht shows, in the first sentence of the *Metaphysics*: "All human beings by nature strive after knowledge." Our natural endowment itself compels us to assign such high rank to knowledge. The capacity for knowing is called *psyche*. The basic assumption from which Aristotle proceeds is therefore not a randomly adopted opinion from tradition; it is necessitated by the fact that we are living beings with a soul, and that it belongs to the essence of the soul to strive after knowledge. This applies, according to Aristotle, not only to human beings: in the final chapter of the *Analytica Posteriora*, he says this capacity is an underlying origin (*hyparchon*) shared by all living beings, manifesting itself in the discriminating power of perception inherent in them by nature. Everything that lives at all is ensouled, and everything ensouled strives, if only in the form of perception, after knowledge. This is the fundamental determination of *psyche* as such.

The sentence is therefore misunderstood if one translates *eidēsis* with Theiler as "science" (*Wissenschaft*). The word *eidēsis* occurs only at this one place in the entire work of Aristotle and is almost unattested before him. Its formation (parallel to *praxis* and *noēsis*) designates not the content but the carrying-out of knowledge — knowledge as *energeia*. Aristotle does not say that science in general is highly valued because of its content; he says something much less obvious: it is a basic assumption of human beings, indeed of living beings in general, that knowing and recognizing as a carrying-out, as a "being-at-work" of the soul, belongs to the domain of what is beautiful and what is highly valued. The sentence speaks not about science but about a constitutive mode of being of the soul.

Almost equally unusual is Aristotle's avoidance of the word *epistēmē* in favor of *historia* for the investigation to be undertaken. *Historia* is a questioning exploration — Herodotus gave his work this title because it contains the results of his exploratory journeys. Aristotle uses *historia* to designate his botanical, zoological, and other empirical-scientific writings, not for metaphysics, analytics, ethics, or the investigation of the basic concepts of physics. Yet the features for which Aristotle here singles out the investigation of the soul — exactness and high rank in the hierarchy of knowledge — are precisely what empirical research, in his scientific theory, does not possess. The puzzle resolves itself when one sees the *Sachproblem*: Aristotle must, if he investigates the essence of the soul, make knowledge as such — its possibility and the various forms of its carrying-out — into his theme. All other sciences presuppose that knowledge is possible. But how should one designate an investigation that has the soul itself as its object? None of the ordinary words for human knowledge can be used, since they all presuppose what is here to be investigated. The only usable word is one that expresses nothing other than investigating and questioning as such. That, in Greek usage, is the primary meaning of *historia*.

From these two unusual terms, Picht concludes that the first sentence speaks not of some particular science but of two primordial modes of carrying-out in which the essence of *psyche* manifests itself: the carrying-out of knowing and the carrying-out of questioning. Correspondingly, the words *peri tēs psychēs* mean not "about the soul" but, as the precise sense of the preposition *peri* requires, "in the environs of soul" or "in the horizon of soul." The first line thus speaks not, as it is usually interpreted, of the science that takes the soul as its object; it speaks of the soul itself — of that *eidēsis*, that carrying-out in which the soul's own and primordial essence manifests itself.

The theory of the levels of knowledge — ascending from perception (*aisthēsis*) through memory (*mnēmē*), experience (*empeiria*), productive knowledge (*technē*) to the various stages of the highest knowledge — underlies the structure of the books *On the Soul*: after a critical discussion of earlier theories in Book I, Books II and III treat the Aristotelian doctrine of the soul in the sequence: perceptive faculty, imaginative faculty, thinking faculty. These three levels of knowledge are discussed by Aristotle in a strict connection with the second fundamental determination of the soul, its determination as the origin of movement. The work thus investigates the essence of the soul with respect to the relationship of knowledge and movement. The word *eidēsis* designates knowledge as movement, forming the heading of the entire work.

Knowledge thus appears in the opening lines in a threefold form: (1) *eidēsis* — the carrying-out of knowledge at all its levels; (2) *hypolambanein* — the basic assumption, prescribed for us by our nature, about the preeminence of knowledge over other vital impulses; (3) *historia peri tēs psychēs* — the exploration that is to make all these structures transparent.

#### 4. Akribeia

Picht has deliberately skipped over the opening words to which everything else in the sentence is attached: the carrying-out of knowledge belongs to what is *kalon* (beautiful) and *timion* (worthy of honor). According to the standard set by these concepts, the higher or lower rank of the levels of knowledge is measured. Two grounds for this high esteem are given: "either with respect to its exactness [*akribeia*], or because it is directed toward what is better and more wondrous."

Picht devotes an extended analysis to the concept of *akribeia*. In modern usage, "exact" means either the precision of analytical knowledge or the accuracy with which something is carried out — in both cases, an agreement. But for the Greeks, in the primary sense, it is not the cognitions but the phenomena that are exact. What is exact is what is pure, unadulterated, whole; what entirely corresponds to what it ought to be; what stands in perfect accord with itself. In the highest sense, what is exact is what is perfectly simple and unchanging. Thus Plato can speak of *auto to akribes* — "the exact itself" — as the highest object of knowledge. Krämer has therefore recommended translating *akribeia* as "absoluteness." Cognition is exact only insofar as it purely recognizes what is of itself exact. *Akribeia* is primarily a property of the phenomena, and only through the phenomena a property of the knowledge that grasps them.

This matters greatly for Aristotle's theory of science, because he divides the sciences according to whether their phenomena are of themselves "exact" or not. The phenomena of mathematics and of First Philosophy — especially the *archai* — are exact. The phenomena of the empirical sciences, of politics and ethics, are by nature manifold and changeable, hence not "exact." It is, according to Aristotle, a deficiency of scientific education to demand in those domains an exactness that does not befit them. The modern assumption that every science must strive for maximal exactness, refining its methods of objectification, is here reversed: for Aristotle, an exact cognition gained through objectification is *inexact* if it is not adequate to the phenomena as they are from themselves.

The concept *akribeia* is ultimately rooted in Plato's concept of the Idea of the Good: *auto to akribes* designates the Idea of the Good in the *Politikos* (284D). Plato determines philosophy as the search for the "clearest and most exact and most unconcealed" — i.e., the truest. The Good is "the exact" because it is unity, standing purely and immovably in unshakeable accord with itself. Plato further connects unity with the concept of measure (*metron*): everything that is gains its determinacy and permanence only by being a unity, and unity is therefore the measure by which a phenomenon is held fast, bounded, and secured in its so-being.

For the first line of *De anima*, this means: when Aristotle speaks of ranking the forms of knowledge by their *akribeia*, he refers to a Platonic-Academic ontology in which the "exact" designates the primary, simple, self-transparent phenomena — the *archai* and *aitiai*. Aristotle shows in his scientific theory that every rigorous science must axiomatically presuppose such primary phenomena, and the knowledge of these phenomena cannot itself be proved. How do we attain the knowledge that underlies all science? The answer is given by the Aristotelian doctrine of the highest faculty of the soul, *nous*. The investigation of the soul thus strives after a grounding of the theory of science. It seeks knowledge of "the exact" as such. Therefore it has, with respect to exactness, an unsurpassable priority, even if it should fail to attain that exactness.

#### 5. The Unity of Aristotelian Philosophy as the Theme of *Peri Psychēs*

The second standard by which the rank-ordering of the levels of knowledge is measured is designated by the words "because it is directed toward what is better and more wondrous." Picht develops this through the Aristotelian teaching, drawn from the introduction to the *Nicomachean Ethics*, that all activities, arts, and sciences have their proper goals, and that these goals form a hierarchy. The overarching science is politics, but politics itself must derive its criteria from the knowledge of what is truly good for human beings. The highest possibility of the human being is the capacity to know what is true; the faculty through which this occurs is *nous*. Ethics and politics thus culminate, like metaphysics, physics, and scientific theory, in the doctrine of *nous*.

Picht then cites *Analytica Posteriora* I, 33, where Aristotle assigns the various faculties of the soul — *dianoia*, *nous*, *epistēmē*, *technē*, *phronēsis*, *sophia* — partly to the domain of physics, partly to the domain of ethics. The words *kat' akribian* thus refer to the physical-metaphysical hierarchy of knowledge, while the words *to beltionōn... einai* refer to the ethical-political hierarchy. Both hierarchies meet at their summit, for "the best in the whole of nature" is God. In the knowledge of God, metaphysics and ethics both reach their peak. The investigation of the soul must bring both parts together and demonstrate their inner connection; therefore the theme of this work is the unity of Aristotelian philosophy.

#### 6. The Grounding of Aristotle's Theology in *Peri Psychēs*

Picht develops the concept of *timion* — "worthy of honor" or "priceworthy" — through *Nicomachean Ethics* I, 12, where Aristotle distinguishes the *timion* from the *epaineton* (the praiseworthy). We praise someone for a quality that shows itself in achievements measured against others. But we would make ourselves ridiculous if we "praised" the gods in the same way, for that would set them in relation to us. The gods can only be called blessed; the honor (*timē*) is owed to what is best. Likewise *eudaimonia* — not "happiness" in the vulgar sense but a god-like condition. The *timion* is identified by Aristotle as divine: "What is origin and ground of goods, that we define as *timion* and divine."

In *Nicomachean Ethics* VI, 7, the highest form of human knowledge (*sophia*) is described as "the most exact of the sciences" whose possessor must "be in the truth concerning the origins themselves" — and as *nous* and *epistēmē* combined, "the knowledge, brought to its summit, of what is most priceworthy [*timiotaton*]." What is *timiotaton* is God — through *Metaphysics* VI, 1, Aristotle identifies the science that studies what is eternal, unmoved, and separable as "theology," and declares it the most priceworthy science concerned with the most priceworthy kind.

For the first sentence of *Peri psychēs*, this means: the carrying-out of knowledge is called *timion* because the highest possibility of knowledge is to know the *timiotaton*, namely God. In this highest form of knowledge, both hierarchies — by exactness and by the hierarchy of ends — converge. The investigation of the soul must be reckoned among the first sciences because it contains both the highest possibility of knowledge and the highest object of knowledge: the *noēsis noeseōs* of God. This establishes the basic hypothesis of the lecture: the books *On the Soul* must be understood as the systematic grounding of Aristotle's theology and thereby of the First Philosophy.

The concept *timion* is connected by both Plato and Aristotle with *kalon* — the beautiful. In Plato, *kalon* designates the pure appearance of what is divine. The Diotima-speech in the *Symposium* reaches its peak at the Beautiful itself; the Beautiful is the appearance of the Idea of the Good. *Kalon* and *timion* thus form a unity representing the highest possibility of *eidēsis*.

Aristotle also follows Plato in holding that wonder (*thaumazein*) is the beginning of philosophy, referring to the famous passage in the *Theaetetus* (155D). Picht interprets Plato's genealogical connection of Iris (the messenger of the gods) with Thaumas (the Wondrous): through the message that wonder conveys, the tidings of what is divine reach us. Wonder springs from the perturbation caused by the encounter with the divine — the consternation before the incomprehensible, the fear before the overwhelming, the admiration before the beautiful.

Picht concludes this section by quoting the passage from *Metaphysics* XII where Aristotle says the ancients handed down in mythical form the teaching that the stars are gods and that the divine encompasses all of nature, and that if one strips away the mythical accretions and retains only the first teaching — that the first substances are gods — one must hold this to have been divinely spoken. The theology of Aristotle appears in this light as an attempt to restore that primordial wisdom. It is in this horizon that he investigates the essence of the soul.

#### 7. The Significance of the Soul-Doctrine for the Totality of the Objects of Knowledge

Picht turns to the second sentence: "Its knowledge seems to contribute greatly both with respect to truth as a whole and especially with respect to nature; for it is, in a sense still to be more precisely determined, the origin of living beings." The phrase "with respect to truth as a whole" explicitly states that the knowledge of the soul is relevant to the entire domain we call "truth" — to all parts of philosophy. Aristotle avoids premature precision, and Picht emphasizes that this caution is characteristic of Aristotle's style of thought: the more carefully one hones one's statements, the more of what determines their formulation remains unspoken. This is the price science pays for striving to make its statements as unassailable as possible.

##### a. Ethics and Politics (E.N. I, 13)

Picht shows that in the last chapter of Book I of the *Nicomachean Ethics*, Aristotle demonstrates that the politician guided by truth must devote his greatest efforts to ethics. The politician's task is to ensure that the citizens can develop their highest capacities — and the highest capacity is the capacity to know what is true. The science of the soul is therefore the common foundational science for both ethics and politics. The whole structure of Aristotle's ethics — the distinction between ethical and dianoetic virtues — is derived from the inner structure of the human soul.

##### b. Time and Physics

The second sentence says the knowledge of the soul is of special significance for physics because the soul is the origin of living beings. But living beings form only one class of beings within *physis*. In the first part of the sentence, *physis* as a whole must be in view if the knowledge of the soul is truly to contribute to "truth as a whole." Picht introduces this through Aristotle's time-treatise in *Physics* IV, where Aristotle asks: "If soul were not, would there be time or not?" Since time is the number (measure) of motion, and numbering requires a soul capable of counting, time without soul and motion without soul cannot be thought. But "soul" here cannot mean only the human soul, since the earth, sea, and stars move in time even without human beings to count. From the coupling of the question about the relation of time and soul with the question about the universality of time, there follows the doctrine of the divine *nous* as the First Mover, developed in *Physics* VIII.

The doctrine of the soul thus contains and grounds the doctrine of the essence of truth itself. Only because the doctrine of the essence of truth is placed at the center of the doctrine of the soul can the doctrine of the soul be constitutively significant for all parts of philosophy. Metaphysics is that form of philosophy which interprets the essence of truth in the horizon of the soul. Only if the soul is the horizon of truth can knowledge be grasped as the authoritative form in which truth shows itself to us.

The second part of the sentence — soul as origin of living beings — refers to Aristotle's enormous corpus of writings on organic nature. Picht outlines the structure of Aristotle's physics as sketched in *Meteorologica* I, 1: the foundational science of the first causes of *physis* and of motion in *physis* as a whole (the eight books of the *Physics*), followed by astronomy and the doctrine of the elements (*De Caelo*), then the doctrine of coming-to-be and passing-away, then meteorology, and finally the enormous corpus on animals and plants. The statement that soul is the origin of living beings means that the doctrine of the soul stands to the sciences of organic nature as the eight books of the *Physics* stand to the sciences of nature as a whole: it contains the science of the principles of organic nature.

The second sentence thus contains, in reverse order, the outline of the work. In its final part (III, 4ff.), what the doctrine of *nous* contributes to the knowledge of truth as a whole is shown. In II, 1-III, 3, the sense in which soul is the origin of living beings is demonstrated. Behind this outline lies a vast systematic problem: only if Aristotle succeeds in showing how soul as a biological phenomenon is connected with soul as the faculty of knowledge of truth does his overall design of physics have inner consistency. The decisive clue is given in the first sentence: the problem becomes soluble only through theology — through the doctrine of the divine *nous*.

### II. The Method of Investigating the Soul and Its Problems (402a 7-10)

#### 1. "It is difficult to gain even a reliable opinion about it"

Picht highlights the sentence he regards as the guiding maxim of the entire first chapter: "In every way and on every account, it belongs to the most difficult things to gain anything like a reliable opinion about it [the soul]." The formula *pantē pantōs* designates totality emphatically. On absolutely every path of investigation and with absolutely every mode of consideration, it turns out to be among the most difficult things to gain — not scientific knowledge, not even a reliable opinion — about the soul. This is an astonishing, almost incomprehensible statement, given that the first sentence had claimed the highest rank for this investigation.

Picht explains the concept of *pistis* through *De anima* III, 3, where Aristotle distinguishes four faculties by which we judge: perception, opinion (*doxa*), knowledge (*epistēmē*), and intellectual intuition (*nous*). *Doxa* is the lowest among specifically human cognitive faculties. With opinion is linked trust (*pistis*) — the certainty that an opinion is true. Only human beings have *pistis*, because only human beings are so constituted by nature that they must ask after the truth of their representations. *Pistis* is further linked by Aristotle to having been *persuaded* (*pepeisthai*), and persuasion to *argumentation* (*logos*). The peculiarity of this trust is that we cannot read off from it whether the opinion in which we place trust is true or false. Therefore Aristotle does not link *pistis* with the forms of thought defined by their truth (*epistēmē* and *nous*).

That Aristotle says it is among the most difficult things even to gain a *pistis* about the soul shows how problematic the science he is building appears to its own founder. Knowledge is a faculty of the soul; if it remains uncertain what "soul" means, it also remains uncertain what "knowledge" and "science" mean. This uncertainty is not a sign of weakness but of the strength of the Aristotelian concept of science: the demands placed on knowledge here far exceed those of all his epigones. The best Aristotle-criticism is always found in Aristotle himself.

#### 2. The Question of the "Essence" of the Soul and the Form of Knowledge About It

Picht translates 402a 7-10: "We seek to look upon and to know its nature and its *ousia*, then everything that presents itself in its environs, of which one part seems to be peculiar experiences of the soul, while another part seems, through its mediation, to underlie the living beings as well as origin."

The form of knowledge sought — *theōrēsai kai gnōnai* — is crucial. *Theōrein* designates the act of grasping something through intellectual vision; it is used by Aristotle not only for the highest form of intuition in which pure *nous* beholds the pure and simple origins of what is, but also for the watchful looking-on with which the contemplative mind accompanies every step of investigation. Aristotle's entire philosophy — empirical research and investigation of principles, physics and metaphysics, ethics and politics — is understood by him as a carrying-out of intellectual intuition (*theōria*). This distinguishes him from modern thought which, under the dominance of logic, understands the acts of thinking as logical functions, as discursive thought. In this fundamental difference lies an unbridgeable gap between Hegel and Aristotle.

*Physis* at this point does not mean nature as a whole but has a special meaning explained in *Metaphysics* V, 4 (1014b35ff.): *physis* is "the *ousia* of what is by nature," the joining of first matter and form and essence. *Psyche* is therefore in an eminent sense *physis*, because soul is the reason why living beings are *physei onta*. In this sense, *psyche* is the *physis* of *physis* — the condition of the possibility of *physis*. The concept of *ousia* designates the form (*eidos*) not in isolation but as the goal (*telos*) of becoming, as *entelecheia*. The investigation thus seeks the *physis* and *ousia* of soul as such — the totality of the moments by which a living being constitutes itself as a living being.

#### 3. The Question of Physis and Ousia of Soul in General

Picht raises the problem that Aristotle speaks here not of the *physis* and *ousia* of a particular living being but of *soul in general*. Soul in general is not bound to a particular matter; it seems to be an abstraction, a mere universal concept. But a mere universal concept has neither *physis* nor *ousia*. Is the singular in the title *Peri psychēs* a mere fiction? This question's reach will become ever clearer as the investigation progresses.

#### 4. What Constitutes the Region of a Science?

Picht develops the concept of *genos* — the "region" that a science investigates — through the example of medicine. The question of which domains of objects belong to the region of medicine was extensively discussed in Greek medicine: the Greeks concluded that the treatment of disease is only one half of medicine; equally important is the preservation of health through proper conduct of life (*diaita*). Furthermore, the Greeks systematically thought through that the human organism is an open system within nature, and that health depends on its relationship with surrounding nature; and even social conditions were recognized as constitutive elements of health. From the circumscription of the region, the entire inner structure of a science depends.

The *ousia* of a region is what must somehow already be known if one is to conceive the idea of developing a science investigating that region. The answer to the question "what is it?" is the *logos tēs ousias* — the definition that circumscribes the *ousia*. "Substance" is the region of a science, and "regionality" is a constitutive feature of *ousia*. Using the modern analogy of "system" to illuminate the concept of *ousia*, Picht suggests that if one understands by "soul" the moving and organizing principle of a determinate class of systems — namely living beings — one can meaningfully ask in general about the structure (*eidos*) of this type of system and about its *physis*.

### III. Retrospect

#### 1. Soul: Signum of the Likeness to God

Opening the summer semester, Picht recalls the fundamental result: "soul" is a concept of metaphysics. Outside the sphere of influence of Plato and Aristotle, the familiar European concept of the soul is unintelligible. The philosophical doctrine that God is pure *nous* was developed in explicit contradiction to the anthropomorphic divine figures of Greek myth: the human being is not to be understood anthropomorphically but theomorphically. Insofar as the human being has soul, he is an image of God. The goal of existence is "assimilation to God according to the measure of possibility" (*Theaetetus* 176B). Philosophy is a path of thinking that leads to assimilation to God, possible only because both God and the subject of thinking are soul. "Metaphysics" designates the form of thinking that comprehends everything from the soul and refers it to God. Under the influence of this philosophical doctrine, European culture has interpreted human existence as essentially soul, and thereby as related to God.

#### 2. The Doctrine of the Soul Within the Design of Aristotelian Philosophy

Picht recapitulates the two grounds for the preeminence of the soul-investigation. First: it possesses priority with respect to exactness — *akribeia* designates the primary, simple, self-transparent phenomena whose knowledge underlies all science, and the investigation of the soul strives for knowledge of these phenomena through the doctrine of *nous*. Second: it is directed toward the best and most wondrous — the *timiotaton*, namely God. At the summit, both hierarchies converge. The three books *On the Soul* are methodically constructed as an ascent that reaches its peak in the doctrine of *nous* and carries this doctrine to the point where it passes over into theology.

Picht adds a critical note: when we study Aristotle today, we are not interested in the content of individual doctrines as such. We are compelled by our historical situation to critically examine the structure of the edifice of metaphysics at whose center the doctrine of the soul stands. If the foundation and framework of the edifice are not sound, its contents too dissolve. Whether we can continue to speak of "soul" and "God" as the last two millennia have done depends on the construction of the edifice.

### IV. On the Structure of *Peri Psychēs*

#### 1. Book I: Method

##### a. Engagement with Other Philosophical Designs

Picht explains why Aristotle, like a physicist rather than a historian, treats the doctrines of his predecessors not as historically unique systems to be faithfully reconstructed but as more or less successful attempts to recognize the one unchanging system that exists — the system of *physis* itself. For Aristotle, there is only one "system": the system of *physis*. The philosophies of individual thinkers are attempts to recognize it. It is therefore philosophically irrelevant to interpret earlier writings with great hermeneutical effort; what matters is to learn what they correctly glimpsed and where they went astray. This attitude was shared by Kant, who aimed to put metaphysics "on the secure course of science" independent of historical accidents and individual philosophers, and by Hegel, whose history of philosophy likewise sought the eternal truth of absolute spirit manifesting in various shapes of human consciousness.

##### b. Critique of Prevailing Prejudices as the First Step of True Knowledge

Picht expounds the Aristotelian concept of dialectic — not the Platonic dialectic (the ascent to the Idea of the Good) but the art of orienting oneself in the field of opinions (*endoxa*) that we hold but cannot prove. By critically examining the *endoxa*, especially as articulated by earlier philosophers, one can work one's way toward the recognition of the true principles. The *endoxa* include both genuine reflections of natural truth and prejudices arising from the customs, laws, and conditions of particular societies. Aristotle's critical survey of predecessors is not a historically faithful account but a dialectical prüfung — examining their doctrines as a judge examines witnesses — from which the catalogue of problems emerges.

##### c. Cartography of the Unsolved Problems

The philosophical yield of such critical analysis is an overview of the problems that must be solved — what Aristotle calls "aporiai." An *aporia* is a dead end, a state of being at a loss. Picht translates and interprets the opening of *Metaphysics* III at length: before working out the science, one must traverse the entirety of the unresolved difficulties. The later solution is the loosening of what was previously bound. One cannot loosen a fetter if one does not know it. The helplessness of thinking reveals the fetter by which it is bound. This connects to the imagery of Plato's cave-allegory, where the prisoners are fettered and liberation comes through being brought to recognize one's own aporiai.

The first step toward founding a science consists in clearly surveying and demarcating the problems that need solving — a "cartography of our own ignorance." Even if one has achieved a complete overview, one still faces the greatest aporia of all: which problem is the first and most fundamental? Only when the fundamental question is uncovered can the remaining questions be brought into order.

##### d. The Aporematic Character of Aristotelian Philosophy

Picht quotes Ross's characterization: "The procedure throughout the Metaphysics never becomes deductive; it always remains aporematic." This means Aristotle does not present a finished doctrine in scientific form but shows how one must proceed to eventually find such a doctrine. The aporiai reveal the fundamental form of Aristotelian thought. What is usually presented dogmatically as Aristotle's "teaching" must be returned to the context of the aporiai and illuminated in its aporematic character.

##### e. The Fundamental Question of the Ousia of the Soul

In the opening of chapter 2, Aristotle states that the starting point of the investigation is "the presentation of what, according to the prevailing view, seems most of all to underlie the soul as origin according to its nature." This points back to 402a 7-8 and identifies the fundamental aporia: the determinations that constitute the *ousia* of the soul are what must stand first in the investigation. The question of the *ousia* of the soul is unfolded in two directions: (1) to the soul belongs movement; (2) to the soul belongs perception (in the widest sense: apprehension of truth). Wherever soul is, there is movement; wherever soul is, truth is recognized. The question of the essence of the soul thus aims at the connection of movement and truth. In the essence of the soul, physics and metaphysics are joined.

#### 2. Books II and III: The Sequence of the Soul's Faculties

Books II and III contain Aristotle's own doctrine, though the word "doctrine" must not be understood dogmatically — the procedure remains aporematic throughout. Book II, 1 gives a first outline; II, 2 carries out a more differentiated structural analysis that reveals the soul manifesting in a plurality of faculties, with different living beings distinguished by the number and internal organization of their psychic faculties. Only the human being possesses all faculties including *nous*, through which he transcends himself as a living being. The chapters II, 4-III, 3 then analyze, ascending from below: the nutritive faculty (II, 4), the perceptive faculty (II, 5-III, 2), and the imaginative faculty (III, 3). The last of these is constructed as a transition to the summit of the work: the section on the thinking faculty (III, 4-8), containing the central discussion of the essence of *nous*. The essential structures of *nous* turn out to be identical with the essential structures of *aisthēsis*, confirming that the *ousia* of the soul, despite its differentiation into various faculties, is to be grasped as a unity.

The last main section of the work (III, 9-11) investigates the second fundamental determination — the soul's relation to movement. The section on the thinking faculty thus has a double function: it is both the summit of the ascending sequence of faculties and the first part of a concluding section whose second part consists of a parallel investigation of the faculty of movement. The double perspective introduced in the second sentence of the first chapter — toward *alētheia* as a whole and toward *physis* — is maintained to the end. The work has a rigorous architecture.

#### 3. Remark on the Literary Form

Picht notes that the books *On the Soul*, despite their clear architecture, are not a completed and self-enclosed work but an unevenly elaborated draft. For the lecture's purposes, what matters is: first, the architecture of the whole; second, selected chapters — the aporiai chapter (I, 1), the chapters on the *ousia* of the soul (II, 1-2), and the chapters on the thinking faculty (III, 4-8), with a concluding outlook on the theology of *Metaphysics* XII.

## Third Part: The Aporiai

### I. The Aporiai Concerning the Knowledge of the Soul

Picht opens Part III by recapitulating the methodological function of the catalogue of aporiai. Philosophical knowledge in Aristotle is *zētēsis* — the search for the presuppositions of a possible science, not that science itself. Even in Aristotle, philosophy remains *philo-sophia*. It is a false and specifically un-Aristotelian concept of philosophy that has led the post-Aristotelian tradition to seek the cognitive content of the Aristotelian writings only in the so-called "doctrine," that is, in the answers to the aporiai. The recognition of the aporiai themselves is rather the greatest and most essential achievement. If one does not have the aporiai before one's eyes, the doctrine too must be misunderstood. Therefore the aporiai-chapter (I, 1) cannot be skipped.

The first sentences of I, 1 (up to 402a 7), which were interpreted at length in the previous semester, do not belong to the aporiai-chapter itself. They form an independent prooimion on the position and significance of the science of the soul, corresponding in function to the first chapter of the *Metaphysics*, which treats "what the nature of the sought-for science is."

#### 1. The Fixing of the Horizon of the Inquiry

At 402a 7, Aristotle formulates a sentence with a different function: "We seek to look upon and to know its nature and its *ousia*, then everything that presents itself in its environs, of which one part seems to be peculiar experiences of the soul, while another part seems, through its mediation, to underlie the living beings as well as origin." This sentence does not formulate an aporia. It circumscribes the goal of the investigation — what is to be known in the sought-for science. In the *Metaphysics*, this corresponds to the second chapter, whose heading reads: "what the goal (*skopos*) is that the investigation and the whole method must hit."

Picht develops the concept of *skopos* — the target at which an archer aims his arrow — and the corresponding concept of *methodos* as a tracking-path (*hodos*) after prey (*meta*), fundamentally different from the modern concept of method since Descartes. Without a clear anticipation of the goal, one would wander aimlessly; one would not even be able to identify the quarry if one stumbled upon it. Only through the clear anticipation of the goal does the search as a whole receive its direction and orientation, becoming a *methodos* — a course toward a goal. The entire investigation is therefore determined by the perspective fixed through this anticipatory grasp of the goal. The anticipatory grasp simultaneously circumscribes the entire horizon in which the investigation moves. Because Aristotle was aware of this with a clarity rarely again achieved, he places the fixing of the *skopos* at the head of both *Peri psychēs* and the *Metaphysics*. He knows that with this single sentence he fixes the trajectory of the entire work.

What is fixed here is twofold: (1) the *physis* and *ousia* of the soul; (2) what presents itself in its environs — the *symbebēkota*, everything that "accompanies" the soul, subdivided into what is peculiar to the soul and what, through its mediation, also underlies living beings as origin. The justification for this circumscription comes from the "First Philosophy" — the science that considers being as being and what underlies it as origin in itself (*Metaphysics* IV, 1, 1003a 21-22). One must study Aristotle's *Metaphysics* to understand what horizon is presupposed and thereby posited in this determination of the goal.

The distinction between *ousia* and *symbebēkota*, which is foundational, has its ground and justification in the fact that Aristotle proceeds from the pure structure of the statements in which we exhibit what is. A statement in its simplest form consists of the connection of subject and predicate. The *subiectum* is the underlying, toward which the predicate is uttered. When I say "this chalk is white," I give to understand through the very form of the statement that the concepts "chalk" and "white" exhibit different modifications of what "this here" is. The primary and underlying thing is designated by the word "chalk." "White" is a property that also occurs at the chalk but does not constitute what the chalk essentially is — it is a *symbebēkos* or *pathos*, a happening or affection. The word "chalk," by contrast, designates the totality of what makes this being what it is: its "beingness," its *ousia*.

Examining the possible predicates more closely, one finds that these additional determinations of the being arrange themselves in certain general dimensions: quality, quantity, place, time, relation, and so on — the famous Aristotelian categories. The categories are not, as they were later interpreted, merely the logical forms of predicate-subject connection. The word *katēgoria* designates the uttering or showing-forth of something toward something. In the primary sense, the determinations exhibited in the categories are, as Aristotle says, *pathē* — happenings or widefahrnisse — of the *ousia*. Only secondarily are these happenings mirrored in the connection of predicates with a subject. But the form of this mirroring has a remarkable property: we can read off from the *logos* as such the pure structures of the connection of happenings with the *ousia*, whereas in the being itself these structures are deeply embedded and concealed. The *logos* gives us something like an X-ray image of what is. That the human being possesses in the *logos* the faculty of recognizing the pure structures of what is — the being as such — distinguishes him so fundamentally from all other living beings that this faculty constitutes the *ousia* of the human being. Hence for Aristotle the "definition" — the circumscription of the peculiar essence of the human being — is that he is the living being that has the *logos*: *to zōon logon echon*. In the section on the thinking faculty (III, 4-8), we shall see what this essential determination means.

The sentence fixing the content of the sought-for knowledge thus presupposes that we are to consider the soul, within the horizon fixed by the categorical doctrine, as a being that — like all other beings — shows itself in the distinction of *ousia* and its happenings as what it is from itself. This presupposition is by no means self-evident. We shall see, both in the aporiai and in Aristotle's attempt to circumscribe the *ousia* of the soul at the beginning of Book II, that Aristotle was highly aware of the problematic character of this approach. But the greatest achievement of Aristotle — the recognition of this problematic — was lost in the later tradition. People presupposed as self-evident that it was justified and meaningful to speak of soul, consciousness, reason, or spirit as though these words designated something that is a being. Correspondingly, God too was spoken of as a being, the highest being, and this beingness of God was explicated through the "ontological" proof. The fixing of the goal of the sought-for science of the soul that Aristotle outlines here determines for more than two thousand years the horizon within which the European human being was able to ask about himself and about God.

The sentence strings together the great fundamental words of Aristotle's philosophy like pearls on a chain. The aporiai-chapter that begins with the next sentence follows exactly the articulation of this sentence: the first part (402a 10-22) treats the difficulties arising when one tries to determine the method of the investigation and the form of the sought-for science; the second part (402a 23-b 16) develops the aporiai of the concepts *physis* and *ousia*; an intermediate piece (402b 16-403a 2) returns to the methodological problematic; the last part (403a 2-b 19) has the *pathē tēs psychēs* — the happenings of the soul, i.e., the *symbebēkota* — as its theme. The intermediate piece shows that the aporiai of the *ousia*-section hang together with the aporiai of the *pathē* section, and that the knowledge we seek must grasp both in their unity.

##### Excursus on the Subtle Nihilism of Exegesis

Picht translates the first sentence of the aporiai proper (402a 10-11): "In every way and on every account, it belongs to the most difficult things to gain anything like a reliable opinion about it [the soul]." He then devotes an excursus to criticizing Theiler's note that "to emphasize the difficulty of an undertaking belongs to a prooemium" — treating the sentence as a standing rhetorical formula or *topos*. Picht identifies in this philological procedure a subtle form of nihilism: by affixing the label "topos of the prooemium," one exempts oneself from attending to the specific sense of the specific statement or even its possible truth. In this subtle way nihilism enters into exegesis. The same procedure is familiar from New Testament form-criticism, where formal-historical analysis is used to strip texts of their content and binding force.

The previous discussion of the function of aporiai-catalogues and the concept of *aporia* has already shown that the detailed account of the specific difficulties of an investigation is for Aristotle the first and essential step of the investigation itself, and that he has philosophical, not literary, reasons for placing this overview at the head. Even granted that understanding, Aristotle's formulation is surprising, almost shocking. He says it belongs to the most difficult things, not to achieve scientific knowledge of the soul, but even to gain *tina pistin* — "something like a reliable opinion." *Pistis* has its fixed position in the scale of forms of knowledge: perception, opinion (*doxa*), knowledge (*epistēmē*), intellectual intuition (*nous*). Within this scale, *pistis* belongs to *doxa* — it is a trust we place in an opinion. The word *pistis* is further relativized by the indefinite pronoun *tis*, meaning not "some" but "something resembling."

What it means when the author of the work foundational for the entire European tradition on the soul claims not to have founded a science but declares that even attaining a *pistis* is among the most difficult things there are, is emphasized by the words *pantē pantōs* — a formula introduced by Parmenides and carrying, wherever it appears in Plato and Aristotle, solemn connotations of totality. These two lines consciously place the entire philosophy of Aristotle in question. They express a resignation toward the goal he has set himself. With appeal to these two lines, one can shake every later Aristotelianism — that of Thomas Aquinas and the Scholastics no less than that of Hegel. Aristotle was no Aristotelian. He understood his philosophy not dogmatically but aporematically. He was a Socratic who had passed through the school of Plato. All of these insights are stolen from us when we content ourselves with labeling this sentence a "topos of the prooemium."

#### 2. The Question of Ground and Origin of What Is

The statement that it belongs to the most difficult things to gain even a reliable opinion about the soul serves as the ground for the entire aporiai-chapter: because the sought-for knowledge is as such problematic, the knowledge of truth must begin with the analysis of this very problematic — that is, with the aporiai that arise when one tries to determine the "nature" of this knowledge. Picht translates the first aporia-section (402a 11-19): "Since the investigation is the same also for much else (I mean the investigation concerning the *ousia* and what something is), perhaps someone might think there is a single method for everything of which we wish to know the *ousia*, just as there is the method of deduction for the incidental essential properties; one must therefore seek this method. But if there is no single and common method concerning the question of what something is, then the investigation turns out to be still more difficult. For it will then be necessary to ascertain for each domain what the mode of proceeding is. And even supposing it were clear whether deduction or division or some other method is the sought procedure, there are still many aporiai and wrong turnings in the problem of from what starting points one must search. For one thing has these, another thing those origins, as for example numbers and surfaces."

Aristotle begins by noting that the question of *ousia* directs the investigation not only in the domain of the soul but in many other fields. For everything that can be investigated, the goal is knowledge of what it is — provided that it "is" in some sense. Without knowing in what sense and what it is, we will probably take a wrong path. Picht insists one must not be deceived by the ideological disputes over ontology currently raging: in the twentieth century it is just as true as in Aristotle's time that every scientific theory, every doctrine, dogma, opinion, and ideology implies a whole net of mostly unarticulated prejudices about what the corresponding "domain of objects" is. If our thinking is to achieve enlightenment, it must bring these prejudices to light and analyze them — that is the task of ontology. At its core, Enlightenment and ontology are identical.

Picht then develops the guiding motif of all Aristotelian philosophy: *to on legetai pollachōs* — "being is exhibited in manifold ways in the *logos*" (*Metaphysics* 1028a 1). He warns against the standard translation that speaks of the multiple "meanings" of being. The subject of this sentence is not the concept *to on* but the being itself. The sentence speaks of how being itself is shown forth in the *logos* — a fundamental difference. Constitutive for the entire philosophy of Aristotle is the doctrine, grounded especially in *Metaphysics* VII-IX, that *ousia* has priority among the manifold forms in which being manifests itself in the *logos*. All other forms in which being can be exhibited are referred back to *ousia*. *Ousia* is *prōtōs on* — that which is in the primary sense. For this reason, Aristotle must, even when he asks about the soul, first attempt to circumscribe its *ousia*.

But the *ousia* of the soul is distinguished from other things we also call *ousia*. In *Metaphysics* V, 8, Aristotle says that *ousia* is called, in one sense, whatever is not predicated of a subject but is itself the subject of which everything else is predicated — as, for example, living beings. But in another sense, *ousia* is called "that which is the ground of being and is contained as underlying origin in what is not predicated of a subject — for example, the soul in the living being." Here *ousia* appears, as it were, in the second power: first, the living being itself is *ousia*; second, the soul is *ousia* because it is the origin contained in the living being as the ground of its being able to be. Already this single passage shows the difficulties one encounters when trying to circumscribe the *ousia* of the soul itself.

Picht then develops the Aristotelian concept of the First Philosophy through *Metaphysics* IV, 1: "There is something like a science that contemplates being as being and what underlies it in itself as origin." This science is not identical with any particular science, for none of the others conducts its contemplation universally over being insofar as it is. They each cut out a portion for themselves and investigate what pertains to it. The First Philosophy grasps being universally and as such. All its findings must therefore be constitutive for every particular science.

To illustrate the Platonic background of this inquiry, Picht develops at length the concept of *idea* through Plato's example of the bridle (*Phaedrus* 246a): what makes every particular bridle a bridle is the idea of the bridle — the relation rider/horse and the rider's intention to guide the horse. This idea is not a mere mental construct; it is inscribed in the nature of things. But it is something other than any material bridle. The material bridles would not exist at all if riders and bridle-makers did not have this optimum before their eyes. The non-sensible possibility of a determinate relation between rider and horse constitutes the being of every particular bridle. That is why Plato calls the *idea* also the *ousia* of the bridle.

When Aristotle replaces the Platonic formula *ontōs on* with his formula *on hē on*, he asks the same question as Plato but under a different perspective. As a being, only the concrete bridle can be considered. But it makes a great difference whether one considers the bridle only under particular aspects or whether one considers it, abstracting from all other features and even from the use it serves, purely with respect to its being. Insofar as the bridle is a being, it is the same as everything else of which we can say that it is. The question of the First Philosophy asks: what constitutive features does absolutely everything that is at all have, solely by virtue of being? This question goes beyond *to on hē on* to the *archai* and highest *aitiai* — the origins and highest grounds of being.

#### 3. Knowing the Knowing Soul — The Fundamental Circle of Knowledge

All of this Aristotle presupposes; all of it must be before one's eyes if one is to understand his aporiai. Already in the first line of *Metaphysics* IV, 1 lies a pointer to the aporia-chain that interests us first — the aporiai connected with the question whether and how such a science is possible at all. The translations read: "There is a science that contemplates being insofar as it is a being." But even Joseph Owens suppresses the fact that Aristotle says *estin epistēmē tis* — "there is something like an *epistēmē*." If one checks how Aristotle defines the essence of *epistēmē* in the *Analytica Posteriora*, it turns out that the First Philosophy cannot in that sense be *epistēmē*. In what sense then can it be science? On what does it rest its certainty? By what methods does it reach its findings? The answer is not given in the treatises on the First Philosophy, where only the "object" — being — is discussed. The answer is found where knowledge is discussed: in *De anima* III, 4-8.

Here lies the first and most abysmal of all aporiai connected with the science sought in *De anima*. On one hand, we can gain a science of the soul only by proceeding as Aristotle describes the procedure of particular sciences: from the totality of what is, a determinate region is demarcated under the heading "soul," and what properties of the soul can be ascertained within this region is investigated. On the other hand, this procedure is possible only because soul belongs to what is; the constitutive structures of being must therefore also pertain to the soul and be presupposed in the particular science. But thirdly, it is the soul itself that recognizes the structures of being; the First Philosophy must for its part presuppose that in the soul lies the faculty of recognizing being as being. Here lies an inescapable circle — the circle of knowledge as such. What is called the "hermeneutic circle" is only a secondary image of this fundamental circle of knowledge.

Rereading Aristotle's opening words: if there were a single method for determining *ousia* in every domain, one could derive this method from the First Philosophy; later generations actually made such use of Aristotle, as in Wolff's ontology, which as *metaphysica generalis* prescribes the method for all particular sciences. Did Aristotle himself understand his ontology this way? Aristotle leaves the question open. He formulates it as an aporia. Even if such a method existed, numerous aporiai and wrong turnings would remain — because every *ousia* has its own *archai*, and without knowing these *archai*, one cannot reach the *ousia* from the right starting point.

This concludes the overview of the first part of the aporiai — those concerning the possibility and structure of a science of the soul. No one who has thought through these few lines can still imagine that Aristotle understood his own philosophy as the finished science whose impossibility he demonstrates through these aporiai. In sharpness of epistemological critique, Aristotle is not surpassed even by Kant's *Critique of Pure Reason*.

### II. The Aporiai Concerning the Ousia of the Soul

#### 1. Soul Has Its Place in the Category of Ousia

Picht translates the opening of the second aporia-section (402a 23-b 1): "First it is presumably necessary to set out in which of the categories and what it [the soul] is — I mean whether it is a 'this-here' and an *ousia*, or a quality or a quantity or any other of the distinguished categories; further, whether it belongs to what is in the constitution of possibility, or whether it is rather a kind of *entelecheia*; for that makes no small difference."

The priority of the question of *ousia* is fixed by Aristotle's making the categorical doctrine the basis for the explication of the beingness of beings. He here calls the categories *genē* (of *to on*). In the Greek word *genos*, Picht explains, Plato and Aristotle still hear that something has its provenance (*genesis*) in its *genos*. The beingness of a particular being (say, this book) has a different provenance than the mode of beingness we have in view when we speak of its color, and this again a different provenance from its size. At one and the same being, features are found that are of quite different provenance yet interpenetrate in this one being. The prior fixing of the *genos* — the domain of provenance to which a phenomenon belongs — decides the entire structure of the theory that explains the phenomenon.

It might seem self-evident that the soul can only have its place in the category of *ousia*. But already in Aristotle's time there were philosophical teachings — such as that of Democritus — which on the basis of a consistent materialism regarded the soul as merely an additional property of the body. Then the soul would have to be housed in another category — in a consistently materialist framework, in the category of quantity or relation. Other theories, like that of Empedocles, traced psychic phenomena back to qualities. It is therefore by no means self-evident that the soul has its place in the category of *ousia*.

#### 2. What Does Unity or Divisibility Mean in Relation to the Soul?

Picht translates the next chain of aporiai (402b 1-9): "It must also be investigated whether it is divisible or without parts, and whether every soul is of the same kind or not; if not of the same kind, whether it differs only in species or also in genus. ... One must take care that it not remain hidden whether its definition is a single one, like that of 'living being,' or whether it is different for each individual case, like that of horse, dog, human being, God — while 'living being in general' is either nothing or something posterior."

These aporiai concern the most fundamental questions. Can one meaningfully say of soul in general that it "is"? By the example of the concept "living being," Aristotle shows how problematic this is. One can say that this dog, this horse, this human being is. But can one say of "living being in general" that it "is"? And if this is questionable, what about "soul"? Must we perhaps say that soul is either nothing at all, or — if anything — something posterior, a universal concept gained by mere abstraction, a mere thought-construct? What holds for soul as such holds equally for everything predicated universally of the soul. If we say the soul has various faculties — the nutritive faculty, the perceptive faculty, etc. — we must ask not only whether the X to which we ascribe these faculties is anything at all, but also whether the nutritive faculty of the dog is the same as that of the horse, and whether the universal concept "nutritive faculty" designates anything of which it is meaningful to assume that it "is."

The formulation of these questions is determined by Plato's famous doctrine of the "parts of the soul." From Plato, Aristotle also inherited the recognition that the word "part" must not be understood crudely, as though the soul were assembled from separable pieces. Plato elucidated the concept of "part" through the concept of *faculty* (*dynamis*). With respect to what do we draw the distinction between different faculties? Each of these faculties is in its essence directed toward something extra-psychic that stands over against it (*ta antikeimena*). The perceptive faculty exists only if there is something that can be perceived. Remove sounds, and there is no more hearing. The soul is thus, through the performances of its faculties, set outside itself. The differentiation of what is outside the soul seems to constitute the differentiation of the soul's faculties. Can we then speak of this differentiation as a differentiation *in the soul itself*? Or must we, in order to find the alleged structure of the soul, analyze the structure of the world into which the soul is set? Does the talk of soul-faculties, indeed of the soul itself, then lose its basis?

#### 3. What Constitutes the Various Soul-Faculties?

Picht translates the next aporia-chain (402b 9-16): "Furthermore, if there are not many souls but only parts [of the one soul], the question arises whether one must first seek the soul as a whole or the parts. It is also difficult to delimit in their particulars how the parts differ from one another by nature, and whether one must first seek the parts or their performances. For example, the intellectual beholding [*noein*] or the faculty of intellectual beholding [*nous*], and the perceiving or the faculty of perception. Likewise with the other parts and performances. And if the performances have priority, one might again fall into an aporia whether one must first investigate the objects correlated with them — for example, the perceptible before the perceptive faculty, and the intelligible [*noēton*] before the faculty of intellectual intuition [*nous*]."

Aristotle proceeds from the hypothesis that the talk of soul-parts is justified, and that different living beings are differentiated by the degree and combination in which they possess various soul-faculties. If every living being has its own soul, and this soul must be considered as something whole and self-enclosed, it is not justified to treat, say, the perceptive and imaginative faculties as two different faculties of one and the same entity. If, conversely, the talk of different soul-faculties and their distribution among different living beings has a sense, it implies that it is meaningful to presuppose something like "soul in general" as a possible unity in which the diversity of faculties can be distinguished and delimited. But new difficulties then arise: from what standpoint do we draw the distinction? If we consider the faculties as "parts," we might think we must first inspect and dissect the "object" soul. If we consider them as faculties, the differentiation of performances will be what allows us to infer the differentiation of faculties. But each performance is essentially directed toward something extra-psychic. Does the differentiation of what lies outside the soul constitute the differentiation of the soul's faculties? Does the soul, examined in this way, dissolve into its world?

Picht notes that Aristotle expressly names "God" (*theou*, 402b 7) at this point, confirming the basic hypothesis that Aristotle, because he thinks God as *nous*, was conscious that the horizon within which he determines the essence of the soul is simultaneously the horizon within which we know God. The questions whether it is justified to determine the soul as *ousia*, and what such a determination means, are not abstract "methodological" questions; their answer simultaneously decides how we think God, and whether the word "God" designates something of which we can say that it "is."

#### 4. Aristotle's Excursus on the Possibilities of a Science of the Soul (402b 16-403a 2)

Picht translates the intermediate piece in which Aristotle returns to the first series of aporiai: "It seems that not only is the knowledge of what something is useful for the contemplation of the grounds of the accompanying happenings of the *ousiai* (as, for example, in mathematics the knowledge of what 'straight' and 'curved' or 'line' and 'surface' is, is useful for discovering to how many right angles the angle-sum of a triangle is equal), but conversely the incidental happenings also contribute greatly to the knowledge of what something is. For when we are able, according to our imagination, to give an account of the incidental happenings — either of all or of most — then we shall also be best able to give an account of the *ousia*. The starting-point of every proof is the *ousia*. It follows that all definitions that do not yield knowledge of how to recognize the incidental happenings, indeed from which one cannot even easily arrive at a conjecture about them, are formal and empty talk."

Here, on the basis of the scientific theory of the *Analytica Posteriora*, an extremely important criterion for the knowledge of *ousia* is formulated. In a deductive science, all propositions can be derived from the underlying axioms and definitions. The definition of the *ousia* must satisfy the condition that the totality of the derivable propositions can be inferred from it by strict proof. This is how one recognizes whether a definition truly exhibits the *ousia* or is merely an abstraction — what Aristotle calls "dialectical and empty talk." One must therefore, when establishing the definition, already have the totality of propositions to be derived from it in view in one's imagination.

Even in mathematics, practitioners never actually proceeded by first formulating axioms and then deriving theorems. They pushed forward into fields of possible discovery with an empirical-like procedure, then checked retrospectively whether the new findings could be derived without contradiction from the axioms, and revised the axioms in the light of new discoveries when necessary.

The science of the soul is not a deductive science like geometry. But the geometric example illuminates the structure and method even of a science of the soul. If already in a deductive science the method consists in the thinking's constant oscillation between the investigation of the *symbebēkota* and the knowledge of the *ousia*, this holds all the more for a science that must, like the science of the soul, grasp a vast quantity of changeable and only empirically knowable *symbebēkota*. Here too the *logos tēs ousias* must satisfy the demand of encompassing, in its possibility, everything that can occur at the soul. But we must hold before our imagination the entire vast field of the possible *pathē* of the soul if we are to arrive at such a *logos*. The analysis of individual *pathē* will be the procedure through which we open access to the knowledge of the *ousia*.

Picht then notes that, in accordance with his plan, he skips chapters I, 2-5 — the section in which Aristotle discusses the doctrines of his predecessors. But even the excerpt from the aporiai-chapter has shown how much is called into question that is still taken as self-evident today, although we do not ourselves understand these supposed self-evidences. A critical discussion of the authoritative doctrines of Greek philosophy on the soul would call into question foundations that still count as our own. Only such a full carrying-through of the process from which the knowledge of the aporiai springs would adequately elucidate the content and horizon of the aporiai themselves.

## Fourth Part: What Is the Soul?

### I. What Is the Soul?

Picht opens Part IV with the first sentence of *De anima* II, 1, which marks our position in the investigation: "The doctrines handed down by the earlier thinkers about the soul have been discussed. Let us now again, as though from the beginning, strike the path upward to the goal, by trying to delimit completely what soul is and what its most general definition might be." The path then unfolds in two stages: the first chapter gives a preliminary sketch, and the second chapter carries through a more differentiated investigation. By the end of chapter 3, it becomes clear how the investigation of *ousia* of soul in general connects with the investigation of particular soul-faculties.

The "most general definition" (*koinotatos logos*) that Aristotle seeks is not a definition in the modern sense. The requirements placed on this *logos* go far beyond anything that today's thought associates with the word "definition." The sought-for *logos* must be an exhibition that delimits what "soul in general and as a whole" is, in such a way that the inner unity of all the soul's faculties and thereby all modifications of the psychic become visible. Only when this condition is fulfilled can we say we have discovered a *horismos* that circumscribes the *ousia* of *psyche* — the goal of the entire work. The so-called "definition of the soul" that Aristotle develops in the first chapter gives, by his own words, only a sketchy outline. It is not the sought-for *horismos* but only the initial hypothesis needed to undertake a methodical investigation at all. This hypothesis is not arbitrarily chosen: it is preceded by the critical discussion of earlier hypotheses in Book I and the analysis of the aporiai.

##### Excursus on the Philosophical Implications of Philological Misinterpretation — Prospect on the Goal of the Path

Picht devotes an extended excursus to a philological dispute with Theiler's translation and editorial decisions. The key phrase *palin d' hōsper ex archēs epaniōmen* (412a 4) — which Picht translates "let us again, as though from the beginning, strike the path upward" — is treated by Theiler as merely marking a compositional seam between texts from different periods. Picht shows through parallel passages in *Physics* VIII that the formula can occur within a single continuous investigation, and that its function is always double: it marks a new approach while indicating that this new approach belongs to a larger investigation. The word *epaniōmen* has in Plato the specific meaning of an ascent to the highest stage of knowledge. Picht raises the open question whether this Platonic resonance is also intended here: the "definition" of II, 1 is expressly designated as a rough sketch; the following investigations lead an ascent through the soul-faculties to *nous*; but the investigation of *nous* shows that even the "definition" of II, 1 must be questioned. The human *nous* cannot be understood independently of the divine *nous*. Only from knowledge of the divine *nous* could the *koinotatos logos* be gained in which the question "What is the soul?" finds its answer. This is why the entire content of the work is designated in the first chapter with strictly Platonic terminology as mere *pistis* (402a 11). If Aristotle envisions a path that is to traverse an ascent to the divine *nous*, then the Platonic meaning of *epanienai* is the most precise way to characterize it.

The investigation aims at completely delimiting what soul is and what its most general definition might be. Aristotle proceeds by sketching an answer in broad strokes in chapter 1 of Book II. But this preliminary sketch must not be understood as a final answer. What we gain at the end of the work is not the definitive answer but a radical formulation of the fundamental aporia of the relationship between movement and truth, contained in the so-called definition. The answer to the riddle posed is the doctrine of the divine *nous* as the unmoved mover.

### II. Ousia and Hylē — The Body-Soul Problem

The chain of *ousia*-aporiai had begun with the question of in which category the soul has its place and whether it is something in the constitution of possibility or a kind of *entelecheia*. The definition of II, 1 contains the answer: it determines the soul as *ousia* and as *entelecheia*. Aristotle can dispense with re-examining the problem of which category the soul belongs to, presupposing that Book I has sufficiently clarified this.

He begins at 412a 6: "We call one domain of being *ousia*." Picht insists that *genos* here does not have the formal-logical sense of "genus" but designates a domain of provenance within the manifold of being. The *ousia* is not one genus among many genera of statements; it is a *genos* of being itself. It cuts through all possible genera and species. Wherever we can speak of being at all, what we say is referred back to *ousia*.

Aristotle then recalls that even of *ousia* itself the principle holds: *legetai pollachōs* — it is exhibited in multiple ways. He distinguishes three: one as *hylē* (matter), which in itself is not a "this-here"; another as *morphē* and *eidos* (shape and form); and the third as what comes forth from these two together. Picht explains each:

**Hylē**: Matter as such is the indeterminate and indeterminable substrate of the movability of everything in nature. Aristotle does not say that one side of *ousia* is matter; he says we exhibit one aspect "as (*hōs*) matter, in the manner of matter." The important *hōs* shows that matter itself has no determination that would allow identifying it as "this-here."

**Morphē and Eidos**: These form a double name — shape and form — and the *ousia*-side they designate is not simple in itself. The distinction is crucial: *eidos* is the pure non-sensible form (e.g., the geometric figure of the circle), while *morphē* is the appearance of this form in the sensible material (e.g., the ring as a shaped piece of bronze). *Eidos* does not arise and cannot perish; *morphē* is the manifestation of *eidos* in matter. The Greek concept of production means: a process through which the non-sensible is brought to appearance in the sensible.

**To ek toutōn**: The third moment — "what comes forth from these" — is *to synolon*, the whole composed of both moments. The question of how matter and form are connected leads to the central problem of Aristotle's ontology, and the determination of the *ousia* of soul arises from his solution to this problem.

Picht notes a remark usually overlooked in Aristotle-interpretation: the *eidos* of *ousia* as such already contains matter as a moment. There is therefore no further matter in which this *eidos* could present itself. Where then is the place for this *eidos*? The place for the *eidos* as such is *nous*. This means the ontology points to a horizon that it does not itself encompass — which is why Aristotle cannot and does not succeed in giving a definition of the soul that includes the faculty of *nous* on the basis of this ontology alone.

### III. Morphē and Eidos — Shape and Form

Picht develops the distinction between *morphē* and *eidos* through *Metaphysics* VII, 8: the *eidos* does not arise in the sensible, and there is no genesis of it; rather, it is what comes to appearance in another (the matter), either through art or through nature. He illustrates through the example of a smith making a ring from bronze: the bronze is the material, the *eidos* is the mathematical figure of the circle, and the smith brings the figure forth into a "this-here." The ring (*morphē*) is not identical with the mathematical circle (*eidos*), for every ring consists of a material; but it is identical with the circle insofar as it is the circle presented in this material. The concrete being (*synolon*) is therefore a third: neither matter alone nor form alone but the unity that emerges from both.

Picht then quotes the fundamental formula from *Metaphysics* VII (1037a 29f.): "The *ousia* is the indwelling *eidos*, from which together with the *hylē* the concrete *ousia* [the whole] is exhibited in the *logos*." After Plato, *ousia* is the *eidos kath' hauto* — the *eidos* in itself. After Aristotle, it is *to eidos to enon* — the *eidos* not in itself but indwelling in the *hylē*. The *eidos* appears in the *hylē* as *morphē*, as shape. *Physis* is twofold: on one side as *hylē*, on the other as *morphē*, and this latter is *telos* (*Physics* II, 199a 30f.). Picht explains that this sentence is incomprehensible if one understands "matter" as the atomists did: stones do not contain in themselves the form of a house. In a sum of matter-parts, form is not contained. Only Kant and Schelling, and now quantum physics, have penetrated to the depth where the indissoluble connection of matter and form becomes transparent.

In Aristotle, because matter is continuum, it is *dynamis* — possibility. The concept of possibility cannot be interpreted as an indifferent field of chance. It can only be defined by adding what is to be possible. The possible is a manifold of structured states. Through Manfred Eigen's theory of the self-organization of matter, this connection has become much clearer.

### IV. Fundamental Concepts of Aristotelian Ontology

#### 1. To ti ēn einai

Picht develops this central formula following Schwegler's classic explanation. The formula contains a gap: one must write *to ti ēn [X] einai*, where X is filled by the designation of that whose being is in question (in the dative). The possessive dative expresses: "what for X being is." The imperfect *ēn* does not designate a temporal past but the priority of nature (*proteron tē physei*): what structurally but not temporally precedes. The *eidos*, which the smith must have before his eyes before beginning his work, has not arisen and cannot perish. It is present in full or not at all. As the ring is forged, this previously present pure form gradually emerges as shape, until the point is reached where the smith can say "the ring is finished." This emergence is *genesis* — coming to appearance. The imperfect *ēn* expresses the Aristotelian thought that what is thus complete and perfect has, considered in isolation, no being at all; it "is" only insofar as it appears in the matter, manifesting itself only in the mode of incompleteness.

The formula *to ti ēn einai* thus designates the *ousia* in the unification of both its moments: to be, in sensible appearance, what for a being its being has been from the very first.

#### 2. Katholou

Picht develops the concept of *katholou* through Plato's analysis in *Theaetetus* 201C-210D of letters, syllables, and words. A word considered as a mere sum (*pan*) of letters is general in the sense of *kata pantos*: the statement "every word consists of letters" holds of every possible word. But a word considered as a whole (*holon*) — as a unity possessing meaning — is general in a different sense: *katholou*. The *horismos* must exhibit not the sum of parts but the unity that makes a *pan* into a *holon*. At the end of *Metaphysics* VII, Aristotle says this unity-grounding principle is not an element but *arche* — the origin of something's being a unity and a whole — and he calls this *arche* *ousia*. The definition says *katholou* what something is: with respect to the whole-being of the whole.

Picht formally specifies (*Analytica Posteriora* 73b 25ff.): what is universal in the sense of *katholou* is also universal in the sense of *kata pantos*; it grasps the individual *kath' hauto* (in itself); and it designates each individual *hē auto* (as what it is). Only when all three conditions coincide is the universality given that Aristotle calls *katholou*.

#### 3. Dynamis and Entelecheia

##### a. The Logos of the Ousia of Soul

To prevent losing the thread, Picht situates the discussion within De an. II, 1: Aristotle begins his development of the definition by recalling the fundamental concepts of Met. VII-IX: *hylē*, *morphē*, *eidos*, *to ek toutōn*. These must be understood not in isolation but in the unity that constitutes the being as a *holon*. The central problem is how the various moments interpenetrate into unity. To convey a first understanding of Aristotle's solution, Picht introduces the further concepts *to ti ēn einai* and *katholou*, and must now also clarify *dynamis*, *entelecheia*, and *energeia*.

##### b. The Question of the Essence of Movement

The "definition" of the soul reads: "The soul is the first *entelecheia* of a physical body that potentially has life" (412a 27f.). To explain this, Picht develops the concept of *dynamis*. The modern interpretation of "possibility" as a modality of thought is blocked by Aristotle's own statement: "Matter is possibility" (412a 9). This concept of matter is close to that of quantum physics and relativity theory but contradicts the atomists' concept. The Aristotelian concept of possibility must be interpreted from the essence of movement. There are two forms of movement: local motion (*phora*) and change of states (*alloiōsis*). Modern science absolutizes local motion and reduces all movement to transport, but for Aristotle change of states — arising and perishing — has priority. All arising and perishing can only be defined through the structures that emerge or dissolve. *Ousia* is the structure that comes to appearance in the movement of matter.

##### c. The Meaning of the Word Entelecheia

Picht refutes the standard etymology (Diels) deriving *entelecheia* from *entelēs* + *echein* (having perfection), because the definition of movement as *entelecheia atelēs* (imperfect entelecheia, *Physics* 257b 8) would then be self-contradictory. Following Jaeger, *en-tel-echeia* means: the constitution in which a being has its *telos* (goal) within itself. Movement is nothing other than the not-yet-having-arrived-at-the-goal in-itself-having of the goal. In the organic realm, the germ already contains the *eidos* prefigured; as the plant moves from "germ" to "tree," the end-state is in a certain way already present within it — *en-tel-echeia*. But it has not yet arrived there, for otherwise it would not need to move. The not-having-arrived-at-the-goal in-itself-having of the goal constitutes the very essence of movement.

##### d. "The Eidos Is Contained in the Hylē"

The paradox: *eidos* = *entelecheia* = *kinēsis*. If this holds, then *eidos* = *kinēsis*. But *eidos* is ungenerated and imperishable — how can it be identified with a movement-concept? The solution lies in the concept of *ousia* as *to eidos to enon* — the indwelling *eidos*. *Ousia* is the structure that emerges in the movement of matter. The *eidos*, despite its timeless self-identity, must be interpreted as a pure movement-concept. This is the innermost center of Aristotelian philosophy, never surpassed in radicality: movement is placed at the very center of thought. Structure must be understood as movement-structure; this is what Aristotle expresses by designating the *eidos* as *telos*, more precisely as *entelecheia*.

#### 4. Energeia

##### a. The Work Is the Goal

Picht returns to *Metaphysics* IX (1050a 21ff.): "The work is the goal; *energeia* is the work; and it extends toward *entelecheia*." *Telos* at Aristotle means neither "goal" in the sense of the end of a path nor "purpose." The correct translation is "immanent structure" — a paraphrase of *to eidos to enon*.

##### b. The Energeia Is the Work

Aristotle distinguishes two structures of producing: *poiēsis*, where the goal lies outside the producing (building a house); and *praxis*, where the goal lies within the carrying-out itself (seeing, hearing). *Entelecheia* has the structure of *praxis*: a movement that contains its goal within itself. The *ergon* is not outside the movement but contained in it. This form of being-in-movement Aristotle designates by his coined term *energeia*.

##### c. The Double Form of Entelecheia

*Entelecheia* has two forms: one as knowledge (the disposition), the other as actual contemplation (the exercise). The soul is *entelecheia* in the sense of knowledge: in the soul's underlying as origin, both sleep and waking are contained. This distinction between first and second *entelecheia* is constitutive for the *ousia* of soul and stands at the center of Aristotle's doctrine of the thinking faculty.

##### d. The Possibility of Having Knowledge as a Constitution of Being

Picht develops this through the extended passage II, 5 (417a 21-b 28), distinguishing three levels: (1) The human being as such is "knowing" because the human species has knowledge as a *hexis* — a constitution of being. (2) The person who has learned the alphabet possesses a faculty he can exercise at will. (3) The person actually contemplating is in the state of *entelecheia*, possessing knowledge in the proper sense. The first two possess knowledge *kata dynamin*. The first is transformed through learning; the second merely transitions from having-but-not-exercising to exercising (*energein*).

Aristotle then draws consequences for the theory of teaching and learning: teaching cannot be understood as filling an empty vessel from a full one. The learner must see for himself that what is taught is correct and develop judgment to distinguish true from false. The teacher too must have understood. Both must possess the capacity to arrive at knowledge from their own resources. The passage from possibility to *energeia* is not an external imposition but a transformation of the entire constitution of being. The natural state of the human being is, according to Aristotle, not ignorance but knowledge.

##### e. The Priority of Entelecheia Before Becoming, According to Time

Picht interprets *De anima* III, 7 (431a 2-4): "Knowledge *kata dynamin* is earlier in time in one and the same individual, but in general and as a whole not even in time; for everything that comes into being proceeds in its being from what is in the state of *entelecheia*." The priority of nature (*proteron tē physei*) lies with the *eidos* — the structure always already prefigured for a being at every stage of its development. This priority stands perpendicular to the flow of arising and perishing, which endlessly reproduces the same structure. The entire doctrine is grounded in the eternity of the cosmos, which in turn rests on the doctrine of God as the unmoved mover.

#### 5. The Concept Zōē

Picht develops the concept of life through the passage II, 1 (412a 11-22), which he had deliberately postponed. Aristotle defines: "*Ousia* seems most of all to be the bodies, and among these the natural ones, for these contain the origins of the others. Of the natural bodies, some have life, others do not. We call 'life' self-nourishment, growth, and wasting away. Therefore every natural body that partakes of life would be *ousia*." The self-movement of life is determined by the drive of everything living toward the development of the structure laid out in it. Because it drives to manifest what is latent in it, it nourishes itself. This self-movement is *entelecheia atelēs*. Already at the most primitive stages of life, movement and truth are connected, for we speak of truth wherever the hidden comes to light.

The concept of life appears even at the summit of Aristotle's theology: *Metaphysics* XII says of God: "Life too underlies him as origin, for the *energeia* of *nous* is life. That one is the *energeia*. His *energeia* in itself is the best and eternal life."

Aristotle then derives the definition of the soul through two steps: (1) Since living natural bodies are *ousia*, and since every *ousia* contains the moments of *hylē* and *eidos*, and since the body corresponds to *hylē* while the soul is what gives life, the soul must be the *eidos* of the living natural body. (2) Since *ousia* is *entelecheia*, the soul is the *entelecheia* of such a body. Combined: "The soul is the first *entelecheia* of a natural body that potentially has life."

#### 6. The Concept Organon

##### a. Organon as Form-Giving Immanent Structure

Picht develops the concept of *organon* through its etymological connection with *ergon* and its use in Plato (*Cratylus*, *Republic* 508B — the eye as "the most sun-like of the organs of perception"). The concept of "tool" (*organon*) was understood by the Greeks not as an instrument of domination over nature but as a means of integration into nature. Only on this basis could Aristotle develop a doctrine of natural bodies in which the concept of *organon* serves to explicate the properly natural in nature — life as the self-movement of natural bodies — in its ontological structure.

Aristotle defined *organon* in *De partibus animalium* (the work founding comparative anatomy). The concept designates the relationship of parts to the whole in a living being, grasped not as *pan* (sum) but as *holon* (organic unity). Parts defined by their function for the whole are organs.

##### b. The Soul "Organizes" the Body as a Whole

The definition is completed: "Such [a body that potentially has life] is every one equipped with organs." The concept of *organon*, derived from *ergon*, is inseparably connected with the concept of *energeia*. Only because Aristotle determined the true being of beings as *energeia* could he discover that the parts of living beings are "organs" — as distinct from parts of inanimate things. The distinction between organic and inorganic nature is grounded in the Aristotelian concept of *energeia*.

##### c. Determination of Life Through Constitutive Functions

Picht presents the famous passage from *De partibus animalium* I, 5 (644b 22-645a 36), in which Aristotle explains why the investigation of even the lowliest living beings is philosophically worthy. Even in creatures that give little pleasure to perception, the creative nature (*dēmiourgousē physis*) grants immeasurable joys to those capable of recognizing causes. In all the works of nature, there is nothing accidental but rather immanent structure (*telos*); and the perfected form (*to hou heneka*) has its place in the domain of the beautiful. Aristotle closes with the anecdote of Heraclitus warming himself at the oven and saying to visitors who hesitated: "Enter without fear, for here too there are gods." So too one must approach the investigation of every animal without squeamishness, for in everything there is something natural and beautiful.

## Fifth Part: The Doctrine of Nous / Aristotle's Grounding of Metaphysics

### I. The Doctrine of Nous

#### 1. Retrospect on the Course of the Lecture Thus Far

Picht opens the final part by recapitulating the trajectory of the entire lecture. The definition of the soul as "first entelecheia of a natural body that potentially has life" can now be understood in its full significance: *physis* is the totality of what moves; within this totality, what moves itself has priority, for self-movement is the primordial form of movement — first entelecheia. Since the soul is the first entelecheia, it is the origin of *physis* as such. Everything else in nature is referred back to this origin. In the theological outlook to come, this holds not only of organic nature but of the inorganic as well: the heavenly bodies are, as it were, the organs of God, who moves and shapes the cosmos as the soul moves and shapes the organic body.

But the definition of the soul contains a pointer to the fact that it does not yet grasp the *ousia* of the soul. Aristotle says the soul is the *first* entelecheia — *entelecheia atelēs*, a not-yet-arrived-at-the-goal in-itself-having of the goal. Soul in this definition is interpreted only from the side of *dynamis*, only from the moment of *hylē*. As long as we grasp a being only in its possibility-being, we have not yet determined its *ousia*. We have recognized why soul as origin of movement is the *eidos* of the natural body, but we have not yet recognized what the *eidos* of soul itself is. The *ousia* of soul as such remains undetermined. The *logos* that the entire tradition has taken as the "definition" of the soul is not yet the *horismos* that would exhibit the *ousia* of the origin of living beings.

But Aristotle gives a quite different hint in the opening sentences of the work: the knowledge of the soul contributes greatly to "truth as a whole." Truth is not mentioned in the so-called definition; the definition speaks only of life. The conjecture therefore presses itself upon us that the fulfilled entelecheia of the soul — its pure actuality — emerges in the knowledge of truth as a whole. This would be not only, as Aristotle teaches in his ethics, the highest fulfillment of human existence; in it, the goal would be revealed toward which all self-movement in nature strives as its first entelecheia.

The sections on the "definition" of the soul, on the perceptive and imaginative faculties (II, 1-III, 3), are an explication of the statement that the soul is the origin of living beings; they belong to physics. The section on *nous*, by contrast, treats the soul insofar as its knowledge contributes greatly to truth as a whole. Here the transition from physics to metaphysics and theology is accomplished. Whether Aristotle succeeds in carrying out this transition without a break determines how far the entire enterprise of his philosophy has succeeded.

Picht also recalls the aporematic character of Aristotle's thought, stressing that the later tradition obscured this fundamental feature by restyling Aristotle into the model of dogmatic school-philosophy. Precisely at the transition to the section on *nous*, it is important to remember that Aristotle presents his entire doctrine of the soul as an aporematic design. The aporiai-chapter (I, 1) connects directly with the section on the thinking faculty, for the real difficulty of determining the soul as *ousia* first breaks open with the doctrine of *nous*. Only here do we press into the domain of the fundamental problems that constitute the grounding of the entire European metaphysics.

#### 2. On the Connection Between Nous and Truth

Picht notes the inadequacy of what can be presented within the lecture: a proper interpretation of the doctrine of *nous* would require a full presentation of the Aristotelian doctrine of truth (especially *Metaphysics* IX, 10), of which we possess only a fragmentary sketch. He also points to Heidegger's doctrine of truth as essentially prompted by the Aristotelian teaching. We are still, he says, in the domain of the preliminaries to an adequate understanding of Aristotelian philosophy — but this is no more discouraging than a mathematics student being told after two semesters that he is still in the preliminaries.

#### 3. Nous Praktikos and Nous Theoretikos

Picht develops the distinction between the practical and theoretical *nous* through *De anima* III, 10 (433a 14ff.): "The practical *nous* differs from the theoretical *nous* by its *telos*. All striving is for the sake of something. That for the sake of which striving is, is the origin of the practical *nous*; the outermost *telos* is the origin of *praxis*." *Praxis* is a carrying-out that contains its goal within itself; in its pure form it is the same as life: first entelecheia, whose self-movement consists in striving toward the *telos* immanent in it. In living beings that lack the faculty of *nous*, this striving proceeds without knowledge of the goal. The human being, by contrast, can recognize the organizing principle of the totality of his psycho-physical constitution. The practical *nous* is the form in which the soul's highest possibility consists not in the hidden carrying of the *telos* but in the knowing in-itself-having of the *telos*. It is the principle of life as such.

The theoretical *nous*, by contrast, has as its *telos* truth as a whole. The human being's thinking is universal: every human, simply by being human, is referred out into the breadth of the universe. The goal toward which this capacity must strive is the pure and untroubled contemplation of the truth of the universe as it really is. This goal far exceeds the goal of the practical *nous*.

Yet these two forms are not two different faculties. One and the same *nous* is, depending on the goal toward which it is directed, either theoretical or practical. The *nous* is a unity, but this unity can only be understood when one penetrates the relationship of truth and movement — of truth and self-movement — of truth and life. The resolution of this riddle Aristotle gives only in his theology.

#### 4. The Nous of Anaxagoras

Picht develops at length Aristotle's relationship to Anaxagoras, whose *nous*-doctrine forms the true background of *De anima* III, 4. The Anaxagoras citation at 429a 18 ("unmixed, so that he might rule") refers not to the human thinking faculty but to the divine *nous* as the origin of all cosmic motion. Aristotle's entire approach, including his doctrine of the *logos*, can be traced back to Plato's famous excursus in the *Phaedo* (96Aff.), where Socrates reports his initial enthusiasm for Anaxagoras's teaching that *nous* is the ground of the universe, and his subsequent disappointment that Anaxagoras could not carry out this program. Aristotle set himself the task of executing Anaxagoras's program using the Socratic method, in a way that Socrates would no longer have needed to be disappointed.

The unity and polarity of knowing (truth) and moving corresponds to the unity and polarity of *nous* and *psyche*. Both Anaxagoras and Aristotle distinguish *nous* from *psyche* while treating them as a single *physis*. The fundamental problem of Greek philosophy since Anaxagoras is the relationship of truth and movement. The entire structure of the three books *On the Soul* is determined by this problem. The execution of Aristotle's design hangs on how the relationship of *psyche* and *nous* is to be grasped.

### II. Aristotle's Grounding of Metaphysics

#### 1. The Share of Human Knowledge in the Divine Nous

The dominant position of the Anaxagoras citation in III, 4 proves that the subject is not the individual thinking faculty of the single human being but the divine *nous*. The question of what enables the individual to think and know truth stands from the outset under the perspective of how human knowledge can participate in the truth of God. The predicates of the divine *nous* that here elucidate its knowledge of truth are the same predicates used in *Physics* VIII, 5 (256b 24f.) to characterize God as the unmoved mover. We do not need *Metaphysics* XII to establish the connection between the *nous*-doctrine of *De anima* and metaphysical theology; we encounter the metaphysical theology already in *De anima* itself.

##### a. Dianoia and Hypolēpsis

Aristotle defines the *nous* as the faculty "with which the soul thinks and makes assumptions." *Noein* is always true; *dianoeisthai* (thinking discursively) can be true or false. *Hypolambanein* (making basic assumptions) similarly involves presuppositions that may require correction. Both *dianoia* and *hypolēpsis* presuppose the possibility of noetic knowledge. This is why Aristotle can use both words to characterize the human *nous*, even though the divine *nous* — which truly beholds what is — knows neither *dianoia* nor *hypolēpsis*.

##### b. Knowing the Truth

The human *nous*, though distinct from the divine, must carry within itself a faculty that is analogous to the divine *nous*; otherwise it could not grasp divine truth. The *nous* is not mixed with the body: were it so, knowledge would be subject to the same qualitative changes as the material body. The truth of 2 × 2 = 4 is not affected by the state of the brain that thinks it. Picht cites Leibniz's *Monadology* §17 to the same effect: one can enter the thinking-machine as into a mill, but one will find only pieces pushing against one another — never anything that could explain a perception. The *nous* as such has no bodily organ.

Aristotle continues: "And well do those teach who say the soul is the region of the Ideas — only not the whole soul but the noetic, and not in the fulfilled entelecheia but only in possibility is it the Ideas" (429a 27-29). This Platonic teaching is corrected: the region of the Ideas is not the whole *psyche* but only the noetic part, and not in actuality but only potentially.

#### 2. "The Nous Is the Region of the Ideas"

##### a. Psyche and Nous

Picht emphasizes that for Plato, the Ideas are not representations in consciousness but the real structures of what is. When Plato teaches that the soul is the region of the Ideas, he means that the pure structures, which appear only in veiled form in the sensible cosmos, show themselves unveiled in the soul as they are from themselves.

##### b. The Aristotelian Concept of Eidos

Through the extended passage 429b 10-22, Aristotle distinguishes between the concrete being and its *to ti ēn einai* (its *eidos*). Flesh is a determinate proportion of its physical elements. The perceiving faculty grasps the sensible aspect (warm, cold); but the *nous* grasps the *eidos* — the pure structure that constitutes what the being always already was.

##### c. Aphairesis

Aristotle calls the lifting out of this pure structure *aphairesis* — literally "abstractio." But unlike modern abstraction (which yields a mere mental representation cut off from the being), Aristotelian *aphairesis* grasps the true structure of the being itself. It lifts out (*hebt heraus*), and the process can be repeated: from the geometric line (which is still bound to the continuum), one can lift out the *eidos* of the straight, arriving at the "twoness" — for the straight line is the projection of the number 2 into the continuum of the surface.

##### d. The Mode of Knowledge of Nous

Picht shows that already sensible perception does not take in things as they are in themselves but their *eidē* — their "views." Every act of perception grasps simultaneously the sensible and the noetic aspect of the being. The perceiving faculty lifts the sensible image from the thing just as, in the ring-and-wax simile, one lifts the impression from the signet ring. Analogously, the *nous* is introduced as a non-corporeal faculty — enabled only by the life of the body — capable of grasping noetic "views," i.e., Ideas. The pure and untroubled possibility of grasping such noetic views is what our soul carries within itself as its *nous*.

##### e. Beholding, Being, and Unity in Parmenides

How are we to understand the astonishing Aristotelian propositions: "The soul is in a certain way all beings" (431b 21); "In the case of what is without matter, the beholding and the beheld are the same" (430a 3f.); "Generally, the *nous* in the mode of actuality is the things" (431b 16f.)? These are Aristotelian paraphrases of Parmenides' sentence: "For the same is beholding and being" (28 B 3). Picht devotes an extended development to Parmenides' insight.

The name "the Being" in Parmenides designates not the individual being but *physis* as a whole. You will not find the wholeness of *physis* without the beholding of the divine *nous*. Conversely — and this is what modern thought finds so difficult — without the beholding, being is not. This is because "being" means essentially "manifesting itself." Whatever does not manifest itself at all is nothing. Even modern physics rests on the axiom: what cannot in principle be verified, is not. Being is necessarily manifest-being. But manifest-being necessarily implies the horizon into which being manifests itself. The unity of this horizon is what Parmenides calls *alētheia* — unconcealment, truth. The sentence means: the same, namely truth, is on one side being and on the other side beholding. The essence of the unity that holds the world together is truth — the belonging-together of beholding and being. This is what philosophy since Parmenides calls identity.

#### 3. God Is Pure Nous

The ontological basis of Aristotle's doctrine is thereby clarified. The difference from Parmenides is that Aristotle speaks of beings in the plural, with the Platonic doctrine of Ideas intervening: the truth of being manifests itself in every individual being as it manifests itself, according to Parmenides, in the unity of being as a whole. In each being there is a kernel to which all the predicates belong that Parmenides ascribes to being as a whole: self-identity, unchangeability, self-transparency, pure presence, identity of being, unity, and truth. What a being is *in itself* — purely as a being — in its truth, Plato calls "Idea."

The *nous* in the human being is the faculty of lifting out this unity from the sensible form and grasping it purely. But this pure view has being only insofar as it manifests itself in the matter. The true *topos eidōn* — the region of the Ideas — is therefore the divine *nous* itself. The human *nous* is the faculty of entering this region through the knowledge of the *eidos*, that is, of arriving at identity with the divine *nous*. Hence Aristotle says: "well do those teach who say the soul is the region of the Ideas, only not the whole soul but the noetic, and not in the fulfilled entelecheia but only in possibility is it the Ideas."

Aristotle's ontology is the science "that contemplates being as being and what underlies it in itself as origin." The being as such — the concrete *synolon* — never appears in sensible experience. The knowledge that being is always a concrete whole is a purely noetic knowledge, and only because it is noetic does it grasp the being as *holon*. A doctrine of the universal materiality of what is can never be grounded on sensory experience; materialism, because it claims universality, is in principle only possible as "idealism" — matter presupposes the *nous* for which it manifests itself as matter.

#### 4. The Transition to Theology (De an. III, 5)

Aristotle sketches his solution to the fundamental problem in chapter III, 5 — the half-page whose "obscurity and extreme brevity" has provoked more commentary than any other piece of ancient philosophy. The brevity is justified, for Aristotle here accomplishes the transition, prepared from the first sentence of the work onward, from the doctrine of the soul to theology — from *nous* as the highest faculty of the soul to *nous* as the horizon of truth as a whole.

Picht translates 430a 10-25 and identifies the two forms of *nous* distinguished in this chapter: the *nous pathētikos* (compared to *hylē*) and the *nous poiētikos* (characterized as the causal and productive principle). The *nous poiētikos* is described through those predicates Aristotle had taken from Anaxagoras in III, 4: separated, impassible, unmixed. He is the divine *nous*, and his *ousia* is *energeia* (430a 18). His activity is compared to light: as sunlight brings forth as actual colors what was only potentially color, so the light of the divine *nous* brings forth, for the human soul that possesses receptivity for such views, the pure and imperishable structures of what beings as such are.

The word *poiein* does not mean "making" or "creating" but "bringing to light" what was hidden in possibility. This is the fundamental meaning of *poiēsis* and also of *technē*: not fabrication but disclosure.

The fulfilled entelecheia — the *eidos* of the soul — is the *nous pathētikos* more precisely determined. For everything that is, the fulfilled entelecheia toward which it strives is pure *energeia*, and as pure *energeia* it is *eidos*: the unity grounding the wholeness of the being. For the soul too, defined as first entelecheia, only in the human *nous* does the unity grounding life as a whole emerge. But the human *nous* is initially not *energeia* but *dynamis* — pure possibility, unfilled openness. Only when it receives light from the divine *nous* does it become, in the highest *energeia* of pure intellectual beholding, what it otherwise only potentially is: the region of the Ideas. Insofar as it actually is this, it is in the strictest sense identical with the divine *nous*. The *eidos* of the human soul is therefore the *nous* of God himself. What in the *nous* of the human soul is *hylē* manifests itself in the fact that the *nous pathētikos* is perishable — the human mind cannot hold fast the pure presence of intellectual beholding; it forgets, and typically has its knowledge only as knowledge of possibility.

#### 5. The Self-Revealing of Truth

Picht concludes by returning to the fundamental question of the entire work: How are truth and movement related? The *energeia* of the *nous* — the *noēsis* — has been compared to light. Just as we cannot see colors without seeing light, we cannot grasp noetic truth without grasping the light of the *noēsis* in which it becomes visible. Through this thought, Aristotle succeeded in thinking even the essence of the divine *nous* as pure movement. God is called the "unmoved mover," but "unmoved" means only that in God, because he is pure *energeia*, there is no alteration of becoming or perishing; it does not mean immobility. He is movement, as light — in the Greek conception — is simultaneously eternal presence and pure *energeia*.

That this pure *energeia* grasps itself is the ultimate consequence of the concept of *entelecheia*. The highest form of eternal movement is reached where its immanent essence lies open before itself and in this openness simultaneously manifests and grasps itself. In this pure beholding of pure beholding, the highest possible form of *praxis* is manifest: a carrying-out that carries the goal of carrying-out within itself. Such a carrying-out Aristotle calls "life." The *noēsis noeseōs* is the life of life in actuality. The human being, through the faculty of the *nous pathētikos*, has as his highest possibility the capacity to recognize the truth of the entelecheia that, as first entelecheia, remains hidden in the soul. In the moment of such recognition, even human thinking is *noēsis noeseōs* — the highest form of life. It bears in Aristotle the name *eudaimonia*.
