# Commentary on Edmund Husserl, *Analyses Concerning Passive and Active Synthesis: Lectures on Transcendental Logic*

## Translator's Introduction

### Section 1: The Historical and Conceptual Context

Steinbock opens by characterizing the *Analyses Concerning Passive and Active Synthesis* as Husserl's investigation into the origin of truth. He immediately signals several of the work's key achievements: an early form of historical reflection and identification of a "crisis" (anticipating the *Crisis* of the 1930s), the description of primordial dimensions of experience, the genealogy of judgment (tracing higher cognitive operations back to their pre-predicative origins), and the deployment of a new genetic phenomenological method. Steinbock notes that while the volume is largely a translation of Husserliana XI (*Analysen zur passiven Synthesis*), the English edition also includes essential additions to the main lecture text, supplementary materials, and a partial reorganization of the material. This signals from the outset that the English edition is not merely a translation but a scholarly reconstruction that aims to restore the lectures to their original comprehensive scope.

Steinbock describes the structure of his Introduction, dividing it into four sections. Section 1 will situate the work historically and conceptually, discuss its composition and revised title, and provide a basic overview. Section 2 will situate the *Analyses* in the context of genetic phenomenology, since this methodological approach is what makes the descriptions in the lectures possible. Section 3 will elaborate on the novel and significant themes—passivity, affective allure, association, motivation, the unconscious. Section 4 will cover editorial notes on the translation and acknowledgements. He mentions that rather than reserving a separate section for the translation of key terms, he integrates terminological clarification into the exposition of sections 2 and 3, and sometimes into footnotes. This is a methodological choice that reflects the fact that in Husserlian phenomenology, terminological decisions are themselves philosophically substantive.

The lectures were offered three times: Winter Semester 1920/21, Summer Semester 1923, and Winter Semester 1925/26. Steinbock situates them as virtually contemporaneous with Husserl's writings on intersubjectivity and individuation (1921–1927), his reflections on the reduction in *Erste Philosophie* (1923/24), and his considerations of cultural crisis and renewal in the *Kaizo* articles (1922–24). The *Analyses* thus occupy both an historical and a conceptual "middle point" in Husserl's corpus. This is significant because it means the lectures were produced during a period of intense methodological and thematic ferment—Husserl was simultaneously rethinking the reduction, developing intersubjective phenomenology, and confronting the cultural-philosophical dimensions of his project.

Steinbock specifies the historical coordinates more precisely. The *Analyses* come twenty years after the *Logical Investigations* (1900/01), fifteen years after the first time-consciousness lectures (1905), and nearly ten years after *Ideas I* (1913). They precede *Formal and Transcendental Logic* and *Cartesian Meditations* (both 1929) by several years, and anticipate the *Crisis* (1934–37) by more than a decade. This situates the lectures squarely between Husserl's earlier static-constitutional work and his later generative-historical phenomenology.

While the major insights will be explained below, Steinbock offers a provisional characterization: the lectures also occupy a conceptual center point. As expressive of Husserl's genetic method, they surpass both the Cartesian static analysis of the *Ideas* and the formalism of the early time-consciousness lectures, and they anticipate Husserl's generative investigations into intersubjectivity, history, and the lifeworld by initiating a regressive style of inquiry into origins that becomes the hallmark of the *Crisis*. The key distinction here is tripartite: static phenomenology (characteristic of *Ideas I*), genetic phenomenology (the method of the *Analyses*), and generative phenomenology (the later investigations into homeworld/alienworld, birth/death, history). The *Analyses* mark the explicit emergence of the genetic dimension.

Steinbock reports on Husserl's fame at the time of the lectures, citing the registrar's lists (*Quästurakten*) at Albert-Ludwigs-Universität Freiburg. Husserl had 176 attendees in 1920/21 (under the title "Logik"), 133 in 1923 ("Ausgewählte phänomenologische Probleme"), and 65 in 1925/26 ("Grundprobleme der Logik"). The declining enrollment numbers are not commented upon, but the lists of attendees include many subsequently famous figures: Alfred Adler, Oskar Becker, Max Horkheimer, Marvin Farber, Ludwig Landgrebe, Hasime Tanabe, and Eugen Fink. This indicates that the lectures were a major intellectual event in 1920s Freiburg and that their influence ramified through several distinct philosophical traditions.

### 1. Passive Synthesis and Transcendental Logic

Steinbock poses the guiding question of this subsection: How did a lecture series concerned with the general problem of logic come to be known worldwide as the "passive synthesis" lectures? He identifies at least two reasons—one editorial (a), one philosophical (b)—and will also explain the composition of the English edition and its revised title.

The first, editorial reason (A): the title was assigned by Margot Fleischer, editor of Husserliana XI, who called the volume *Analysen zur passiven Synthesis* (1966). Husserl's own titles for the lectures—"Logic," "Selected Phenomenological Problems," "Fundamental Problems in Logic"—were too broad for the collection Fleischer assembled. The title "Transcendental Logic" (which Husserl had written on the manuscript folders) was still too imprecise. Fleischer wanted to capture Husserl's own characterization of the investigations as *Urkonstitutionen* (primordial modes of constitution). She considered the title "Transcendental Aesthetic" but rejected it because of Kantian associations that would mislead the reader. She settled on "passive synthesis," a phrase that occurs at least half a dozen times in the text and that has proved well-suited to the material.

The second, philosophical reason (B): the title is not the sole reason for the lectures' acclaim. While passive synthesis is fundamental and occupies a large portion of the investigations, the broader context is transcendental logic. Steinbock then explains this context. Husserl's *Formal and Transcendental Logic* (1929) was conceived as an "Introduction" to phenomenology, proceeding from the natural attitude of the mathematician and logician. Formal logic—understood both as the apophantic science of propositions and deductive relations and as the formal ontology of individual objects—cannot be self-sufficient; it requires an investigation into the subjective accomplishments that constitute mathematical and logical truths, i.e., a "transcendental logic." But even transcendental logic demands a deeper founding: a critique of logical reasoning must understand how a streaming egoic life of consciousness can be constituted as a true being, and must appeal to a theory of experience and actuality that founds active cognition and its ideal objects. This establishes the architectonic: formal logic → transcendental logic → transcendental aesthetic (the investigation of passive synthesis as the foundation for active logical operations).

Steinbock describes a peculiar but typical phenomenological "zig-zag" movement. Although *Formal and Transcendental Logic* was intended as another "introduction" to phenomenology and followed the *Analyses* chronologically, it can itself be read as an introduction to the project of the *Analyses*. This is because the regressive movement from formal logic to its transcendental conditions leads ultimately to the passive sphere that the *Analyses* investigate. The temporal order of publication inverts the order of founding.

Steinbock explains the content of Husserl's actual "Introduction" to the 1920/21 lectures (published as Part 1 of the English edition). Husserl traces the term "logic" back to Plato, to the Greek roots in *logos*, and to the more original *legein* as "gathering together" and "expounding upon." In logic Husserl detects a vocation of the critical justification of reason—logic as the science of all sciences. As a radical and universal a priori theory of science, logic should not be an axiomatic-formalistic deductive system abstracted from existing sciences. Intrinsic to all factual sciences is an animating teleological orientation: even if we never encounter this teleological idea as such, it functions guidingly—even implicitly—when we practice science. If the sciences today treat their objects in a detached and fragmented manner, this expresses a detachment from "the aim, sense, and possibility of genuine science." They have lost the sense of their own orientation.

Despite this self-forgetfulness, a phenomenological investigation will not simply discard the sciences in their current cultural forms. As scions of the instituting idea of logic, the special sciences still harbor their internal sense even in their self-forgetfulness. By examining the science of logic as handed down, we can gain a "leading clue" to logic's vocation of critical self-justification and as the universal theory of principles and norms of all sciences. This passage is important because it establishes Husserl's characteristic method of "leading clues" (*Leitfäden*): one does not simply bypass existing science but interrogates it for the teleological sense it implicitly carries.

Steinbock describes the "tragedy" Husserl laments: the sciences have inverted the original relation between logic and science. Three consequences follow: (1) the sciences have made themselves autonomous; (2) in this self-sufficiency they have become splintered; (3) logic has been reduced to a sub-discipline, a pragmatic technology borrowing from mathematics, brushed aside with scorn. Through this inversion, the sciences have lost their internal sense and landed in a "self-forgetfulness of scientific objectivism." Husserl observes that logic lost its historical vocation and remained far behind in its development. The paradox is that the autonomy of the sciences from logic produces only a putative autonomy—one that worsens their inability to be self-sufficient, since they are ignorant of their own sense and without foundation. This prefigures the *Crisis* diagnosis of the "crisis of European sciences."

For Husserl, this rupture, this constitutive abnormality, amounts to a crisis in the "spiritual common good of humanity." But if we want to be more than professionals and specialists—if we want to take ourselves as human beings "in the full and highest sense"—we are called to raise ourselves above the self-forgetfulness of the theoretician who lives in his accomplishments without having a thematic view of the accomplishing life itself. Steinbock notes the resonance with the *Kaizo* articles (1922–24), which are concerned with the crisis of human culture and its "renewal" as an ethical humanity, and which also presuppose a genetic method.

Phenomenology as transcendental philosophy wants to recover the philosophical spirit of logic, and the way proposed in these lectures is a genetic one. By clarifying origins—not as static, but as originating—we can recover logic's lost sense. Only a transcendental logic can be an ultimate theory of science, for it treats objects of thought as accomplishments of thinking. Transcendental phenomenology makes this possible because it inquires back from ready-made propositions and theories to the thinking life in which they are accomplished; it goes back more deeply to the experiencing life in which objects are pregiven; and most radically, it understands how the life of consciousness itself can be constituted as a "true being," as an ideal correlate of possible verifications.

To undertake transcendental logic, "tremendous transcendental-phenomenological preliminary work must be accomplished." This entails tracing the accomplishments of thinking to their genetic origins in passive, pre-cognitive syntheses. Husserl incorporates a regressive, archeological movement from active cognitive dimensions to passive kinaesthetic ones. It is in this sense that *Formal and Transcendental Logic* becomes an introduction to and preparation for the *Analyses*. But this regressive movement is also preparatory for the inverse, ascending direction the *Analyses* themselves will take.

Once we have regressed to the origins of constituting life, we describe this life "by beginning from below and ascending upward" to show how genuine thinking emerges, how it is motivated, and how it is built up from the most basic structures of consciousness. The *Analyses* undertake a "transcendental-phenomenological aesthetic" as founding for a transcendental-phenomenological logic, investigating the systematic connections of passive sense formation. Only from the sphere of passivity can we grasp the most fundamental shortcoming in the foundation of traditional logic, one concerning the validity of logical norms, the principle of contradiction and the law of the excluded middle. A genetic method allows us to elucidate the dynamic formations of sense in the passive sphere as foundational for logic. Hence, "paradoxically," a critique of logical reason that departs from formal and transcendental logic cannot be limited to the sphere of logic—it demands a transcendental aesthetic.

The transcendental aesthetic as preliminary work entails not just recovering the foundation for active syntheses and cognitive operations, but describing the passive sphere of experience in its own integrity—its own essential laws, its contributions to the constitution of evidence, and the modalizations of evidence peculiar to it. Concretely, this means bracketing all judicative knowing, determinative and predicative thought, and focusing on the occurrences of apperception, the objects of possible perception with their sense-forms of time and spatiality, and investigating how sense unities are constituted through associative syntheses. It will require investigations into sensibility as the continual constitution of space and time through self-temporalization in time-consciousness and lived-bodily kinaesthesis.

Steinbock notes that a fully developed transcendental aesthetic would extend to all features of space-time constitution, including the generative analysis of space and time in terms of earth-ground and world-horizon—investigating lifeworlds in terms of their normative significance as "home" and "alien." The *Analyses* from the 1920s did not go this far; they stay on the level of genetic phenomenology, within the span of individual facticity or the intragenerational constitution of community. This remark is crucial for locating the *Analyses* within Steinbock's own tripartite framework (static–genetic–generative): the lectures operate at the genetic level but open toward the generative dimension that Husserl would explore later.

On the one hand, the genetic methodological movement provides further philosophical justification for the lectures' fame as the "passive synthesis" lectures. But the explication of passive synthesis does not complete the *Analyses*, since a transcendental aesthetic must ascend to a transcendental logic. The rubric of passive synthesis, though accurate, misses the broader context. This is especially important given the inclusion of new manuscript materials concerning active synthesis and motivation for formal ontologies. In addition to Husserl's actual "Introduction" (Part 1) and the material on active synthesis (Part 3), the title has been modified in consultation with the Husserl-Archives in Leuven to reflect this broader framework: *Analyses Concerning Passive and Active Synthesis: Lectures on Transcendental Logic*.

### 2. The Composition of the Analyses

Husserliana XI is not the complete lecture series but a portion, supplemented by appendices and essays. The English edition includes all of Hua XI plus four new manuscript texts—two that complete the lecture series (Parts 1 and 3 of the Main Text) and two that complement the supplementary materials.

Steinbock describes the Main Texts. The first new text is Husserl's actual "Introduction" (Part 1: "Preliminary Considerations of a Transcendental Logic"), which sets up the framework for the genetic investigations and shows the necessity of beginning with the associative laws of passive synthesis. This text from 1920/21 (manuscript F I 37) was published only in 1974 as an appendix to the Husserliana edition of *Formale und transzendentale Logik*. Part 2 represents the main text of Hua XI and stems from manuscripts F I 37 and F I 38. Steinbock gives it the subtitle "Passive Synthesis: Toward a Transcendental Aesthetic." Here Husserl discusses evidence, modalization, his phenomenology of association, and the roles of affection and attention for the constitution of sense and for the genesis of a phenomenological in-itself.

The second new addition, Part 3 ("Active Syntheses: Toward a Transcendental, Genetic Logic"), was recently published as *Aktive Synthesen* (Husserliana XXXI, edited by Roland Breeur, 2000). This manuscript from F I 39 originally belonged to the same lecture series. The fact that Husserl continued to rework and integrate this Part into his lecture pagination supports treating the entire lecture series as an integral whole.

Steinbock summarizes the arc: Part 2 traces the constitution of sense through passive associative syntheses up to the constitution of the object as such through remembering and expectation. Part 3 picks up on the transition from passive to active spheres and describes various levels of "objectivation"—the movement from the perceptual to the judicative, from the pre-predicative to the predicative, culminating in the transcendental grounding of formal logic. Together, the three Parts give the most complete presentation of Husserl's lecture series to date.

The Supplementary Texts are divided into four sections. Section 1 reproduces the earliest original version of the lectures from 1920/21 (Husserl "unfortunately reworked" them in 1923 and "partly improved, partly spoiled" certain sections in 1925/26). Passages in italics were left intact and reappropriated in later lectures. Section 2 contains all appendices from both Fleischer's and Breeur's editions. Section 3 presents two supplementary essays on perception, time-consciousness, and objective sense. Section 4 supplies texts on phenomenological method, particularly the relation between static and genetic phenomenology. Steinbock departs from the German edition by placing these methodological texts at the very end, because they deserve their own prominence—genetic method is essential to the entire undertaking and makes the *Analyses* possible.

### Section 2: Genesis and Genetic Phenomenological Method

Steinbock warns against characterizing phenomenological method merely as a way of circumscribing modes of givenness. The phenomenal field can itself overstep the bounds of a presupposed methodological orientation, demanding the formulation of a new methodology. This is exactly the situation Husserl found himself in by 1921, when he was led to formulate explicitly the difference between static and genetic phenomenological methods.

Husserl's writings on static and genetic method show the distinctive traits of each and how they are organized through "leading clues." Husserl was not the first to distinguish static and genetic elements—he himself refers to the difference in terms Dilthey used for psychology: "descriptive" (*beschreibende*) and "explanatory" (*erklärende*). Brentano's distinction between descriptive and genetic-physiological psychology also lurks in the background. But these precedents do not mitigate Husserl's originality: because he had described genetic matters exceeding the scope of static constitution (apperception, normality/abnormality, kinaesthesis, association—all under the title of "primordial constitution"), Husserl was provoked by the matters themselves to catch up reflectively with his own descriptions. The distinction between static and genetic methods is internal to the movement of phenomenology itself.

Steinbock clarifies what "static" means in Husserl. It involves two things: (1) a constitutive approach concerned with how something is given or modes of givenness, and (2) a concern with essential structures. A static method can address both phenomenological (constitutive) and ontological (essential) dimensions. Thus it can interrogate the interplay of intention and fulfillment, noetic qualities, noematic features, as well as structural or essential possibilities (formal/material essences, typicalities, regions). Starting from static method betrayed a two-fold methodological prejudice: it was assumed better to begin with constitutive questions rather than taking the being of things for granted, and better to proceed with something at rest rather than in process—to start with the "simple" and advance to the "complex." Husserl thus granted methodological priority to constitutional problems that did not broach temporal genesis.

Husserl understands three variations of genesis: (1) genesis within the purely active sphere (rational acts); (2) genesis between active and passive spheres (tracing the origins of activity in passivity); (3) "primordial constitution" as a phenomenology of passive experience, including apperception, motivation, affection, association, kinaesthesis. All three are ultimately bound to the analysis of the genesis of the monad.

That Husserl began with static structures does not mean genesis was absent from his horizon. To prefer stasis, even in the form of ignorance, is already to acknowledge genesis for later work—implicitly rooting stasis in genesis. This is why Husserl provocatively asks whether one could even undertake a static phenomenology with the genetic dimension fully suspended. He thought the best way to handle more complex matters (self-temporalization, cultural communities, historicity—"generativity") was to prepare with static investigations.

Yet after explicitly tackling genesis, Husserl retroactively understood genesis to be not more complex than stasis, but rather more concrete and more fundamental. Static matters were now seen as more abstract, not simpler. This inversion was only discerned after arriving at genesis through the leading clue of stasis—even though genesis cannot be derived from stasis. Genesis is more fundamental than stasis, though pedagogically stasis guided us to it. This is a crucial insight for understanding the entire architectonic: the order of discovery (from static to genetic) inverts the order of founding (genetic is more fundamental than static).

Ontological questions function as "leading clues" to constitutive questions. Husserl was initially wary of genesis (in the *Logical Investigations*, empirical psychology imputed to ideal objects a subjective genesis instead of taking logical entities as self-given). But after 1915 he confronted genesis forcefully. Earlier themes—motivation, association (*LI*), kinetic syntheses of perception and kinaesthesis (*Thing and Space*, 1907)—were still too implicit and abstract. Even the time-consciousness lectures are not full-fledged genetic analysis because they are "too formal": "Mere form is admittedly an abstraction, and thus from the very beginning the analysis of the intentionality of time-consciousness and its accomplishment is an analysis that works on [the level of] abstractions." Remaining solely on the level of temporal modes (impression, retention, protention) is too formal. The problems of association and affection, and the individuation of the monad, are what bring genesis into play.

Attending to constitution is not automatically attending to genesis; genesis is precisely the genesis of constitution and operates as genesis in a monad. Genetic phenomenology concerns monadic individuation and its genesis. The monad is a process of becoming in one unique time with one unique ego. It exists as having been and as transcending toward futural becoming. Everything is interrelated in the dynamic unity of the monad. The monad is a "living unity" capable of having "unconscious" dispositions. Habitualities (precipitations of acts) recede from the living present and can become sedimented to the null-point of active vivacity; yet there is a dynamic interplay between act and affection: the habitual character of the monad can affectively provoke sense, prefiguring a perceptual or judicative world from the density of personal character. This density, by which the monad retains its identity passively, points to the monad's concrete individuation as a unique "unity of its living becoming."

Phenomenology of genesis is the phenomenology of primordial becoming in time—the genesis of one shape of consciousness emerging from another, acquiring temporal opacity. It is the phenomenology of what Husserl calls "facticity." In the important Appendix 8, Husserl describes the temporality of the individual within a genetic register: transcendental subjectivity neither is born nor dies. This "immortality" rests on two things: first, as constitutive of time (the source of temporalization), transcendental subjectivity cannot be contained within time; second, the investigation is limited by genetic parameters—genetic phenomenology can account for how remembering potentially spans constitutive gaps like sleep and fainting, but cannot cover more radical breaks like birth and death.

Only when phenomenology is broadened to a generative dimension do birth and death become integrated into constitutive world features—because now one investigates the individual being constituted within generative homeworlds and alienworlds, admitting of transcendental successors and progenitors. Within genetic phenomenology, however, it is correct to insist on the immortality of transcendental subjectivity. Steinbock also notes that Husserl revises his earlier view (from the Third *Logical Investigation* and *Ideas I* §15) that the concrete is independent: in fact, what is concrete should be regarded as non-independent, and only abstraction views "phases" as concrete and independent. This holds for temporal phases, living presents, and even individuated monads in relation to intermonadic community.

Simply naming two different methodological dimensions is not sufficient—the very formulation of static and genetic methods itself demands a genetic description. Expressing the differences between them as a relation of "leading clue" produces a ripple effect within transcendental method. First, one now moves regressively from the natural attitude and essential structures to constitutive matters, taking the "natural concept of the world" (i.e., the lifeworld) as a leading clue. Static phenomenology is now "the phenomenology of leading clues, the phenomenology of the constitution of leading types of objects." Second, the entire static method becomes a leading clue to genetic method—genetic phenomenology works from the results of static constitutive phenomenology and follows the histories of the constitution of objects and the monad itself. Third, this produces a reversal: genesis now orders the investigation into static constitution and structure, and one may need to revise previous static results from the perspective of genesis.

Steinbock characterizes these essays on method as giving a privileged glimpse into a pivotal moment in phenomenology. But the formulation of static and genetic methods would not be the ultimate story for phenomenology. It does provide an opening for the novel themes that animate the *Analyses*.

### Section 3: Novel Themes in the Analyses

Husserl's writings were mostly not intended for publication; they have the character of experimental investigations with repetitions, sharp transitions, laconic phrasing, and incomplete sentences. Because so little was published in his lifetime and because of editing difficulties, we labored under a myopic view of Husserl as merely a philosopher of consciousness, an egological solipsist, or a transcendental idealist detached from life. But with more publications and translations, we have gained a broader view: thousands of pages on intersubjectivity (not just ego–alter ego but homeworlds and alienworlds), the lived-body and kinaesthesis, normality and abnormality, spatiality and temporality, ethics, God, aesthetics.

The *Analyses* are privileged not merely because they enact a genetic method, but because they introduce genetic themes. What we are privy to are extremely intricate analyses of intentionality, evidence, and modalization, integrated into a genetic phenomenology of association that unfolds in both passive and active spheres. Steinbock will address themes under two headings: (A) passivity and passive synthesis, (B) affection and association.

### A. Passivity and Passive Synthesis

Passivity designates a dimension of experience that regressive inquiry unearths, serving as a point of departure for explicating motivations leading "up" to cognitive activity. More particularly, passivity is the realm in which, through fundamental laws of association, affective forces spur egoic attentiveness, enabling acts of remembering and expectation that constitute objects as in-themselves-for-us. But Husserl overburdens the term with multivalent, often cryptic significance. Steinbock discerns five interrelated meanings.

(1) Passivity as a lawful, fundamental regularity of sense-constitution—a mode of sense-genesis ("passive genesis"). Since there is also genesis peculiar to activity, passive genesis is "primordial genesis" or "primordial constitution." Noetically, this regularity is expressed as present perception passing into retentional lingering, linking up with previous retentions and motivating protentions. Time-consciousness provides the primordial form of the unity and identity of an object, and the forms of connection (coexistence and succession). Noematically, genesis is articulated in terms of the concordance or discordance of appearances—how appearances synthetically form unities of identity or fields of difference. When an appearance does not fulfill what was prefigured, the sense is "crossed-out" rather than fulfilled: this is "passive modalization." This modalization also radiates back into retentional phases through "retroactive crossing out." Evidence, on the other hand, is seeing the self of the object as meant in fulfilling synthesis. "Confirmation" is a passive synthetic function of corroboration; "verification" belongs to the active sphere and requires cognitive interventions.

(2) Passivity as a sphere in which the ego is not active. Passive syntheses are productions of sense formed through associative connections prior to higher activities. But passivity does not mean no ego is present—only that the ego is not actively participating. This leads Husserl to the paradoxical formulation of "active passivity": a constitution and acquisition of sense that is not nothing, is somehow "subjective" (there is intentionality of drive, tendencies of desire), but does not stem from an activity proceeding from the ego. Husserl appeals to Kant's "productive synthesis" in the A-edition of the *Critique of Pure Reason*, where imagination is independent of the Understanding. The expression "passive synthesis" is oxymoronic from a Kantian perspective, but Husserl's analyses challenge the schism between sensibility and understanding: if truth is not alien to sensibility, then passive syntheses have epistemic import, and a transcendental aesthetic cannot be foreign to truth, evidence, and their modalizations.

(3) Passivity determined *ex negativo*: active processes include judgment, predication, reflection, and linguistic acts. Passivity is thus basically equivalent to perceptual, pre-predicative, pre-reflective, and pre-linguistic experience, with an implicit teleological orientation to reason. Since space-time constitution implies bodily predispositions, passivity also suggests a realm of bodily habits once actively acquired but now sedimented. Husserl understands passive experience as "aesthetic" in the Greek sense of *aesthesis*, and as constituted through the kinaesthetic character of experience. Unlike Kant, this does not mean pre-meaningful sensibility is apprehended by the understanding; rather, the origin of meaning must be sought in passive intentionality, and *aesthesis* (passivity) is foundational for *logos* (activity)—hence a transcendental aesthetic prepares the work for a transcendental logic.

(4) Passivity as the sphere of pregivenness (*Vorgegebenheit*) and objectlike formations (*Gegenständlichkeiten*). It is no coincidence that Husserl's use of "pregivenness" comes into full bloom around the time of the *Analyses*. In the genetic explication of passivity, where objects are not yet constituted by an active ego, Husserl must speak of a mode of self-presentation that is not yet the givenness of an object in the genuine sense. Something is pregiven insofar as it exercises an affective allure without being grasped egoically. Such a "something" is an *Gegenständlichkeit* (objectlike formation)—something exhibiting the basic structure of an object but more "elementary" than an object in the full sense. Something is "given" insofar as the ego yields to the allure, turns attentively, and lays hold of it in egoic acts. For an object to become an "object" in the proper sense, it must be constituted with the intervention of rememberings.

(5) Passivity as the basis for activity. The emphasis on passivity arises about the same time as that of genesis, because inquiry into genesis implies the emergence of activity from passivity. Husserl attempts to give an account of the genesis of reason through ascending modes of attentiveness. Passivity is a "founding" level of experience, the "subsoil" of creative life, providing the "basic, essential conditions of the possibility of a subjectivity itself." In sum: passivity indicates a primordial regularity of sense-genesis in which the ego does not participate; it is a pre-reflective dimension of pregivenness of objectlike formations, founding for activity. Discerning this sphere allows Husserl to describe the motivation from passive to active under the rubric of fundamental laws of association.

### B. Affection and Association

In the ordinary attitude, the whole object seems already there, ready-made. A phenomenological shift shows this is a pretension in two ways: first, the thing gives itself incompletely through perspectives; second, the in-itself is only constituted as in-itself-for-us. Husserl's analyses of affection attempt to describe the fundamental regularities in the constitution of sense through passive syntheses of association that show how something can present it-self-to-us as in-itself.

From at least 1905 to the end of his life, Husserl devoted thousands of pages to time-consciousness, which describes the most general and basic syntheses connecting objectlike structures according to coexistence and succession. But merely providing these forms of connection is still formal and abstract. Time-consciousness is only time-consciousness when viewed integrally with deeper constitutive structures. If we want to investigate what gives unity to a particular concrete object, "the analysis of time alone cannot tell us, for it abstracts precisely from content." We require a phenomenology of association and an inquiry into "primordial phenomena": similarity, uniformity, concretion, fusion (homogeneity), contrast, discretion, prominence (heterogeneity), and gradation. Central to these is the phenomenon of affection.

(1) *Affective Allure*. Steinbock explains the term *Reiz*, which he translates as "allure." The term gained currency in 18th- and 19th-century medicine and physiology, referring to muscle contractions or nerve stimulation, and was imported into German psychology as a naturalistic determination or cause. Wundt and Fechner reduced normality/pathology to quantitative variations of intensity. Husserl gave *Reiz* a "fundamentally new sense" by bringing it into the motivational relation between the lived-body and intentional objectlike formations, and implicating it in apperception and associative connection. Since the draw is motivational and not causal, Steinbock translates *Reiz* as "allure" rather than "stimulus" or "excitation." Affection is thus an "enticement to be" on the part of the object, a motivational solicitation or pull to attentiveness—not a contextless force.

(2) *Motivation*. Motivation was explained in *Ideas II* in contrast to causal law: it is a "because-thus" relation of efficacy peculiar to the personalistic attitude. In the *Analyses* it is deepened in three ways: (A) motivation pertains to both passive and active spheres and the relationship between them; (B) motivation is described in the context of modalizations of being and belief; (C) motivation is brought into the framework of affection. Regarding (B), among possible modalizations, *possibility* stands out—Husserl distinguishes open possibilities (indeterminate scope of determinations) and enticing possibilities (exercising a demand for a particular determination). Some possibilities have more "weight" than others; motivation is clarified as a demand on the part of the object that entices its constitution in varying degrees. Regarding (C), the notion of motivation, interpreted as enticing possibilities, is articulated in the context of affection—Husserl speaks of the demand as an "affective force" that is "in relief."

(3) *Affective Prominence*. While Gestalt psychology showed that perception requires a figure-ground structure, Husserl's genetic inquiry goes further: it asks whether sense-unities are formed prior to or only along with affective force. Because Husserl focuses on the emergence of prominence in the genesis of passivity—on the becoming of sense as it is in the process of becoming determinate—his analyses lead to the conclusion that something coming into relief is always already an affective relief. Prominence is always affective prominence. But in the living present, many things exercise affections that rival for attention. Even though affective force exists, it need not draw active attention; it may only be "perceived" in passive attention as it "knocks at the antechamber of the ego." This is why Husserl rejects an all-or-nothing account of affection and analyzes the gradation of affective forces.

(4) *The Unconscious and Repression*. A completely undifferentiated field of affective forces is possible only after something has exerted affective force in the living present, such that the zero of affective force occurs as a limit (*limes*) in the retentional past. This dormancy corresponds to a zero-point of affective force that Husserl terms "the unconscious." For Husserl (unlike Freud), the unconscious is the nil of the vivacity of consciousness—not a nothing, but a nothing of affective force that has arisen from original constitution. Objectlike formations in the unconscious are no longer affective but can be reawakened through associative syntheses. "Repression" for Husserl means the affective eclipse of one thing by another within a field—not based on the present/past distinction but on affective force: something represses another because it exercises stronger affective force in context. There are still affective forces in passivity that are not nil, as well as modes of attention, even if they have not surpassed the threshold of active awakening.

(5) *Self-Givenness and the Object It-Self*. The insight that prominence is always affective prominence confirms that intentionality is a dynamic cooperative structure, a "constitutive duet." Husserl attributes ipseity (*Selbst*) not only to the subject but also to the object: the object has its own density and otherness that both solicits and evades us. Steinbock translates *Selbstgegebenheit* as "self-givenness," where "self" refers to the ipseity of the object in the intentional relation. The object it-self is never experienced neutrally; it exercises affectively significant allure on the perceiver to be constituted as such. The object in-itself-for-us is the normatively significant "optimal"—the genetic concept of the optimal replaces or deepens the static notion of the noematic core from *Ideas I*. The thing itself is the optimal or the system of optima, experienced as "saturated" for a perceiver in the lifeworld context.

(6) *Association, Remembering, and the Object*. The self of the object is constituted when the objectlike formation emerges from indeterminacy into prominence through affective allure. One motivation for the Division on association is prefigured in Division 2: the investigation into the origin of truth and the role of remembering as a source of definitiveness in constituting the identical object. Although association includes many passive syntheses (pairing, similarity, fusion, contrast), Husserl favors the affective awakening as a specifically reproductive temporal act—remembering. Steinbock notes Husserl's contribution: he took association out of both objective and subjective explanatory frameworks by submitting it to phenomenological clarification. Association designates an a priori essential lawful regularity of genetic passive constitution, irreducible to habit or mental processes. It brings associative syntheses into an intentional and motivational (not causal) nexus. Association is not juxtaposition of objective facts but an internal referring. Husserl's phenomenology of association also challenges the form/content dualism: since an actual formation of sense presupposes affective force, his earlier hyle/morphe schema is implicitly called into question.

Why is remembering privileged? Two reasons. First, through remembering, consciousness becomes an object for itself—time-consciousness becomes its own first transcendence. Consciousness is in a chiasmic relation to itself through remembering. Second, remembering confers temporal density worthy of the name "evidence." While perception makes present originaliter and has indefeasible momentary validity, "what good is it since its validity is only momentary?" The momentary phase is an abstraction. A genuine object is first constituted with the help of remembering: I can only return to something as "the identical" through remembering. Expectation also plays a constitutive role by projecting a norm around which perceptual fulfillment is achieved.

### C. Activity and Active Synthesis

If we lived only in passivity, we would be blind to the sphere of true being. Part 3 describes higher levels of attention and active objectivation. Once having moved from passivity "up" to activity, Husserl reinterprets the "higher" level as "concrete" and understands that passivity and activity are internal differentiations of cognitive experience—we can only access passivity by presupposing and abstracting from activity. The constitution of something as an "object" in the "complete and genuine sense" requires that it be constituted as identical with itself and as the thematic object for the ego in identifying activity.

Active objectivation is understood as a genetic gradation of thematic accomplishments: cognitive interest, explication, determinative identification, conceptualization, as-such judgments. Each level entails new attitudes, new active syntheses, and corresponding alterations in thematization. Steinbock sketches the broad parameters: cognitive interest goes beyond receptivity by taking something as in principle explicable. Examination is a special process in which several things are called to attention in unity, explicating a substrate in its properties (S is determined as a, β, γ). The interest proceeds to newer concentrations; what was content becomes thematic object, entering into partial identity with S. This enrichment is a result of passive synthesis. But this passing from object to object is not yet determinative synthesis—not yet judging.

Judgment is a determining process that actively relates one theme to another (S is a). The proposition is the "What" of the judicative act—the *propositio*. Substantivizing the proposition in "that" statements (that S is a) yields a new level of objectivation: the state-of-affairs (*Sachverhalt*). This founds other determinative judgments: whole and parts, combinations of parts, relationships between connected elements. The universal investigation into the categorial realm constitutes formal logic. When motivated by the association of similarity and trained on the universal, we reach conceptualization: grasping the One or universal as a unique field of interest—not attributing similar features to different objects, but meaning the same color in all instances, subsuming individuals under the *eidos*.

The field of openness constituted with the universal unleashes the "whatsoever" (*Beliebigkeit*). New species and thematic formations emerge. By assuming the as-such into a theme, we have universal and particular conceptual grasping. Operating in the scope of pure concepts yields pure as-such judgments, judgments of laws, and possibility judgments. Free variation reveals lawful regularity. "With this, the realm of universal judging is opened up, the realm of law-giving." These syntheses, occurring in natural experience, are not yet "critical" and must be submitted to transcendental clarification. A transcendental aesthetic provides the foundation for transcendentally formed disciplines and for a transcendental logic as the most universal theory of norms.

## Main Texts

## Part 1: Preliminary Considerations for the Lecture on Transcendental Logic

### 1. Introduction

Husserl opens his lecture series by stating his intention to present fundamental considerations toward a phenomenological logic. He immediately clarifies what he does *not* mean by "logic": neither a subordinated theoretical-normative special science as it is usually understood, nor logic in the sense the modern mathematician has shaped it as a special mathematical discipline. Logic in its full and universal sense—the sense these lectures will have in view—is the science that consciously reappropriates the task enjoined upon it from its historical origin in Platonic dialectic: namely, the task to be a universal theory of science, and simultaneously a theory of science in principle. A "theory of science in principle" signifies a science that is in principle a science of all sciences as such. This opening is programmatic: Husserl is positioning his entire investigation as a retrieval of logic's original philosophical vocation, against its modern reduction to a formal or mathematical subdiscipline.

Logic as a theory of science is the science of the a priori of all sciences as such—the theory of what gives them sense as formations of practical reason, what they must necessarily fulfill to be what they claim to be. As a pure, a priori theory of science, logic wants to bring to light "pure" generalities according to the Socratic-Platonic method. It does not wish to follow empirically the path of the pregiven so-called "sciences" (cultural forms bearing the name "science") and then abstract empirical types from them. Rather, free from all ties to factuality, it wants to bring to complete clarity the teleological idea one always obscurely has in mind when operating from purely theoretical interest. Pursuing the pure possibilities of cognitive life in general, it seeks to illuminate the essential forms of genuine knowledge and science, their essential presuppositions, and the necessary methods leading to them. In all of this lie the necessary norms against which the extent to which a factual science conforms to the idea of science can be measured. The sense of "formal" here consists in nothing other than this: the guiding question is not about a particular science, but about the aim, sense, and possibility of genuine science as such. This establishes the critical-normative function of logic: it is not empirical generalization but eidetic clarification of the conditions of genuine science.

Husserl offers a capsule history. What we call science in the narrow sense today developed from logic—first from the normative guidelines elaborated in Platonic dialectic. The saying that all sciences have arisen from the "maternal ground of philosophy" fits especially well for logic and for the particular sciences. In a broader sense, we give the name "science" to pre-Platonic cosmologies, to cultural formations of other peoples, even to astrologies and alchemies—but at best these are inchoate preliminary stages of science.

Science in the new sense first arises from the Platonic founding of logic—from radical, critical reflection on the essence and eidetic demands of genuine knowledge and science, and from the disclosure of norms according to which science is henceforth consciously directed toward normative justification. This is justification from pure principles, i.e., a logical justification. Science in the new sense no longer operates naively from theoretical interest; it strives to justify every step from principles. The original sense is that logical insight from the pure idea of knowledge precedes and guides factual method a priori—it is not that the fact of some naively arising science would pose as a norm. This establishes the priority of principled insight over factual methodology—a key Husserlian thesis.

Plato's logic arose as a reaction to the universal denial of science by sophistic skepticism. If skepticism denied the possibility of science, Plato had to consider what is in principle possible, and found it critically. Since science as such was in question, one could not presuppose the fact of science—Plato was led down the path of the pure idea. His purely ideal logic shaped pure norms (not read off from factual sciences) with the mission of making factual science possible and guiding it practically. And it did: it helped fashion new mathematics, natural science, etc., whose developments continue as our modern sciences.

However, the original relation between logic and science has become "curiously inverted" in modern times. The sciences made themselves autonomous, cultivating differentiated methods in the spirit of critical self-justification that had become second nature—but doing so at a "higher level" of naivete, dispensing with justification from pure principles by recourse to the pure idea. Logic lost its vocation as torchbearer of method. Even the 17th-century reformation by Galileo, Descartes, and Leibniz was still determined by logical reflection on the nature of genuine natural knowledge. But in the following epoch, the sciences turned into autonomous branches no longer bothering with logic, even brushing it aside with scorn. Logic departed from its own proper sense: instead of pursuing pure essential norms, it copied norms and rules from the factual sciences, especially the natural sciences. This historical diagnosis is foundational for Husserl's entire project—it identifies the disease (the inversion of the logic-science relation) that the lectures will seek to cure.

This inversion signals a deeper tragedy of modern scientific culture than what is usually lamented. The usual complaint is that no one can any longer command all the specialized knowledge. But the shortcoming is more essential and more radical in the literal sense: it concerns the rootedness of the sciences—a rootedness in principle—and their unification from these roots. This shortcoming would remain even if an encyclopedic mnemonic technology could give us command of all established facts.

What is lacking are the centering ideas that would illuminate all thinking in the special sciences and spiritualize their results by relating them to "eternal poles." Lacking is what removes the blinders necessary for specialized work, and the capacity to integrate the sciences into a universal nexus of knowledge understood as necessary in principle. But even more is lacking: the reference back to the phenomenological primordial sources of all knowledge, the deepest founding of all objective sciences arising from the universality of knowing consciousness. A systematic fundamental science providing ultimate understanding of all theory from the originally sense-giving sources of knowledge-accomplishing subjectivity is what is needed. This is one of the most important programmatic passages in the Introduction: it identifies not just disciplinary fragmentation but the absence of transcendental self-understanding as the deepest problem.

If the highest task of knowledge is not only calculating the course of the world but understanding it (citing Lotze), then we must raise ourselves above the self-forgetfulness of the theoretician who, devoted to matters, theories, and methods, knows nothing of the anteriority of his accomplishment and the motivations compelling it—who lives in them but does not thematically view the accomplishing life itself. Understanding comes only through a clarification of principles that descends into the depths of transcendental, phenomenological interiority—an interiority that investigates the sense-giving and accomplishment of reason in its essential necessity, carried out in the interplay of transcendental nexuses of motivation. Only through such clarification will we also understand the true sense of that being science wanted to bring to light as true nature, as the true world of spirit.

Only a transcendental science—directed into the hidden depths of accomplishing cognitive life, and thereby clarified and justified—can be the ultimate science. Only a transcendentally clarified world can be ultimately intelligible; only a transcendental logic can be the ultimate theory of science, the deepest and most universal theory of principles and norms, transforming the positive sciences into clarificatory and intelligible sciences. While the contemporary positive sciences initially fill the novice with enthusiasm, in the end they leave him deeply dissatisfied—provided that he wants to be more than a professional and specialist, that he wants to understand himself as a human being "in the full and highest sense," wants to understand the world, and wants to pose questions of ultimate knowledge and conscience.

We sense this, and are particularly sensitive in "our unfortunate time" to the fact that the sciences lack philosophical spirit—the spirit of ultimate purity and clarity, above all the spirit of phenomenological, transcendental clarity. This is precisely why we lament that "we do not become wiser and better through them, as is certainly their pretension." This is a striking passage: Husserl links the crisis of the sciences to the failure to fulfill their ethical-existential promise—genuine science should make us wiser and better as human beings.

If we seize upon the idea of logic again as broadly as its original intention demands, and animate it with the transcendental spirit awakened in the modern era but not yet arrived at pure self-consciousness, then we must say that what the modern sciences lack is true logic as the mother of their true method: logic that lights the way with a most profound self-knowledge of knowledge and renders all scientific activities intelligible. Such logic does not want to be a mere technology for pragmatic accomplishments called "scientific." It wants to precede all possible sciences as a justifying system of principles of all objective justification, understanding itself through an absolute method. Just how little the sciences are capable of self-sufficiency is seen in the unresolved conflict about the true sense of their foundations. Only transcendental logic allows one to understand that the positive sciences bring about only a relative, one-sided rationality that leaves complete irrationality as its necessary counterpart. Only a comprehensive rational science is science in the highest sense, as ancient philosophy originally wanted to be.

Husserl concludes the Introduction by acknowledging the magnitude and difficulty of the task. He can only give his audience a look into some of the profound levels of this universal logic. The reason he cannot make it thematic in its entire universality is not only the magnitude and difficulty but above all the recognition that "tremendous transcendental-phenomenological preliminary work must be accomplished from the very beginning." Even though historically the outlines of positive sciences and theoretical logic developed first, phenomenological investigations form "what is first in itself"—that from which all fundamental forms of logical structures must proceed according to intelligible motivation. The lectures will be exclusively occupied with such transcendental-logical foundations. This announces the decisive methodological choice: rather than proceeding from formal logic downward, the lectures will begin with the transcendental-phenomenological foundations—the primordial constitutive work that makes logic possible.

### 2. "Thinking" as the Theme of Logic. Speaking, Thinking, What is Thought

Husserl now turns to a philological-phenomenological analysis of the term *logos*. The word has many meanings arising from intelligible modifications of the more original meanings of *legein*: gathering together, expounding upon, then expounding by words, by discourse. In developed language, *logos* sometimes means "word" and "discourse," sometimes what the discourse is about (the matter), and further, the thought put in sentence-form, the spiritual sense of the linguistic proposition—the theorem as something meant by the grammatical expression. In the case of universal words, *logos* means the universal concept belonging to them as sense. This is a characteristic Husserlian move: tracing philosophical concepts to their philological-historical roots in order to excavate sedimented layers of meaning.

Additionally, *logos* refers to the intellectual act itself—the activity of stating, asserting, or other modes of thinking in which sense-content is generated relative to objects or states-of-affairs.

All these meanings assume a specific sense—especially where scientific interests are in play—by the idea of a norm of reason entering the sense. Then *logos* means reason itself as a faculty, but also rational (i.e., evidential) thinking or thought directed toward evident truth. More specifically, *logos* means the faculty of forming legitimate concepts, and even this signifies both the rational formation of concepts and the legitimate concept itself. Even more specifically, the "scientific" element of sense comes to the fore: scientific concept, scientific concept-formation, scientific thinking, or the corresponding intellectual faculty.

Taking this manifold of meanings as a clue to the idea of a science of logos, rich themes are opened for research and normative application. If we concern ourselves with the second and third groups of meanings, the theme of reason as the faculty of correct, evidently justified thinking will lead beyond the question of how acts ground habitual faculties, and will lead to the question of the nature of the "rational" acts of thought in question. But before the specific quality of rationality can be considered, the quality of thinking itself must become a theme, prior to all distinctions of rational and irrational. The sense of *logos* leads chiefly to conceptual thinking and thought. But conceptual thinking does not exhaust thinking in the broadest sense.

Since human thinking is normally carried out linguistically, and all rational operations are practically bound to discourse, and since all critique makes use of language as intersubjective critique, not only acts of thinking and thoughts are at issue but discourse, statements, stated thoughts. This arrives at the first group of meanings of *logos*. The first group can be brought under three headings: speaking, thinking, what is thought. Only human beings have language and reason; only human beings generate cognitive formations in the form of thinking and bring them to linguistic expression. This passage connects the analysis of *logos* to the threefold structure (speaking—thinking—what is thought) that will organize the following sections.

### 3. The Ideality of Linguistic Phenomena

The three headings are still ambiguous. First, regarding speech or language: we distinguish the articulated word spoken at present (a sensuous, acoustical phenomenon) from the word and proposition itself—or string of propositions making up a discourse. It is for good reason that we speak of a "repetition of the same words and sentences." In a treatise or novel, every word is singularly unique and cannot be duplicated by repeated reading. We distinguish the treatise itself (as a grammatical composition of words) from its manifold uttered reproductions, and likewise from its manifold documentations on paper, in print, on parchment, on clay tablets. The one linguistic composition is reproduced a thousand-fold—"the same book" with "the same name," "the same treatise"—and this sameness holds already in a purely linguistic regard.

Language as a system of signs provides subtle and wondrous problems, one being the ideality of language—usually completely overlooked. Language possesses the objectivity of objectlike formations of the spiritual or cultural world, not that of mere physical nature. This is analogous to other spiritual formations: an engraving is distinguished from its thousand reproductions—the engraved image itself is identically ideal, read off from every reproduction. The Kreutzer sonata in distinction to any arbitrary reproduction: the sonata is an ideal unity, its sounds are ideal unities, not physicalistic sounds. Just as the sonata is reproduced in real reproductions, so are its sounds reproduced with every reproduction. What is at issue for all linguistic formations concerns them not only as expressing something but already as concrete unities of the body of language and expressed sense—even with respect to their corporeality (*Leiblichkeit*), which is already a "spiritual corporeality." The word itself, the grammatical sentence itself, is an ideal unity not duplicated with its thousand-fold reproductions. This passage establishes a crucial phenomenological point: linguistic expressions are ideal identities, not reducible to their physical instantiations—a thesis Husserl had argued since the *Logical Investigations* but now recasts in the context of his genetic investigations.

The one who expresses himself lives in the practical intention to articulate this or that view. This must not be understood as if one first formed an opinion and then sought suitable words. In solitary thought, thinking is carried out from the very outset as linguistic. The thought we have in mind and bring to expression is already equivocal, though still incompletely determined. All meaningful speech as the concrete unity of the linguistic body and linguistic sense is a "spiritual formation."

The great problems concerning the clarification of sense and the transcendental constitution of objectivities of the spiritual world are an entire domain unto themselves. It suffices to note that language becomes an issue for logicians only in its ideality: as the identical grammatical word, the identical grammatical proposition over and above actual or possible realizations—analogous to how the theme of the aesthetician is the particular work of art itself, not the ephemeral physical complex. If absolutely faithful reproduction were possible, originals would lose scientific privilege for the aesthetician; they would retain only affective value.

### 4. Thinking as a Sense Constituting Lived-Experience

Husserl now considers the second term: "thinking," whose sense must be extracted from the connection of "language and thinking." In its broadest sense, "thinking" nearly encompasses the entire psychic life: "the human being expresses his psychic life in language." But we must be careful: the human being does not actually express all of psychic life in language. We can provisionally delimit what is expressed by noting that something is meant with every word and relation of words in normally functioning, expressive speech. (Parrots do not really speak; deceptive speech is excluded.) Corresponding to the unity of discourse is a unity of meaning; corresponding to the divisions and forms of discourse are divisions and formations of meaning. This meaning is not external to the words; rather, in speaking we carry out an internal act of meaning (*Meinen*) that melds with the words, animating them. The result is that words incarnate meaning within themselves.

The first and broadest sense of "thinking" encompasses those psychic lived-experiences in which the act of meaning consists—the act in which meaning is constituted for the speaking or comprehending subject. For example, when we pronounce a judgment, we carry out a unity of internally "thoughtful" assertion with the words of the statement. Whatever other psychic accomplishments make the words come about, we attend only to the judicative acts that function as sense-giving, constituting the judicative meaning within themselves. Many psychic lived-experiences remain out of consideration: indicative tendencies, phenomena of pointing-toward what is meant, tendencies relating to the interlocutor. What holds for the assertoric statement holds generally: a wish ("God be with me!"), an order, a question—each involves a lived-experience belonging to the primary function of expression, the function of expressing something. Thinking in this broadest sense means each lived-experience that, during speaking, belongs to the primary expressive function—the lived-experience in which the sense to be expressed is constituted in consciousness: judging, wishing, willing, questioning, supposing.

Husserl retains this broadest concept (while noting it is not the traditional concept determinative of logic) and insists on examining it scientifically. The universality of the coinciding of language and thinking designates two parallel domains: possible expressions and possible senses (possible expressive intended meanings). In their intertwined unity they yield the domain of current, concrete, sense-fulfilled discourse. Every assertion is at once speech and judicative intended meaning; every pronounced wish is at once optative speech and the current wish-meaning. But there is more than a duality: we must distinguish the act of intending from the intended meaning, the act of judging from the judgment, yielding a threefold relation. What Husserl investigates is an important general character of all lived-experiences exercising a sense-giving accomplishment in normal discourse and comprehending listening. This threefold distinction (act of intending—intended meaning—that which is intended) is fundamental for the entire Husserlian framework and connects directly to the noesis-noema-intentional object correlation.

### 5. Sense-Constituting Lived-Experiences as Egoic Acts

All such lived-experiences are not only modes of consciousness in general but egoic acts. Husserl introduces the distinction between wakefulness and deep dreamless sleep. Even if we cannot say anything about the content of torpid consciousness, we can describe the typical essence of the contrast. In sleep there is experiencing taking place, but no perception in the genuine sense, no cognitive theme, no judgment, no emotional interest, no love, hate, desire, or willing. What distinguishes the lived-experiences of wakefulness? Response: psychic life is wakeful insofar as the ego carries out specific egoic functions in the present—I perceive, I turn toward an objectlike formation, I am absorbed in it; I remember; I make comparisons; I am inclined to love or hate; I carry out striving, consider means, decide and act. In all these, the ego is the "peculiar center" of lived-experiencing, the identical pole, the center of actions and passions (including states: I am sad, delighted, happy). "I" here is not empty, but neither does it refer to the corporeal human being or the entire psychic life; it is identified in reflection as the center of life to which perceiving, judging, feeling, willing are related.

The ego is everywhere living in these acts, related through them to the perceptual, judged, or willed object. The ego is not a box containing egoless lived-experiences, not a slate upon which they appear and disappear, not a bundle or flow of consciousness. The ego manifests in each lived-experience of wakefulness as pole, as ego-center, as outward or inward radiating point—yet not as a part or piece. To grasp this point thematically requires a peculiar reflection going in the opposite direction. The structure of the lived-experience, its directional structure going toward what is presented, wished for, etc., points back to the ego as outward radiating point. All such lived-experiences in the unity of a stream manifest the identically same ego: I who perceive am the same I who judges, feels, desires, wants—and only by this identity can I say these are all my acts. This is a "curious polarization of the stream of consciousness." The central ego is only graspable as the subject of its acts; from this, every other concept of the ego (personal, psychophysical) gets its sense.

The wakeful life of an ego contains not only egoic lived-experiences in which the ego emerges as present functional center (*ego cogito* in the Cartesian sense), but also a background of non-wakefulness, constantly and with eternal necessity. When I perceive an object—look at it, grasp it—there is always an unnoticed, ungrasped background. We distinguish the primarily noticed from what is secondarily noticed and from what goes unnoticed. In passing from one object to another, the first is no longer primarily observed but is still held in an attentive and conceptual grasp; it continues to belong to me in a modified way. I am still present as central, wakeful ego. But in contrast, there is a broad lived-experiential field that has not entered into such a relation with the ego. This passage is pivotal for the entire problematic of the *Analyses*: it introduces the foreground/background distinction that will be deepened through the concepts of affection, prominence, and the gradation of attentiveness in the passive sphere.

Continuing the foreground/background analysis, Husserl elaborates: the wakeful ego has a constant, broad horizon of background lived-experiences to which the ego is not present. These may be sensations (like sounds to which the ego is not alert), physical objects appearing in surrounding space without an "I perceive" or "I take note," affects intertwined with background lived-experiences spilling over into a general atmosphere of well-being or malcontentment, and even tendencies or drives that incline the ego without the ego being present. There may also be flashes of insight, imaginings, memories, theoretical insights, or stirrings of the will that the ego does not take up. Only when the ego carries them out do they get the shape of *ego cogito*. The distinction between wakeful egoic life and non-wakeful egoic life is that in the latter, no lived-experience in the specific sense of wakefulness is there at all, and no present ego is there as its subject. This passage is crucial for understanding the later analyses of affection: the background is not a dead zone but a field of potential allures—sensations, affects, drives—that "knock at the door of the ego" without necessarily being admitted.

### 6. Foreground Lived-Experiences and Background Lived-Experiences

Husserl clarifies that every act in the specific sense has the fundamental character of being a consciousness of something—an "intentional lived-experience." Perception is perception of a house; cognition is cognition of something known; in every judgment there is a judged state-of-affairs; in every desiring, something desired. This is the broad concept of intentional lived-experience. But even background lived-experiences are intentional. A universal life of consciousness encompasses both the specific ego-consciousness acts and the background consciousness. Within wakeful life, we constantly have a visual space filled up and present to consciousness. If we take note of a single tree in a landscape, the whole landscape with its multiple objects is given to consciousness as a spatial field. Background objects are objects through the fact that they appear—their lived-experiences have the character of intentional lived-experiences. The egoic act in the specific sense is a special form of carrying out intentional lived-experiences. Husserl notes that he originally introduced the term "act" in the *Logical Investigations* for the broadest concept of intentional lived-experience; now he says "egoic act" or "act in the restrictive sense" for acts with the distinctive form of ego-execution.

In the life of consciousness there is constant transformation of modalities of execution: foreground lived-experiences (egoic acts) lose their form of execution and take on the altered background form, and vice versa. Cognitive acts, acts of pleasure, volitional acts do not simply disappear when we no longer carry them out from the standpoint of the ego; they become background lived-experiences.

Background lived-experiences are modified through and through relative to their corresponding acts, yet they share something essential in common—we still speak of "the same" judgments, wishes, etc. But they are not actually the same. It is not like shoving things in a room into dark corners where they remain unchanged. The moment a background lived-experience becomes present—when the ego becomes an ego carrying out acts through it—it has become completely and essentially transformed. And yet the judging shoved into the background is still judging of this and that; background perception is still perception of the same thing. It belongs to the very essence of a lived-experience that it is a consciousness of the same thing in the transition from one mode of execution to another. This founding of unity and sameness through their intentional content makes intelligible our talk of acts becoming "latent" and "patent." A counter-argument that emerges in the background during a conflict is initially a latent act; its intentionality is a hidden intentionality, until we intervene and actualize it. This analysis of latent and patent intentionality is foundational for the later investigations into awakening and reproductive association: the background is not empty but harbors intentional content that can be reactivated.

### 7. The Interconnection between Expressing and Signifying as the Unity of an Egoic Act

Returning to the clarification of thinking, Husserl notes that whenever we actually speak or participate in discourse by understanding it, this actuality consists in the execution of egoic acts. The speaking person means something in the spoken words, and this act of meaning—this "thinking" belonging to speaking—is an act carried out by the ego. What the ego intends in these acts is what it means in speaking, what the words "express." Further, the lived-experiences in which words themselves are produced for us also have the character of egoic acts. Words as actually spoken do not emerge in a background distant from the ego; as speakers, we generate them and are directed toward them in acts, not in latent intentionality. The synthetic unity of the act of meaning with words belongs to the specific participation of the ego. In verbal consciousness, words have the character of signs with indicative tendencies radiating from them toward what is meant. This intertwining produces the result that expression and what is expressed are not juxtaposed but make up a unity of consciousness in which word and sense are constituted. If we strip away the indicative tendencies, we no longer have words at all—just meaningless sounds like a parrot's "talk."

Even the intentionality unifying word and sense has the character of patent intentionality; the pure ego is present. The ego seizes the word, grasps its indicative tendency, willingly allows itself to be guided by it into the execution of thinking, and oriented by what is thought as meant by the words. But we do not intend the words themselves in this normal functioning. We *can* intend them in other acts—making them our "theme" as grammarians do, carrying out theoretical judgments and discourses about them. Then the difference emerges between words that are our grammatical theme and words we use to express our thoughts about those themes. Both are given to consciousness in essentially different manners: in one case through acts of interest, in the other not. Husserl distinguishes the broadest sense of "interest" (the ego being present in and directed toward something in any act) from the normal concept of interest, which means the peculiar mode of carrying out acts whereby something is given to consciousness as a theme.

The sense-giving act is a thematic act of interest in which what is given to consciousness has the preferential character of thematic intending. This character enters into background modifications, as do all structural features of actual discourse; but these modifications do not require further special exposition. In actual speaking, what I state is my theme, my "what I intend" in the moment of speaking. If I assert, my thematic act is judging; if I express a wish, wishing is the thematic act; in interrogative discourse, the interrogative act has thematic form. There is accordingly a multiplicity of acts synthetically connected in unity—both moving laterally along the discourse (word to word, sentence to sentence) and at every cross-section (word-consciousness and sense-consciousness organized by an overarching indicative act).

### 8. Theme, Interest, Indication

Picking up from the previous lecture, Husserl provides the character of thinking with "a necessary depth." If we compare the mode of execution in which the ego carries out the word-constituting act versus the sense-constituting act, we encounter a sharp contrast. The sense-constituting act is the act of meaning or intending: what is expressed in "geometry is the science of space" is what the speaker judicatively "means." But while meaning the judgment "with" the words, the speaker does not mean the words themselves. The words are given to the ego quite differently from the way the judged content is given—the latter is the *terminus ad quem*, having a special priority. Having words in mind in specific acts is not yet to say the acts are directed toward the words by intending them. We *can* become especially interested in them, making them our "theme" as grammarians—but then they are given to consciousness in an essentially different manner. The normal concept of interest means a peculiar mode of carrying out acts whereby something is a theme "that one has one's eye on."

Husserl distinguishes "intending" or "meaning" used for all acts (depicting the ego's directedness toward intentional content) from specifically thematic intending. There is a theme in every discourse, but the words themselves are not the theme—the theme resides in what is meant. The sense-giving act is interlaced as a thematic act, an act of interest. Different degrees of interest exist, and modes of interest that are not merely differences of degree. An intuitive regard of objects can be of more or less interest; and while the ego has its primary theme, it can take an interest in additional events as secondary themes, interests of a second order.

Returning to expression, Husserl highlights the curious interconnection between the function of sense as thematic and the function of indication. The word points away from itself and to the sense in normal discourse—it directs interest. The word-sign, not a matter of interest in itself, serves to draw attention to the sense as something that matters to the ego. This analysis applies to every kind of sign. The moment our interest is directed toward the signs themselves (rupturing normal function), abnormality shows up in the lived-experience—one feels it goes against the grain, violating not merely a habit but a "practical imperative." We have thus gained deeper insight: sense-giving thinking cannot be just any act, but only one with the general character of a thematically intending act—judicative, presumptive, dubitative, optative, or volitional.

### 9. The Regression from Theoretical Logos to the Pre-theoretical Sense-Giving Life of Consciousness

Husserl announces a broadening beyond the narrow realm of thinking as the sense-giving function peculiar to statements. Each preceding step of analysis has already elicited the demonstration of a general character of consciousness pointing beyond this narrow field. Now the investigation enters the broadest generality—no longer a matter of words and statements, though it does concern sense-givenness and the rational/irrational distinction that belongs to the theme of every logic.

The genuine theme of logic alludes to the group of significations most rich in content: those relating to reason, specifically scientific reason, and its achievements—the entirety of what "scientific theory" and "scientific system" express. Logic should encompass principles, theorems, deductions, and proofs in their systematic interconnections, as presented in an ideal textbook as "the spiritual common good of humanity." The term "rational" is normative: what is rational is the true, the genuine; even irrationality strives toward it but lacks it due to unclarity and confused method. Logic bears on science in the genuine sense and wanted from the beginning to be the universal theory of science, the science of the essence of genuine science in general. Under this guiding idea—initially unclear—the essential features of genuine science were to be brought to light, and the goal was to gain an evident norm for all procedures of practical reason. Since this concerns both the accomplishing activity and its objective results, the efforts of elucidation have a two-fold direction: subjectively toward the activity of knowing, and objectively toward theory.

Only in the modern era has it been seen (or first felt obscurely, then seen with evidence) how this two-fold structure demands deep investigations. When systematic portions of sciences were won in a "naive evidence" (as in ancient Euclidean mathematics, early astronomy and mechanics), these models were held onto conceptually, and the focus was predominantly on what was objectively available. Theories consist of propositions; they progress from true propositions to true propositions; insight grasps truth. Immediately evident propositions lead through deductions to conclusions, the whole forming the unity of truth as theory. These are linguistically expressive formations, but the linguistic element is irrelevant; prominent in the variation of language is the pure thought, the identical proposition, the judgment—to which evidence and truth or falsity adhere. Logic thus directed itself toward a theory of theory, examining pure unities of significance in a descriptive-classificatory manner, distinguishing general forms of judgments and their elements, and systematically producing forms of deduction. One then examined which forms yield general conditions of possible truth and falsity. The Aristotelian syllogistic and later formal logic arose in this way, offering the beginnings of a doctrine of forms and of validity—but nothing more with respect to a theory of theory. On the subjective side (critique of knowledge), scant little was accomplished: since Locke, the naturalism of psychology was unable to grasp consciousness from within, producing "absurd theories of knowledge." The marriage between the pure ideal theories of formal logic and epistemological investigations remained completely unintelligible.

Only with phenomenology did we first have avenues of access, methods, and insights making possible an actual theory of science—through its radicality in going back to sense-giving consciousness and the whole of conscious life. Phenomenology inquires back from ready-made propositions and theories to thinking consciousness and the broader nexus of conscious life in which formations are constituted; and more deeply, from all types of objects as substrata of possible theories to experiencing consciousness and its essential characteristics. It has allowed us to see in a presuppositionless manner the feature of intentionality as the fundamental essence of consciousness, and to develop the hidden implication of one consciousness in another, making intelligible how objectivity as true being is shaped as an accomplishment in the subjectivity of conscious life. Going back from "dead" objective theory to the living, streaming life of evident judging and deducing, one is immediately led to the fact that what shows itself as the accomplishment of thought rests upon deeper accomplishments of consciousness. All theoretical knowledge leads back to experience. Under the rubric "experience," a sense-giving accomplishment is already carried out—a highly ramified, intricate one—and only the rational operation, shaped in free spontaneity, can function as a verifying foundation of genuine theory.

It is impossible to understand what thinking is (a highly built-up accomplishment) in order to be expressible by language and provide a science, if we do not go back prior to this thinking—back to those acts and accomplishments that make up the most expansive part of our life. A pre-theoretical and even pre-linguistic life resides in this expansiveness, one that immediately ceases to be in its original, primitive peculiarity with every expression. Husserl sets the task of the further lectures: to open up this expansive world of the interiority of consciousness and, under the guiding viewpoint of a theory of science, beginning from below and ascending upward, to show how genuine thinking emerges, how it is motivated, and how it is built up in its founded accomplishment. This is the decisive programmatic statement for the entire lecture series: the method will be an ascending genetic one, beginning from the lowest levels of passive sense-constitution and tracing the motivated emergence of higher cognitive operations.

Husserl recapitulates: thinking is sense-giving. We had distinguished sense-giving thinking from what is thought—judicative intending and the judgment itself, optative intending and the wish itself, volitional intending and what is willed. The word "meaning" (*Meinung*) is used for both; likewise, "judgment," "wish," "decision," "question" are ambiguous. In modern psychological, logical, and ethical language, both sides are indistinguishably muddled, though the clarity of these differences is fundamental for all these disciplines. We distinguish the intending and the intended meaning, the sense-giving act and the sense. When a thematic act is attached to words, what is meant is called the "sense" of the word, its "significance" (*Bedeutung*). But independently of any word-function, every act has a sense-content. We must liberate the concept of sense from its relation to expressions. Every intentional lived-experience possesses its intentional sense; this becomes a specifically meant sense when the ego carries out acts thematically. This liberation of the concept of sense from expression is a key move, opening up the investigation to the entire field of pre-linguistic intentional life—which is precisely the domain of passive synthesis.

### 10. Perception and Perceptual Sense

Husserl begins with external perception. Observing an unchanging object at rest—a tree—we pass over it with our eyes, step closer or farther, see it from different sides. Throughout this process the object is constantly given as unchanged, as the same; yet the "perceptual images," modes of appearance, and aspects constantly change. In a constant variation of modes of appearance, we have a consciousness running through them, a consciousness of the one and same object. This variation is given to consciousness yet hidden in a certain way: in the natural attitude turned outward toward things, we do not consciously notice the variation. Husserl speaks of a "turning of attention"—more precisely, a reflection. We speak of reflection wherever, in any conscious lived-experience, a direction of the thematic regard is prefigured from the start as normal, a necessary thematic attitude from which we must turn away to get hold of something new. In external perception, the thematic basic attitude is directed toward the external object. A reflective conversion of the thematic regard makes our perceptual images graspable.

Through reflective variation, we see a thoroughgoing unity of the perceiving lived-experience. However we conceive it as temporally articulated, we find it composed of perceptions, each with its content of appearance (constantly different), each with its object appearing "there in the flesh." But this object is the same in all stretches and phases, thanks to a thoroughgoing "coinciding" carried out in perception. It is the same for consciousness! The appearances themselves do not coincide (they are always different and temporally spaced); yet there is a "coinciding" expressed in the evidence that in every modified appearance, the same tree appears. The thematic intending intends the overall identical object. Husserl names this: the sense or objective sense of perception. In general, instead of carrying out a lived-experience naively, we can make it thematic by reflecting on it, and we always find that they make possible an evident consciousness of the identity of content—what two consciousnesses intend as the same. This is the objective sense. This passage establishes one of the fundamental concepts of the entire work: the objective sense (*Sinn*) as the identical pole of a manifold of varying appearances, constituted through a synthesis of coinciding.

Husserl warns against a misunderstanding. The perceived tree is naturally and simply there as an existing reality in naive perception. This does not rule out deception. If perception has demonstrable legitimacy through further experience, the tree exists as part of nature; if the tree exists, perception has demonstrable legitimacy. These are evidently equivalent. But the objective sense of perception is *not* identical with the question of the actual perceptual object or the actual natural object. When we speak of sense, we are not concerned with whether perception is legitimate. We inquire only into what perceptual lived-experiences bear in their very essence—irrevocably, regardless of any judgment of legitimacy. We do not ask whether the tree has a place in the totality of legitimately posited realities. Perception possesses in itself what appears to it as such—its perceptually meant object. Perception is an intentional lived-experience having immanently, within itself, an intentional object as an inseparable sense. If we judge this sense, we judge something demonstrable in an evident manner—having immanent being even if the perception turns out to be deceptive. This is the phenomenological reduction applied to perception: we bracket the question of actual existence and attend to the immanent sense-content of the perceptual act itself.

By shifting our way of speaking, "perceptual object" refers to judgments about reality (as in normal perceptual judgments about surrounding things), while "perceived tree as such" refers to purely immanent objects. What is designated as "the tree" in the latter case is the immanent sense-content of perception—a sense-content is not a tree, not a thing in factual nature. A shift of significance has occurred, marked by quotation marks: "tree" (in quotation marks) versus tree simpliciter—analogous to "Socrates" (the word) versus Socrates (the person).

The considerations yield a fundamental distinction in relation to perception: (1) the full, concrete lived-experience of perceiving; (2) the variable manifold of appearances and aspects necessarily belonging to every phase, combined through a peculiar synthesis of "coinciding" that forms unity in the consciousness of the same object—accessible only through reflective change of perspective; (3) the same object in quotation marks, the identical appearing object in each appearance—the intentional object as such.

Husserl adds a self-critical note: the introduction of the concept of sense is unclear. Sense is initially introduced as the intentional object—what is meant or intended as such. But that is ambiguous. When we carry out the phenomenological reduction, every "act" has its meant object containing all modalities of being. But then a schism arises between the intentional content and the intentional modal-character, which looks like a distinction between two components: the intentional content or "material" (*Materie*) and the "quality"—the intentional object as what is merely presented (*das bloß Vorgestellte*) and what is modally qualified. This is an entirely different concept of sense and intentional object. Husserl acknowledges these distinctions must be made, even if provisionally, and promises they will be pursued in the following exposition. This passage is significant because it shows Husserl himself recognizing the ambiguity in the concept of *Sinn* that the subsequent analyses will need to disambiguate—a disambiguation that takes different forms depending on whether one is operating in a static or genetic register.

## Part 2: Analyses Concerning Passive Synthesis: Toward a Transcendental Aesthetic

### Self-Giving in Perception

### §1. Original Consciousness and the Perspectival Adumbration of Spatial Objects

Husserl opens Part 2 with a striking claim: external perception is a constant pretension to accomplish something that, by its very nature, it is not in a position to accomplish. It thus harbors an "essential contradiction," as it were. This will become clear once we grasp intuitively how the objective sense exhibits itself as unity in the unending manifolds of possible appearances; how the continual synthesis of coinciding allows the same sense to appear; and how a consciousness of ever new possibilities constantly persists beyond the factual, limited courses of appearance. This opening statement sets the tone for the entire investigation of passive synthesis: perception operates under a structural tension between what it claims to give (the whole object) and what it can actually deliver (always only partial, perspectival givenness). This is not a defect to be overcome but an essential feature of transcendent perception.

The perspectival adumbration (*Abschattung*) through which every spatial object invariably appears only manifests the object from one side. No matter how completely we perceive a thing, it is never given with all its characteristics from all sides at once. Every aspect, every continuity of adumbrations, offers only sides. And this is not mere statement of fact but inconceivability: it is inconceivable that external perception would exhaust the sensible-material content of its object, that a perceptual object could be given in the entirety of its sensibly intuitive features from all sides at once in a self-contained perception. This fundamental division between what is genuinely perceived and what is not genuinely perceived belongs to the primordial structure of the correlation between external perception and bodily object. Viewing the table from a particular side, the table still has a non-visible back side, a non-visible interior—indexes for a variety of possible visibility. The full thing, according to the very sense of perception, has sides that would be brought to genuine perception only in other perceptions. This is the Husserlian principle of inadequacy applied to transcendent perception: the given is always a partial self-presentation of a fuller object that exceeds any single perceptual act.

Perception is original consciousness (*Originalbewußtsein*). But in external perception there is a curious schism: original consciousness is only possible in the form of an actually and genuinely original conscious-having of sides *and* a co-conscious-having of other sides that are not originally there. The non-visible sides are "co-meant" as co-present, but they do not appear genuinely—they are not there like reproductive aspects or intuitions that exhibit them. We can nevertheless produce such intuitive presentifications (*Vergegenwärtigungen*) whenever we like: viewing the front side, we can orchestrate a reproductive course of aspects through which the non-visible side would be presented. But this merely presentifies a course of perceptions in which we would see the object from ever new sides. What characterizes the actually seen side as a mere side is a non-intuitive pointing beyond or indicating—what provides for the fact that the side is not taken for the thing, but that something transcending the side is intended as perceived. Noetically: perception is a mixture of actual exhibiting and empty indicating. Noematically: what is perceived is given in adumbrations such that each particular givenness refers to something not-given belonging to the same object.

Every perception, or noematically every single aspect, points to a continuity of possible new perceptions in which the same object would show from ever new sides. In every moment of perceiving, the perceived is a "system of referential implications" (*Verweisen*) with an appearance-core upon which appearances have their hold. Husserl uses a vivid personifying figure: the object "calls out to us" in these referential implications—"There is still more to see here, turn me so you can see all my sides, let your gaze run through me, draw closer to me, open me up, divide me up." This rhetorical device is not merely pedagogical; it captures something phenomenologically precise about the soliciting character of the perceptual object—what Husserl will later thematize as affective allure. The identical object is a constant *x*, a constant substrate of actually appearing moments and of indications of moments not yet appearing. These indications are at the same time tendencies—indicative tendencies pushing toward appearances not given. They are whole indicative systems, systems of rays pointing toward manifold systems of appearance. They are pointers into an emptiness, since the non-actualized appearances are neither consciously actual nor presentified. Everything genuinely appearing is so only by virtue of being intertwined with an intentional empty horizon—a "halo of emptiness" that is not a nothingness but an emptiness to be filled out, a determinable indeterminacy.

Despite its emptiness, the sense of this halo of consciousness is a prefiguring that prescribes a rule for the transition to new actualizing appearances. Seeing the front of the table, I am also conscious of the back side through an empty pointing ahead, however indeterminate. But no matter how indeterminate, it is still a pointing to a bodily shape, a bodily coloring, etc. Only appearances that adumbrate things of the prefigured kind and that determine more closely what is indeterminate within this framework can be integrated concordantly—can stay the course of an identical *x* of determination. This holds for every perceptual phase: each has a new empty horizon, a new system of determinable indeterminacy, a new system of progressing tendencies with corresponding possibilities of entering into determinately ordered systems of possible appearances, together with their inseparable horizons. Aspects are nothing in themselves; they are appearances-of only through the intentional horizons inseparable from them.

Husserl introduces the distinction between inner horizon and outer horizon. The division between what is genuinely perceived and what is co-present entails a distinction between determinations actually appearing in the flesh and those still ambiguously prefigured in full emptiness. But what actually appears is also laden with a similar distinction: even the side already seen calls out for closer examination—"Draw closer, closer still; you will get to see even more." What is already seen is laden with anticipatory intention: a framework prefiguring something new, an *x* to be determined more closely. There is a constant process of anticipation, of pre-understanding. In addition to this inner horizon, there are outer horizons—prefigurings for what lacks even an intuitively given framework. This inner/outer horizon distinction will become architectonically important throughout the *Analyses*: it structures the entire phenomenology of fulfillment, disappointment, and the gradual determination of sense.

### §2. The Relationship of Fullness and Emptiness in the Perceptual Process and the Acquisition of Knowledge

For deeper understanding we must attend to how fullness and emptiness relate at each moment—how emptiness adopts fullness in the flow of perception, and how fullness becomes emptiness again. In the continual progression of perception, protentions are continuously fulfilled by what occurs anew in the form of the primordial-impressional Now. As each external perceiving progresses, protention has the shape of continuous anticipations becoming fulfilled: from the indicative systems of horizons, certain indicative lines are continually actualized as expectations, which are continuously fulfilled in more closely determined aspects. The previous lecture approached the unity of external perception from different directions. External perception is a temporal run-off of lived-experience where appearances concordantly pass into one another forming the unity of coincidence corresponding to the unity of sense. This flux is a systematic network of progressive fulfillment of intentions that goes hand in hand with an emptying of already full intentions. Every momentary phase is a network of partially full and partially empty intentions: genuine appearances constitute fulfilled intention (though only gradually, since inner horizons remain unfulfilled), while the empty outer horizon tends toward fulfillment.

Viewed more precisely, the process of perception is a process of acquiring knowledge. We must distinguish further between fulfillment and the process of determining more closely. While the empty horizon fashions its next fulfillment in the march of perception, this fulfillment does not merely consist in tracing over in intuition the prefigured sense. Indeterminacy belongs essentially to the empty premonition—the presentiment of what is to come. Indeterminacy is a primordial form of generality whose nature is to be fulfilled only by "specification." As long as specification itself has the character of indeterminacy, it can attain further specification in new steps. This process of fulfillment is also a process of knowing more closely: not only a momentary acquisition of knowledge, but a process within knowledge that is abiding and habitual. The primordial place of this accomplishment is the continuously co-functioning retention. The continually progressing fulfillment is at the same time a continually progressing emptying: as a new side becomes visible, a formerly visible side gradually disappears. But what has become non-visible is not cognitively lost. Thematic perception does not merely drive at possessing some new aspect from moment to moment; rather, it drives at fashioning a unity of originary acquisitions through which the object reaches an abiding epistemic possession.

We understand original acquisition of knowledge by observing: the process of determining more closely imparts a specific moment of sense; while it vanishes from genuine perception in the progression, it remains held retentionally. (This already takes place prethematically, in background perceiving; in thematic perception, retention has the character of keeping-a-hold-of.) Accordingly, the empty horizon—into which what is new enters by virtue of retention—has a character other than the empty horizon before it originally appeared. Having already seen the back side of an unfamiliar object and turning back to the front, the empty premonition of the back side now has a determinate prefiguring it did not have previously. The unfamiliar object is transformed in the perceptual process into a familiar object. At the end, I have what I started with—a perspectival appearance—but now the empty-consciousness has an articulated, systematic sense sketched in. What was previously a mere framework of wide-ranging generality is now specified meaningfully. If I turn back to earlier determinations, the consciousness of recognition arises: "I already know all that." Now mere bringing to intuition takes place—a fulfilling confirmation of empty intentions—but no longer the process of determining more closely. This passage is fundamental for the later analyses of evidence and the in-itself: it shows how the perceptual process generates epistemic content that accrues to the object as an abiding possession, constituting familiarity out of unfamiliarity through the retentional preservation of fulfilled sense.

### §3. The Possibility of Our Acquired Knowledge Being Freely at Our Disposal

By acquiring knowledge originally, perception also acquires permanent, lasting possession—a possession freely at our disposal at any time. The empty retention remaining behind can be freely filled up by re-perception in the sense of re-cognition: walking around the object, drawing nearer, touching it, I can experience again all the familiar sides. That a renewed perception is possible for transcendence characterizes the fundamental trait of transcendent perception: through it alone, an abiding world is there for us, a reality that can be pregiven and freely at our disposal.

An essential further observation: if a second thing appears that accords with an earlier familiar thing with respect to the side genuinely seen, then according to an essential law of consciousness—by virtue of an inner coinciding with the earlier thing awakened through the "association of similarity"—the new thing receives the entire epistemic prefiguring from the earlier one. It is *apperceived* with the same non-visible qualities as the previous one. This prefiguring, this "acquisition of an inner tradition," is also freely at our disposal in actualizing perception. This is a crucial passage because it introduces the concept of apperceptive transfer through associative similarity—one of the fundamental mechanisms of passive synthesis. It explains how familiarity propagates from one object to another: not through active judgment but through passive association that transfers the entire horizonal structure from one thing to a similar one.

How does this having something freely at our disposal look upon closer inspection? What makes possible the free foray into our world thoroughly interwoven with anticipations? Husserl privileges the normal and basic instance: the constitution of unaltered spatial things. (Whether unperceived alterations can be known is a higher-level clarification presupposing the possibility of knowing existence at rest.) Every perception implicitly invokes an entire perceptual system; every appearance implies an entire system of appearance in the form of intentional inner and outer horizons. We cannot imagine a mode of appearance in which the appearing object would be given completely. No final presentation in the flesh is ever reached. Every appearance implies a *plus ultra* in the empty horizon. Perception in fact and by its nature constantly pretends to accomplish more than it can accomplish. Every perceptual givenness is a constant mixture of familiarity and unfamiliarity, pointing to new possible perceptions that would issue in familiarity.

Husserl examines the formation of unity through coinciding in the transition of appearances (approaching, walking around, eye movement). The fundamental relationship is intention and fulfillment. The empty pointing ahead acquires corresponding fullness; since its nature is determinable indeterminacy, it also brings closer determination—a new "primordial-institution" (*Urstiftung*) or primordial-impression, since a moment of primordial originality emerges. What is already given points to new modes of appearance through its halo; when occurring, these partly confirm and partly determine more closely. By virtue of inner intentions (unfulfilled and now being fulfilled), what has already appeared becomes enriched. The empty outer horizon intertwined with appearances achieves partial fulfillment; the unfulfilled part passes into the horizon of new appearance. What has already appeared is partially lost as it moves from givenness, but it is not lost—retained in empty consciousness, the perceptual object keeps its determination of sense and points in a determinate manner to what was previously seen. The process of perception is a constant process of acquiring knowledge that holds on to what was acquired epistemically and fashions an ever more enriched sense.

Which lines are brought to fulfillment from the system of unfulfilled intentions depends upon the direction of the perceptual process—which continuous series of possible appearances are realized. Along a given line, empty intentions are transformed into expectations; these are continuously fulfilled in the sense of arousing and steadily fulfilling expectations stemming from current kinaestheses, while remaining empty horizons are left in dead potentiality. The integral harmony in the coinciding of adumbration-appearances concerns not only whole appearances but all their differentiable moments and parts: corresponding to every filled spatial point of the object is an entire series of appearances, continuously passing into one another, exhibiting that point. Within every temporal point of the momentary appearance, reciprocal intentions are fulfilled simultaneously and reciprocally—transitions involve dynamic displacement, enrichment, and impoverishment. The object appearing constantly new, constantly different, is constituted as the same in these "exceedingly intricate and wondrous" systems of intention and fulfillment. But the object is never finished, never fixed completely.

Husserl now turns to kinaesthetic motivation—a side of noematic constitution essential for the objectivation of the perceptual object. The courses of appearance go hand in hand with the orchestrating movements of the lived-body (*Leib*). The lived-body is constantly there as an organ of perception—an entire system of compatibly harmonizing organs. It is the perceiving-lived-body, subjectively movable, self-moving. The system of "movement-sensations" (kinaestheses) in eye movements, head movements, etc., does not simply run parallel to appearances; kinaesthetic series and perceptual appearances are related through consciousness. Viewing an object, I am conscious of my eye position and—in the form of a systematic empty horizon—the entire system of possible eye positions at my disposal. What is seen in a given eye position is so enmeshed with the entire system that I can say with certainty: if I were to move my eyes in this or that direction, specific appearances would run their course in a determinate order. Every series of kinaestheses proceeds in a manner totally different from sensible data: freely at my disposal, free to inhibit, free to orchestrate—an originally subjective realization, carried out in the consciousness of the free "I can." Appearances form dependent systems; only as dependent upon kinaestheses can they pass into one another and constitute a unity of sense. Through the interplay of independent variables (kinaestheses) and dependent variables (appearances), the transcendent perceptual object is constituted as more than what we directly perceive—an object that can completely vanish from perception and yet persist. This passage connects directly to your work on Husserliana XVI (*Thing and Space*), where the kinaesthetic-appearance correlation is analyzed in detail. Here in the *Analyses*, Husserl is integrating the kinaesthetic analysis from 1907 into the broader framework of genetic phenomenology and passive synthesis.

Husserl describes a "constitutive duet" in every perceptual process: (1) the system of free possibilities of movement is intentionally constituted as a practical, kinaesthetic horizon, actualized each time we run through single paths of movement with the character of familiarity (i.e., fulfillment); every eye position, every position of the thing-body is conscious as a place in a system of places, with an empty horizon that is a horizon of freedom; (2) every visual or tactile appearance is ordered to the current consciousness of the parts of the lived-body, creating a horizon of possible series of appearances belonging to freely possible series of movement. Regarding transcendent temporality: every path of actualization would yield continuous series of appearances exhibiting the object for one and the same expanse of time, from different sides; all determinations known through this process would be co-existent. The term "constitutive duet" is significant: it expresses the cooperative, co-dependent structure of intentionality—neither the subject's kinaesthetic freedom nor the object's appearance-system is intelligible in isolation.

### §4. The Relation of *esse* and *percipi* in Immanent and Transcendent Perception

All the foregoing holds only for transcendent objects. An immanent object, like a lived-experience of black, offers itself as a lasting object and in a certain way through "appearances" as well—but only as any temporal object in general, through temporal modifications. The spatial object is also temporal, so the same holds for it. But it has a second, special way to appear. Directing attention to the temporal fullness and especially to the primordial-impressional phases, we encounter the radical difference between transcendent and immanent appearances. The immanent object has only one possible way to be given in the original in every Now, and every mode of the past has only one series of temporal modifications. But the spatial object has infinitely many ways to be given originally—appearing from its different sides. Every past phase has infinitely many ways to exhibit its fulfilled points of time. The concept of appearance thus has a new and unique sense for the transcendent object.

Considering exclusively the Now phase: for the immanent object, appearance and that which appears cannot be separated. What arises anew is the particular new black-phase itself, without being exhibited. Appearing means here nothing other than a to-be devoid of any exhibiting that points beyond, and a to-be-conscious-of in the original. But for the transcendent object, the thing of which we are conscious in the flesh is given only in and through an appearance: exhibiting and exhibited, adumbration and adumbrated must be distinguished. Shifting from the noematic to the noetic attitude: a transcendent object like a thing can only be constituted when an immanent content is constituted as substratum—this immanent content is substituted for the function of "adumbration," of exhibiting appearance. The optical appearance arising in each Now is a complex of surface color moments that are immanent data—we are conscious of them as originally as any simple sensation. Yet the matter does not rest with mere immanent existence: in the immanent data, something is exhibited in the unique manner of adumbration which the immanent data themselves are not—an identical spatially extended body-color is exhibited in the alteration of immanently sensed colors. All noematic moments contained in the object are constituted by means of immanent data of sensation, by virtue of the consciousness that "animates" them. This is the apprehension–content of apprehension schema (*Auffassung–Auffassungsinhalt*), which Husserl elsewhere calls the hyle/morphe schema. Here in the *Analyses*, Husserl is still employing this schema while simultaneously (as Steinbock noted in the Introduction) moving toward its implicit overcoming through the phenomenology of affection and association.

Husserl characterizes consciousness's accomplishment of bestowing on mere immanent sensible data the function of exhibiting something objectively "transcendent" as *apperception*. He warns against speaking of "represented and representing," of "interpreting" data of sensation, or of a function that "outwardly signifies through this interpreting"—adumbrating and exhibiting in data of sensation is totally different from interpretation through signs. Immanent objectlike formations are not given through apperception: for them, "being given to consciousness in the original" and "being," *percipi* and *esse*, converge in every Now. But they are largely bearers of apperceptive functions through which something non-immanent is exhibited. For transcendent objects, *esse* is in principle distinguished from *percipi*. In every Now of external perception, genuine perceiving is a conscious-having of what is adumbrated *originaliter*—not a pure and simple having in which conscious-having and being coincide, but a mediate consciousness: only apperception is had immediately (sense-data referring to kinaesthetic data, plus apprehensive apprehension), through which an exhibiting appearance is constituted. The external object is in principle never purely and simply had in its original ipseity.

It is more important for our ends to recognize as inconceivable that a spatial object, getting its original sense through external perception as adumbrating perception, could be given through immanent perception—whether by human or superhuman intellect. It follows as inconceivable that a spatial object could be exhibited in a discrete, self-contained manner from one moment to the next along with its complete ensemble of features. This is "inadequate givenness" as opposed to adequate givenness. Husserl expresses this in a striking theological formulation: "worse service cannot be rendered God than conceding him the ability to make an odd number even and to transform every absurdity into truth." Inadequate modes of givenness belong essentially to the spatial structure of things; any other way of givenness is absurd. We can never think the given object without empty horizons and apperceptive adumbration. Genuine exhibition is itself not a pure possession on the model of immanence with its *esse* = *percipi*; it is a partially fulfilled intention containing unfulfilled indications. The talk of inadequation as a haphazard lack that a higher intellect could overcome is "totally preposterous." This is Husserl at his most uncompromising: the perspectival, adumbrational character of transcendent perception is not an empirical limitation but an eidetic necessity—not even God could perceive a spatial thing in the manner of immanent perception.

Husserl formulates a principle: whenever we speak of objects, the sense of this speaking originally stems from perceptions as lived-experiences originally constituting sense and objectlike formation. But the constitution of an object as sense is in principle unique for every basic type of object. Perception is not staring blankly at something lodged in consciousness; for every imaginable ego-subject, every objectlike existence with specific content is an accomplishment of consciousness. Every basic type of object requires a different intentional structure. An object that is, but is not and in principle could not be an object of consciousness, is pure non-sense. Every possible object of a possible consciousness is also an object for possible originally giving consciousness—"perception." It is absurd to demand of a material object a perception with the structure of immanent perception, or conversely. Both sense-giving and sense require one another essentially—concerning the essential typicality of their correlative structures.

The nature of originally transcendent sense-giving through external perception is that its accomplishment is never finished as one expanse of perception progresses to another. This accomplishment does not simply bring to intuition something new in a fixed pregiven sense; rather, in perceiving, the sense itself is continually cultivated in steady transformation, constantly leaving open the possibility of new transformations. In concordantly progressing perception, we can distinguish between an unceasingly changing sense and an identical sense running through it. Every phase has its sense in the How of determination of original exhibition and horizon; this sense is flowing, new in every phase. But the unity of the substrate *x*—which holds sway in steady coinciding and is determined ever more richly—runs through this flowing sense. An idea lying in infinity belongs to every external perception: the idea of the completely determined object, where every determination would be purified of all indeterminacy, and the full determination itself would be devoid of any *plus ultra*.

This is an unattainable idea. The essential structure of perception excludes a perception that would furnish absolute knowledge—one in which the tension would collapse between the object in the How of determination (changing, relative, incomplete) and the object itself. The possibility of a *plus ultra* is in principle never ruled out. It is the idea of the absolute self of the object and its absolute, complete determination—its absolute individual essence. In relation to this infinite idea, every perceptual object in the epistemic process is a flowing approximation. We always have the external object in the flesh, yet it is always at an infinite mental distance. What we grasp pretends to be its essence, and is, but only in incomplete approximation—grasping something of it while also grasping into an emptiness crying out for fulfillment. What is constantly familiar is constantly unfamiliar, and "from the very beginning all knowledge seems to be hopeless." But Husserl resists hasty skepticism. For immanent objects the situation is entirely different: perception constitutes them in their absoluteness; they are not constituted by constant sense-modification in the sense of approximation. What has been constituted as present in the Now is an absolute self without unfamiliar sides.

Rejecting hasty skepticism, Husserl distinguishes: (a) the particular empty horizon prefigured by the running course and attached to the momentary phase with its prefiguring, and (b) a horizon of empty possibilities without prefiguring. Prefiguring means an empty intuition providing a general framework of sense. It belongs to the essence of such a prefiguring intention that pursuing an appropriate direction of perception would yield either fulfillment (the process of determining more closely) or, as a counterpart, disappointment, annulment of sense, and crossing out. There are also partial horizons without firm prefiguring—definite prefigured possibilities coexist with counter-possibilities for which there is no support and which remain constantly open. For example, a shooting star flashing in my visual field while gazing at a star-studded sky is a completely empty possibility not prefigured in the sense. This passage introduces the concepts of disappointment and crossing-out that will be taken up immediately in Division 1 on Modalization. The distinction between prefigured possibilities and open counter-possibilities will become the basis for Husserl's analysis of open versus enticing possibilities in the chapters on modality.

Confining ourselves to the positive sense-giving process and its prefigurings, the question is whether an enduring, ultimately abiding self of the object is attainable in going from empty prefiguring to fulfilling determination—or whether not only new features enter the horizon of perception but even the features already grasped imply further determinability *in infinitum*, maintaining the character of the unfamiliar *x* that can never gain final determinacy. Is perception an "exchange" that can in principle never be "cashed in"—whose realization leads to new exchanges *in infinitum*? Fulfillment is exhibited in the flesh, but with empty inner horizons. Is there anything in what has become exhibited that brings definitiveness? We feel it cannot be so, and looking more deeply into the structure of perception, we come across the peculiarity that solves the difficulty initially for praxis and its intuitive sensible world. Even in incomplete fulfillment, genuine appearances as fulfillments of prefigured intentions point ahead to ideal limits as goals—these are reached by continuous series of fulfillment. This happens not for the entire object at once, but for features that have come to actual intuition: every appearance belongs to a type of series realizable in kinaesthetic freedom, in which at least some moment of the shapes would achieve its "optimal givenness, and therefore its true self."

The phantom (*Phantom*)—the sensibly qualified bodily surface—functions as a basic frame for the perceptual object. The bodily surface exhibits itself in diverse appearances; for each we have distant and near appearances, more favorable and less favorable, and in ordered series we arrive at *optima*. Distant appearances point back to near appearances where form and fullness appear at best in total overview. This optimal form gives a framework for further optimal determinations through drawing closer. "The thing itself in its saturated fullness is an idea located in a sense belonging to consciousness and in the manner of its intentional structures"—a system of all optima. Thematic interest comes to rest when certain optimal appearances satisfying practical interest are won. What suffices practically counts as the self: the house "in its true being" is quickly given optimally for a buyer or seller; for a physicist, such experience would seem completely superficial. This passage introduces the concept of the *optimum* that Steinbock highlighted in the Translator's Introduction as the genetic deepening of the static notion of the noematic core. The optimum is always relative to a practical interest and a lifeworld context.

Husserl concludes the self-giving in perception section by noting that all these highly ramified intentional analyses belong to a universal genesis of consciousness—especially the genesis of consciousness of a transcendent reality. The theme of *constitutive* analyses is to make understandable how perception brings about its sense-giving and how the object is constituted through empty intending as always only exhibiting optimal appearance-sense in a relative manner. The theme of *genetic* analyses is to make understandable how, in the development proper to the structure of every stream of consciousness (which is at the same time the development of the ego), those intricate intentional systems develop through which finally an external world can appear to consciousness. This draws the essential distinction between constitutive (static) and genetic analyses that will govern the entire remainder of the lectures. The constitutive question asks *how* perception gives sense; the genetic question asks *how the structures enabling perception develop* in the life of consciousness.

### Division 1: Modalization

### Chapter 1: The Mode of Negation

### §5. Disappointment as an Occurrence that Runs Counter to the Synthesis of Fulfillment

Husserl announces an expansion in a new direction. Up to now, the investigation has covered continuous, unitary courses of perception in which the unity of an object is maintained concordantly, through the unanimity of a coinciding that bestows fulfillment upon aroused intentions. The process was a constant expansion of knowledge, proceeding through discrete syntheses of perception: a familiar thing gets perceived again with simultaneous remembering of previous perceptions—in straightforward recognition. New acquisition continues previous acquisition with respect to new sides. But all of this concerns syntheses of fulfillment, of concordance. There is, however, an occurrence that runs counter to fulfillment: disappointment. There is an occurrence that runs counter to determining more closely: determining otherwise. Instead of knowledge being preserved and enriched, it can be placed in question, annulled. There is something like the difference between modalized consciousness of being and originally non-modalized consciousness of being. This opening marks the decisive transition from the smooth concordance of normal perception to the disruption of that concordance—modalization. The entire Division 1 will investigate how concordance can break down and what structures of consciousness emerge in its wake: negation, doubt, possibility, and the relation of passive to active modalization.

*[Commentary continues in subsequent sessions. The next section will continue with §§5–7 (The Mode of Negation), then §§8–9 (The Mode of Doubt), §§10–13 (The Mode of Possibility), and §§14–15 (Passive and Active Modalization).]*

Husserl recapitulates: the intentional systems in normal perception (the apprehensions apperceiving sensation-complexes) have the character of actual or potential expectations. If I instigate a kinaesthetic series, appearances run their course in a motivated succession according to my expectation. In the normal case, all fulfillment progresses as fulfillment of expectations—systematized expectations, systems of rays that, in being fulfilled, also become enriched: the empty sense becomes richer, fitting the prefigured sense. But every expectation can also be disappointed. Disappointment essentially presupposes partial fulfillment; without a certain measure of unity maintaining itself, the unity of the intentional lived-experience would crumble. Yet despite abiding unitary content of sense, a break takes place and the lived-experience of "otherwise" springs forth. Husserl also notes a form of "otherwise" without a break—a disappointment of regular style that, by virtue of its regularity, can be anticipated and prefigured in the empty horizon: this is a steady consciousness of alteration, fundamental for the constitution of change. Change is a continuous process of becoming otherwise that maintains unity—the object remains concordantly the same as the substratum of its continuous alterations.

Husserl gives the paradigmatic example: a unitary object that abides concordantly in the continuity of original experience, "getting known" better and better. But then, contrary to all expectation, green rather than red shows up on the back side becoming visible; instead of the ball-shape indicated by the front side, an indentation appears. Prior to the ensuing perception, perception was intentionally prefigured toward red and ball-shaped; its referential indicators were determinately directed. Rather than being fulfilled and ratified, the prefigurings became disappointed. The general framework of sense is retained and fulfilled, and only at this point—after we already have these intentions—does "something else" occur: a conflict between the intentions still living and the newly instituted contents of sense. There is a system of continual concordance insofar as the insertion of the new framework into the old restores concordance; but in a partial system, there is a superimposing group of intentions in the relationship of disappointment with those upon which they superimpose. After green and indentation are seen concordantly, the entire perceptual sense is altered—and the alteration radiates back to the preceding perception: all previous appearances are "reinterpreted" in their very sense as green and indented. This does not take place in explicit acts, but if we were to go back actively, we would find the altered interpretation. Layered beneath the new concordance is something that does not accord with it. At the place where green and indented emerged, there is not only conflict involving contrary determinations (ball-shaped and indented, red and green), but the empty red-intention is "annulled," negated by the superimposing green-perception; with it, the thing that bore "red" at that place is crossed out and reinterpreted: it is "otherwise."

### §6. Partial Fulfillment—Conflict through Unexpected Sense Data—Restored Concordance

Husserl uses the beginning of a new lecture to situate the analysis. The study of perceptual intentional accomplishments enables deeper insights into the essence of modes of being and their intentional constitution. In normal perception, the perceived object gives itself as existing actuality in a straightforward manner. But "being" can be transformed into "dubitable," "possible," "supposed"; "non-being" can occur; and then the emphatic "it really is" arises. Correlatively (noetically), we speak of a believing inherent in perceiving—judicative perception. This believing can pass over into doubt, into taking-something-to-be-possible, into rejecting, or into affirming active acceptance. What was debated under the "theory of judgment" since Mill, Brentano, and Sigwart is at its core the phenomenological clarification of the essence and logical function of certainty of being and modalities of being. Consciousness necessarily equips sense with modalities of being in every sense-giving; the source of radical clarifications is perception, and transcendent perception is privileged. The modalities that occur in perception are not coincidentally shared with judgment—modes of belief necessarily play their role in all modes of consciousness.

Every phase of perception presents a system of rays of actual and potential expectations. During normal perception, there is continuous inciting of actualizations and continuous fulfillment of expectations (always a process of determining more closely). But disappointment is a possibility running counter to fulfillment. For a unity of intentional process to be maintained, a certain measure of thoroughgoing fulfillment must be presupposed; correlatively, a certain unity of sense must be maintained throughout changing appearances. What happens when disappointment occurs? Husserl returns to the example: a uniform, well-rounded red ball, perceived concordantly for some time. As perception progresses, a non-visible part gradually appears, and contrary to the prefigured sense ("uniformly red, uniformly ball-shaped"), the consciousness of "otherwise" disappoints the expectation: "Not red, but green, not ball-shaped, but indented." A general framework has been maintained in thoroughgoing fulfillment; only a part of the anticipating intention is affected—the part belonging to that surface place—and the corresponding sense-part gets the character of "not so, rather otherwise." A conflict occurs between still-living intentions and newly instituted contents of sense and belief with their proper horizons.

But there is not only conflict. By being presented in the flesh, the newly constituted sense "throws its opponent from the saddle." Covering it over with the fullness of presentation in the flesh as the sense now demanded, it overpowers the former, which was only an empty anticipation. The new sense "green" in its primordially impressional force of fulfillment is a certainty with primordial force that overpowers the certainty of the anticipation of "being red." We are now conscious of the overpowered anticipation as bearing the character of "null." The "being green and indented" that occurs in the primordial impression advances the preceding series of appearance in a concordant procession. This passage identifies the fundamental mechanism of negation: the primordial-impressional force of what is actually given in the flesh overpowers the merely empty anticipation. Negation is not a logical operation applied externally but a lived-experiential event of overpowering.

### §7. Retroactive Crossing Out in the Retentional Sphere and the Transformation of the Previous Perceptual Sense

A certain duplication in the content of sense essentially belongs to the phenomenon. Just as the unexpected "otherwise" covers over and voids the prefigured sense, so too does something take place retroactively for the entire preceding series. The perceptual sense is changed not merely in the momentary primordial-impressional expanse. The noematic transformation radiates back in the form of a retroactive crossing out in the retentional sphere, transforming the accomplishment of sense that stemmed from previous perceptions. The previous apperception harmonized with "red" and "round" is "reinterpreted" *implicite* as "green" and "indented." If we were to bring the retentional elements to intuitive givenness in explicit remembering, we would find not only the previous prefiguring in its original motivation, but superimposed upon it the corresponding transformed prefiguring now pointing to "green" and "indented"—characterizing the conflicting moments of the old prefiguring as void. Since these are moments of a unitary sense, the entire sense of the series is altered modally and simultaneously duplicated: we are still conscious of the previous sense, but as "painted over," and where the corresponding moments are concerned, crossed out. This concept of retroactive crossing out (*rückwirkende Durchstreichung*) is one of the most important innovations of the *Analyses*. It reveals that modalization is not merely a forward-looking event but has retroactive efficacy—the new sense restructures the entire retentional past.

What we are studying is the original phenomenon of "otherwise," annulment, nullity, or negation. The superimposition of new sense over constituted sense takes place through repression; correlatively in the noetic direction, a second apprehension lies over the first and contends with it. Belief clashes with belief. The conflict consists in the "annulment" of an anticipating intention—the annulment of an expectation through a full primordial impression, for which "disappointment" is another expression. Specifically, it is an annulment that concerns an isolated component while concordance of fulfillment advances for the remaining components. The objectlike moment "red" and its anticipated "being" is immediately affected; it primarily bears the character of "not." Only as a result is the thing itself (the substratum of presumed red) crossed out in the process of belief. After the crossing out, we have once more a perception like normal perception insofar as the sense-transformation produces thoroughgoing concordance. Yet there is a difference: the system of old perceptual apprehension is retained in retention; we are still conscious of it, but with the character of being annulled.

Husserl draws the most important results. First, an original negation essentially presupposes the normal, original constitution of the object: the constitution must be there to be modified. Negation is a modification of consciousness showing up as such in its own essence. Second, the original constitution (in external perception, the apperceptive apprehensions) can undergo modification at any time through disappointment of protentional, expectational belief. This occurs through superimposition of countervailing intentions: the affected intentions alter their entire intentional accomplishment. The green opposing the intention toward "red" does not alter the intention toward "red" insofar as we remain conscious of it as such; but the character of "annulled," "invalid" emerges, and red has the modal character of "void." What is newly perceived has the character of "being valid." In such a contrast, every normal perception is a consciousness of validity—every perception in which disappointment or similar occurrences have not yet arisen. Comparing unaltered consciousness with consciousness altered by crossing out in view of content of sense: the intention is transformed, but the objective sense remains identical. The objective sense still remains the same after being crossed out—precisely as a crossed out sense. Thus, content of sense and its modality of being are distinguished: straightforward, uncontested concordance on the one hand; being contested and crossed out on the other. This establishes a fundamental principle: negation does not destroy sense but modally qualifies it. The objective sense persists as the same sense now bearing the character of "null" or "void."

### Chapter 2: The Mode of Doubt

### §8. Conflict between Two Superimposed Perceptual Apprehensions Having the Same Hyletic Stock

Husserl considers a related type of occurrence: doubt, which can be resolved either through negation or affirmation. The example is seeing something initially as a human being that becomes dubious and is finally disclosed as a wax figure—or conversely, doubt resolving affirmatively. During doubt, two perceptual apprehensions overlap. One lives in the normally flowing perception (we see a human being concordantly); then, rather than a clean decisive disappointment, the full concrete content of genuine appearance suddenly gains a second overlapping content: the visual appearance previously endowed with intentions giving the sense "human lived-body" now has "dressed up wax figure" superimposed. Nothing has changed in what is actually seen; they share apperceived clothes, hair, etc.—but in one case it is flesh and blood, in the other, wax. At the ultimate structural level: one and the same stock of hyletic data is the common support for two overlapping apprehensions. Neither is crossed out during doubt; they stand in mutual struggle, each having its own force, each motivated by the previous perceptual situation. Demand opposes demand. There remains an undecided struggle. Since an objectlike formation is constituted by empty horizons together with the common intuited core, we have a bifurcation of original, normal perception into a doubled perception—two interpenetrating perceptions sharing a common core-content. But this conflict also means mutual repression: if one apprehension overpowers, we see a human being; the other is suppressed. Then the wax-figure apprehension obtrudes, and the human-being apprehension is suppressed.

This holds not only for the Now-phase but retroactively: the crumbling of singular-sense consciousness into many-sense consciousness advances into retentional consciousness. Memory is no longer there with singular sense but has taken on the same doubling. Of utmost importance: the doubling is not really a doubling of perceptions. If one apprehension changes to another, the human being stands in presentation in the flesh, then a wax figure—but in truth neither is there as the human being was prior to doubt. The mode of consciousness has altered: instead of being given straightforwardly as being there, it is now questionable, dubious, contentious—contested by another givenness permeating it and in conflict with it. Consciousness gives its object in the flesh but with a variable mode of being or validity. Original normal perception has the primordial mode "being valid simpliciter"—straightforward, naive certainty. The appearing object is there in uncontested certainty. What is uncontested points to possible contestations and breaks; by becoming bifurcated, they undergo modification in mode of validity. In doubt, both presentations in the flesh have the mode "questionable"—each is in dispute, contested by the other. What has been demonstrated for perception must be carried over to memory, since modalization also occurs in memory by virtue of radiating back into retention.

### §9. Resolving Doubt through the Transition to a Ratifying Certainty or Negation

The possibility of deciding and resolving belongs to the essence of doubt; doubt itself means indecisiveness. In perception, decision is carried out as we progress to new appearances: a fitting fullness suiting an expectation is integrated into one of the empty horizons engaged in mutual dispute. This is the most original form of decision. Under the given intentional situation, new sense-data demand apprehensions completing the uncontested intentions, quelling the contention and annulling what motivates doubt through the force of a primordial impression. "Fulfillment through a primordial impression is the force that mows everything down." We move closer, clasp with our hand, and the dubious intention gets the priority of certainty through concordant transition to new appearances that negate the opposing apprehension through fulfilling weight. With respect to one instance, negation occurs; in the opposite case, affirmation—a ratification of the original perception that had become dubious, and the appearing object receives the modal validity character of "yes, really."

Even the ratifying "Yes," like the "No," is a mode of modifying certain validity, distinct from the entirely original, unmodified mode. But "modalization" is ambiguous: on the one hand, each transformation of validity-mode as distinct from original naive certainty; on the other, a transformation where certainty ceases to be certainty. Passing through doubt to affirmative decision restores certainty, but consciousness is altered—it has the character of resolved consciousness, expressed in "yes," "in effect," "truly so." Everything consciousness undergoes through changes and transformations remains sedimented in it as its "history"—the destiny of consciousness. Since consciousness is consciousness of something, every transformation shows up in the sense as a modality. Observing consciousness at variance with itself: in doubt, the disjunctive "A or B" is constituted; in negation, "not A but rather B"; in affirmation, "not Not-A, but A indeed." Straightforward "being" is transformed into "doubtful being" or "questionable being," then through decision into "not being" or "being indeed." Noetically, we find original naive perceptual certainty or belief, then modified modes: doubtful uncertainty, negating as negative decision, affirming as renewed becoming-certain in the form of ratifying experience. Active acceptance is different from naive certainty and presupposes passing through uncertainty. The concepts of "true" and "false" here are expressions for modes of being—and all analysis of the origins of these concepts must begin at this point.

### Chapter 3: The Mode of Possibility

### §10. Open Possibilities as the Indeterminate Scope of Intentional Prefiguring

Husserl addresses possibility and probability, falling within the scope of uncertainty—not merely privation of certainty but modes that do not pertain to decision at all. Several concepts of possibility emerge. First: open possibilities. What is intentionally prefigured in the apperceptive horizon of perception is not possible but certain. Yet manifold possibilities are always included in such prefigurings. The prefiguring for the non-visible side is an indeterminately general prefiguring—a noetic trait of consciousness emptily pointing ahead, correlatively a noematic trait of what is prefigured. The back side's color is not prefigured as an entirely determinate color for an unfamiliar thing; "a color" is prefigured, possibly with certain patterns. But there is indeterminacy. Pointing ahead has the mode of naive certainty—but according to the sense in which it gives something: what is certain is "some kind of color in general," indeterminate generality.

Husserl clarifies through an experiment: belonging to the general structure of every empty intention is the possibility of explicating it through presentifications. We can freely form presentifications bringing the non-visible to intuition. Doing this, entirely determinate colors appear; but we can vary these freely within the scope of indeterminacy. What does this mean? If directed purely toward a bringing-to-intuition (quasi-fulfillment through presentified perceptual series), a concrete determinate color sets in—but this determinate color has not been prefigured, not demanded. If other presentifications occur with other colors, certainty extends to these no more: none is settled in advance, none demanded. In contrast, in actual fulfillment through actual perception, the appearance fulfilling the indeterminately prefigured is constituted as certainty—a specification that determines more closely, a gradation of knowledge. But illustrative presentification is merely endowed with the mode of certainty to the extent it retains indeterminacy: the fortuitously given color is a fortuitous color for which some other could arise. The general indeterminacy has a range of free variability; what falls within is encompassed implicitly, but not positively motivated or prefigured. This constitutes the concept of open possibility.

### §11. Enticing Possibilities as the Tendencies to Believe within Doubt

Motivation prefigures something positively yet in the mode of uncertainty. Looking back to the phenomenon of doubt: whenever we speak of doubt, we also speak of propensities to believe. What occurs on the front side may prefigure something determinate for the back side—but ambiguously. In a conflict of consciousness (thing vs. piece of scenery), the struggle can take the form of a static state of suspension. But when the ego directs itself toward it and carries out presentifying intuition, the struggle passes to a dynamic play of opposing senses—doubting vacillation issuing in a propensity to believe for each side. By exclusively surrendering to the motivations of one side while the other is out of action, the ego experiences a force of attraction, a propensity to turn toward it in certainty. The same holds for the opposing intentions. Normal egoic perception is modalized into "enticements to believe." From the side of objective senses, we speak of "enticements to be"—the object exerts on the ego an enticing demand to be, "just as if the object were its hostile partner." The sense itself has the "propensity to be." This enticement determines a fundamentally different concept of possibility than the concept of open possibility. The concept of enticing possibility is one of Husserl's most important innovations in the *Analyses*. It bridges the analysis of modalization with the later analysis of affection: the enticing possibility is already an affective phenomenon, a demand issuing from the object-side of the intentional relation.

### §12. The Contrast between Open and Enticing Possibilities

An open possibility does not imply a propensity; it does not exert an enticing demand to be. Nothing speaks in favor of it; there is no demand directed toward it. Husserl calls the new possibilities "problematic" or "questionable" possibilities—also "enticing possibilities." They designate a totally different kind of modalization than open possibilities, with a fundamentally different origin. Open possibility can be characterized as a modalization of certainty, but the modalizing consists in the fact that an indeterminately general intention (itself having the mode of certainty) implicitly bears a diminution of certainty for all conceivable specifications. Each specification fulfills the demand in the same way; something of demand belongs to each, but no actual demand aimed at any particular specification is directed from the beginning. This is entirely different with enticements, where each one is intended in its particularity.

We have determined a closed and exactly limited group of modalities from the primordial mode of straightforward naive certainty—modalizations by virtue of conflict, an originally and straightforwardly certain demand opposing other demands. Problematic consciousness with problematic possibility belongs to this group. A fundamental distinction is drawn between modalities arising from conflict and the modality of open specification. Problematic possibilities appear with different weight: the enticement is more or less enticing; the conflict creates a noematic unity of opposition, of possibilities bound together through it.

### §13. Modes of Certainty As Such in Their Relation to Enticing and Open Possibilities

Husserl examines a unique group of modes of certainty distinguished by differences in "purity" or "completeness." I may be entirely certain ("It is so"), yet opposing enticements exist—another being stands as enticing possibility. These opposing possibilities have differing weight, exercising stronger or weaker pull without determining me. The concept of "conviction" belongs here: different witnesses with different weight, and I decide for one testimony. This can yield "overpowering weight" leading to decision while the opposing testimonies retain some weight. Or I can mark differing weights without deciding, withholding opinion and waiting for decisive experience. These are modes of conviction. Modes of impure (incomplete) certainty refer to the realm of what is enticing. Husserl grounds this phenomenologically: something entices as a possibility; something speaks in favor; opposing possibilities exist. Or only one possibility comes consciously to the fore. The enticing possibility may be merely conscious through its enticement ("I do not let myself be determined"); or I may go along, being drawn toward it; or I may straightforwardly yield—deciding, becoming subjectively certain ("presumption" or "presumptuous certainty"). This impure certainty is gnawed at internally by opposing possibilities whose weight still weighs upon us.

Husserl distinguishes between "leeways" (*Spielräume*) of open possibilities (where the same thing speaks in favor of all equally) and the more restricted sense of something speaking in favor. "Empirical, primitive certainties" are those that implicate leeways of specifications within the certainty of general prefiguring. External perception belongs here: each perception implicates a leeway at every moment within general prefiguring, but nothing speaks for specifications in their particularity. Pure certainty has no opposing motives; impure certainty does. Against the immanent sphere (with its evident impossibility of being crossed out), another opposition emerges: empirical-primitive certainties (implicating leeways, where non-being is possible but not motivated) versus absolute certainties (whose non-being is ruled out, with no leeways). This connects to modes of evidence: leeways of "real possibilities" given in evidence vs. apodictic exclusion of opposing possibilities. Various types of certainty—empirical and apodictic—emerge. Certainty can be "modalized" (ceasing to be certainty): passing into enticement, into doubting, into calling-into-question despite certainty.

### Chapter 4: Passive and Active Modalization

### §14. Position-Taking of the Ego as the Active Response to the Modal Modifications of Passive Doxa

Husserl addresses the ambiguity in speaking of a decision "that comes to pass on its own" versus "the deciding position-taking carried out on the part of the ego." Everything disclosed so far was initially modalization taking place purely in the intentionality of perception, potentially as completely passive. Now passivity and activity of the ego must be distinguished: (1) modal modifications of passive doxa—passive expectations, their inhibitions, etc.; (2) responsive position-taking proceeding from the ego. With this distinction, the concepts of belief and modalities of belief themselves undergo modification. Two layers emerge: (1) in passivity, syntheses of concordance or discordance, uninhibited fulfilled intentions or inhibited crossed-out intentions, with correlative noematic modes of being; (2) the active position-takings of the ego—active decisions, convictions, letting oneself be convinced, taking sides—with their noematic correlates. These active position-takings are not merely making passive intentionality patent or becoming attentively aware of enticements and nullities. The ego passes its own judgment, decides for or against. This is the specific source of what we normally mean by "judging." These position-takings are completely non-independent: they presuppose the occurrences of passive doxa. This passage establishes one of the most consequential distinctions in the *Analyses*: the difference between passive modalization (which occurs in the intentionality of perception itself, prior to any egoic intervention) and active position-taking (which is the ego's response to what has already occurred passively). All active judgment presupposes its passive motivational ground.

The ego does not always take a judicative position. When it simply perceives, apprehending what is presented in experience by itself, there is no motive for position-taking provided nothing else is present. Opposing motives must be in play; disjunctive possibilities in tension must be at hand. Judging is always deciding this or that—deciding-for or deciding-against, active acceptance or dismissal. But this must not be conflated with the modes of being themselves (straightforward "being," the "null," etc.) already appearing in the objective sense by merely becoming patent. The noetic Yes and No arise from position-taking specifically as judging—with the noematic correlate of "valid" or "invalid" declared by the ego. Judging in the specific sense is the ego's act of *posito*—positing in the dual shape of assenting decision or dismissal. Where judging arises in the primordial sphere of motivating perception, two opposing position-takings become possible. Both are non-independent, having their motivation in what goes on in perception itself. The motivational foundation for decision is the restoration of perceptual concordance; the ego is affected by all this, at variance with itself, finally becoming united. The ego actively takes up its position, appropriates what is concordantly given as being simpliciter. Active acceptance carries out a peculiar appropriation—establishing this being as valid "for me from now on and abidingly."

An important moment emerges concerning judicative decision-making: it is not merely making something present or making the intentionality of perception patent, but appropriation through which the active, striving ego appropriates an abiding knowledge. What accrues has the character of being valid "from now on" and "continually"—this belongs to the essence of declaring-something-as-valid. In positing something valid, I mean it is settled for me, established for the future. If in the sphere of predicative judgment and communication, this would be articulated as "I ascertain" or "I assert that." But the communicative relation is not found in judgment's first originality. Regarding the negated opposing apprehension: it is retained; the ego was previously drawn to it. There are affective motives that orient the regard in that direction—but here the ego responds by declaring it invalid. Negating and affirming do not exhibit two interchangeable "qualities" (like red and blue); speaking of "quality" is inappropriate. Negation is a process of *striking down validity*—a secondary intentional character. For logic: deciding-in-favor is appropriation as henceforth valid; deciding-against is rejection of validity that was expected. The "invalid" corresponds to negation—but by a shift in attitude, I can make it valid: by affirming, the "no" or "null" enters the content of what is established. Logic reduces everything to establishing judgments—statements establishing that something is so, or establishing that it is not so—hence a privileged concept of judgment with one "quality." Still, decision itself develops in opposing modalities.

Active acceptance and resolved rejection are not the only modalities of position-taking. "Doubt" as active comportment ("I doubt whether...") also corresponds to the split perception modalized as doubtful. The ego can be at variance with itself on the motivational ground of perception's split intentionality—torn, inclined now this way, now that. This "inclination-toward" in active doubting means more than mere affective pull: I go along with one possibility, confer something of validity on it, but inhibited again by opposing possibilities. This "going along with" is motivated by the weight of possibilities themselves; a judicative tendency issues from the enticing possibilities. I carry out something like a momentary decision but am stopped by opposing possibilities making an affective claim. The inhibition is not mere privation but a mode of inhibited decision—arrested along the way. Taking the side of a possibility and closing oneself off to others: a genuine but impure decision, gnawed at, not made with good logical conscience. Presumption or taking-to-be-likely: if I consider possibilities with different weight, the strongest motivates a privileged active acceptance—but not establishing or asserting as being simpliciter. With overpowering weight, I can pass to certainty, but an impure conviction.

### §15. Questioning as a Multi-Layered Striving toward Overcoming Modalization through a Judicative Decision

Questioning is intertwined with doubting. Within the passive sphere—intuition split in intentional conflict—the disjunctivum corresponds to both doubting and questioning. In their rivalry, A, B, C are apprehended and united in conflict: "either A or B or C is." This is what we find in expressing the active question and doubt. A unitary field of problematic possibilities in the passive sphere precedes questioning and doubting. Either the theme of a question is a problematic singularity (with opposing terms unthematic: "Is that a wax figure?"), or the entire problematic disjunction ("Is that a wax figure or a human being?"). Questioning as an ego-activity: the passive disjunctive tension motivates active doubting, displacing the ego in an act-schism. This essentially implies uneasiness and an original drive to get beyond it—to return to normal unanimity. There arises a striving toward firm, pure decision. Questioning gives rise to this striving. The recurring experience of losing established concordance can provoke further motivation—a drive to overcome renewed insecurity—directed not merely toward judicative deciding but toward a *definitive, secure* judgment that the ego can justifiably ground without lapsing again into embarrassing modalizations.

Husserl distinguishes two kinds of questioning. (1) Straightforward questioning is striving from modal modification toward a firm judicative decision. The proper sense of questioning is manifest through responding: with the response, a tension-releasing fulfillment of striving occurs. Various possible responses correspond to diverse modes and levels of satisfaction. "Is A?" yields "Yes, A is!" or "No, A is not"—both firm modes of judgment as possible responses. Since the questioning endeavor is fulfilled in corresponding judgments, the questioner already anticipates possible forms of response; they occur in the articulation of questions as contents (prospective, neutral judgments pointing to Yes and No). Even doubting within developed consciousness is comportment in relation to prospective possible judgments. Complete disjunctive questions show disjunctive prospective judgments; responses follow accordingly. But not every decision has the mode of firm certainty: taking-it-to-be-likely is also a decision, resolving indecisiveness insofar as the ego endorses one possibility. Further mitigated responses are possible ("I am inclined to believe that A is")—though the practical intention of questioning aims at "knowing," at decision in a special sense.

Husserl addresses whether questioning itself belongs to the modalities of judgment. Questioning belongs to the sphere of judgment and knowledge inseparably—and to logic as the science of cognitive reason. But only because the judicative life is a medium for a peculiar wishing, striving, willing, acting whose goals are judgments of special form. All reason is at the same time practical reason, including logical reason. Questioning is a mode of comportment related in practice to judging—I am frustrated when I cannot reach a decision. Questioning is not merely a static state of wishing but a directedness striving toward judicative decision, belonging to the sphere of volition. When practical ways of bringing about the decision become visible, it becomes resolved willing and acting. Primitive questioning is a practical striving toward judicative decision—a habitual practical attitude, always on the verge of passing to corresponding volitions, endeavors, actions, trying out methods for solving problems. Husserl also acknowledges levels of questioning: questioning can find its firm reply through assertion with which we are satisfied, but one can "question the answer itself"—asking whether the answer is really secure, definitive, resistant to future disappointment.

### Division 2: Evidence

### Chapter 1: The Structure of Fulfillment

### §16. Fulfillment: Syntheses of Empty Presentation and Corresponding Intuition

Husserl opens Division 2 by recapitulating the progress of the lectures. Before the Christmas break, the investigation had acquired an orderly series of insights: the study of perception uncovered the moment of belief (passive doxa) and attended to its modalizations. What was demonstrated for perception is mirrored *mutatis mutandis* in each mode of intuition, including remembering (characterized as a "re-perceiving"). The doxic events in the passive sphere were then contrasted with the higher judicative activities founded in them—yielding an initial understanding of the opposition between passive experiential accomplishment and the spontaneous accomplishment of the ego in the strict sense (judging, deciding, actively appropriating and establishing acquisitions of knowledge).

The investigation now turns to the peculiar characteristics and accomplishments in the sphere of judgment that are of particular importance for logic, and that are already encountered in passivity or mere receptivity: the functions of fulfilling confirmation (*Bewahrheitung*) and corroboration. Steinbock's crucial translator's note explains that Husserl distinguishes *Bewahrheitung* (confirmation, belonging to the passive sphere) from *Bewährung* (verification, belonging to the active sphere). These special synthetic functions had been encountered earlier without sufficient clarification. Perception is a process streaming from phase to phase, each harmonized in a unity of synthesis—a consciousness of one and the same object constituted originally. Each phase has primordial impression, retention, and protention; unity arises as protention is fulfilled through the primordial impression of the contiguous phase. Concretely, perceptual lived-experience is continuously being fulfilled—a unity of continual concordance. When concordance is ruptured, modalization occurs and we no longer have normal perception.

We also speak of fulfillment with respect to expectations arising within presenting life: we expect something; it occurs, confirming the expectation in the most original confirmation of ratifying perception. What interests us is an original confirmation in which a presenting intention is fulfilled by a synthesis of the intended object with the object itself—the nature of evidence. Making a presentation evident is bringing it to originally fulfilling confirmation. This is not an arbitrary synthesis of identification but a synthesis of a presentation that is not self-giving with one that is self-giving. The important distinction between empty and full (intuitive) presentations especially becomes an issue for confirmation-syntheses. Even a perception can occur as a mere intention becoming fulfilled in new perceptions (drawing nearer to a tree, determining it more closely). But every external perception harbors inner and outer horizons: it simultaneously points beyond its content—where there is no horizon, no empty intention, there is no fulfillment. An adequately given immanent datum does not admit of further confirmation in the Now; but it occurs as fulfillment of the preceding phase's anticipation—and this is a definitive, absolute fulfillment, or evidence.

It may seem that the unity of a fulfillment-synthesis would be characterized by an empty consciousness being synthetically united with an appropriate intuition, whereby what is emptily intended and what is intuited coincide in the consciousness of the same object. But this characterization would not work, for not every bringing to intuition—not every fulfillment—is confirming. It is fundamentally important to distinguish between different possible syntheses pertaining to intuitions and empty presentations, characterized by the types of intuitions and empty presentations founding them. Without distinguishing these, one might overlook crucial distinctions.

### §17. Description of the Possible Types of Intuition

Husserl surveys the types of intuition. Perception is the primordial mode of intuitiveness (understood as doxic positionality). It contrasts with presentification (*Vergegenwärtigung*), which has various forms: intuitive remembering (presentification of a past perception), presentifications of something present that are not perceptions (e.g., making intuitively present the back side of a familiar thing, or intuitively presentifying Berthold's Fountain in Freiburg—not as past but as now actual), and intuitive expectations or presentifications of what is to come from the future (foreseeing a futural duration). Husserl notes that in perception we are "horizonally" co-conscious of past and future (but emptily), and these can be exposed in intuitive manner. Remembering has a relation not only to the past but to a current present and future; expectation has a relation to present and past. All types are structurally intertwined. Only when understood in their structural interrelatedness can we understand their synthetic interrelatedness and how they function as confirming or confirmed. This holds for intuitions and equally for empty presentations.

### §18. Description of the Possible Types of Empty Presentation

Corresponding to every mode of intuition is a possible mode of empty presentation. Empty presentations can only be called "presentations" in an inauthentic sense—they do not actually present anything; nothing is built up in them as being. What is genuinely presented is what is intuited in an originary manner. Yet empty presentations do present "this or that"—and we can always place what is given to consciousness alongside an intuition of it. In the synthesis, we gain an evidence-consciousness: the same object emptily meant is there in intuition genuinely. Corresponding to every intuition is an empty presentation (since no intuition disappears without a trace): every such empty presentation is a retention, and its necessary connection to past intentions characterizes a fundamental law of passive genesis. This extends into every intuition itself: every Now-phase arising primordially-impressionally is continually modified in retention—the first aspect of fundamental lawfulness of original time-consciousness.

Not all empty presentations have the same nature as retentions. Alongside retentions, there are protentions: the second aspect of genetic primordial lawfulness. Just as a retentional horizon is invariably connected to each impressional present, so is a protentional horizon. Husserl asks whether these two empty presentations are essentially homogeneous. In passivity, we spoke of protention as an expectation, with the "colorful image of the present meeting the future with open arms." We could not use such expressions for retention. In mindful perception, the ego follows the protentional continuity—the directedness-ahead already lying in passive perception becomes patent. But there is no corresponding directedness in the retentional continuity following the trail of pasts pushed back further and further. The egoic regard can be turned backward, but there is a tremendous difference between the direction in perception itself (which one follows in the protentional case) and the egoic regard (which must go against the grain in the retentional case).

To clarify, Husserl draws upon other empty presentations structurally related to protentions but different from retentions: co-presentifications (*Mitgegenwärtigungen*), the genetically more original modes of making co-present. When a perceptual presentation of a seen room awakens an empty presentation of the antechamber, the synthesis is not one of identifying coinciding but a directed ray from perception through to what is emptily presented—the one "directed toward," the other "directed in itself." Awakening means both the emergence of a synthesis of directedness and the oriented structure it produces. These syntheses are not actively instituted by the ego but produced in pure passivity. They concern presentations that point beyond themselves to other presentations, which thereby gain the character of specific "intention"—teleological directedness, being-intended, meantness. For want of terms, Husserl uses the apposition "passive": passive intention. The synthesis in which this intention arises is named associative synthesis—the most important and completely universal functioning shape of passive genesis.

Husserl argues that not all presentations intend an object. All retentions arising originally in time-consciousness are of this sort (unlike protentions). Retentions synthetically cohere with one another and with the primordial impression, but this synthesis proper to original time-consciousness is not associative—retentions do not arise through associative awakening directed backward from the impression and do not have a directedness radiating toward the emptily presented past. Therefore Brentano was phenomenologically incorrect to characterize the connection of retentions to impressions as "original association." Association is only at work in the protentional path of time-constitution, alongside the continual retentional path. This does not rule out that retentions can assume intentional character later through subsequent association—for example, when a newly appearing tonal phrase reminds us of a previous one still held retentionally, producing an associative awakening directed from present toward retentional past. From the realm of forgetfulness, stale retentions can be awakened, initially taking on the shape of an empty retention that is in relief and has the character of passive intention. The distinction between mere retentions prior to association and their intentional shape is fundamental.

### Chapter 2: Passive and Active Intentions and the Forms of Their Confirmation and Verification

### §19. Picturing, Clarifying, and Confirmation in the Syntheses of Bringing to Intuition

Husserl asks whether the concept of intention previously obtained suffices for the concept of fulfillment as confirmation, or merely provides a framework for further distinction. Considering the possibilities of bringing to intuition for intending empty presentations, he identifies two essentially different modes: (1) the mode that is merely clarifying, merely disclosive—"picturing" an expectation, picturing how it will arrive. The generality of expectation means this disclosure only clarifies those components that were foreseen within what was prefigured; but since intuition must yield a completely pictured image (a concrete one), there is a remnant outside the coinciding characterized as mere filling. (2) An entirely different mode: the specific fulfillment of intention. For expectation, this means a synthesis with an appropriate perception—the merely expected object identified with the actually arriving object. This is genuine confirmation. Merely filling emptiness is not yet fulfillment of intention—what occurs beyond what is prefigured is characterized not as mere filling but as determining more closely, and has the character of fulfillment.

Comparing with retentions: only directed retentions (those made into intentions through associative awakening) are at issue for bringing-to-intuition syntheses. Here, clarifying and confirming are not sharply distinguished as they are with protentions. If an intending retention passes to an appropriate intuition (an intuitive remembering), the synthesis accomplishes both sense-clarification and confirmation simultaneously. Every synthesis of bringing-to-intuition for memories of the past must accomplish both *a priori*—there is no mere picturing where memories of the past are concerned. A remembering can also be picturing, but it cannot *merely* be picturing; it is simultaneously and necessarily self-giving and thus fulfilling-confirming. Picturing and determining-more-closely are always intermingled in remembering; a purely fulfilling confirmation is an ideal limit-case (*limes*), while in the opposite direction there is no limit—empty remembering does not admit of thoroughly picturing disclosure that would not be confirming.

### §20. Intention Toward Fulfillment is the Intention Toward Self-Giving

The intention is directed toward its object; it does not want to be merely empty—it wants to go to the object itself, to an intuition giving the object itself as a self-giving seeing. But the intention is unsatisfied even when prefigured elements are fulfilled; it strives onward from one closer determination to another. Indeterminate generality is always only a form for fulfillment to be accomplished in ever closer determination. Husserl makes explicit what was implicit: directedness is tendentious, a striving—and as tendency, it is "driving at" satisfaction. This satisfaction is only possible in a special synthesis bringing the presented object to self-givenness. Satisfaction is merely relative and leaves a remainder of dissatisfaction so long as intuition still contains indeterminacy or mere filling. Fulfillment has a double meaning: one side is equally expressed by "satisfaction"—the relaxation of striving in which the sought goal is reached.

Within genetic analysis, an affection precedes receptive action. A presentation occurring in the background affects the ego—a tendency heads toward the ego; the ego reacts by turning toward, and the presentation assumes the shape of grasping. This yields the normal concept of doxic intending. But if the directedness should have the shape of an intention terminating in confirmation, the presentation now has the form of a striving issuing from the ego—an *intentio* that intends the true self. It is a presenting endeavor that wants to realize itself in continuous acquisition of knowledge, in fulfilling grasping of the self constantly determining more closely. Husserl then asks whether the character of specific intending can already exist without the participation of the ego. Not every consciousness has a striving or directedness (as shown for original retentions); but it is a general essential possibility that every consciousness, through motivation (associative awakening in the passive sphere), take on orientation and the intending directedness toward self of being.

Retention and protention are brought closer together through the clarification of intention. By virtue of its origin, protention (unlike retention) is essentially a meaning-intention: an *intentio* directed into the future, an intending and striving directed forward. Even an intending retention (memory of the past, tendentiously directed) has the intention toward the past made available in intuitive remembering in the mode of self-givenness—it is intending-ahead in a certain respect, though "ahead" does not have the temporal sense of "future." Generally, every intention whatsoever is anticipatory, due to the striving that is directed toward something achievable only through realization. But a paradox arises: the past has long since been realized and cannot be realized again. This becomes clear by focusing on what kind of striving functions here and what goal it has.

### §21. Epistemic Striving and Striving after the Effective Realization of the Presented Object

Husserl distinguishes epistemic striving from practical striving to realize something in the world. The protentional intention in connection with perception harbors at every moment the anticipatory certainty of what is to come—completely without our help. While listening to a familiar melody, we are certain of completely determined contents to come. Aesthetic pleasure or displeasure does not imply fulfillment or disappointment for the expectation qua protention. There is no room for wishing that it should be, or willing to realize it. Yet every intending-meaning process is a tendency-toward, a striving. The key: it is a differently directed striving—not toward being (which is already certain), but toward the experiential seeing of the self of what is taken for being. The anticipatory intention bears on a situation where what is meant will come in grasping the self—i.e., in the mode of being presented in the flesh. The memorial intention is directed toward a past that will become self-given. The unique acquisition of the synthesis of confirmation is: first, the coinciding at the end (emptiness getting fullness of self, with the character of "verified intending," saturation); second, the striving becomes satisfied, the goal characterized as achieved.

The essential distinction between doxic intending in the lowest sphere of presentation—presenting meaning, with a striving prevailing in it—and every other striving. In every presenting we have the presentational content as material of belief and belief itself: sense-material and modal moment "being with certainty." If the presenting has the normal form of an intending intention in certainty, valuing and striving motivated by valuing can ensue. But such striving toward actuality aims to realize value and fulfill valuing intentions, not to verify. Husserl then distinguishes two things under the rubric of "intention": (1) a consciousness does not necessarily have a directedness toward its object—only when an associative ray awakens it does it have directedness; such awakenings have the character of tendencies with gradations of intensity; (2) as a further moment, the tendency toward terminating in the self—self-giving presentations necessarily have intending directedness toward the self, and when this directedness terminates in its goal, the intention is fulfilled. Concretely, self-giving presentations are always at the same time intending insofar as they contain anticipatory intentions becoming fulfilled in continuous progression—concrete perception and self-giving are only possible as continual synthesis of fulfillment.

### §22. The Different Relationships of Intention and the Intended Self. Secondary Verification

Husserl distinguishes between the intentional ray and the intuition into which it enters—between the particular consciousness-of and the *intentio* traversing it as goal-directedness. Corresponding to the fundamental distinction between self-giving and non-self-giving intuitions is a distinction between corresponding empty presentations: retention and protention as empty presentations have fundamentally different kinds. Uncovering retention leads to self-giving; it already has the self potentially within itself—the self as an originally held, safe-guarded self still conscious and held. But the empty expectation does not have self within itself; its potentiality is only the intuition that pictures but does not have self given within itself. Empty retention is an empty presentation of the self; empty expectation is an empty pre-presentation, not of the self.

This enables understanding that a possible level of ratification lies prior to verifying the self—for example, presentations radiating back into the past already have a certain character of fulfillment as soon as the past attains even an empty awakening. In the functioning of fulfillment, anticipating presentations are constantly involved (perceptions draw such presentations as horizons). Conflict between anticipating intentions inhibits self-giving. The self-giving of the whole perception is determined not only by what is genuinely perceived but by concordantly harmonizing anticipatory intentions. Where anticipation is concerned, there is one-sided and reciprocal corroboration—ratification in a certain respect—even though no actual verification takes place. And in contrast: abrogation, inhibition, doubt, annulment. Husserl introduces "experiencing intuitions" as self-giving, and notes the Humean concept of impression (broader sense) is thereby clarified. Rememberings count as self-giving in a modified way: they offer the "past in the flesh"—a direct return to the past as having-been perceived, possessing the self of having-been in original re-execution. Perception is original acquisition; remembering is originally going back to what is acquired, having it at disposal again. Both are mutually related in knowledge—this relation first makes knowledge possible. An object grasped in the flesh can only be there as existent for the subject when it is "the identical" to which the subject can return in iterable rememberings.

Running through passive life are ever newly interweaving syntheses of fulfillment—a continual striving after intuition realizing the meant self. The fulfilling self has the character of truth and abiding validity. Evidence is nothing other than consciousness of the *adaequatio rei et intellectus* carried out in the originality of possessing the self. But does evidence yield truth in the full sense? Truth is definitiveness. Yet possessing the self can come into conflict with another experience; modalizations can occur. Can this go on *in infinitum*? And if definitiveness exists, how would we know it? This question opens Chapter 3. Meanwhile, Husserl describes secondary verifications within the primitive level: intendings can accord with intendings even without fulfillment, producing ratification without original confirmation. Empty intendings can contend, give rise to irresolvable doubt, then be resolved through certainties negating one horn. In every self-giving, ratifying and corroborating functions play a role—their accord belongs to self-giving fulfillment; their discordance can awaken modalizations. The "horizons" of perceptions are another name for empty intentions that must accord should perception be normal. Conflict in any sense-sphere inhibits belief-fulfillment and the unity of perception.

### Chapter 3: The Problem of Definitiveness in Experience

### §23. The Problematic Character of a Verification that is Possible for All Intentions and Its Consequence for Belief in Experience

Husserl now confronts the central problem: the universal synthesis of harmonizing intentional syntheses corresponds to "the" world with its universal belief-certainty. Breaks occur—partial beliefs crossed out, doubts arising—but proper to every disbelief is a positive belief of new relevant sense; the world gets an altered sense through particular changes yet remains one and the same enduring world, "corrected in its particular details." The problem: doxic life in pure passivity takes the shape of passive intention—striving toward self-givennesses, issuing in syntheses of fulfillment. The fulfilling self has the character of truth and abiding validity. This seems exactly what active cognition accomplishes. But does evidence—the immediately seen *adaequatio*—already yield truth in the full sense? Truth is definitiveness, and experience can come into conflict with experience. Can this go on *in infinitum* such that definitiveness is never reached? The problem concerns the very foundations of traditional logic: the principle of contradiction and excluded middle. These principles presuppose that every belief has a definitive truth or falsity—but we have not yet acquired these concepts in their full sense.

Husserl draws a parallel with mathematical judgment, where validity or invalidity is "decided in itself" in advance—whether we ever carry out verification or not. But turning to external experience constituted in passivity: is it an essential law that every belief is decided in advance? Even in the immanent sphere, every Now brings new data, and in spite of aroused anticipations, it cannot be foreseen why it must necessarily be decided which data will occur in the future. For the transcendent world: there are prefigurings for further experiences, motivated experiential belief corroborated through innumerable accordances—but in the final analysis, is it not possible for further experience to continue contrary to all expectations? Can the entire perceptual world-order be destroyed? Most human beings believe the world's course depends on accidents or divine whim; the conviction in universal causality was forged late. External experience leads *eo ipso* into infinity—each experience is still an open intention with unfulfilled dimensions. Can a synthetically progressing acquisition of knowledge come to an end?

### §24. Development of the Problem of the In-itself for the Immanent Sphere

Turning to the immanent sphere: can self-givenness be entirely relative, not containing any definitive self? Can every self-giving be rendered invalid through conflict with other self-givings *in infinitum*? Immanently constituted being in its living present is not only self-giving but essentially incapable of being crossed out. But "what good is it, since its validity is only momentary?" Where we speak of a true self and definitively verified presentation, we reach beyond momentary consciousness through rememberings—securing the verified self as identical and incapable of being crossed out. The temporal form is the form of objects pretending to have their in-itself. All talk of objects leads back to remembering. Even when considering a noema or a momentary present, we grasp it as what can be presentified and identified in iterated memories. The question of how an objectlike formation in-itself is constituted leads to the problem of the constitution of an in-itself of remembering—how remembering can be justified and to what extent it can become a source of definitiveness.

The living present with its living retention is not capable of being crossed out—doubt is not possible here. But we cannot identify an in-itself here. A sound fading away can be held attentively or exercise a special allure through associative awakening, taking on the character of an intention. This provocation is possibly discharged in a remembering that arises as fulfillment of the intention. Remembering is something essentially different from retention—not a reanimation or increase in clarity. Every retention has its intentional mode only at its place in the streaming perceiving. But remembering is a kind of re-perception: a being constituted anew, beginning with the primordial-Now, and a retentional fading-away in the mode of reproduction. In connection with just-past retention, remembering necessarily occurs in a coinciding of sense and being. This can be repeated. But what about the incapability of being crossed out peculiar to remembering? Is deception in remembering possible even for the immanent sphere?

### §25. Rememberings as the Source for an In-Itself of Objects

Husserl distinguishes close and distant memories. For close memories (rememberings awakened through still-living retention), what has just past and is intuitively grasped once again is absolutely incapable of being crossed out—even if remembering is repeated. But we do not grasp the self and its identity without incompleteness and degrees: remembering wavers in clarity, with different moments more or less veiled as if by a "fog of unclarity." Yet what is just past is absolutely secure—incapable of being crossed out, indubitable—with respect to everything given according to quality, intensity, timbre. It is itself there passing through the fog, in all relative unclarity, but not in an entirely obvious manner—not as realized ultimately. A necessary coinciding forming identity exists throughout alteration of different levels of clarity, with enhancement toward an ultimate, most genuine self—the self completely evident in its appearance, but which is only an idea, an ideal *limes*. The less clear remembering is less saturated; the clearer is more saturated, a "more intensive" self-givenness. But if it is intuitive remembering at all, it gives one self and not another.

For distant memories (reviving a long submerged past): every remembering has its original justification—corresponding to every remembering is a necessary idea of a self incapable of being crossed out. An intuitive distant memory, when steady and synthetically iterable, has with respect to its objectlike formation essentially only one possible way of passing into doubt and turning out null: as a confused muddle of rememberings. Becoming discordant leads to bifurcation—the distant memory splits into several memories, the unitary objectlike formation disclosed as the fusion of single objects belonging to separated memories with partially different determinations. Each split memory can in turn lose concordance and split further. But the content of every memory characterized as false is only false with respect to the unity of the combined whole—it remains correct with respect to its parts. And this splitting cannot continue *in infinitum*: it is a muddling up of discrete matters and must come to an end. What appears in memory cannot be completely empty; its self-giving has its source in actual self-givings. We are necessarily referred back to the idea of a chain of pure self-givings no longer capable of being crossed out—identifiable in content and repeatable in complete identity and concordance. But degrees of clarity persist, and the idea of most complete self-givenness remains a *limes*. The active ego, guided by the experience that memory can be deceptive, strives to check its memories, clarify them, investigate intentional nexuses, disclose illusion, and advance to the true self. But necessary for further understanding is the elucidation of the origins of error in passivity—in its most original shape of commingling. This problem leads to the phenomenology of association.

This concluding passage is architectonically crucial: the entire Division on Evidence has driven toward the recognition that the constitution of objects as having an "in-itself" depends on remembering, and that the reliability of remembering requires understanding the passive genesis of error through association. Division 2 thus both motivates and necessitates the move to Division 3 (Association), which is the heart of the *Analyses*.

### Division 3: Association

### Chapter 1: Primordial Phenomena and Forms of Order within Passive Synthesis

### §26. The Position and the Delimitation of Themes Concerning a Phenomenological Doctrine of Association

Husserl opens the central Division of the *Analyses* by sharply distinguishing phenomenological association from the psychologist's concept. "Association" characterizes a form and lawful regularity of immanent genesis constantly belonging to consciousness in general—not a form of objective psychophysical causality determining the emergence of reproductions in human and animal psychic life. Within the phenomenological reduction, what is there is not the world as reality with its causalities, but only phenomena in their intentionality. In the streaming present of consciousness we find perceptions with retentional components fading into the distant horizon of retention, and also emergent rememberings. Between the noematic components of something present and something remembered, a phenomenologically peculiar connective trait obtains: something present recalls something past. The phenomenology of association is "a higher continuation of the doctrine of original time-constitution"—through association, the constitutive accomplishment is extended to all levels of apperception. Kant already saw that under the rubric of association, not mere accidental facts but absolutely necessary lawful regularity is manifest without which subjectivity could not be. But Kant's doctrine lacks phenomenological eidetic analysis—it does not show what is actually at issue under the rubric of association with respect to elementary facts and essential laws.

Husserl distinguishes a first group of phenomena (reproductive association proper—the splitting, fusion, and commingling of rememberings) from a higher level (inductive or anticipatory association—the genesis of expectations and apperceptions with their horizons). Within reproductive association, immediate association operates through a "bridging term" of similarity. Husserl observes that associative phenomena draw attention to the fact that consciousness need not necessarily be consciousness of a single object for itself—there is the opposing possibility of many elements fused into an implicit unity within one consciousness. A white homogeneous square is given as undifferentiated unity, yet each one can be divided; its phases are "just not prominent for themselves." This has a phenomenological basis. For the time being, we can only catch sight of direct associations by having prominent objectlike formations which, as such unities, recall other past unities.

Through worked examples (cirques recalling cirques, a turn of phrase recalling a conversation by the sea, a Titian painting transporting us to the Uffizi Gallery), Husserl demonstrates both immediate and mediate association, the frequently unnoticed course of associative chains, and the essential law that every awakening presupposes a "bridging term" of similarity. A present enters into a universal synthesis with another past present—a synthesis serving as framework for special reproductions. The problem: how certain similarities among varieties become privileged to build a bridge, and how each present can enter into relation with all pasts, extending beyond living retention into the realm of things forgotten. Only through this can we solve the fundamental problem of how the pure ego has behind itself an endless field of past lived-experiences as its own—a unity of past life in principle accessible through rememberings. This concerns the basic essential conditions of the possibility of a subjectivity itself: what must belong to it so that it can have the sense of an existing subjectivity being for itself, constituting itself as being for itself.

### §27. The Presuppositions of Associative Synthesis. The Syntheses of Original Time-Consciousness

The first presupposition of association is the synthesis continually accomplished in original time-consciousness. In the concretely full, streaming living present, present, past, and future are already united. But this manner of consciousness is incomplete: retentions are empty and sink into undifferentiated background; protentional consciousness is especially empty. Yet without this beginning there would be no progress. Time-constitution is the "A" in the ABCs of constituting all objectivity—a universal formal framework in which all other syntheses must participate. Husserl pays tribute to Kant's "nearly overwhelming genius" in sketching a system of transcendental syntheses in the A-edition Transcendental Deduction, but notes Kant only addressed the higher problem of spatio-worldly object constitution. The deeper, essentially prior problem is the constitution of the inner-world: the subject's stream of lived-experience as being for itself. What is constituted universally through time-constituting syntheses is known as coexistence and succession of all immanent objects. But time-consciousness is only interested in the necessary temporal form—"mere form is admittedly an abstraction." The analysis of time alone "cannot tell us" what gives unity to the particular object with respect to content or what makes up content-differences, "for it abstracts precisely from content." This passage is architectonically decisive: it establishes that the analysis of time-consciousness (Hua X) is necessary but insufficient for genetic phenomenology. Something beyond temporal form—namely, association as a content-constituting synthesis—is required.

### §28. Syntheses of Homogeneity in the Unity of a Streaming Present

Remaining in the synthetic unity of a streaming present (setting aside remembering, expectation, phantasy, and all higher activities), Husserl investigates new primordial syntheses among prominent objects determined with respect to content. The most general connections are similarity or uniformity and non-similarity: connections of homogeneity and heterogeneity. These are accomplishments of consciousness's synthesis of homogeneity. Several discrete color-data in the visual field are grouped together by virtue of similarity—in different ways, with different degrees. Uniformity makes the strongest connection (the most complete kinship). Husserl describes the kinetic form of comparison: transitioning from one relatum to another, uniform characteristics appear as "repetition" of the same; with mere similarity, a kind of overlapping occurs—consciousness of the first object is preserved through transition and coincides with the second. In uniformity: pure congruence. In similarity: synthetic coinciding in a commonality, yet synthetic conflict as particular matters repress one another reciprocally (red square overlapping with blue square—similarity but not uniformity, red and blue clash). The static connection of uniform or similar discrete contents is itself already a mode of coinciding *par distance*. An affective tendency simultaneously belongs to it: prominent elements function affectively, and a connection through homogeneity exercises a unitary and augmented affective force on the ego—prior to higher activities of cognitively fixing the common element.

### §29. Primordial Forms of Order. Supplementing the Previous: The Phenomenon of Contrast

Husserl investigates order as a phenomenon of the sphere of homogeneity—disorder (random spots in a visual field) vs. ordered sequence (graduation from red to blue, triangles in increasing size). Primordial phenomena include uniformity and gradation series. Temporal succession provides the most original ordering principle: the formal aspect of time already contains relationships of gradation built up as the most original concatenation. Content-determined gradations can link up in temporal sequence to form unitary series. In coexistence, Husserl analyzes the visual field as having possible series characterized by direction (right-left, above-below). Form and content are distinguished: the form of positional order, and the ordered content at this or that visual locality. He introduces the crucial primordial phenomenon of contrast: everything in relief within a field comes into relief *from* something in that field. Concretion (growing together) and discretion (not being joined) are opposed: uniform and very similar things "grow together," while prominent data set themselves off from backgrounds through contrast. Both fusion and contrast belong to homogeneous multiplicities. This yields a specific concept of differentiation foundational for all subsequent analyses of affection and prominence.

### §30. Individuation in Succession and Coexistence

Husserl confronts the problem of individuation within immanent time-consciousness. The distinction between temporal uniformity and temporal identity is fundamental: successive sounds may have uniform duration, but the uniform element is the uniformity of temporal shape, not the identity of temporal expanse (the expanse of temporal loci). Every immanent temporal object has its temporal shape (duration, length). But objects can have the same temporal shape without being identical—what individuates them is their unique temporal locus. Even objects uniform in all material respects are individuated by their different temporal positions. Time and space (for the visual field) are *principia individuationis*—the forms of possible individuality of objects given in the living present. This connects directly to Husserl's analyses in *Thing and Space* (Hua XVI) of space as a principle of individuation for transcendent objects, here transposed to the immanent sphere.

### §31. Problems of a Phenomenology of Sense-Fields

Husserl acknowledges the complexities of sense-field phenomenology without pursuing them fully. Each sense-field (visual, tactile, acoustic) has its own local ordering structures and its own mode of individuation in coexistence. The visual and tactile fields have authentic spatial locality; the auditory field lacks this. These differences in field-structure bear on how objects within each field can be ordered, compared, and brought into associative connection. Husserl notes that pursuing these problems would require a full phenomenology of the sense-fields, which lies beyond the scope of the present lectures, though it belongs essentially to the transcendental aesthetic that these lectures are undertaking.

### Chapter 2: The Phenomenon of Affection

### §32. Affection as Effecting an Allure on the Ego. Contrast as its Fundamental Condition

Husserl now turns to the phenomenon of affection. By affection (*Affektion*) he understands the allure (*Reiz*) given to consciousness—the peculiar pull that an object exercises on the ego. This pull is relaxed when the ego turns attentively toward it, progressing from there toward self-giving intuition, disclosing more and more of the object's self, striving toward acquisition of knowledge. Consciousness constitutes partly explicit (prominent, actually affecting) objects and partly implicit ones (parts and moments not yet in relief) that still have a tendency toward affection. Sensible data send "affective rays of force" toward the ego pole, but in their weakness may not reach it—not actually becoming an allure that awakens. Affection presupposes prominence, and within the impressional sphere, prominence takes place through fusion under contrast. Affection is a function of contrast, though not of contrast alone. Connected to the gradation of contrast is a gradation of affection: extremes of contrast are so strong they "drown out" all competing contrasts. Diverse objects can be prominent through contrasts in different fields (colors, noises, odors), competing for the ego's attention. What gives a single prominent datum the priority of affection? It is dependent upon others for its affective force—a relativism of affective tendencies governed by laws and ultimately essential laws.

### §33. Laws of the Propagation of Affection

Husserl asks whether there are laws of the propagation of affection—laws governing how intentional awakening spreads. The most privileged case is where affection results in attentiveness, grasping, acquisition of knowledge. Through multiple worked examples, Husserl demonstrates: (1) the propagation of affection through homogeneity—when one datum in a group becomes affective, affection spreads to similar data in the group, potentially making the whole group affective as a unity; (2) the propagation through the horizonal structure of the object—when one side of a thing exercises affection, its horizonal references can propagate affection to co-meant but not yet given sides; (3) the propagation across sense-fields—what is seen can awaken associated tactile or auditory expectations. The laws of propagation are not causal but motivational: an affective force radiating from one formation can augment or diminish the affective force of connected formations. The synthesis of homogeneity plays a fundamental role: uniform or similar elements "support" each other's affective force, while heterogeneous elements can inhibit each other. A key result emerges: the formation of groups and configurations in the passive sphere is itself dependent on the interplay of affective forces—it is not that groups are first formed and then become affective, but that the grouping itself is an affective accomplishment.

### §34. The Problem of the Relationship between Affection and the Formation of Unity

Husserl poses the decisive question: Is the formation of sense-unities given prior to or only along with affective force? Are there unities of sense first constituted as prominent that only subsequently become affective, or does the actual emergence of these unities coming into relief depend upon the play of affective forces? Through careful analysis, Husserl reaches the conclusion that something coming into relief is always already an affective relief—prominence is always affective prominence. An actual connection, an actual formation of unity always and necessarily presupposes affective force or affective differentiation. Without affection there would not be a living present articulated with objects. This result implicitly challenges the hyle/morphe schema: if there are no "neutral" sense-data awaiting apprehension but only already-affective formations, then the picture of raw sensuous material receiving form from an interpretive act is fundamentally inadequate. The primordial phenomena are not undifferentiated "stuffs" but already affectively charged sense-unities—"original singularities" or associative concretions that, as affectively prominent, exercise allure on the perceiver.

### §35. The Gradation of Affection in the Living Present and in the Retentional Process

Husserl analyzes the gradation of affective forces in the living present and in the retentional process. In a field of affective tendencies rivaling one another for attention, some are more significant than others; rivalries can occur within the same sense-field or across sense-fields. Even though affective force exists, it need not draw active attention—it may only be "perceived" in passive attention as it "knocks at the antechamber of the ego." Husserl rejects an all-or-nothing account and analyzes degrees of affective force. In the retentional process, affective force undergoes a characteristic modification: as contents recede from the living present, their affective force diminishes, eventually reaching a zero-point. A completely undifferentiated field of affective force—the zero-limit—corresponds to what Husserl terms "the unconscious." The unconscious is not a nothing; it is a nothing of affective force that has arisen from original constitution, a nothing with respect to accomplishments presupposing affectivity above the zero-point. Objectlike formations in the unconscious can be reawakened because constituted sense is implied in the unconsciousness. When there is no affection coming from objects, they have "slipped into sheer nightfall"—into the unconscious. This passage establishes Husserl's distinctive concept of the unconscious as the *limes* of affective force in the retentional process, fundamentally different from the Freudian concept.

### Chapter 3: The Accomplishment of Affective Awakening and Reproductive Association

### §36. The Function of Awakening in the Living Present

Husserl distinguishes two senses of awakening: awakening something already given to consciousness as for itself, and awakening something concealed. Every living present brings ever new perceptual data—an ever new source of affective force that can spread over nexuses in an awakening manner, spread over unities constituted in retention, and make possible syntheses of fusion, connection, and contrast. An influx of affective force from the impressional sphere can enable a retention (empty of particular affective content) to restore what is concealed in it concerning an overcast content of sense. The new force can halt the process of affective clouding-over. Husserl describes how an element in the living present can awaken a similar element in the retentional sphere through the bridge of similarity: the impressional data exercise an affective "pull" on similar retentional formations, bringing them back above the threshold of affective prominence. This is the fundamental mechanism of reproductive association within the living present.

### §37. Retroactive Awakening of the Empty Presentations in the Distant Sphere

Beyond the living present, Husserl analyzes retroactive awakening reaching into the distant sphere of empty retentions—formations that have sunk below the zero-point of affective force into the "unconscious." Here the awakening proceeds by affective rays radiating backward from current impressional data or from freshly awakened retentions through the bridge of similarity. A present impression can awaken an empty distant retention that bears a similar content, bringing it back to prominence as an empty but now-directed presentation. This awakened retention has the character of a passive intention and can in turn awaken further retentions in a chain of mediate associations. The genetic significance is profound: this is the mechanism by which the ego can reach back into its forgotten past, constituting its temporal identity beyond the immediate span of living retention.

### §38. The Transition of Awakened Empty Presentations in Rememberings

Husserl describes how awakened empty presentations can transition into intuitive rememberings. The empty presentation, having been brought back to prominence through associative awakening, carries within it a tendency toward fulfillment—a reproductive tendency that can lead to the emergence of an intuitive remembering in which the past is brought to self-givenness. This transition is not guaranteed: it depends on the strength of the awakening, the degree of retentional preservation, and the absence of inhibiting factors. When it does occur, the synthesis of the awakened empty presentation with the emerging intuitive remembering constitutes a confirmation—the past is verified as having been. This passage connects the phenomenology of association back to the problematic of evidence and the in-itself developed in Division 2.

### §39. The Difference Between Continuous and Discontinuous Awakening

Husserl distinguishes continuous awakening (where the transition from empty presentation to intuitive remembering proceeds smoothly, as in the case of a melody just heard being replayed in memory immediately) from discontinuous awakening (where a present impression suddenly awakens a distant, disconnected past through a striking similarity, producing a "flash" of memory without continuous mediation). Continuous awakening typically operates within the span of the living present and its immediate retentional surroundings. Discontinuous awakening leaps across temporal distances and is the more characteristic form of what is traditionally called "association." Both forms presuppose the bridge of similarity, but they differ in the temporal span traversed and in the manner of the transition.

### Chapter 4: The Phenomenon of Expectation

### §40. Motivational Causality Peculiar to Expectations

Husserl turns from reproductive association (directed toward the past) to the genesis of expectations (directed toward the future). Expectations arise through a peculiar motivational causality: past regularities constituted through reproductive association generate anticipatory tendencies. When a pattern has been repeatedly experienced (A followed by B), the occurrence of A in the present awakens not only the memory of past A-B sequences but also a protentional tendency toward B—an expectation. This is "inductive association" at its most primitive level. The motivational causality peculiar to expectations is not the objective causality of natural science but an immanent, intentional motivation: the past prefigures the future not through causal law but through the sedimented results of associative syntheses. Expectation is thus founded in reproductive association—we can only anticipate on the basis of what we have experienced.

### §41. Strengthening and Inhibiting Expectational Belief. The Function of Expectation for the Formation of Configurations

Husserl analyzes how expectational belief is strengthened or weakened. Repeated confirmation of expectations strengthens the motivational force of the anticipation—the more often A has been followed by B, the stronger the expectation of B when A recurs. Conversely, disappointment weakens expectational belief. But there are also inhibiting factors: competing expectations arising from different associative nexuses can weaken or suppress a given expectation. The interplay of strengthening and inhibiting factors produces the characteristic structure of empirical belief—a belief that is neither apodictic nor arbitrary but graded in "weight" or "probability." Husserl also describes the function of expectation for the formation of configurations: expectations project a future gestalt that organizes present experience. When we hear the first notes of a familiar melody, the expectation of the whole melody configures our listening, directing attention to the anticipated pattern. In this way, expectation is not merely passive reception of what comes next but an active (though pre-egoic) structuring of the perceptual field. This concluding analysis links the phenomenology of association back to the opening problematic of the lectures: the genesis of the cognitive structures that make knowledge—and ultimately logic—possible. The configurations formed through expectation are the pre-predicative ancestors of the logical forms investigated in formal logic.

The progression from Division 1 (Modalization) through Division 2 (Evidence) to Division 3 (Association) traces a deepening trajectory: from the disruption of concordance, through the problem of how truth and the in-itself are constituted, to the fundamental mechanisms of passive genesis that make both concordance and its disruption possible. Division 4 will return to the problem of the in-itself, now equipped with the associative analyses that make it tractable.

### Division 4: The In-Itself of the Stream of Consciousness

### Chapter 1: Illusion in the Realm of Remembering

### §42. Overlapping, Fusion, and Conflict of Rememberings of Different Pasts

Husserl investigates how discordance among memories is possible—how a muddle of different pasts can arise, producing the appearance of a unitary past that later turns out to be illusion through splitting. Remembering is nothing other than the phenomenon of awakening a past objectlike formation originally constituted in the living present, now sunken back into retention and become completely void of force. Since the process of wakeful consciousness and sinking into quiescent sleep never breaks off, the sediments of the unconscious are continuously layered upon one another, and the potentiality of awakening continues to infinity. Belonging ideally to every remembering is a possible continuity of awakening leading to the living present. Every single remembering and every continuous series must, it seems, be concordant. How should error or illusion have sense here? Since original constitution fashions sense originally, it cannot falsify its sense—and remembering as mere reproduction should not be able to do so either. If we cannot demonstrate a source of error, this seems to be a fault in the theory.

Husserl explains how discordance becomes possible genetically. Memory-sedimentations are ordered in the memorial subsoil in a fixed sequence. When several retentions occupying diverse positions are awakened together by virtue of commonalities, they can never be remembered at the same time in a completely intuitive manner—an essential law that the original temporal field can only be filled out once in completely intuitive manner. Two reproductions compete for the formal system of the living Now: if both have uniform affective force, a contest ensues. The least priority of reproductive force gives preference to one; the other becomes suppressed. If an associative connection exists between both reproductions (a bridging term of similarity), the similar terms coincide and fuse with varying intimacy according to their degree of similarity. Through the coinciding similar terms, both reproductions stand in a relation of overlapping with respect to their temporal fields. What extends beyond the coinciding exists in "negative coinciding"—a relation of conflict and correspondence dictated by the uniform formal structures (temporal form, field-forms, spatial forms of orientation). The concretion of R excludes the concretion of R'—they can only become intuitable one after the other.

### §43. The Possibilities of a Repressed Memory Breaking through to Intuition. The Disclosure of Illusion through the Transition to Higher Levels of Clarity

Husserl identifies several possibilities when the affective force of one reproduction R is depleted and the forces of R' become preponderant. (1) The terms of R' emerge from the dark, the reproduction spreads along connections and is raised to increasing clarity; eventually the entire past present comes to life, the bridging term breaks away from its fusion, and we live a portion of the past present while the associating situation submerges entirely. (2) Both the stimulating intuition R and the breaking-through elements of R' prevail without linking up, each bearing its own protentional horizon of repressed supplementary elements—the fixed formal frameworks are spottily filled out with contents of R and R'. This can produce a "piebald muddle" or a "combined image"—rememberings linking up to form a connected remembering that joins elements of different memories into an intuitively concordant but illusory image. What makes the fusion into an illusory image is the force of apperception: if components a and b have frequently been connected in a present, they will be taken as connected here too. But this is illusion—the components have repressed supplementary elements that "protest from the subsoil" against the illusory demands, though the protests are too weak to lead to clear doubt.

The illusion will be disclosed as illusion as soon as the affective force of the suppressed elements becomes livelier and wins out, leading to reproductive development and unfolding—both situations and their segregated temporal fields diverge intuitively. The illusory image shows itself as a conglomeration and fusion of different memorial images concordant in themselves. Every disclosure of illusion is carried out in the transition to higher levels of clarity; every disclosure of a reproduced intuition through continual confirmation takes place in progressive clarification.

### Chapter 2: The True Being of the System of the Immanent Past

### §44. Confirmation of Self-Givennesses by Expanding into the Outer Horizon, on the One Hand, and by Approximating the Idea of Absolute Clarity, on the Other

The illusion as illusion is disclosed in principle only in the transition to higher levels of clarity—a principle holding generally for all deceptions and their disclosure. Every confirmation is a process of bringing something concealed to light, bringing it to the clarity of self-giving. Self-givings have concealments; what remains empty or veiled can be brought to light as new self-givings. Verification can take place positively or negatively in two mutually related types: concordantly confirming and discontinuing. Required for confirmation of concrete, full self-giving is not only the foray into the outer horizon (fulfillment through appropriate genuine self-givings), but all self-givings demand further confirmation—progressive clarification. All self-giving stands under laws of the gradation of clarity, whose ideal *limes* is absolute or "pure" self-giving. The further this graduating process goes, the clearer and purer the self-giving with respect to the *limes*. This is a universal law of consciousness: consciousness exists in the form of constant original self-giving (perception) and reproductive self-giving (memory). The dual path to the self is: the path of expanding self-giving through broadening rememberings, and the path of self-givings approaching their *limes*, approaching the idea of absolute clarity.

### §45. The Primordial Transcendence of the Past of Consciousness and the Idea of its Complete Self-Giving

All self-givings called upon to bring out the true and identifiable self are in a certain sense "transcendently" giving—rememberings arise in the immanence of the primordial present, but what they presentify (the past) is transcendent to the lived-experience and to the entire stock of things constituted originally in the present. Our entire past of consciousness is given transcendently. The stream of consciousness is the first transcendent in the manner of a primordial source—the transcendent self primordially instituted in the streaming present, issuing in self-giving and verification through rememberings. Consciousness lives with streaming and simultaneously becomes objectlike for its ego—an object as the transcendent self coming to incomplete and approximate self-giving in rememberings. For the ego, the idea of a true self (the idea of the true past of consciousness as the idea of complete self-giving) is two-fold: one concerns orientation toward clarity and its *limes*; the other concerns expansion. This idea is itself essentially a streaming one, because the object (consciousness) is a stream carrying out ever new primordial institutions.

Husserl articulates the fundamental result: essentially, and understood from a priori laws of genesis, living streaming consciousness contains a realm of true being that gets continually richer, becoming an abiding realm of objectlike formations in themselves—pregiven, available for the active ego's apprehension, identification, verification. This in-itself of the stream of consciousness is on another level of being insofar as its future is not likewise in itself. Consciousness is not only a streaming present with occasional rememberings—it is a fact available for the ego, a truth the ego can ascertain. Every past lived-experience has been: in itself. And yet for the ego—something available, truly existing, recognizable. If consciousness did not bear within itself an "in-itself," no empirical ego could attribute a stream of consciousness to another ego. It is therefore "the most radical problem of a transcendental logic to understand how—in the streaming egoic life that is the life of consciousness—this life itself can be constituted as a true being; as a true being, that is, as the ideal correlate of possible verification, as an ideal norm which, according to essential laws, lies in consciousness." The ego has a first, absolutely and necessarily constituted sphere of the in-itself—a first sphere of absolutely secure, true objectivity without which it is inconceivable as ego. This passage is one of the most important in the entire *Analyses*: it establishes that the in-itself of the stream of consciousness is constituted in passivity through the interplay of retention, association, and remembering, and that this constitution is the condition of possibility for all higher-level constitution—including the constitution of the objective world.

### Chapter 3: The Problem of a True Being for the Future of Consciousness

### §46. Disappointment as an Essential Moment of Expectation

The considerations had no bearing on the future. While streaming consciousness constantly projects a protentional horizon, it is not clear that this prefiguring should be so determined that a norm of true being is secured for the future. What I have experienced actually was—I can awaken and legitimate it in pure immanence. But for the future? Expectations can only be fulfilled through perceptions, and are thus essentially susceptible to disappointment. Perception brings something new; that is its nature. Even if motivation is evident and outweighs all counter-forces, perception first decides, and something new can be "a slap in the face to all expectation." The "must" of expectation is not absolute necessity of being but necessity of anticipated being. The ego has a more or less determinately anticipated future; only the form of a possible future and the fact that something in general will arrive is a priori incapable of being crossed out. The future is the realm of the unfamiliar—initially not a realm of the in-itself, but a realm of indeterminacy. And yet expectation can also constitute an objectlike formation and make the future determinate.

### §47. The Constitution of the Objective World in Its Significance for the Determinate Prefiguring of Futural Consciousness

How does the immanent stream of consciousness accomplish the determination of the future? Through the fact that it constitutes within itself an objective world in a transcendent intentionality. Things are constituted in immanent lived-experiences but not as lived-experiences—they are essentially constituted as intentional unities of actual and possible perception, extending beyond current perceptions as unities appearing in them. Things are constituted as enduring unities reaching via experienced past and present into the non-experienced and into the future: the thing is in itself not only with respect to past and present but also what it will be. In lived-experiences of external perception, such existence is constituted with the sense-giving of the in-itself—everything constituted as determinable indeterminacy. A being that can be legitimated, a true being in itself, lies at the basis. If nature presents itself as a universal nexus in which objective past, present, and future are determined in themselves, then by virtue of the constitution of nature, a rule encompassing all consciousness including its future is prefigured. There are norms of verification, originally prefigured ways of possible verification discoverable by the active ego. The spatial-temporal world and the correlative regulation of consciousness exists for the ego as pregivenness, availability, readiness for cognitive activity. Even a consciousness without regularity (if it were conceivable) would still have the general structure of time-consciousness prefiguring certain binding relationships. But an infinitely much richer organization—the constitution of an objective world—encompasses consciousness as a whole and in advance for the future.

### Transitional Methodological Considerations

### §48. Consciousness as a Storied Structure of Constitutive Accomplishments. The Disciplines of a Systematic Investigation

Husserl draws together the great theme of transcendental philosophy: consciousness in general as a "storied structure" (*Stufenbau*) of constitutive accomplishments in which ever new objectivities of ever new types are constituted at ever new levels. All other levels are taken up into higher ones but not lost in them—they remain ready at any time for corresponding orientations and demonstrations. Nothing can be grasped in a stream of consciousness without this consciousness having accomplished the corresponding intentional genesis from its material of hyletic components and according to essential laws. Consciousness is an incessant process of becoming—not a mere succession but an incessant constituting of objectivities in graduated levels. "It is a never ending history. And history is a graduating process of constituting higher and higher formations of sense through which prevails an immanent teleology. And belonging to all sense is a truth and a norm of truth."

The path of radical phenomenological investigation must trace this storied structure. It takes its departure from the objective world as naively presented; physical nature becomes distinct as a lower level (abstracting from all creative accomplishments of the free ego). The study of ready-made intentionality within external perception offers a "static understanding" of noematic contents—a phenomenology of the variety of phenomena coalescing noematically in a spatio-temporal infinity with causal dependencies. This opens to a phenomenology of lived-corporeality and psychophysical animality. But the general theory of belief points further down the path: the preferred consideration of concordant nexuses must be superseded by consideration of modalization, bifurcation, negation, and verification. What was accomplished for the self-objectivation of consciousness with respect to its past must now be accomplished for the constitution of nature—the entire network of concordant and discordant modes of givenness must be integrated into the history of constituting consciousness and made intelligible genetically.

These investigations lead to an entire range of transcendental disciplines: a transcendental science of nature (transcendental physics), encompassing a transcendental science of space and time; a transcendental psychophysics and psychology; a transcendental science of personalities; a transcendental sociology; a transcendental science of culture. They all treat, statically and genetically, the corresponding constitutive problems. These disciplines stand in intimate relationship to the a priori ontologies that explicate the pure essence of regions of objectivity—ontology of nature (geometry, doctrine of time, pure mechanics), etc. These ontological fundamental concepts serve as norms and as leading clues (*Leitfäden*) to transcendental analyses. The transcendental phenomenologist takes nature purely as the correlate of consciousness. Ontological fundamental concepts serve as indices for systems of verification—the systematic series of self-giving terminating in the true self. The phenomenologist asks: what belongs essentially to physical nature?—then sees how it gives itself to consciousness and gets legitimated in verifying nexuses. Phenomenology and ontology exist in a kind of alliance, but differ fundamentally: the ontologist takes the idea of nature as an ideal reality and investigates its properties; the transcendental phenomenologist has as theme the idea of a consciousness in general in which an objectlike formation in the form of spatiality can be given.

---

This completes Part 2 of the Main Text. The trajectory of the *Analyses* through passive synthesis can now be seen as a whole: from the initial investigation of self-giving in perception (the essential contradiction of transcendent perception, the adumbration principle, the kinaesthetic correlation), through the analyses of modalization (negation, doubt, possibility, the passive/active distinction), through the analyses of evidence (the types of intuition and empty presentation, the concept of intention as tendentious directedness, the problem of definitiveness), through the heart of the work in the analyses of association (primordial phenomena of order, the phenomenon of affection, awakening, reproductive and anticipatory association), to the culminating analyses of the in-itself of the stream of consciousness (illusion in remembering, the true being of the immanent past, the problem of the future, and the constitution of the objective world as securing a determinate future). The Transitional Methodological Considerations then look forward to the "storied structure" of constitutive accomplishments—the ascending levels from transcendental aesthetic to transcendental logic—that Part 3 will begin to investigate.

## Part 3: Analyses Concerning Active Synthesis: Toward a Transcendental, Genetic Logic

### Introduction: Circumscribing the Investigations into the Active Ego

### §49. The Relation between Activity and Passivity

Husserl opens Part 3 by turning to the transcendental consideration of the accomplishments of activity. A consciousness of the object is actually and genuinely carried out only first in egoic acts; an object as object is only first there for the active ego. All concepts referring back to the concept of the object—"identical sense," "being," "modalities of being," "true being," "verification"—get their genuine character only within the framework of activity. Yet we can only speak of the lower levels of passivity if we already have before us something constituted in activity. If we abstract from activity, the lower level is essentially still indeterminate—we can only have purity of understanding the lower level with successive investigation of the higher. Moreover, every accomplishment of activity itself sinks in a regulated manner into passivity, sedimented in the accomplishments of original passivity. Passivity is what is in itself first because all activity essentially presupposes a foundation of passivity and an objectlike formation already pre-constituted in it. The active accomplishments of the ego operate in the medium of attentive turning-toward and its derivatives. Turning attention is the bridge to activity; all genuine activity is carried out in the scope of attentiveness. Affection is the counter-mode of attentiveness within passivity—affections proceed to the ego from the background, and the ego complies by directing itself toward what exercises affection.

### §50. The Constitutive Accomplishments of Affect-Consciousness. Affection of Feeling and Turning Toward in Feeling. Will and Desire

Husserl addresses affect-consciousness (*Gemütsbewußtsein*), which plays a constant role in the passivity of conscious life but was previously left aside. The investigation had operated in the sphere of presentation (*Vorstellungssphäre*)—the sphere of objectivating consciousness. What is constituted in passivity as identical can lead to a feeling already within passivity: pleasurable or unpleasurable, agreeable or disagreeable. The consciousness of the object founds a novel layer of intentionality of feeling (*Gefühlsintentionalität*) exhibiting novel intentionality. This layer yields an accomplishment with respect to the object (a new character sedimented in the noema, e.g., "pleasurable"), but the objective sense of the underlying object does not undergo enrichment through it—nothing enters into the objective sense, it is not determined more closely. The intentionality of feeling has nothing to seek down the path of objectivating identification. Yet all consciousness is irrevocably bound to objectivating consciousness; what feeling brings can become identifiable through foundation-relations in which the founded consciousness has its object in what the underlying consciousness instituted.

Husserl distinguishes objectivating affection (the allure on the ego as ego of possible knowledge) from the affection of feeling. Further, he distinguishes desire from feeling: desire is tendentious like the tendency of turning-toward, but with an entirely different character—striving after or driving away from. Its fulfillment is relaxation through realizing, in the change to joy of fulfillment. Volition is not mere desire; it belongs to the sphere of pure activity. Husserl tentatively suggests that will is not a separate mode of consciousness but a special higher form of activity appearing under certain essential conditions presupposing objectivations and intentionality of feeling.

### §51. Playful and Positional Consciousness

Husserl distinguishes positional consciousness (serious positing of being and modalities of being) from playful consciousness (phantasy as the general modification of "as if"). The difference between actual perceiving and quasi-perceiving (e.g., viewing a painted phantasy-landscape) is not conflict and resolution but the character of play—phantasy-objects are quasi-actualities, mere "images." Phantasy signifies a general modification pointing back to unmodified (positional) consciousness. Corresponding to every type of positional consciousness is a phantasy-correlate: phantasy-experiencing, phantasy-wishing, phantasy-desiring. The correlates of phantasy are fictions; the correlates of positionality are actualities. Phantasy has its own constitutive accomplishment: concordantly maintained fictions yield the image of a concordant being. Every formation crystallizing in free activity is a "free possibility, a possibility of phantasy." An important connection between positional and phantasy consciousness occurs when actuality is refabricated in assumption ("assuming that this house were green")—the positional accomplishment of assumption is a spontaneous activity presupposing phantasy.

### Chapter 1: Active Objectivation

### §52. Cognitive Interest and Striving for Knowledge

Active objectivation necessarily refers back to realms of objects already pre-constituted in the potentiality of the background—partly well-known objects with constituted structures, partly unfamiliar objects bearing the apperceptive imprint of actively constituted objects. An object exercising affection without any traits from active accomplishments is actually a limit-concept, an abstraction. Despite all passive positionality, without active formation, we cannot expect the firm path of knowledge given by guiding ideas. The ego must continually intervene with new formations and not allow objectivities to decay in the passive background. Taking as a point of departure positional pregivennesses in perception: an unfamiliar object emerges, we follow the affection, turn toward it, grasp it. In firm directedness toward the object, there is an intention intending beyond what is given toward a progressing *plus ultra*—a striving onward founded in an interest in the enrichment of the self of the object. This interest is a feeling—the joy in enrichment and the striving to appropriate the self ever more completely. This striving can take the form of a will to knowledge. The interest described is the motive of active objectivation, of "knowledge or cognition"—therefore called "cognitive interest."

### §53. Explicative Acquisition of Knowledge

Operating with the idea of pure, unbroken cognitive interest: the concept of theme designates the object as substrate and center of unitary interest. In perception of a thematically grasped object, the "examination" (*Betrachtung*—Husserl notes the antiquated sense as "striving after") goes immediately onward. The continuity of examining falls into discrete, distinct steps—a chain of single graspings forming an internally connected poly-thetic unity. Examining the object, different features strike the eye: luster, surface color, shape, portions, etc. Single acts follow one another. The object has properties; the examination follows inner affections and prominences, issuing in the progressive acquisition of knowledge of inner determinations. If the object is called S and its inner determinations a, β, γ, then the process does not simply issue in a sequence of disconnected graspings (as if there were a mere change of themata). Rather, in the entire process, S keeps the character of the theme—getting a hold of it moment by moment, property by property: each property is "something of object S, something from it and in it." The indeterminate theme S becomes the substrate of emergent properties constituted as its determinations.

### §54. The Active Synthesis of Identification. The Most Strict Sense of Objectivation

How does S remain the dominating theme when we pass to graspings of α, β? The explicative synthesis involves a peculiar identification: not total identity (as when we identify the same object in different presentations), but partial identification in which an object and its determination coincide, in which the consciousness of the substrate and of the property originally arises. In the discursive activity, an active synthesis of identification between S and α founds the active fulfillment of the thematic intention. S passes from the beginning mode of undetermined generality to the noematically new form of a determined S—the subject of determination. The cognitive movement then passes to β: the regard concentrated on α is expanded to be deepened in β, but the acquisition of the first partial knowledge is not lost. After being carried out, S is from now on the S determined as α for all further cognitive steps, with new modes: Sα, Sαβ, Sαβγ, etc. After the current processes of knowledge-acquisition, the object is constituted abidingly as determined by the respective determinations. It has internally taken up, as habitual knowledge, the sense-formations constituted in the described acts. "Object" in the complete and genuine sense is identical with itself and is originally constituted as the thematic object for an ego in identifying activity. There are as many fundamental formations of identification as there are modes and sides of objectivation.

### Chapter 2: The Fundamental Structures and Fundamental Forms of Judgment

### §55. The Possible Objectivation of Judgment and of its Components. Syntactic Matter, Syntactic Form, and Syntagma

In determinative judging, S is objectlike in the form of a substrate; the determination is constituted in the form of a property; both are non-independent and constituted in relation to one another. Together they constitute a harmonizing unity—a self-contained noema with objective sense and mode of being. If we call the objectivating process "judging" and each self-contained determinative process a "judicative act," then corresponding to it correlatively is the judgment or the judicative proposition (*propositio*). Husserl introduces three fundamental concepts: (1) syntactic form—the component of the form of judgment; (2) syntactic matter (judicative core)—the thematic content maintained identically in different syntactic forms; (3) syntagma—the unity of matter and form. The judgment is a syntactic unity completely self-contained, having syntactically formed matters in particular divisions with their encompassing syntactic unity.

### §56. The Ideality of the Judicative Proposition in the Sense of Omni-Temporality

In the progressive process of determining, judging builds upon judging correlatively building a judgment. The original core of judgment's being is a being in the form of temporality—but not the being of an *individuum*. The difference between individual and ideal temporal being is fundamental. The a-temporality of judgments is itself a distinctive shape of temporality: *omni-temporality*. A judgment is constituted in the process of becoming with its determinate temporal locus, possibly in many temporal acts at many temporal positions. But the proposition itself does not have any binding temporal position. Its ideality as the idea of a synthetic unity of becoming is the identity of something emerging at each temporal position in a temporal manner, yet the same "omni-temporally." Supra-temporality signifies this omni-temporality—a supra-temporal unity found in the temporal manifold.

### §57. The Forms of Judgment Issuing from Determinative and Total Identifications

Husserl systematically develops the forms of judgment. The most simple determinative form: "S is p." The chain: "S is p, q, r, etc." with various possible terminations (limited or open with the "etc."). The "etc." is a fundamental form: an open horizon for new properties, expressing the style of an open process. The forms can be multiplied through various operations: S returning to primary grasping after determination by p, yielding "S is p" and "S is q" which, under unitary thematic interest, are bridged through passive coinciding of S into a judgment built from two judgments. Contrasting forms arise when identifying connection is carried out on the determination-side: p becomes independent as substrate ("nominalization" or "becoming independent"), yielding subordinate clauses ("This thing is saturated-red"). Complete identifications yield judgments on a par with determinative ones ("S is identical to S'"), yielding many regulated combinations.

### Chapter 3: The Syntactic and the Object-Theoretical Directions of Examination

### §58. Sensible and Intellectual Objects. Substrate-Objects and Determination-Objects. Independent and Non-independent Objects

Husserl investigates the objects constituted through judgment. Sensible objects are constituted in simple sensibility; intellectual objects are constituted through intellectual activity (judgments, propositions, states-of-affairs). Substrate-objects are objects in the form of subject; determination-objects are constituted in the form of predicate or property. Independent objects can stand for themselves; non-independent objects exist only as determinations of other objects. These distinctions are not merely grammatical but ontological—they concern the mode of being of what is constituted in judging.

### §59. Whole and Part. Sets as Special Cases of Wholes

Husserl develops the part-whole analysis in the context of active synthesis. Wholes are constituted through the active synthesis of parts; sets (*Mengen*) are special cases of wholes constituted through collective grasping ("and"-connection). The relation of whole to part involves material connection: the parts are dependent upon one another and upon the whole. In contrast, the members of a set need not have any material connection—they are united merely through the collective act. The difference between whole and set corresponds to the difference between materially relevant relations and mere relations of comparison. A state-of-affairs is a whole insofar as it can be divided up, but a whole is not a state-of-affairs.

### §60. Non-independent Members of Judgment, Independent Judgments and Interconnections of Judgment

Husserl analyzes the connections between judgments: how non-independent members of judgment function within larger judicative unities, how independent judgments can be interconnected through overarching syntheses, and how the forms of judgment-connection arise (conjunction, disjunction, hypothetical connection). These interconnections constitute higher-level objectivities—states-of-affairs that are themselves complexes of simpler states-of-affairs.

### §61. Logical Sense

Husserl introduces the concept of logical sense as distinguished from perceptual or noematic sense. Logical sense is constituted through the specifically judicative operations—it is the What of the judicative proposition, the propositional content in its syntactic articulation. The concept of logical sense is broader than that of linguistic meaning (Bedeutung): it encompasses the entire structured content of judgment including its syntactic forms.

### §62. The Distinction between State-of-affairs and Judicative Proposition

Husserl distinguishes the state-of-affairs (*Sachverhalt*) from the judicative proposition (*Urteilssatz*). The proposition is the correlate, the "What" of the judicative act. When we substantivize the proposition in the form of "that" statements ("that S is a"), we have a new level of objectivation fulfilling the proposition: the state-of-affairs, the judgment maintained as valid. This distinction is foundational for formal logic and for the theory of truth.

### Chapter 4: The Gradation of Objectivation

### §63. Two Dimensions of the First Level of Objectivation: Mere Intuition and the Dimension of Explicative Examination

Husserl surveys the gradation of objectivation as a genetic gradation of thematic accomplishments. The first level is that of intuition that examines prior to all explication: the thematic object (the one enduring tone) with its flowing What-content. The second level is examination that delves into and extends beyond. The examination that delves into content is explication: what was previously merely thematic content now becomes thematic object, entering step by step into partial unity of identity with the S being held onto. The examination that extends beyond passes over to different objects where commonalities are constituted passively through connecting syntheses. In both types, passive synthetic unities are produced containing excitations for possible determinations—but this is not yet the activity of determinative synthesis, not yet judging.

### §64. The Second Level of Objectivation: Active Relating and Determining. The Task of a Universal Theory of Relation

The second main level is that of judgment—the determining process found prior to conceptualization: the activity of relating one theme to another. Here the enrichment of sense passively produced in examination occasions an active transition to activities of determination that form relations. States-of-affairs are constituted as the products of determinative activity. The universal investigation into the categorial realm, according to its forms and pure laws, makes up the discipline called "formal logic"—a systematic disclosure of radically different genera of relations possible for the object in formal generality: forms and pure laws of states-of-affairs, a formal classification and axioms of relations. Husserl calls for a universal theory of relation as one of the fundamental tasks of formal logic.

### §65. The Third Level of Objectivation. The Conceptualizing Judgment on the Basis of the Consciousness of the Universal

**§65a. Relations of Comparison, Association of Similarity as its Foundation, and Direction of Interest toward the Universal** (pp. 346–350): The third level concerns conceptualizing judgment. Relations of comparison (uniformity, similarity) serve as the transition. These relations are unique: they can place actualities in relation to possibilities; they can connect what is given without material connection. Association of similarity is the source of the consciousness of the universal. In the synthesis of coinciding that forms uniformity, an *ἕν ἐπὶ πολλῶν* (the unity of a universal) is originally constituted as object. The direction of interest toward the universal is not that of determining one uniform object in relation to another, but of grasping the One being constituted in the coinciding of individually grasped uniform objects.

**§65b. The Universal as a Novel Intellectual Object** (pp. 350–352): The universal is a novel objectlike formation, an intellectual objectlike formation from the original wellspring of activity—on the basis of sensibility. The universal of repetition (with open unending scope) is the concrete *eidos*, the concrete species or essence. Different species can themselves be compared, yielding species-universals and genera in a hierarchy. Similarity yields an analogue of partial identity; its gradation has uniformity as its *limes*. The universal of similarity is disclosed as a higher species through the transition from lowest species of similar elements to corresponding higher species.

**§65c. As-Such-Judgments** (pp. 353–355): Thinking takes on the form of the function of the "as such" (*Überhaupt*), governing the entire judicative thinking of the higher level. The universal is constituted in relation to a field of openness, unleashing the function of the "whatsoever" (*Beliebigkeit*). We can choose any "this" or "that" whatsoever as the determinative theme. The whatsoever enters as a thematically formative activity into judging itself, constituting new forms: As-Such-objects and As-Such-relations, i.e., universal and particular judgments. It is fundamentally different to have this table before us thematically and determine it, versus making a judgment about a table as such—about any table whatsoever. In the latter case, this table is characterized as example, as whatsoever, as "representative," and the theme is a table as such. "With this, the realm of universal judging is opened up, the realm of law-giving, the knowledge of law-giving for itself and for all judgments as such, and therefore for all possible objectlike formations." Possibility judgments and law-judgments are articulated; free variation reveals lawful regularity. These syntheses and categorial objects occurring in natural experience are not yet "critical" and must be submitted to transcendental clarification.

---

This completes the Main Text of the *Analyses Concerning Passive and Active Synthesis*. The arc from Part 1 through Part 3 traces the complete trajectory of Husserl's transcendental logic: from the preliminary considerations establishing the vocation of logic and the necessity of beginning with passive sense-constitution (Part 1), through the comprehensive investigation of passive synthesis in its four dimensions—modalization, evidence, association, and the in-itself of consciousness (Part 2), to the ascending investigation of active synthesis—cognitive interest, explication, determinative judgment, the structures and forms of judgment, whole-part analysis, logical sense, the gradation of objectivation from mere intuition through determination to conceptualization, and the constitution of the universal and as-such judgments (Part 3). The movement from the transcendental aesthetic (Part 2) to the transcendental genetic logic (Part 3) is not a mere juxtaposition but a genetic ascent: each higher level of active objectivation is shown to be motivated from below by the passive syntheses investigated earlier, and each active accomplishment in turn sinks back into passivity as sedimented habitual knowledge.

## Supplementary Texts

### Section 1: First Version of Main Text Part 2 (1920/21)

This section reproduces the earliest original version of the lectures from 1920/21, corresponding to §§12–40 of the Main Text Part 2 that Husserl "unfortunately reworked" in 1923 and "partly improved, partly spoiled" in 1925/26. Italicized passages were left intact and reappropriated in later lectures. The section comprises 26 numbered subsections covering the same thematic territory as the Main Text but in their original formulation.

**§§1–4: Modalities of Being; Prominence; Modalization of Immanent Objects; Types of Lived-Experience as Formal Structures** (pp. 357–367): Husserl opens the original version by criticizing the misunderstanding of modalities of being by philosophical logicians and epistemological psychologists (especially Brentano and Meinong), who had distinguished "mere presentation" from "judgment" in every perception. Against this, Husserl argues that there is nothing like a "mere perceptual presentation" without a modality of being. Belief and its modification is not something added to intentions but a "recasting of concordance"—as inseparable from the intention as timbre is from the tone. One must not treat the noema as a "piece in consciousness" and then add a mode of being. What constitutes the modalizing accomplishment is the system of intentions running their course in concordance or inhibition. Husserl then addresses how non-prominent and prominent elements function in consciousness, the modalization of immanent objects (not only transcendent ones), and the crucial point that "types of lived-experience" are not empirical facts but formal structures of consciousness as such—essential structures that any possible consciousness must exhibit.

**§§5–9: Presentifications; Retention and its Modalization; Empty Expectation; Presentifications of What is Present** (pp. 367–374): Husserl analyzes the various forms of presentification (Vergegenwärtigung) as necessary components of perceptual lived-experience: concrete retention and its modalization, empty concrete expectation and its modalization, and concrete empty presentifications of what is temporally present. Even presentifications of something present are universal occurrences of consciousness—a point essential for understanding how the co-present world is constituted.

**§10: Fundamental Types of "Presentations"** (pp. 375–379): Husserl lays out the basic typology of presentations: perception, retention, protention, remembering, expectation, co-presentification—each with its distinctive temporal orientation and mode of giving its object.

**§§11–12: Fulfilling and Merely Disclosive Intuitions; Further Clarification** (pp. 379–386): These sections present the original version of the distinction between fulfilling and merely disclosive (picturing) intuitions—the same distinction developed more fully in Division 2 of the Main Text. The key insight: not every bringing-to-intuition is confirming, and the difference between disclosure and genuine fulfillment is fundamental for the theory of evidence.

**§§13–16: Passive Processes of Experience; Transcendental Logic; Corroboration and Verification; Verifiability of Experiential Belief** (pp. 385–400): Husserl recapitulates the general framework of transcendental logic, the distinction between passive corroboration (Bewahrheitung) and active verification (Bewährung), and poses the question whether every experiential belief is verifiable—the same question that drives Division 2, Chapter 3 of the Main Text.

**§§17–22: The In-Itself; Memorial Illusion; Remembering and Association; Kant's Productive Imagination; Development of the Problem** (pp. 400–414): These sections present the original versions of the analyses of the in-itself of consciousness, the origin of memorial illusion through commingling, and the role of association and remembering. Husserl's discussion of Kant's doctrine of the synthesis of productive imagination is significant: he identifies Kant's "productive synthesis" with what he calls passive constitution—"nothing other than the team-work of the constantly higher developing intentionalities of passive consciousness." He cites the A-edition of the *Critique* because there the power of imagination is independent of the Understanding rather than subordinated to it.

**§§23–26: Immediate and Mediate Awakening; Association in the Impressional Sphere; The Lawful Regularity of Retention; Expectation and Association** (pp. 414–424): The concluding sections of the First Version cover associative awakening (immediate and mediate), the role of association in the impressional sphere for remembering and protention, the lawful regularity of retention, and the connection between expectation and association—the same themes that became Division 3, Chapters 3–4 and Division 4 in the later version.

### Section 2: Appendices

The Appendices constitute a massive supplementary apparatus of 39 appendices, keyed to specific sections of the Main Text. They range from brief clarificatory notes to substantial independent essays. I treat them here in groups corresponding to the Divisions of the Main Text they supplement.

**Appendices to Part 2 (Appendices 1–28):**

**Appendices 1–4 (To §§6–15): Modalization** (pp. 425–444): Appendix 1 describes the phenomenon of conflict without regard to position-taking—isolating the purely passive level of conflict prior to any egoic intervention. Appendix 2 analyzes sense and modality of being in both perception and remembering, deepening the parallel between perceptual and memorial modalization. Appendix 3 treats the evidence of possibilities as such and the modal modification *in infinitum*—the iterable character of modalization. Appendix 4, on levels of decision, receptivity, and spontaneity, provides a more nuanced account of the gradations between pure passivity and full spontaneous activity, identifying "receptivity" as a transitional level.

**Appendices 5–7 (To §§16–20): Evidence** (pp. 445–449): Appendix 5 distinguishes intuitive presentations from empty presentations more carefully. Appendix 6 analyzes the relationship between sense and intuition. Appendix 7 treats belief and intention, deepening the analysis of the tendentious character of doxic intending.

**Appendix 8 (To §§24–25): The Apodicticity of Remembering** (pp. 451–472): This is the longest and most important appendix in the collection, comprising 12 sub-sections. It addresses the consequences of assuming that remembering is dubious, two types of transcendental reduction with respect to remembering, evidences within the flux of perception, remembering as reproduction and its relation to retention, levels of clarity of remembering, deception and apodicticity in remembering, the modes of the past of something repeatedly remembered, remembering and its horizon of expectation, remembering distant pasts, and—most strikingly—the immortality of the transcendental ego and the impossibility of the transcendental ego being born. Husserl argues that within genetic phenomenology, transcendental subjectivity as the source of temporalization cannot be contained within time, and hence neither is born nor dies. Only a broadening to generative phenomenology would integrate birth and death as constitutive world-features. The appendix concludes with apodicticity of remembering versus expectation, and a recapitulation.

**Appendices 9–10 (To §25): Modes of Givenness; Possession of the Self in Remembering** (pp. 474–477): These appendices distinguish proximity and distance with clarity from obscurity as veiledness and nebulousness, and analyze the dialectic of possession and concealment in the memorial relation to the past.

**Appendices 11–15 (To §§26–27): Association and Time-Consciousness** (pp. 477–491): Appendix 11 clarifies the concept of associative causality—a motivational, not natural-scientific, concept. Appendix 12 provides a crucial note on the fundamental founding of the doctrine of original time-consciousness, indicating its limits. Appendix 13 treats the primordial present and retentions. Appendix 14 analyzes the accomplishment of the association of simultaneity—how coexistent data are synthetically connected. Appendix 15 treats unitary consciousness and its correlate, the identical object—the constitutive accomplishment of identity through time-consciousness.

**Appendices 16–19 (To §§28–36): Similarity, Uniformity, Association** (pp. 491–519): Appendix 16 treats the connection of similarity. Appendix 17 analyzes sensible connection of similarity, sensible uniformity, and *eidos*—providing an important link between the passive-genetic analyses and the theory of essences. Appendix 18 treats association and synthesis in their general relationship. Appendix 19, "On the Phenomenology of Association," is a substantial essay providing a broader overview of the phenomenology of association as a whole.

**Appendices 20–25 (To §§30–40): Individuation, Affection, Awakening, Expectation** (pp. 519–535): Appendix 20 treats time as the form of individuality and subjective transformation. Appendix 21 analyzes sensible, multi-radiating affection and the sensible group as a genuine collective objectlike formation. Appendices 22–23 address the empty horizon and the knowledge of it, and the question concerning the potentiality of the empty horizon. Appendix 24 treats the effect and cause of awakening. Appendix 25 analyzes kinaestheses and potential expectations.

**Appendices 26–28 (To §§45–47): Remembering, Evidence, Determinability** (pp. 536–547): Appendix 26 treats the repetition and essential identity of rememberings—the condition under which iteratively produced rememberings constitute the same past. Appendix 27 distinguishes two fundamental concepts of evidence: self-givenness as such (the broader concept) and pure self-giving (the stricter concept approaching the *limes* of absolute clarity). Appendix 28 addresses the problems of the definite determinability of the world—whether the objective world is in principle fully determinable, connecting the passive-synthesis analyses to the problematic of natural science and the ideal of complete determination.

**Appendices to Part 3 (Appendices 29–39):**

**Appendices 29–31 (To §§52–55): Theme, Determination, Syntactic Formation** (pp. 548–553): Appendix 29 clarifies theme and theoretical interest. Appendix 30 treats determining as a cognitive act. Appendix 31 analyzes syntactic formation in more detail than the Main Text.

**Appendices 32–36 (To §§56–59): Ideality, Determination, Substrates, Multiplicity, Sets** (pp. 553–565): Appendix 32 distinguishes the ideality of objects of sense from the ideality of the species—a crucial distinction for understanding how the omni-temporality of propositions differs from the ideality of universals. Appendix 33 treats main and subordinate determination and the linguistic forms corresponding to them. Appendix 34 distinguishes absolute substrates from substrates that have become independent through nominalization. Appendix 35 treats multiplicity and judgments of multiplicity. Appendix 36 analyzes the relationship between set and whole in greater detail.

**Appendices 37–39 (To §§63–64): Object and Interest, Categorial Relations, Theory of Relation** (pp. 566–576): Appendix 37 provides a much fuller analysis of the distinction between object and content of interest—a distinction introduced in §63 but deserving its own extended treatment. Appendix 38 treats categorial and non-categorial connections and relations—a fundamental logical distinction. Appendix 39 sketches the task of a universal theory of relation, providing a programmatic outline for the systematic investigation of all possible forms of relation among objects.

### Section 3: Related Essays

Two substantial essays, stemming primarily from 1920/21 with some pages possibly from 1923.

**A. Perception and its Process of Self-Giving (pp. 577–590):** Three subsections: (1) Immanent and Transcendent Perception—recapitulating and deepening the analysis of §4 of the Main Text, emphasizing how objects pregiven passively in experience differ fundamentally from objects generated in spontaneous acts. (2) Temporal and Spatial Perspectives—analyzing the perspectival character of temporal and spatial givenness and how they differ structurally. (3) Time and Space as *principia individuationis*—developing the thesis that time and space function as forms of possible individuality, connecting to the analyses of §30 in the Main Text and to the broader problematic of *Thing and Space* (Hua XVI). Husserl here articulates the earth-ground as constitutive of the lived-body and the spatiality of nature—pointing toward the generative dimension that exceeds the scope of the *Analyses* proper.

**B. Consciousness and Sense—Sense and Noema (pp. 591–623):** Nineteen subsections providing an extended meditation on the relationship between consciousness, sense, and noema. Key themes include: the relationship between perception and memory (§1); presentification and pictorial imaging (§2); the phenomenon of "self-forgetful remembering" where the ego is not "awake" in memory (§3); the complexity of the ego (§4); memories of the future and memories of the present (§5); the clarification of the immanent sense-structure of memories (§6); time as the form of all senses of the object (§7); the relationship between Now and originariness (§8); time-consciousness (§9); the structure of perception and consciousness in general (§10); noematic and noetic directions of description (§11); identical sense and noematic modes (§12); the relationship between primordial impression, retention, and protention (§13); retention and remembering (§14); remembering and objectivation, including the concept of "object" (§15); the temporal extension of the object as the extension of sense (§16); reproductive sense and modes of the past (§17); the noematic attitude (§18); and the object-pole and whether the objective sense is ideally identical (§19). This essay provides the most sustained treatment in the entire volume of the noematic dimension of the analyses and its relationship to the concept of sense (*Sinn*) as developed across Husserl's work from the *Logical Investigations* through *Ideas I* to the genetic phenomenology of the *Analyses*.

### Section 4: On Static and Genetic Phenomenological Method

Three texts from manuscript B III 10 and Hua XIV, representing the first explicit formulation of the distinction between static and genetic phenomenological methods. Steinbock placed these texts at the end of the English edition to give them prominence as the methodological key to the entire undertaking of the *Analyses*.

**A. Static and Genetic Phenomenological Method (pp. 624–634):** Husserl distinguishes two kinds of laws of genesis: (1) laws for the sequences of particular events in the stream of lived-experience (e.g., the necessary connection of retentions to elapsed lived-experiences, laws of association for the emergence of reproductions and expectations); (2) lawful regularities that regulate the formation of apperceptions. Static phenomenology proceeds from objectlike formations as leading clues and asks how they are constituted in consciousness—it is "the phenomenology of leading clues, the phenomenology of the constitution of leading types of objects in their being." It construes essential possibilities for lived-experiences and their nexuses, and thereby for a monad in which they may occur, without inquiring after the genesis of the monad. "But attending to constitution is not attending to genesis, which is precisely the genesis of constitution and operates as genesis in a monad." Genetic phenomenology, by contrast, attends to original becoming in the temporal stream—itself an originally constituting becoming—and to the "motivations" that function genetically, showing how consciousness arises out of consciousness and how constitutive accomplishments are carried out in the process of becoming. Static constitution becomes a leading clue to genetic constitution; the results of static analysis are then taken up and reinterpreted from the genetic perspective. The "natural concept of the world" (the lifeworld) can itself function as a leading clue. Husserl describes the reversal: what was initially seen as "higher" and "more complex" (genesis) is retroactively understood as more concrete and more fundamental, while the "simple" static structures are now grasped as more abstract.

**B. The Phenomenology of Monadic Individuality and the Phenomenology of the General Possibilities and Compossibilities of Lived-Experiences. Static and Genetic Phenomenology (pp. 635–645):** Husserl distinguishes: (1) phenomenology of possible phenomena and phenomenal interconnections in monads in general; (2) phenomenology of monadic individuality—the laws establishing what the individual unity and discreteness of a monad requires, what belongs necessarily to it as its proper nature, its universal form, and what species of elements this form necessarily contains. The individual unity of the monad is subject to laws of genesis. The phenomenology of absolute individuality must clarify the development of individual phases arising from one another, each having its law of individuality. Laws of compossibility in temporal coexistence and succession, and laws of necessary succession, are distinguished. The question of ordering necessary phenomenological investigations—static and genetic phenomenology—is the fundamental methodological question. Husserl asks whether static phenomenology might need to be understood from the very beginning as concerned with constitutive occurrences (i.e., as already implicitly genetic). The laws of the individuality of the monad concern "the genesis of constitution"—the problem that drives the entire investigation of the *Analyses*.

**C. The Intersubjective Validity of Phenomenological Truth (pp. 646–648):** In this final text, Husserl addresses the question whether phenomenological truths, gained through the egological reduction, can claim intersubjective validity. After the phenomenological reduction, the ego can say *ego cogito, ego sum* in absolute Cartesian evidence. All truths adopted by the phenomenological ego cannot straightforwardly have the sense of intersubjective validity. Essential truths have logical general validity (the universality of the "as such"), but the question of validity for every possible knower points to a different direction. All truths are referred back to possible doxic consciousness and its subjects, hence to phenomenological essential truths. The intersubjective validity of phenomenological essential truths is indubitable and in principle prefigured in the purely phenomenological sphere. Within eidetic-transcendental phenomenology, the possibility of phenomena of alien ego-subjects in open multiplicity belongs to the dominant realm. Through eidetic variation, the general operative nexus emerges: a possible pure ego as such standing in relation to an open unending multiplicity of other egos in relationships of empathy, I-you relationships, and communicative interaction. Every truth into which the phenomenological ego has insight is thereby intersubjectively valid—valid beyond the empirical ego. This text, despite its brevity and sketchiness, indicates the direction in which the genetic phenomenology of the *Analyses* would need to be expanded toward a generative phenomenology of intersubjective constitution.
