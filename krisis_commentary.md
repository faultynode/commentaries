# The Crisis of the European Sciences and Transcendental Phenomenology: A Paragraph-by-Paragraph Commentary

## Preface

Husserl opens the work by announcing that the present treatise, begun here and intended to continue in a series of further articles in the journal *Philosophia*, attempts to ground the unavoidable necessity of a transcendental-phenomenological reorientation of philosophy. It does so by way of a teleological-historical reflection on the origins of our critical scientific and philosophical situation. The work is thereby to serve as an independent introduction to transcendental phenomenology. The key claim is that the path into phenomenology is not merely systematic but must pass through history — specifically, through a critical retrieval of the hidden teleology of modern European intellectual life. The phrase "teleological-historical reflection" (*teleologisch-historische Besinnung*) signals that Husserl is not doing empirical history but a reflective inquiry into the inner meaning-direction of the philosophical tradition.

The second paragraph of the preface explains the occasion: the work grew out of the elaboration of ideas that formed the essential content of a lecture cycle delivered in November 1935, at the invitation of the *Cercle Philosophique de Prague pour les recherches sur l'entendement humain*, half in the hospitable rooms of the German University and half in those of the Czech University in Prague. This situates the text biographically: it belongs to Husserl's last years, composed under conditions of political exclusion from German academic life, and carries the urgency of a thinker aware that time is short.

## I. The Crisis of the Sciences as Expression of the Radical Life-Crisis of European Humanity

### § 1 Is there, in view of their constant successes, really a crisis of the sciences?

Husserl anticipates that the very title of his lectures will provoke objection. He says he must be prepared for resistance in a setting dedicated to the sciences. A crisis of the sciences as such — can this be spoken of seriously? Is not such talk, heard often today, an exaggeration? A crisis of a science means nothing less than that its genuine scientificity, the whole way it has set itself its task and developed its methodology, has become questionable. This may apply to philosophy, which in the present threatens to succumb to skepticism, irrationalism, and mysticism. It may apply to psychology insofar as it still raises philosophical claims and does not simply wish to be one positive science among others. But how could one speak seriously of a crisis of the sciences as such, including the positive sciences — pure mathematics, the exact natural sciences — which we never cease to admire as models of rigorous and supremely successful scientificity? Husserl here performs a rhetorical strategy: he inhabits the voice of the objector, the working scientist confident in method, in order to give that voice full expression before turning it against itself.

The next paragraph continues the objection. Certainly the sciences have proven changeable in the overall style of their systematic theory and methodology. They recently broke through a rigidity that had threatened under the title of "classical physics" — threatening as the supposed classical completion of their centuries-tested style. But does the victorious struggle against the ideal of classical physics, or the ongoing dispute about the properly meaningful structure of pure mathematics, mean that previous physics and mathematics were not yet scientific? Or that they, even if burdened with certain obscurities or blindnesses, did not still acquire evident insights in their fields of work? Are these not compelling insights for us as well, we who have been freed from those blindnesses? Whether physics is represented by Newton, Planck, Einstein, or whoever else in the future, it was always and remains exact science. It remains so even if those are right who maintain that an absolutely final form of the overall structure of its theory is never to be expected or striven for. Husserl grants the objector the full weight of this argument: the positive sciences demonstrably work, they yield cumulative results, they progress through revolutions without losing their scientific character.

A third paragraph extends the concession to the human sciences (*Geisteswissenschaften*). Something similar holds for another great group of sciences that we customarily count among the positive sciences — namely, the concrete human sciences — however matters stand with their contested relation to the ideal of natural-scientific exactitude, a questionability that also already concerns the relation of the biophysical ("concretely" natural-scientific) disciplines to those of the mathematically exact natural sciences. The rigor of the scientificity of all these disciplines, the evidence of their theoretical achievements and their continually compelling successes, is beyond question. Only for psychology, however much it claims to be the abstract, ultimately explanatory foundational science for the concrete human sciences, will we perhaps not be so sure. But assessing the evident gap in method and achievement as one of a naturally slower development, one will nonetheless fairly generally let it pass as well. In any case, the contrast between the "scientificity" of these groups of sciences and the "unscientificity" of philosophy is unmistakable. Husserl here concedes the first protest of the scientists, confident in their method, against the title of the lectures — but this concession is strategic, setting up the reframing that follows.

### § 2 The positivistic reduction of the idea of science to mere factual science. The "crisis" of science as the loss of its significance for life

Husserl pivots to a different line of approach. Perhaps from another direction of consideration — namely, proceeding from the general complaints about the crisis of our culture and from the role assigned to the sciences therein — motives arise for subjecting the scientificity of all the sciences to a serious and very necessary critique, without thereby giving up their primary, methodologically unassailable sense of scientificity. He announces that this altered direction of consideration is indeed what he intends to set in motion.

The next paragraph begins to carry out the pivot. In this altered perspective, we will soon become aware that the questionability at which psychology has been ailing, not just in our day but for centuries — a crisis peculiar to it — has a central significance. This crisis brings to light puzzling, irresolvable unintelligibilities even in the modern mathematical sciences, and along with this, a kind of world-riddle foreign to earlier times. All of these lead back to the riddle of subjectivity and therefore hang inseparably together with the riddle of psychological subject-matter and method. This is offered only as a first anticipatory indication of the deeper meaning of the project.

Husserl then describes the historical turning point. Our starting-point is a reversal in the general evaluation of the sciences that occurred at the turn of the last century. It concerns not their scientificity, but what they had meant and could mean for human existence. The exclusivity with which, in the second half of the nineteenth century, the entire worldview of modern human beings let itself be determined by the positive sciences and be blinded by the "prosperity" owed to them signified an indifferent turning-away from the questions that are decisive for a genuine humanity. Mere factual sciences make mere factual human beings (*Bloße Tatsachenwissenschaften machen bloße Tatsachenmenschen*). This is one of the most famous sentences in the *Crisis*. It announces the existential register: the crisis of the sciences is not internal to their methodology but concerns their capacity to address questions of human meaning.

The reversal in public evaluation was especially unavoidable after the First World War, and in the younger generation it has become an outright hostile mood. In our vital need, Husserl reports the younger generation saying, this science has nothing to say to us. It excludes in principle precisely the questions that, for human beings given over to the most fateful upheavals in our unhappy times, are the most burning: the questions about the meaning or meaninglessness of the whole of this human existence. Do they not demand, in their generality and necessity for all human beings, also general reflections and their answering out of rational insight? They concern, after all, human beings as freely deciding in their comportment toward the human and extra-human environment, as free in their possibilities for rationally shaping themselves and their surroundings. What does science have to say about reason and unreason, about us human beings as subjects of this freedom? Physical science obviously has nothing to say — it abstracts from everything subjective. As for the human sciences, which in all their particular and general disciplines do consider human beings in their spiritual existence, in the horizon of their historicity — their strict scientificity demands, we are told, that the researcher carefully exclude all evaluative positions, all questions about the reason or unreason of the human beings and cultural formations under study. Scientific, objective truth is exclusively the determination of what the world, the physical as well as the spiritual world, factually is.

But can the world and human existence in it truly have a meaning, Husserl asks, if the sciences let only the objectively determinable count as true, if history has nothing further to teach than that all forms of the spiritual world, all the life-bonds, ideals, norms that gave human beings support at any time, form like fleeting waves and dissolve again, that it was always so and always will be, that reason must always turn into unreason, beneficence into torment? Can we content ourselves with this? Can we live in this world whose historical course is nothing other than an unceasing concatenation of illusory surges and bitter disappointments? Husserl here presses the existential charge to its limit. The question is whether a science that has nothing to say about meaning deserves the name of a guide for human life.

### § 3 The grounding of the autonomy of European humanity through the new conception of the idea of philosophy in the Renaissance

Husserl turns to history. It was not always so that science understood its demand for rigorously grounded truth in the sense of that objectivity which governs our positive sciences methodically and which, extending far beyond them, has given support and general currency to a philosophical and worldview-based positivism. The specifically human questions were not always banished from the realm of science, nor was their inner relation to all sciences left out of consideration. As long as things stood otherwise, science could claim a significance for European humanity, which from the Renaissance onward was reshaping itself entirely anew — indeed, the leading significance for this reshaping. Why it lost this leadership, why an essential change occurred — the positivistic restriction of the idea of science — this is important to understand for the purposes of these lectures.

European humanity in the Renaissance undertakes a revolutionary reversal. It turns against its previous, medieval mode of existence, devalues it, and wills to reshape itself in freedom. Its admired model is ancient humanity. It wills to reproduce that mode of existence in itself. What does it grasp as the essential thing about the ancient human being? After some hesitation, nothing other than the "philosophical" form of existence: freely giving oneself, one's entire life, its rule from pure reason, from philosophy. Theoretical philosophy is the first thing. A superior world-contemplation, free from the bonds of myth and tradition altogether, is to be set to work — a universal knowledge of the world and of humanity in absolute freedom from prejudice. Philosophy as theory makes not merely the researcher free; it makes every philosophically educated person free. To theoretical autonomy there follows practical autonomy. In the guiding ideal of the Renaissance, the ancient human being is the one who shapes himself with insight born of free reason. This means, for the renewed "Platonism": it is a matter of reshaping not only oneself ethically but the whole human environment, the political and social existence of humanity, from free reason, from the insights of a universal philosophy.

In accordance with this ancient model, which first took hold in individuals and small circles, a theoretical philosophy was to come into being once more — not taken over in blind traditionalism, but arising afresh from one's own research and critique. Husserl emphasizes that the idea of philosophy received from the ancients was not the scholastic concept familiar to us, which covers only a group of disciplines: it changed not insignificantly soon after being taken up, but formally it retained in the first centuries of the modern era the sense of the one all-encompassing science, the science of the totality of what is. Sciences in the plural are only non-independent branches of the one philosophy. In a bold, indeed extravagant intensification of the sense of universality — which already sets in with Descartes — this new philosophy strives for nothing less than to encompass, in the unity of a theoretical system, all questions that are at all meaningful, in an apodictically evident methodology and in an infinite but rationally ordered progression of research. A single edifice of definitive, theoretically connected truths, growing from generation to generation to infinity, was to answer all conceivable problems — problems of fact and problems of reason, problems of temporality and of eternity.

The positivistic concept of science in our time is therefore, historically considered, a residual concept. It has dropped all the questions that were included in the now narrower, now broader concepts of metaphysics, including all the obscurely so-called "highest and ultimate questions." All those questions that were excluded share an inseparable unity: they contain, whether explicitly or implicitly in their meaning, the problems of reason — of reason in all its particular forms. Reason is explicitly the theme in the disciplines of knowledge (true and genuine, rational knowledge), of true and genuine valuation (genuine values as values of reason), of ethical action (truly good action, action from practical reason). Here reason is a title for "absolute," "eternal," "supertemporal," "unconditionally" valid ideas and ideals. When the human being becomes a "metaphysical," a specifically philosophical problem, it is as a being of reason that it is in question. If its history is in question, what is at stake is "meaning," reason in history. The problem of God obviously contains the problem of "absolute" reason as the teleological source of all reason in the world, of the "meaning" of the world. Naturally, the question of immortality is also a question of reason, as is no less the question of freedom. All these "metaphysical" questions, broadly taken, the specifically philosophical ones in the ordinary way of speaking, exceed the world as the universe of mere facts. They exceed it precisely as questions that have the idea of reason in mind. And they claim a higher dignity over against questions of fact, which in the order of questions lie beneath them. Positivism decapitates philosophy, so to speak.

Already in the ancient idea of philosophy, whose unity lies in the inseparable unity of all being, a meaningful order of being was co-intended, and accordingly, metaphysics — the science of the highest and ultimate questions — was accorded the dignity of the queen of sciences, whose spirit first gave the ultimate meaning to the cognitions of all other sciences. The renewing philosophy took this over too, and indeed believed it had discovered the true universal method by which such a systematic philosophy, culminating in metaphysics, must be built up — indeed, seriously, as *philosophia perennis*. From this we understand the élan that animated all scientific undertakings in the eighteenth century, which called itself the philosophical century, and filled ever-wider circles with enthusiasm for philosophy and all the individual sciences as its branches. Hence that ardent educational drive, that zeal for a philosophical reform of education and of the entire social and political forms of human existence, which makes this much-maligned Age of Enlightenment so worthy of veneration. We possess an imperishable testimony to this spirit in the glorious Schiller-Beethoven hymn "Ode to Joy." Today we can only follow this hymn with painful feelings. No greater contrast is thinkable than that with our present situation.

### § 4 The failure of the initially successful new science and its unclarified motive

If the new humanity, animated and made happy by that high spirit, could not endure, this could only happen because it lost the impetus-giving faith in a universal philosophy of its ideal and in the viability of the new method. And so it actually happened. The method proved itself capable of working in the positive sciences with unquestionable successes. But in metaphysics, or in the specifically philosophical problems, things stood otherwise, although there too there was no lack of hopeful, seemingly well-succeeding beginnings. The universal philosophy in which these problems were — unclearly enough — united with the factual sciences took the form of impressive but unfortunately not self-unifying but rather successively displacing system-philosophies. One might still have been convinced in the eighteenth century that unification would come, that a structure impervious to critique and expanding theoretically from generation to generation would be reached — as was indisputably the case in the positive sciences to universal admiration. But this conviction could not be maintained in the long run. Faith in the ideal of philosophy and method directing the movements from the beginning of the modern era began to waver.

This happened not merely for the external motive that the contrast between the persistent failure of metaphysics and the unbroken and ever more enormous swelling of the theoretical and practical successes of the positive sciences grew to immense proportions. Such things had their effect on outsiders and on the scientists who, in the specialized operation of the positive sciences, had increasingly become unphilosophical specialists. But even in researchers wholly filled with the philosophical spirit and therefore centrally interested in the highest metaphysical questions, an ever more pressing sense of failure set in — and indeed in them from the deepest, albeit wholly unclarified, motives, which protested ever more loudly against the well-rooted self-evidences of the governing ideal. Now begins a long period, reaching from Hume and Kant into our own day, of passionate struggle to achieve a clear self-understanding of the true grounds of this centuries-long failure. Naturally a struggle taking place in the very few who are called and chosen, while the mass of others quickly finds its formula for calming itself and its readers.

### § 5 The ideal of universal philosophy and the process of its inner dissolution

A strange turn of the whole of thinking was the necessary consequence. Philosophy became a problem to itself and, understandably at first, in the form of the possibility of a metaphysics, which, following what was said earlier, implicitly affected the meaning and possibility of the entire problematic of reason. As for the positive sciences, they stood at first as unassailable. But the problem of a possible metaphysics encompassed *eo ipso* the problem of the possibility of the factual sciences as well, since these had their relational meaning, their meaning as truths for mere domains of what is, within the inseparable unity of philosophy. Is reason separable from what is, where cognizing reason determines what is? The question suffices to make the anticipatory indication understandable: the entire historical process has a very remarkable shape, one coming into view only through an interpretation of the hidden innermost motivation — not the shape of a smooth development, not that of a continuous growth of enduring spiritual acquisitions or of a transformation of spiritual forms, concepts, theories, systems explicable from the accidental historical situations. A determinate ideal of a universal philosophy and of an accompanying method makes the beginning, as a primal institution (*Urstiftung*) of the philosophical modern era and of all its lines of development. But instead of this ideal being able to take effect in actuality, it undergoes an inner dissolution. This motivates, in opposition to the attempts to carry it forward and to renew and stabilize it, revolutionary new formations, more or less radical. Thus what now actually becomes the problem — that of the genuine ideal of a universal philosophy and its genuine method — becomes the innermost motive force of all historical philosophical movements. But this means that ultimately all the modern sciences, in the sense in which they were grounded as branches of philosophy and which they then permanently carried within themselves, fell into a peculiar crisis, increasingly felt as puzzling.

This crisis does not attack the specialized scientific character of the sciences in their theoretical and practical successes, yet it thoroughly shakes their entire truth-meaning. What is at stake is not a matter of a special cultural form, "science" or "philosophy," as one among others in European humanity. For the primal institution of the new philosophy is, according to what was said earlier, the primal institution of modern European humanity, and indeed of a humanity that, in contrast to the previous medieval and ancient one, wills to renew itself radically through its new philosophy and only through it. Accordingly, the crisis of philosophy means the crisis of all modern sciences as members of the philosophical universality — a crisis at first latent, but then increasingly coming to light — of European humanity itself, in the entire meaningfulness of its cultural life, in its entire "existence."

The skepticism regarding the possibility of a metaphysics, the collapse of the faith in a universal philosophy as guide of the new human being, means precisely the collapse of the faith in "reason," understood as the ancients opposed *episteme* to *doxa*. It is reason that ultimately gives meaning to all that is supposedly real, to all things, values, ends — namely, their normative reference to what, since the beginnings of philosophy, the word truth — truth in itself — and correlatively the word being — *ontōs on* — designates. With this, the faith in an "absolute" reason, from which the world has its meaning, also falls — the faith in the meaning of history, the meaning of humanity, in its freedom, namely as the capacity of the human being to give rational meaning to its individual and universal human existence. If the human being loses this faith, it means nothing other than: it loses the faith "in itself," in its own true being, which it does not always already have, not already with the evidence of the "I am," but only has and can have in the form of the struggle for its truth, for making itself true. Everywhere, true being is an ideal goal, a task of *episteme*, of "reason," set against the being that is in *doxa* unquestioningly "self-evident," merely supposed. In the ground, everyone knows this distinction, referred to their true and genuine humanity, just as truth as goal, as task, is not alien even in everyday life — though here only in isolation and relativity. But philosophy exceeds this preliminary form. In the first, original primal institution, ancient philosophy grasps the extravagant idea of a universal cognition referred to the totality of what is, and sets this as its task. But precisely in the attempt at its fulfillment — and this already makes itself felt in the opposition of the ancient systems — the naive self-evidence of this task is increasingly transformed into an unintelligibility. More and more, the history of philosophy, seen from within, takes on the character of a struggle for existence — a struggle of the philosophy that lives out its task straightforwardly, in the naive faith in reason, against the skepticism that negates or empiristically devalues it.

The person of the present, having become what they are in this development, finds themselves in the greatest danger: of sinking in the skeptical deluge and thereby losing their own truth. In this distress, reflecting, our gaze wanders back over the history of our present humanity. Self-understanding and thereby inner stability can be won only through the clarification of the unity-meaning that has been innate in it from its origin, with the newly instituted task that moves philosophical attempts as their motive force.

### § 6 The history of modern philosophy as the struggle over the meaning of the human being

Considering the effect of the development of philosophical ideas on humanity as a whole (that is, the part that does not itself philosophically investigate), Husserl draws a broad conclusion. The inner understanding of the movement of modern philosophy — unified despite all its contradictoriness — from Descartes to the present first makes possible an understanding of this present itself. The true, alone significant battles of our time are the battles between the humanity that has already collapsed and the humanity that is still on solid ground but is struggling for that ground or for a new one. The actual spiritual battles of European humanity as such take place as battles of the philosophies — namely, between the skeptical philosophies (or rather, unphilosophies, which have retained only the word but not the task) and the actually living philosophies. But the vitality of the latter consists in this: that they struggle for their genuine and true meaning and thereby for the meaning of a genuine humanity. To bring latent reason to a self-understanding of its possibilities, and thereby to make the possibility of a metaphysics visible as a true possibility — this is the only way to put a metaphysics, or a universal philosophy, into the laborious course of actualization.

With this alone is it decided whether the *telos* inborn in European humanity with the birth of Greek philosophy — to will to be a humanity out of philosophical reason and to be able to be only as such, in the infinite movement from latent to manifest reason and in the infinite striving of self-normation through this its truth and genuineness as a humanity — is a mere historical-factual delusion, an accidental acquisition of an accidental humanity amid wholly other humanities and historicities; or whether, rather, what first broke through in Greek humanity was something that is essentially contained in humanity as such, as its *entelechy*. Humanity in general is essentially human-being in generatively and socially connected humanities, and if the human being is a rational being (*animal rationale*), it is so only insofar as its whole humanity is a humanity of reason — latently directed toward reason or openly directed toward the entelechy that has come to itself, has become manifest for itself, and now, in essential necessity, consciously guides the becoming of humanity. Philosophy, science, would accordingly be the historical movement of the disclosure of the universal reason "inborn" in humanity as such.

This would truly be so, Husserl adds, if the movement — still unfinished to this day — had proven to be the entelechy that has come into the course of pure effectuation in the genuine and right way; or if reason had in fact become fully conscious for itself in its essentially proper form — that is, manifest in the form of a universal philosophy proceeding in consistently apodeictic insight, normating itself through itself in apodeictic method. Only then would it be decided whether European humanity bears an absolute idea within itself and is not merely an empirical anthropological type like "China" or "India"; and whether the spectacle of the Europeanization of all foreign humanities betokens the rule of an absolute meaning belonging to the meaning of the world, rather than a historical nonsense of the world. Husserl does not assert European superiority as a fact but poses it as a philosophical question: is the claim of reason merely parochial, or does it express something universally human that happened to emerge first in Greece?

We are now certain that the rationalism of the eighteenth century — its way of seeking the demanded ground of European humanity — was a naivety. But is the genuine meaning of rationalism to be abandoned along with this naive and, when thought through consistently, even absurd rationalism? And how does it stand with the serious clarification of that naivety, that absurdity, and with the rationality of the praised and demanded irrationalism? Must not irrationalism, if we are to listen to it, convince us as one that reasons and grounds? Is its irrationality not perhaps once again a narrow-hearted and bad rationality, and a worse one than that of the old rationalism? Is it not even the rationality of "lazy reason," which evades the struggle for a clarification of the ultimate pre-givennesses and the goals and ways that are, from them, ultimately and truly rationally prescribed?

### § 7 The project of the investigations of this work

Husserl now addresses his audience directly, as fellow philosophers. What can and must reflections of the sort just carried out mean for us? Were we here just to hear an academic speech? Can we simply return to the interrupted professional work on our "philosophical problems" — that is, to the further construction of our own philosophy? Can we do this seriously, with the certain prospect that ours, like that of all present and past fellow-philosophers, will have only its fleeting daily existence within the flora of ever newly growing and dying philosophies?

Herein lies our own distress — the distress of all of us who are not literary-philosophers but, educated by the genuine philosophers of the great past, live for truth and only so living are and want to be in our own truth. But as philosophers of this present we have fallen into a painful existential contradiction. The faith in the possibility of philosophy as a task, and thus in the possibility of a universal cognition, we cannot give up. In this task we know ourselves to be called as serious philosophers. And yet — how to hold fast to the faith that has meaning only in relation to the one, single goal common to us all: philosophy? Husserl here performs a moment of existential crisis within the philosophical vocation itself.

We have also already realized, at least in the most general terms, that human philosophizing and its results have, in the total human existence, a significance that is anything but that of merely private or otherwise limited cultural purposes. We are therefore — how could we ignore it — in our philosophizing, functionaries of humanity. The quite personal responsibility for our own genuine being as philosophers carries within itself at the same time the responsibility for the true being of humanity, which is being only as being toward a *telos* and, if at all, can come to actualization only through philosophy — through us, if we are in earnest philosophers. Is there here — in this existential "if" — an evasion?

Our first historical reflection has not only made clear the factual present situation and its distress as a sober fact; it has also reminded us that we, as philosophers, are heirs of the past in our goal-setting, our concepts, our problems, our methods. It is clear that what is needed — and what else could help here? — is thorough historical and critical self-reflection to provide, before all decisions, for a radical self-understanding: by inquiring back into what was originally willed as philosophy, and was continuously willed through all the historically communicating philosophers and philosophies. This under critical consideration of what, in goal-setting and method, displays that ultimate originality of origin which, once beheld, apodictically compels the will.

How this is actually to be carried out, and what the apodicticity that ultimately decides our existential being as philosophers is actually supposed to mean, is at first unclear. Husserl announces his own path: henceforth we go together, armed with the utmost skeptical but by no means in advance negativistic attitude of mind. We try to break through the crust of the externalized "historical facts" of the history of philosophy, questioning, exhibiting, testing their inner meaning, their hidden teleology. Gradually, on this path, at first little noticed but ever more pressingly, possibilities for wholly new turns of view announce themselves, pointing to new dimensions. Questions never asked arise, fields of work never entered show themselves, correlations never radically understood and grasped. Finally, they compel a fundamental transformation of the overall meaning of philosophy as it was the "self-evidently" accepted meaning through all historical shapes. With the new task and its universal apodeictic ground, the practical possibility of a new philosophy is demonstrated — through the deed. But it is also shown that the entire philosophy of the past was inwardly directed toward this new meaning of philosophy, although this was unknown to it. In this respect, the tragic failure of modern psychology in particular will become understandable and illuminated.

Husserl closes with a statement of intellectual humility and existential seriousness. He attempts to lead, not to instruct; only to exhibit, to describe what he sees. He raises no other claim than to be able to speak, first of all to himself and accordingly also before others, to the best of his knowledge and conscience, as one who has lived through the fate of a philosophical existence in its full seriousness.

## II. Clarification of the Origin of the Modern Opposition between Physicalistic Objectivism and Transcendental Subjectivism

### § 8 The origin of the new idea of the universality of science in the transformation of mathematics

Husserl sets up the project of Part II. The first task is to understand the essential transformation of the idea, the task of universal philosophy, which took place at the beginning of the modern era with the taking up of the ancient idea. From Descartes onward, the new idea governs the entire course of the philosophical movements and becomes the inner motive of all their tensions. The transformation initially sets in as one affecting outstanding individual sciences of the ancient heritage: Euclidean geometry and the rest of Greek mathematics, and subsequently Greek natural science. In our eyes these are pieces, beginnings of our developed sciences. But one must not overlook the enormous transformation of meaning by which first mathematics — as geometry and as formally abstract theory of number and magnitude — was given universal tasks of a principally new style, foreign to the ancients.

The ancients had already, guided by the Platonic doctrine of ideas, idealized empirical numbers, magnitudes, spatial figures, and transformed the propositions and proofs of geometry into ideally geometric ones. With Euclidean geometry, the highly impressive idea had arisen of a systematically unified deductive theory, resting on "axiomatic" fundamental concepts and propositions, proceeding in apodeictic inferences — a whole of pure rationality, a whole of nothing but unconditional, immediately and mediately evident truths. But Euclidean geometry and ancient mathematics as a whole know only finite tasks, a finitely closed a priori. To this also belongs the Aristotelian syllogistic, as an a priori superior to all others. So far the ancients came, but never so far as to grasp the possibility of the infinite task that for us is self-evidently bound up with the concept of geometrical space and of geometry as the science belonging to it.

For us, there belongs to ideal space a universal, systematically unified a priori, an infinite and yet — despite the infinity — internally closed and unified systematic theory, which, ascending from axiomatic concepts and propositions, permits every conceivable shape inscribable in space to be constructed in deductive univocity. What "exists" in geometrical space is already decided in all its determinacies in advance. Our apodeictic thinking "discovers" only what is already in advance, what already is in truth, progressing stage by stage into infinity. The conception of this idea — of a rational infinite totality of being with a rational science systematically dominating it — is the unheard-of novelty. An infinite world, here a world of idealities, is conceived as one whose objects do not become accessible to our cognition individually, incompletely, and as if by chance, but rather a rational, systematically unified method reaches — in infinite progression — ultimately every object according to its full in-itself-being.

But not only with respect to ideal space. Even more remote from the ancients was the conception of a similar but (as having sprung from formalizing abstraction) more general idea: that of a formal mathematics. Only in the beginnings of the modern era does the real conquest and discovery of the infinite mathematical horizons begin. There arise the beginnings of algebra, of the mathematics of continua, of analytic geometry. With the boldness and originality proper to the new humanity, the great ideal of a rational, all-encompassing science is very soon anticipated from here — or rather the idea that the infinite totality of what is in general is in itself a rational totality, which is to be mastered correlatively by a universal science, and indeed exhaustively. Long before this idea has matured, it is already determinative for further development as an unclear or half-clear presentiment. In any case, the new mathematics does not stand alone. Immediately its rationalism spreads to natural science and creates for it the completely new idea of mathematical natural science: the Galilean, as it was for a long time rightly called. As soon as this enters into the course of a successful realization, the idea of philosophy (as science of the world-all, of the totality of what is) is transformed altogether.

### § 9 Galileo's mathematization of nature

Husserl opens the central section — the famous "Galileo paragraph" — with a historical-conceptual framing. For Platonism, the real had a more or less perfect *methexis* (participation) in the ideal. This gave ancient geometry possibilities for a primitive application to reality. In the Galilean mathematization of nature, however, nature itself is idealized under the guidance of the new mathematics; it becomes — expressed in modern terms — itself a mathematical manifold.

He then poses the guiding questions: What is the meaning of this mathematization of nature, and how do we reconstruct the line of thought that motivated it? The world is given pre-scientifically in everyday sensory experience as subjective-relative. Each of us has their appearances, and for each they count as the truly real. We have long become aware of this discrepancy in our positing of being through our commerce with one another. But we do not therefore think there are many worlds. We necessarily believe in the world with the same things that merely appear differently to us. Do we have nothing more than the empty necessary idea of objectively existing things in themselves? Is there not in the appearances themselves a content that we must ascribe to true nature? Husserl here is reconstructing the "self-evidence" that motivated Galileo's thinking — without himself endorsing it as ultimately justified.

#### a) "Pure geometry"

Husserl examines the presuppositions of pure geometry, which Galileo received as an old tradition in living further development. He distinguishes sharply between the bodies and shapes of the intuitive life-world and the ideal limit-shapes (*Limesgestalten*) of geometry. In the intuitive surrounding world, we experience "bodies" — not geometrical-ideal bodies, but those we actually experience with their actual experiential content. However we may mentally rework them in phantasy, the free "ideal" possibilities we so gain are nothing less than the geometrically ideal possibilities, not the geometrically "pure" shapes — "pure" bodies, "pure" straight lines, "pure" planes. Geometrical space thus does not mean imagined space; phantasy can only transform sensible shapes into sensible shapes. Such shapes, whether in reality or phantasy, are thinkable only in gradations: of the more or less straight, flat, circular, etc.

The things of the intuitive surrounding world stand, in all their properties, in the flux of the merely typical. Their identity with themselves, their self-sameness and temporary endurance in sameness, is merely approximate, and likewise their sameness with others. This holds correspondingly for the abstractly grasped shapes of empirically intuitable bodies and their relations. These gradations characterize themselves as those of greater or lesser perfection. Practically, there is a perfect as such in the sense that a particular practical interest is fully satisfied. But in the change of interests, what is completely exact for one is no longer so for another. Yet in the human horizon, the ideal of perfection pushes ever further out. From here onward, we always already have an open horizon of conceivable, ever further improvable perfection.

Without going deeper into the essential interconnections, we can already understand that from the practice of perfection, in freely entering the horizons of conceivable perfection in the "again and again," limit-shapes everywhere prescribe themselves — invariant and never-to-be-attained poles toward which the respective series of perfection runs. The entire practice of idealization creates a domain of "pure" shapes, whose universal form is the itself co-idealized spatiotemporal form. Instead of real praxis, we now have an ideal praxis of "pure thinking" that keeps itself exclusively in the realm of pure limit-shapes. These have become habitually available acquisitions through the historically long-since-developed method of idealization and construction, to be exercised in intersubjective communalization. Through this mathematical practice we achieve what is denied us in empirical practice: "exactness." For the ideal shapes, the possibility arises of determining them in absolute identity, of recognizing them as substrata of absolutely identical and methodically univocally determinable properties. Husserl stresses that these ideal acquisitions remain objectively cognizable and available, like all cultural acquisitions arising from human productive achievement, even without their meaning-formation being explicitly renewed each time — they are apprehended through sensory embodiment (language, writing, drawings) and operatively handled.

The transition from the pre-scientific measuring art to the fully developed geometry is carefully traced. Measurement creates the possibility of univocally determining empirical shapes in terms of basic empirical shapes, making them intersubjectively and practically determinable. In the course of the awakened striving for "philosophical," objective cognition of the true being of the world, the empirical measuring art and its practically objectivizing function were idealized under a shift from practical to purely theoretical interest, thus passing over into the purely geometrical procedure of thought. The measuring art thus becomes the pathbreaker for the finally universal geometry and its "world" of pure limit-shapes.

#### b) The fundamental idea of Galilean physics: nature as mathematical universe

The relatively developed geometry that was available to Galileo was already traditionally given as a guide for his thinking, referring the empirical to mathematical limit-ideas. The measuring art too was already in tradition, in its intention toward ever-increasing accuracy of measurement. Galileo tells himself: wherever such a methodology has been developed, the relativity of subjective apprehensions, essential to the empirically intuitive world, has been overcome. For through this method we gain an identical, irrelative truth of which everyone capable of understanding and practicing the method can convince themselves. Here, then, we cognize truly existing being itself — though only in the form of a steadily improvable approximation from the empirically given toward the geometrical ideal shape, which functions as a guiding pole.

But this whole pure mathematics has to do with bodies and the bodily world only in an abstraction — namely, only with the abstract shapes in spatiotemporality, and moreover with these only as purely "ideal" limit-shapes. Concretely, however, we are given the real and possible empirical shapes only as "forms" of a "matter," of a sensory filling (*sinnliche Fülle*) — what presents itself in the so-called "specific" sense-qualities: color, tone, smell, and the like, in their own gradations. The question therefore arises: how can the concrete world — not just its abstract shape-side — be mathematized?

#### c) The problem of the mathematizability of the "fillings" (*Füllen*)

Husserl now addresses why the sensory qualities cannot be directly mathematized. These qualities too appear in gradations, and in a certain way measurement belongs to them too — "estimation" of the "magnitude" of cold and warmth, roughness and smoothness, brightness and darkness. But there is here no exact measurement, no increase in exactness and methods of measurement in the way that geometry provides. Where we moderns speak of measurement, of magnitudes, of methods of measurement, we generally already mean those referred to idealities, those that are "exact." We have only one, not a double universal form of the world; only one geometry, a geometry of shapes, not also a second one of fillings. The limit-shapes of these qualities are not idealizable in an analogous sense; their measurements cannot be referred to corresponding idealities of a constructible, already in ideality objectivized world. The concept of "approximation" does not have an analogous sense here — that of an objectivizing achievement.

The "indirect" mathematization of that side of the world which does not in itself have a mathematizable world-form is thinkable only in the sense that the specifically sensory qualities experienceable on intuitive bodies are, with the shapes essentially belonging to them, "twinned" in a quite special regulated way. There is an a priori pre-determination that the shape-side of the bodily world requires not merely a side of filling running through all shapes, but that every change, whether it concerns shape-moments or filling-moments, runs according to some causalities — immediate or mediate — that precisely require them. But it is not a priori evident that every experienceable change of specific qualities of intuitive bodies is causally dependent on occurrences in the abstract world-stratum of shapes — that it has, so to speak, its counterpart in the realm of shapes. Stated thus, this thought could appear downright adventurous.

#### d) Motivation of the Galilean conception of nature

Husserl asks what within the pre-given, already partially mathematized world could suggest the Galilean fundamental thought. There were, of course, very meager occasions for various but disconnected experiences within the pre-scientific total experience that suggested something like indirect quantifiability of certain sensory qualities. The Pythagoreans were already stirred by the observation of the functional dependence of pitch on the length of a vibrating string. Many other causal connections of a similar kind were generally known. In principle, every concretely intuitive process in the familiar surrounding world contains easily noticeable dependencies of filling-events on those of the shape-sphere. But in general there was no motive for analytically attending to the intertwining of causal dependencies. In their vague indeterminateness, they could arouse no interest. Matters stood differently where they took on a character of determinacy that made them suitable for determinate induction — and this leads back to measurement of fillings.

Not too far a stretch, Husserl suggests, for Renaissance people, who were inclined toward bold generalizations everywhere, and in whom correspondingly extravagant hypotheses immediately found a receptive public. Mathematics as the realm of genuine objective cognition was, for Galileo and already before him, at the focal point of the interest moving the "modern" human being for a philosophical world-cognition. There must be methods of measurement for everything that geometry encompasses in its ideality and apriority. And the whole concrete world must prove itself as mathematizable-objective if we follow up those individual experiences and really measure everything geometrically presupposable in them. If we do this, the side of specifically qualitative occurrences must be indirectly co-mathematized.

In explicating the Galilean self-evidence of a universal applicability of pure mathematics, Husserl notes that in every application to intuitively given nature, pure mathematics must abandon its abstraction from the intuitive filling, while leaving intact the idealized shapes. Through this, a co-idealization of the associated sensory fillings takes place in one respect. The concrete bodily world is thus furnished with infinities not only of shape but also of fillings. But this still does not yet give that "indirect mathematizability" that constitutes the properly Galilean conception of physics. What has been gained so far is initially only a general thought, or precisely expressed, a general hypothesis: that a universal inductivity reigns in the intuitive world, one announcing itself in those everyday experiences but hidden in its infinity.

For Galileo, however, this was not understood as a hypothesis. A physics was for him very nearly as certain as previous pure and applied mathematics. The hypothesis also immediately prescribes the methodical course of realization. First, it was a matter of gaining ever more methods of measurement, developing methods beyond those already factically developed — for example, measuring speeds and accelerations. But also pure mathematics itself needed richer development in constructive quantification — what later led to analytic geometry. Discovery is a mixture of instinct and method, Husserl notes. One must ask whether such a mixture can, in the strict sense, be philosophy, science, or world-cognition in the ultimate sense that alone could serve for a world- and self-understanding.

#### e) The confirmatory character of the fundamental hypothesis of natural science

The Galilean idea, Husserl observes, is a hypothesis of a most remarkable kind: actual natural science through the centuries of its confirmation is a confirmation of a correspondingly remarkable kind. For the hypothesis remains despite the confirmation, and further and forever remains, a hypothesis; the confirmation (the only one conceivable for it) is an infinite course of confirmations. It is the proper essence of natural science, it is a priori its mode of being, to be hypothesis unto infinity and confirmation unto infinity. In the infinite progression of correct theories, gathered under the title "natural science of a given time," we have a progression of hypotheses that are throughout hypotheses and confirmations. In the progression lies ascending perfection; taken as a total for the whole of natural science: it comes ever more to itself, to its "definitive" true being. But true nature lies in the infinite, not as does a pure straight line; even as an infinitely remote "pole," it is an infinity of theories and thinkable only as confirmation, hence referred to an infinite historical process of approximation.

#### f) The problem of the natural-scientific "formula"-meaning

Husserl now examines the decisive achievement of natural-scientific method. The actual assignment of the hypothetically substructed mathematical idealities — once one has them and retains their original meaning as still alive — suffices, through a mere thematic turn of gaze, to grasp the series of intensifications of intuitions (now counting as approximations) indicated by the quantities of functional coordination (the formulas), or to make them livingly present. In other words: once one is at the formulas, one already possesses in advance the practically desired foresight of what is to be expected in empirical certainty, in the intuitive world of concretely actual life, in which the mathematical is only a special praxis. The achievement decisive for life is thus the mathematization with its achieved formulas.

From this consideration one understands that the passionate interest of the natural scientist was directed immediately, with the first conception and execution of the method, at this decisive basic piece of the designated total achievement — at the formulas, and under the title "natural-scientific method" at the artful method of obtaining them. And again, it is understandable that one would be seduced into grasping in these formulas and their formula-meaning the true being of nature itself.

This "formula-meaning" now requires, with respect to the externalization of meaning that unavoidably sets in with the artful development and practice of the method, a closer clarification. The measurements yield measured numbers, and in general propositions about functional dependencies of measured magnitudes, instead of determinate numbers, numbers in general, stated in general propositions expressing laws of functional dependencies. Here the immense, in a certain direction blessed, in another fateful effect of the algebraic modes of designation and thought must be considered, which spread in the modern era since Vieta, that is, already before Galileo. This signifies an enormous expansion of the possibilities of arithmetical thinking inherited in the old primitive forms. It becomes a free systematic a priori thinking about numbers in general, number-relations, number-laws, entirely detached from all intuitive reality. The same is applied in all extensions, in geometry, in the whole pure mathematics of spatiotemporal shapes, and these are now thoroughly algebraically formalized in methodical intention. There arises an "arithmetization of geometry," an arithmetization of the whole realm of pure shapes. This arithmetization leads, as if of itself, in a certain way to the emptying of its meaning. The really spatiotemporal idealities transform themselves, so to speak, into pure numerical shapes, into algebraic formations. In algebraic reckoning, one lets the geometrical meaning recede of itself, indeed drop altogether; one calculates, only remembering at the end that the numbers were supposed to mean magnitudes. One does not calculate "mechanically" as in ordinary number-calculation; one thinks, invents, possibly makes great discoveries — but with an unnoticed shifted "symbolic" meaning.

Husserl traces this process through to its highest stage and simultaneous excess: a completely universal "formalization." This happens through the development of the algebraic theory of numbers and magnitudes into a universal and purely formal "analysis," "theory of manifolds," "logistic." Leibniz first beheld, far in advance of his time, the universal, internally closed idea of a highest algebraic thinking, a *mathesis universalis*, and recognized it as a task for the future. In its full and entire meaning, it is nothing other than a thoroughly executed formal logic — a science of the meaning-shapes of "something in general," constructible in pure thinking in empty-formal generality. Under it, the so-called "definite" manifolds are distinguished, whose definition through a "complete axiom system" gives the formal substrate-objects contained in them a peculiar totality in all deductive determinations, with which, one may say, the formal-logical idea of a "world in general" is constructed.

#### g) The emptying of meaning of mathematical natural science through "technization"

This outermost expansion of the already formal but limited algebraic arithmetic immediately has its application in all "concretely material" pure mathematics and thereby in mathematized nature; but also application to itself. It becomes, like arithmetic before it, drawn into a transformation through which it practically becomes an art — a mere art of obtaining results through a calculative technique according to technical rules, whose actual truth-meaning can be gained only in a thinking actually exercised on the themes themselves with material insight. Only those modes of thought and evidences are now in action that are indispensable to a technique as such. One operates with letters, connective and relational signs (+, ×, = etc.) and according to the game-rules of their combination — in fact essentially no differently than in a card or chess game. The original thinking, which properly gives this technical procedure meaning and gives the regular results truth, is here excluded.

In itself, Husserl concedes, the progression from material mathematics to its formal logicization, and the rendering-independent of the expanded formal logic as pure analysis or theory of manifolds, is something thoroughly legitimate, indeed necessary; likewise the technization with the occasional complete losing of oneself in merely technical thinking. All of this can and must be fully consciously understood and practiced method. But it is such only if care is taken that dangerous shifts of meaning are avoided — namely, by keeping the original meaning-bestowal of the method, from which it has the meaning of an achievement for world-cognition, permanently actually available; indeed more, that it is freed from all unquestioned traditionality that already, in the first invention of the new idea and method, let moments of unclarity flow into the meaning.

The whole interplay between experimental physics and mathematical physics and the enormous work of thought actually accomplished therein runs in a transformed horizon of meaning. One is to some degree conscious of the distinction between *technē* and science, but the reflective return to the proper meaning that the artful method is supposed to achieve for nature halts too early. It no longer reaches far enough even to lead back to the level of the idea of a mathematization of nature as prescribed by the creative Galilean meditation.

#### h) The life-world as the forgotten meaning-fundament of natural science

Husserl now draws attention to what is highly important: a substitution, already accomplished in Galileo, of the mathematically substructed world of idealities for the only real world, the really perceptually given world, the world that is ever experienced and experienceable — our everyday life-world (*Lebenswelt*). This substitution was immediately inherited by his successors, by the physicists of all the following centuries. This is one of the most consequential passages in the *Crisis*: it introduces the concept of the Lebenswelt as a critical tool, not as a nostalgic return to pre-scientific naivety, but as an identification of the ground that all science presupposes but forgets.

Galileo was himself an inheritor with respect to pure geometry. The inherited geometry and the inherited manner of "intuitive" conceiving, demonstrating, "intuitive" constructing, was itself no longer original geometry — was already, in this "intuitiveness," emptied of meaning. In its own way, ancient geometry was already *technē*, removed from the primordial sources of truly immediate intuition and originally intuitive thinking, from which so-called geometrical intuition first drew its meaning. Prior to the geometry of idealities came the practical art of land-measurement, which knew nothing of idealities. Such pre-geometric achievement was, however, the meaning-fundament for geometry — the fundament for the great invention of idealization. It was a fateful omission that Galileo did not inquire back into the originally meaning-giving achievement that, as idealization, was exercised on the primordial ground of all theoretical and practical life — the immediately intuitive world — and that yields the geometrical ideal formations.

Thus every occasional (or "philosophical") reflective return from the artful work to its proper meaning always halts at idealized nature, without carrying the reflections radically through to the ultimate purpose that the new natural science, together with the geometry inseparable from it, growing out of pre-scientific life and its surrounding world, was from the beginning supposed to serve — a purpose that must lie in this life itself and be referred to its life-world. The person living in this world, including the natural scientist, could pose all practical and theoretical questions only to this world; theoretically could concern only it, in its openly infinite horizons of unknownness. In the geometrical and natural-scientific mathematization, we measure out for the life-world — for the world constantly given to us as actual in our concrete world-life — in the open infinity of possible experiences, a well-fitting garb of ideas (*Ideenkleid*), that of the so-called objectively scientific truths.

The garb of ideas "mathematics and mathematical natural science" — or the garb of symbols, of symbolic-mathematical theories — encompasses everything that, for both scientists and educated people, represents and disguises the life-world as the "objectively actual and true" nature. The garb of ideas brings it about that we take for true being what is a method — a method there to improve, through "scientific" ones in a *progressus in infinitum*, the originally alone possible crude foresights within the life-worldly actually experienced and experienceable. The disguise of ideas makes it that the proper meaning of the method, the formulas, the "theories" remained unintelligible and was never understood in the naive origination of the method.

Thus the radical problem never became conscious: how such a naivety was actually possible as a living historical fact and continues to be possible; how a method that is really directed at a goal — the systematic solution of an infinite scientific task — and that incessantly yields indubitable results for this purpose, could ever have arisen and then could continue throughout centuries to function usefully, without anyone possessing a real understanding of the proper meaning and the inner necessity of such achievements. Does not science and its method resemble an obviously very useful and in this respect reliable machine, which everyone can learn to handle correctly, without in the least understanding the inner possibility and necessity of such achievements? But could geometry, could science have been designed in advance like a machine, from a similarly perfect — scientific — understanding? Would that not lead to a *regressus in infinitum*?

Galileo, the discoverer — or, to do justice to his predecessors, the completing discoverer — of physics is at once a discovering and a concealing genius. He discovers mathematical nature, the methodical idea; he breaks the path for the infinity of physical discoverers and discoveries. He discovers, against the universal causality of the intuitive world (as its invariant form), what has since then been called the causal law without further ado, the "a priori form" of the "true" (idealized and mathematized) world. All of this is discovery-concealment, and we take it to this day as plain truth.

#### i) Fateful misunderstandings as consequences of the unclarity about the meaning of mathematization

Husserl continues to examine the fateful consequences. Galileo is called the discoverer of physics, of physical nature, but he is at the same time a discovering and concealing genius. The mathematized nature, the Galilean nature, is taken as the nature itself. The garb of ideas that we throw over what is given in intuition — over the life-world — is mistaken for nature in itself. Husserl emphasizes that Galileo himself already stands in tradition: the ancient geometry he inherited was already technized, no longer in living possession of its original meaning-sources. The idealization through which geometry arose out of the pre-geometric measuring art was no longer reflectively available. And so Galileo could take over the assumption that the geometrical is immediately applicable to the world as its true in-itself — an assumption whose self-evidence was in fact a semblance.

Husserl traces the continuation of this error through the entire development of modern natural science. What is decisive is not merely an external error of application but a structural forgetting: the meaning-fundament of the entire enterprise — the life-world as the ground of all idealization — drops out of view. The formulas, the theories, come to be taken as the things themselves. The scientist moves within the mathematized nature as if it were the only world, while the actually experienced world from which all idealization grew and to which all scientific prediction must ultimately refer remains unthematized.

#### k) Fundamental significance of the problem of origin of mathematical natural science

Husserl insists on the principled significance of the problem of origin. The problem is not merely a historical curiosity but concerns the meaning and legitimacy of the entire modern scientific enterprise. If the meaning of mathematical natural science grew out of a praxis of idealization on the ground of the life-world, and if this meaning-origin has been forgotten through centuries of unreflected tradition, then the understanding of what natural science actually achieves — and what it does not achieve — depends on a retrieval of that origin. This retrieval is not a return to the pre-scientific, but a deepening of self-understanding. It is the task of the phenomenologist to uncover the layers of sedimented meaning and to restore the living evidence that gives the formulas their actual cognitive content.

#### l) Methodical characterization of our exposition

Husserl offers a methodological self-reflection on his own procedure. He has been engaged not in an external history of science but in an "intentional history" (*intentionale Historie*) — an inquiry into the meaning-genesis of the ideas and methods of modern natural science. The goal is not to reconstruct what Galileo happened to think psychologically, but to lay bare the structure of motivations, presuppositions, and meaning-transformations that constituted the Galilean idea. This intentional-historical approach is the method appropriate to the teleological understanding announced in the Preface. It does not replace empirical history but goes deeper, asking not merely what happened but what it meant — what was implicitly intended and what was unwittingly concealed.

### § 10 The origin of dualism in the governing exemplary role of natural science. The rationality of the world "more geometrico"

Husserl highlights an essential piece of the new way of looking at nature. Galileo, in his orientation toward the world from the standpoint of geometry and of what appears sensibly and is mathematizable, abstracts from subjects as persons of a personal life, from everything spiritual in every sense, from all cultural properties that accrue to things through human praxis. In this abstraction, the pure corporeal things result — but they are taken as concrete realities and in their totality become thematic as a world. One may well say that only through Galileo does the idea of a nature as an in-itself real, self-enclosed corporeal world come to light. Along with the mathematization, which too quickly became a self-evidence, this yields as consequence a self-enclosed natural causality in which all events are univocally and in advance determined. Thereby the dualism that immediately appears in Descartes is also prepared.

The conception of nature as an encapsulated, in itself real and theoretically self-enclosed corporeal world immediately brings with it a complete transformation of the idea of the world in general. It splits, so to speak, into two worlds: nature and the psychic world — the latter never attaining an independent worldliness through its mode of reference to nature. The ancients had individual investigations and theories about bodies, but no closed corporeal world as the theme of a universal natural science. They also had investigations of the human and animal soul, but could not have had a psychology in the modern sense. The splitting and transformation of meaning of the world was the understandable consequence of the exemplary role of natural-scientific method. In the mathematization of nature, the coexistence of the infinite totality of its bodies in spatiotemporality was supposed, considered in itself, as a mathematically rational one. This rationality had to become the model for all genuine cognition: the world must be in itself a rational world in the new sense of rationality taken from mathematics and mathematized nature, and philosophy must accordingly be constructible as a unified rational theory *more geometrico*.

### § 11 Dualism as the ground for the incomprehensibility of the problems of reason, as the presupposition of the specialization of the sciences, as the basis of naturalistic psychology

If the rationally natural-scientific nature is a world of bodies existing in itself, then the world in-itself had to be a world split in a previously unknown sense — split into nature in-itself and a distinct mode of being: psychic being. This immediately brought problematic difficulties, especially with respect to the idea of God. Does not rational being, to be thinkable at all, presuppose rational theory and a subjectivity accomplishing it? Does not nature, and the world in-itself altogether, presuppose God as absolutely existing reason? The separation of the psychic created increasing difficulties wherever problems of reason became pressing. Yet the force of rationalistic motives was still unbroken, and everywhere one proceeded with full confidence in the comprehensive execution of a rationalistic philosophy.

Every establishment of a special science was guided by the idea of a rational theory corresponding to it — a correspondingly rational domain. The specialization of philosophy into specialized sciences has accordingly a deeper sense, related exclusively to the modern orientation. Galileo's natural science did not arise through a specialization; rather, the subsequent new sciences specialized the idea of a rational philosophy motivated by the new natural science. Naturally, as soon as Descartes proclaimed the idea of rational philosophy and the separation of nature and mind, a new psychology appeared as the first requirement. It was immediately a psychology of a style wholly foreign to earlier ages, concretely designed as a psychophysical anthropology in the rationalistic spirit.

Husserl traces the consequences through Hobbes and Locke. The naturalism of Hobbes aims to be physicalism and has the model of physical rationality as its exemplar. The dualistic split works out in all subsequent sciences, producing split disciplines. Locke's image of the *white paper*, the *tabula rasa*, on which psychic data come and go with some regularity, like corporeal processes in nature, conveys the new naturalistic psychology's character. All the new sciences seem to succeed. Where physicalistic rationalism was not seriously feasible — as in metaphysics — one helped oneself with unclear weakening. Spinoza's *Ethica* stands as the classical example of the attempt to build a systematic philosophy *ordine geometrico*, comprehending nature, mind, and God within a single rational system.

### § 12 Overall characterization of modern physicalistic rationalism

Philosophy in its ancient origin willed to be "science" — universal cognition of the universe of what is, not vague and relative everyday cognition (*doxa*), but rational cognition (*episteme*). But the true idea of rationality and of universal science was not yet achieved in ancient philosophy — so believed the founders of the modern era. The new ideal was first possible on the model of the newly formed mathematics and natural science. What is the universal science of the new idea, ideally completed, other than omniscience? The world is apodictically to be seen as a rational systematic unity in which all details must be rationally determined to the last. Its system-form — at least the mathematical part — is in advance available and known. It need only be determined in its particularity, which is unfortunately possible only inductively. This is the — admittedly infinite — path to omniscience. One lives in the blissful certainty of a path running from the near to the far, from the more or less known to the unknown, as an infallible method of extending cognition, in which everything would really be cognized in its full "in-itself-being" — in infinite progression. With the ever-growing and ever more perfect cognitive mastery over everything, the human being also wins an ever more perfect mastery over its practical environment. Therein is also included the mastery over humanity itself, an ever greater power over its fate, and thus an ever fuller "blessedness." The human being is thus truly an image of God. The philosopher has, correlatively with the mathematization of the world, idealized himself and God in a certain way mathematically.

### § 13 The first difficulties of physicalistic rationalism in psychology: the incomprehensibility of performing subjectivity

Much earlier, however, the problematic character of the mathematization of the world — or of a rationalization unclearly modeled on it — made itself felt in the new naturalistic psychology. For its domain included the rational cognitive activities and cognitions of the philosophers, mathematicians, and natural scientists, in which the new theories arose as their spiritual formations and carried the ultimate truth-meaning of the world. This created such difficulties that already with Berkeley and Hume a paradoxical skepticism grew — felt as absurdity but not rightly graspable — that directed itself precisely against the models of rationality, against mathematics and physics, seeking to devalue their fundamental concepts and even the meaning of their domains as psychological fictions. In Hume it went to the end, to the uprooting of the entire ideal of philosophy. Affected was not merely the modern philosophical ideal but the entire philosophy of the past, the entire posing of the task of a philosophy as universal objective science. A paradoxical situation: highly successful and daily growing achievements of a great series of new sciences lay before everyone. And yet this entire achievement, this evidence itself, had become, in a certain new direction of gaze from psychology, completely unintelligible. World-riddles of a previously undreamt-of style now entered the scene, and they conditioned a completely new kind of philosophizing — "epistemological," "theory-of-reason" — and soon also systematic philosophizing of a completely new goal and method. This greatest of all revolutions designates itself as the turning of scientific objectivism into a transcendental subjectivism.

### § 14 Anticipatory characterization of objectivism and transcendentalism. The struggle between these two ideas as the meaning of modern intellectual history

Objectivism moves on the ground of the world given as self-evident through experience and asks about its "objective truth" — about what is unconditionally valid for every rational being, about what the world is in itself. To accomplish this universally is the business of *episteme*, of ratio, of philosophy. Thereby the ultimately existing is to be reached, behind which it would make no rational sense to inquire further.

Transcendentalism, by contrast, says: the meaning-of-being of the pre-given life-world is a subjective formation, an achievement of the experiencing, pre-scientific life. In it the meaning and the validity-of-being of the world is built up. As for the "objectively true" world of science, it is a formation of a higher level on the basis of pre-scientific experiencing and thinking. Only a radical inquiry back into subjectivity — into the subjectivity that ultimately brings about all world-validity with its content — and into the what and how of the rational achievements, can make objective truth intelligible and reach the ultimate meaning-of-being of the world. Thus what is first in itself is not the being of the world in its unquestioned self-evidence; rather, what is first in itself is subjectivity, as that which naively pre-gives the being of the world and then rationalizes or, what amounts to the same, objectivizes it.

But here a countersense threatens in advance, since it initially appears self-evident that this subjectivity is human being, hence psychological subjectivity. The mature transcendentalism protests against psychological idealism and claims, while contesting objective science as philosophy, to bring a completely new kind of scientificity — transcendental scientificity — onto the scene. The entire history of philosophy since the appearance of "epistemology" and of serious attempts at a transcendental philosophy is a history of the tremendous tensions between objectivistic and transcendental philosophy. The clarification of the origin of this inner splitting and the analysis of the ultimate motives of this most radical transformation of the idea of philosophy is of the greatest importance.

### § 15 Reflection on the method of our historical mode of observation

Husserl pauses to reflect on the character of his own procedure. The kind of observations he has to carry out is not that of historical observations in the usual sense. What is at stake is making the teleology in the historical becoming of philosophy — especially modern philosophy — intelligible, and simultaneously achieving clarity about ourselves as its bearers, in our personal intentional will its co-performers. We try to understand the unity that rules in all historical goal-settings, in the opposition and cooperation of their transformations, and through a constant critique that keeps in view the historical total-connection as a personal one, finally to behold the historical task that we can acknowledge as the only one personally our own — beheld not from outside, from fact, as though the temporal becoming in which we ourselves have become were a merely external causal succession, but from inside.

We who not only have a spiritual heritage but are also through and through nothing other than historically-spiritually-become, have only thus a truly our own task. We gain it not through the critique of some present or traditionally transmitted system, but only from a critical understanding of the total unity of history — our history. A historical backward-reflection of the kind in question is therefore really a deepest self-reflection aimed at a self-understanding of what one is actually after — as the one one is, as a historical being. Essentially, every primal institution (*Urstiftung*) has a final institution (*Endstiftung*) set as task for the historical process. It is accomplished when the task has come to completed clarity and thereby to an apodictic method. Philosophy as infinite task would thereby have come to its apodictic beginning, to its horizon of apodictic continuation.

### § 16 Descartes as primal founder both of the modern idea of objectivistic rationalism and of the transcendental motive that explodes it

Husserl returns to the historical narrative, turning to Descartes as the primal-founding genius of the entire modern philosophy. After Galileo had accomplished the primal institution of the new natural science, it was Descartes who conceived the new idea of universal philosophy and immediately set it into a systematic course of realization. It is Descartes who, with his *Meditations*, turns the philosophizing ego back upon itself — but he does so, Husserl will argue, in a way that contains both the founding gesture of modern objectivistic rationalism and the seed of the transcendental motive that will ultimately blow it apart.

### § 17 Descartes' return to the "ego cogito." Interpretation of the Cartesian epoché

Husserl now examines the movement of the first two Cartesian Meditations in a perspective that brings out its general structures — the movement to the *ego cogito*, the ego of the *cogitationes* of respective *cogitata*. He notes with characteristic irony that this is the "beloved examination question for philosophical children," yet in truth the first Meditations contain a depth so hard to exhaust that even Descartes could not do it — so little that he let the great discovery he already held in his hands slip away again. Every independent thinker should study these first Meditations with the greatest depth, not put off by the apparent primitiveness, by the foreknown use of the new thoughts for paradoxical and fundamentally wrong proofs of God, or by other obscurities.

Husserl reconstructs the Cartesian epoché: philosophical cognition must rest on a ground of immediate and apodictic cognition. The survey of one's previous convictions reveals that doubts or possibilities of doubt announce themselves everywhere. In this situation, it is unavoidable to begin with a radical skeptical epoché that questions the universe of all previous convictions, barring in advance all judgmental use of them. This Cartesian epoché is of an unprecedented radicalism: it encompasses not only all previous sciences (including mathematics), but even the pre- and extra-scientific life-world — the world of sensory experience that is always given in unquestioned self-evidence. For the first time, the lowest level of all objective cognition — experience in the ordinary sense, "sensory" experience, and correlatively the world itself — is placed in question "epistemologically."

If all positions regarding the being or non-being of the world are suspended, the ego performing the epoché is not enclosed within the objective domain of the epoché but is in principle excluded from it. It is necessarily the performer of the epoché. Herein lies the sought apodictic ground. However far I may push doubt, it is absolutely evident that I would still be — as doubter, as negator of all. A universal doubt cancels itself. The apodictic evidence "I am" stands at my disposal. But this evidence also includes a great manifold: *sum cogitans*, which concretely states *ego cogito — cogitata qua cogitata*. This encompasses all *cogitationes* and their streaming synthesis to the universal unity of a *cogitatio*, in which, as *cogitatum*, the world had and has validity-of-being for me — only now, as philosophizing, I may no longer simply carry out these validities and employ them cognitionally. My entire experiencing, thinking, valuing, and other act-life remains for me, only now what stood before my eyes as "the" world has become a mere "phenomenon." Here we would have an absolutely apodictic sphere of being, contained under the title Ego.

Through the epoché, Husserl continues, I have pressed forward to the sphere of being that in principle precedes every conceivable being-for-me and their spheres of being as their absolutely apodictic presupposition. I, the performing-I of the epoché, am the sole absolutely indubitable, the one that in principle excludes every possibility of doubt. Whatever else presents itself as apodictic — mathematical axioms, for example — leaves possibilities of doubt open; their claim to apodicticity is justified only if a mediate and absolutely apodictic grounding succeeds that leads them back to this sole absolute primal evidence.

### § 18 Descartes' self-misinterpretation: the psychologistic falsification of the pure ego won through the epoché

Husserl now reveals what was intentionally concealed in the preceding exposition. A hidden ambiguity in the Cartesian thoughts comes to light: there are two possibilities for grasping these thoughts, of which for Descartes only one was the self-evidently given one. The meaning of his presentations is factually univocal — but unfortunately this univocity stems from the fact that he does not really carry through the original radicalism of his thoughts, does not really subject all his pre-judgments and the world to the epoché. The ego is not a residuum of the world but the absolutely apodictic positing enabled solely by the epoché. The soul, however, is the residuum of a prior abstraction of the pure body, and this abstraction does not occur in the epoché but in the attitude of the natural scientist or psychologist on the natural ground of the pre-given world. A breach of consistency has entered through the identification of the ego of the epoché with the pure soul. The entire acquisition — the great discovery of this ego — is devalued by an absurd substitution: a pure soul has no meaning in the epoché at all, except as "soul" in "brackets," that is, as mere "phenomenon," just like the body. The new concept of "phenomenon" that first grows with the Cartesian epoché must not be overlooked.

Husserl remarks that in centuries almost no one took offense at the "self-evidence" of the possibility of inferences from the ego and its cogitative life to an "outside," and essentially no one posed the question whether, with respect to this egological sphere of being, an "outside" could have any meaning at all — which admittedly makes this ego into a paradox, the greatest of all riddles. Perhaps much, indeed for a philosophy everything, depends on this riddle.

### § 19 Descartes' overriding interest in objectivism as the ground of his self-misinterpretation

The Meditations worked themselves out in the fateful form of a substitution of the ego's own psychic I for the ego, psychological immanence for egological immanence, the evidence of psychic "inner" or "self-" perception for egological self-perception — and they have had this historical effect right up to the present day. Descartes does not make clear to himself that the ego — his de-worlded I through the epoché, in whose functioning *cogitationes* the world has all the meaning-of-being it can ever have for him — cannot occur as a theme in the world, since everything worldly draws its meaning from precisely these functions. Still less was it accessible to him that the ego, as it comes to discovery in the epoché as existing for itself, is not yet "an" I that can have other or many fellow-I's outside itself. It remained hidden from him that all such distinctions as I and You, inside and outside, first "constitute" themselves in the absolute ego.

### § 20 "Intentionality" in Descartes

The founding first Meditations were thus actually a piece of psychology. A most significant but quite undeveloped moment is to be highlighted: intentionality, which makes up the essence of egological life. Every *cogitatio* has its *cogitatum*: experiencing, thinking, feeling, willing something. Every one is in the broadest sense a "meaning" (*Meinen*), and thus to each belongs some mode of certainty. One already sees that the problem-title of intentionality inseparably encompasses the problems of understanding or reason. But there is no question of a real posing and treatment of the theme "intentionality." The whole supposed founding of the new universal philosophy from the ego is also to be characterized as an "epistemology" — a theory of how the ego in the intentionality of its reason brings about objective cognition, which for Descartes means: cognition transcending the ego metaphysically.

### § 21 Descartes as point of departure for the two lines of development: rationalism and empiricism

The line of development from Descartes leads in two directions. The rationalistic line proceeds through Malebranche, Spinoza, Leibniz, through the Wolffian school to Kant, the turning point. Here the spirit of the new rationalism unfolds in great systems: the conviction that the method *more geometrico* can realize an absolutely grounded, universal cognition of the world thought of as a transcendent "in-itself." Against this conviction — against such a reach of the new science into a "transcendent" — reacts English empiricism, though itself also strongly determined by Descartes. It is a reaction similar to that of ancient skepticism against the systems of rational philosophy. The new skeptical empiricism begins already with Hobbes. For us, however, Locke's critique of the understanding and its continuations in Berkeley and Hume are of greater interest, because of their immense further effects on psychology and epistemology.

### § 22 Locke's naturalistic-epistemological psychology

In the empiricist development, the new psychology demanded as correlate by the splitting off of pure natural science comes to its first concrete execution. Locke's great work aims from the outset at a new attempt at what Descartes' Meditations were supposed to accomplish: an epistemological grounding of the objectivity of the objective sciences. Of the depths of the Cartesian epoché and the reduction to the ego, Locke senses nothing. He simply takes over the ego as soul, which in the evidence of self-experience learns to know its inner states, acts, and capacities. Only what inner self-experience shows, only our own "ideas," are immediately evidently given; everything external is inferred.

Especially fateful for future psychology and epistemology is that Locke makes no use of the Cartesian first introduction of *cogitatio* as *cogitatio* of *cogitata* — of intentionality. He is blind to the entire distinction. The soul is grasped as a self-enclosed real, like a body; in his famous simile, like a writing tablet on which psychic data come and go. This data-sensualism with the doctrine of outer and inner sense dominates the psychology and epistemology of the centuries right up to the present day.

### § 23 Berkeley. — David Hume's psychology as fictionalistic epistemology: the "bankruptcy" of philosophy and science

The naiveties and inconsistencies of Locke lead to a rapid further development of his empiricism. The foundation remains sensualism. Berkeley reduces the corporeal things appearing in natural experience to the complexes of sensory data themselves. Hume goes to the end: all categories of objectivity — scientific and pre-scientific — are fictions. Mathematical concepts are fictions; so is the identity of persisting bodies, the identity of the person, and causality ("necessary succession"). The world in general, nature, the universe of identical bodies, the world of identical persons — and thereupon objective science — all transform into fiction. In truth, Hume ends in a solipsism. Like all skepticism and irrationalism, the Humean version cancels itself.

### § 24 The genuine philosophical motive of the shattering of objectivism, hidden in the countersense of Humean skepticism

Why does Hume's *Treatise* represent so great a historical event? The Cartesian radicalism of presuppositionlessness demanded subjectively directed considerations, the return to the cognizing I in its immanence. The empiricist skepticism brought to light what already lay undeveloped in the Cartesian fundamental consideration: that the entire world-cognition, pre-scientific as well as scientific, is an enormous riddle. Once it is universally seen — from Hume and from Berkeley with respect to nature — that "world" is a validity sprung from subjectivity, with all its content, the naivety of the talk of "objectivity" that leaves the actually concretely performing subjectivity entirely out of question is no longer possible once life comes into the focus of attention. The world-riddle in the deepest and ultimate sense — the riddle of a world whose being is being out of subjective performance — this and nothing else is Hume's problem. Kant, however, never pressed forward to this riddle itself.

### § 25 The "transcendental" motive in rationalism: Kant's conception of a transcendental philosophy

Hume holds a special place in history through the turn he effected in Kant's thinking. But Kant's system is not a continuation of Hume. His interpretation of Humean skepticism is conditioned by his own descent from the Wolffian school. The "revolution of the way of thinking" is directed not against empiricism but against the rationalism that had its great fulfiller in Leibniz and its most effective form in Wolff. Kant undertakes to show: if common experience is to be real experience of natural objects that are to be cognizable in objective truth, then the intuitively appearing world must already be a formation of the capacities "pure intuition" and "pure reason" — the same ones that express themselves in mathematics and logic. Reason has a double way of functioning: its systematic self-explication (pure mathematics as theory) and its constantly hidden functioning as constituting understanding for the continuously becoming meaning-formation "intuitive surrounding world."

Like the intuitive corporeal world, the natural-scientific world altogether is subjective formation of our intellect, only with the proviso that the material of sensory data stems from a transcendent affection by "things in themselves" that are in principle inaccessible to (objective-scientific) cognition. Kant's system is the first and, in inspiring scientific seriousness, executed attempt at a really universal transcendental philosophy, intended as strict science.

### § 26 Preliminary discussion of our guiding concept of the "transcendental"

Husserl clarifies his own use of the word "transcendental" in the broadest sense: it designates the original motive, meaning-giving in all modern philosophies since Descartes, that seeks in all of them to come to itself and to achieve its genuine and pure task-form and systematic effectuation. It is the motive of inquiring back into the ultimate source of all formations of cognition, of the cognizer's reflection on himself and his cognizing life, in which all scientific formations that count for him are purposively enacted, stored up as acquisitions, and have become or become freely available. This source has the title I-myself, with my entire actual and possible life of cognition, ultimately my concrete life altogether. The entire transcendental problematic revolves around the relation of this I — the "ego" — to my soul on one hand, and to the world of which I am conscious and whose true being I cognize in my own formations of cognition on the other.

### § 27 The philosophy of Kant and his successors in the perspective of our guiding concept of the "transcendental." The task of a critical position-taking

Husserl returns to Kant for a final assessment of Part II. Kant's system is indeed "transcendental-philosophical" in the defined general sense, though it is far from achieving a really radical grounding of philosophy. Kant never engaged the enormous depths of the Cartesian fundamental consideration. The conviction shared by all the great systems of German Idealism is that the objective sciences, however they estimate themselves as sites of the only true method and treasure-houses of ultimate truths, are not yet seriously sciences, not cognitions from ultimate grounding; they are also not cognitions of what is in ultimate truth. Only a transcendental-subjective method and, carried through as system, transcendental philosophy achieves that. Their meaning is not that the evidence of positive-scientific method is a deception, but that this evidence itself is a problem: the objective-scientific method rests on a never-questioned, deeply hidden subjective ground, whose philosophical illumination first brings out the true meaning of the achievements of positive science and correlatively the true meaning-of-being of the objective world.

## III. The Clarification of the Transcendental Problem and the Function of Psychology Related to It

### A. The path into phenomenological transcendental philosophy through the inquiry back from the pre-given life-world

### § 28 Kant's unspoken "presupposition": the self-evidently valid surrounding life-world

Husserl opens Part III by exposing Kant's unexamined presuppositions. Kant is certain that his philosophy overturns the prevailing rationalism, yet he himself has no awareness that his own philosophizing rests on unquestioned presuppositions. What Kant offers demands new work and above all critical analysis. One example of a great discovery — a mere pre-discovery — is the doubly functioning understanding: on the one hand, the understanding that explicates itself in normative laws through explicit self-reflection; on the other, the hiddenedly governing understanding, functioning as constituting understanding for the continuously formed and further-forming meaning-shape "intuitive surrounding world." This discovery could never be really grounded or even fully understood in the manner of the Kantian theory, as a result of his merely regressive method.

Husserl then begins to lay out the pre-given ground that Kant's critique of reason presupposes. Presupposed by the Kantian questions is the everyday life-world as existing — in which we all, including I the philosophizing one, have conscious existence — and no less the sciences as cultural facts in this world with their scientists and theories. We are in it objects among objects; yet on the other hand we are subjects for this world, as the experiencing, thinking, evaluating, purposively acting I-subjects for whom this surrounding world has only the meaning-of-being that our experiences, thoughts, valuations have given it. Husserl here describes the double status of the human being: as object within the life-world and as subject for whom the life-world has meaning.

He then describes the structures of perceptual consciousness in detail: the difference between change in the perceived object and change in the modes of appearance; perception as the primal mode of intuition presenting in originary self-presence; the role of kinaestheses and the lived body (*Leib*) in all bodily experience. The body is at all times present in the perceptual field in a completely unique sense of being — designated by the word "organ" in its original meaning: that wherein I as I of affection and action exist in a completely unique and completely immediate way, as that in which I immediately kinaesthetically govern. Husserl details the correlation between kinaesthetic systems and perceptual aspects: the manifold of appearances in which a body is perceivable as this one and same body corresponds in a distinctive way to the kinaestheses belonging to it.

He describes how we as concrete, full I-subjects — each as the full I-the-human-being — belong to the perceptual field and the world. All of this, Husserl insists, are the most obvious self-evidences. In life one certainly need not discuss them. But as a philosopher? Does not an infinite realm of never-questioned validities-of-being open up here, which are constant presuppositions of scientific and philosophical thinking?

It belongs to the self-evidences presupposed by all scientific thinking that the world is — always in advance — and that every correction of an opinion already presupposes the existing world, namely as a horizon of what is at the time undoubtedly accepted as existing. Objective science too poses questions only on the ground of this world that is constantly in advance, from pre-scientific life onward, existing. But questions of a new and soon most puzzling dimension arise concerning all these manifold presupposed validities.

### § 29 The life-world can be disclosed as a realm of subjective phenomena that have remained "anonymous"

As soon as we inquire back into those self-evidences — of which Kantian thinking, like everyone's thinking, makes use as unquestioned ready-made self-evidences — as soon as we become conscious of them as "presuppositions" and honor them with a universal and theoretical interest of their own, an infinity of ever new phenomena of a new dimension discloses itself to us, coming to light only through consistent penetration into the meaning- and validity-implications of those self-evidences. These are thoroughly purely subjective phenomena — not mere factual occurrences of psychophysical courses of sensory data, but spiritual courses that as such exercise in essential necessity the function of constituting meaning-formations.

No objective science, no psychology that wished to be a universal science of the subjective, no philosophy has ever made this realm of the subjective thematic and thus really discovered it. It is a realm of a thoroughly self-enclosed subjectivity, existing in its own way, functioning in all experiencing, all thinking, all living — hence inseparable everywhere — and yet never caught sight of, never grasped and conceptualized. Can philosophy fulfill its primal-founding sense as universal and ultimately grounding science if it leaves this realm in its "anonymity"?

### § 30 The lack of an intuitively exhibiting method as the ground for Kant's mythical constructions

One complains about the obscurities of Kantian philosophy, about the incomprehensibility of the evidences of his regressive method, his transcendental-subjective "capacities," "functions," "formings." In fact, Kant falls into a peculiar kind of mythical discourse, whose word-meaning indeed points to something subjective, but a mode of the subjective that we cannot in principle make intuitable for ourselves — neither through factual examples nor through genuine analogy. His transcendental concepts have a peculiar unclarity that for principled reasons can never be converted into clarity, never translated into a directly evidence-creating meaning-formation. Things would stand quite differently if Kant, not bound as a child of his time by its naturalistic psychology, had really radically taken on the problem of a priori cognition.

### § 31 Kant and the inadequacy of the psychology of his time. The opacity of the distinction between transcendental subjectivity and soul

Husserl traces the historical roots of the problem. The pre-given starting-point of all cognitive riddles corresponded to the development of modern philosophy according to its peculiar rationalistic ideal of science. In the construction of one of these sciences — psychology — riddles arose that called the whole of philosophy into question. The psychology of Locke found particularly interesting themes in the merely subjective of appearances and in everything that from the subjective side damages rationality. Was it not genuinely required that Locke took the sciences as psychic achievements and everywhere posed the questions of origin? This was indeed done by Locke in a superficiality and in a naturalism that culminated in Humean fictionalism. So Kant could not simply fall back on Locke's psychology. But was it right to drop the general character of the Lockean question? Must not every question prompted by Hume initially be correctly grasped as a psychological one?

When Kant in his questioning and regressive method constructs a transcendental subjectivity through whose hidden transcendental functions the world of experience is necessarily formed, he falls into the difficulty that a special property of the human soul (itself belonging to the world and thus co-presupposed) is supposed to accomplish the forming of this entire world. As soon as we distinguish this transcendental subjectivity from the soul, we fall into an unintelligibly mythical domain.

### § 32 The possibility of a hidden truth in Kant's transcendental philosophy: the problem of a "new dimension." The antagonism between "surface life" and "depth life"

Should a truth nevertheless dwell in the Kantian theory — as is in fact the case — this would only be possible if the transcendental functions belong to a dimension of living spirituality that, due to very natural hindrances, had to remain hidden from humanity and even from the scientists of millennia, while it can yet be made scientifically accessible through a method of disclosure appropriate to it. Husserl invokes the Helmholtzian image of surface-beings who have no inkling of the depth-dimension in which their surface-world is a mere projection. Everything that can become conscious to people in their natural world-life — whether experiencing, cognizing, purposively planning, acting — all of this remains on the "surface," which is yet only, though imperceptibly, the surface of an infinitely richer depth-dimension. A peculiar antagonism exists between engagement with this dimension and the occupations that make up the naturally normal human world-life.

### § 33 The problem of the "life-world" as a partial problem within the general problem of objective science

Husserl reminds us that science is a human spiritual achievement that historically and for every learner presupposes the intuitively given surrounding life-world. If science poses and answers questions, they are from the beginning questions on the ground of this pre-given world. Should this pre-given world itself become a theme, this requires a special care of preliminary reflection. The way the life-world emerges here as a scientific theme makes it appear as a serving, partial theme within the full theme of objective science as such. But it is clear that the question of the distinctive and constant meaning-of-being of this life-world for the human beings living in it has a perfectly good sense on its own. A life-world existed for humanity always before science. One can pose the problem of the mode of being of the life-world in and for itself, placing oneself wholly on the ground of this plainly intuitive world, putting out of play all objective-scientific opinions and cognitions.

### § 34 Exposition of the problem of a science of the life-world

Husserl now undertakes a detailed exposition of how a science of the life-world might look, working through a series of sub-questions.

In subsection (a), he draws the difference between objective science and science in general (*Wissenschaft überhaupt*). Objective science is not the only form of knowledge; the life-world has its own kind of truths, its own evidences, which are prior to and presupposed by all objective-scientific truths. The truths of the life-world are "subjective-relative," but they are not for that reason arbitrary: they have their own kind of evidence, their own kind of verification. A science of the life-world would have to capture what belongs to the life-world purely as life-world, before any scientific idealization.

In subsection (b), he examines the use that the objective sciences make of subjective-relative experience and the science that pertains to it. The objective sciences constantly fall back on the life-world: the physicist sees his measuring instruments, hears clicks, estimates visible magnitudes. The life-world is the constant ground of all scientific activity, even if it is never itself thematized by the sciences.

In subsection (c), Husserl asks whether the subjective-relative is the subject-matter of psychology. The answer must be nuanced: the subjective-relative is indeed connected with subjectivity, but psychology as a positive science has itself been shaped by the objectivist ideal and therefore cannot serve as the science of the life-world without transformation.

In subsection (d), the life-world is characterized as the universe of what is in principle intuitable — the "objectively true" world, by contrast, as in principle unintuitable, a "logical" substructure. The actually experienced world of everyday life, with its colors, sounds, warmth, is the only world we ever really live in. The world of physics is a theoretical construction layered over this experienced world.

In subsection (e), the objective sciences themselves, as subjective formations — as formations of a particular, theoretical-logical praxis — belong to the full concretion of the life-world. The sciences are themselves cultural formations within the life-world; they do not stand above or outside it.

In subsection (f), the problem of the life-world, instead of being a partial problem, turns out to be a philosophical universal problem. Once one has grasped that the life-world is the universal ground of all science, and that this ground itself demands investigation, the problem of the life-world absorbs all other philosophical problems.

### § 35 Analytic of the transcendental epoché. The first step: the epoché from objective science

Husserl explains that the peculiar nature of the task requires the access-method to the new science's working-field to be articulated in a multiplicity of steps, each of which has the character of an epoché — an abstention from natural-naive and already operative validities. The first necessary epoché, the first methodical step, has already come into our field of vision through the preceding preliminary reflection, but it requires an express universal formulation. What is required above all is the epoché with respect to all objective sciences. This does not mean merely an abstraction from them, as if we were fictively rethinking present human existence as though no science occurred in it. Rather, what is meant is an epoché from every co-performance of the cognitions of the objective sciences, an epoché from every critical position-taking interested in their truth or falsity, even with respect to their guiding idea of an objective world-cognition. In short, we perform an epoché with respect to the whole of objective theoretical interests.

In this epoché, however, the sciences and scientists have not disappeared for us. They are further what they earlier also were: facts in the unitary context of the pre-given life-world; only we no longer function as co-interested parties, as co-workers. We institute in ourselves only a special habitual direction of interest, with a certain vocational-like attitude to which a special "vocational time" belongs. Husserl stresses the vocational character of the phenomenologist's attitude: it is a habitual epoché that has its times of actualized work, while other times are devoted to other interests. This seemingly casual comparison carries a deeper point: the epoché is not a permanent negation of the world but a freely adopted attitude that one enters and leaves.

Yet Husserl immediately qualifies this comparison against trivialization. The equating of the new science with all "civic" vocations, indeed even with the objective sciences, signifies a kind of making-trivial. Perhaps it will even show itself that the total phenomenological attitude and its epoché are in their essence called upon to bring about a complete personal transformation, comparable initially with a religious conversion, but which beyond that harbors the significance of the greatest existential transformation that is assigned to humanity as humanity.

### § 36 How can the life-world, after the epoché from the objective sciences, become the theme of a science? Principled separation between the objective-logical a priori and the a priori of the life-world

Husserl opens with the question: is the life-world, as a universal scientific theme, already freed simply by the epoché vis-à-vis objective science? Do we thereby already have themes for scientifically universally valid statements, statements about scientifically determinable facts? How are we to understand the life-world as a field of such determinable facts established in advance? The life-world is the spatiotemporal world of things as we experience it in our pre- and extra-scientific life and know beyond the experienced as experienceable. We have a world-horizon as a horizon of possible thing-experience. Things: that means stones, animals, plants, also human beings and human formations; but all is there subjective-relative. If we now set the goal of an unconditionally valid truth about objects for all subjects, we are already on the way to objective science and have, with this goal-setting, exceeded the pure life-world with a kind of hypothesis.

Yet the embarrassment vanishes, Husserl notes, once we reflect that this life-world, in all its relativities, has its general structure. This general structure, to which everything relatively existing is bound, is not itself relative. We can attend to it in its generality and, with appropriate care, determine it once and for all and equally accessibly for everyone. The world as life-world has already pre-scientifically the "same" structures that the objective sciences presuppose as a priori structures and systematically unfold in a priori sciences. Pre-scientifically the world is already spatiotemporal; but with respect to this spatiotemporality, there is no talk of ideal mathematical points, "pure" straight lines, planes, or mathematical infinitesimal continuity. The life-worldly bodies are real bodies, but not bodies in the sense of physics. The categorical of the life-world has the same names but does not, so to speak, concern itself with the theoretical idealizations and hypothetical substructures of the geometers and physicists.

The idealizing achievement that brings about the higher-level meaning-formation and validity-of-being of the mathematical and every objective a priori is accomplished on the basis of the life-worldly a priori. This latter must first become a scientific theme in its own proper character, and then systematically the task must be posed of how, on this ground and in what ways of new meaning-formation, the objective a priori comes about as a mediate theoretical achievement. There would thus be needed a systematic separation of universal structures: universal life-worldly a priori and universal "objective" a priori. The so-called completely independent logic that the modern logisticians believe they can develop as the universal a priori fundamental science for all objective sciences is nothing other than a naivety. Its evidence lacks the scientific grounding from the universal life-worldly a priori that it constantly presupposes in the form of self-evidences never scientifically universally formulated. Only when this radical fundamental science exists can that logic itself become a science.

### § 37 The formally most general structures of the life-world: thing and world on the one side, thing-consciousness on the other

If we seek the formally general, the invariant persisting through all change of relativities in the life-world, by looking about freely, we hold involuntarily to what alone determines for us in life the sense of the word "world": the world is the totality of things, of things distributed in the world-form of spatiotemporality in a double sense "locally" (according to place in space, place in time) — the spatiotemporal *onta*. Here would lie the task of a life-worldly ontology, understood as a concretely general essential doctrine of these *onta*. But instead of lingering, Husserl prefers to advance to a much greater task that also encompasses this one.

To pave the way to this new thematic — also essentially concerning the life-world, yet not ontological — Husserl offers a general consideration. The life-world, in a recollection of what was repeatedly said, is for us the waking living always already there, in advance existing, "ground" for all praxis, whether theoretical or extra-theoretical. The world is not given to us, the waking, always somehow practically interested subjects, occasionally, but always and necessarily as the universal field of all actual and possible praxis, as horizon. Life is constantly living-in-world-certainty. Waking life is being awake for the world, constantly and actually "conscious" of the world and oneself as living in the world, really experiencing, really performing the certainty of being of the world.

There exists a fundamental difference in the manner of world-consciousness and thing-consciousness, while on the other hand the one and the other form an inseparable unity. Things, objects, are "given" as counting for us in some modes of certainty-of-being, but in principle only in such a way that they are conscious as things, as objects in the world-horizon. Each is something, "something from" the world, the world of which we are constantly conscious as horizon. This horizon is on the other hand conscious only as horizon for existing objects and cannot be actual without separately conscious objects. The world is not a being like a being, like an object, but existing in a uniqueness for which the plural is senseless. Every plural and each singular lifted from it presupposes the world-horizon. This difference of the mode of being of an object in the world and of the world itself obviously prescribes to both the fundamentally different correlative modes of consciousness.

### § 38 The two possible basic ways of making the life-world thematic: the naively natural straightforward attitude and the idea of a consistently reflective attitude directed at the "how" of the subjective manner of givenness of the life-world and the life-worldly objects

This most general feature of waking life is now the formal framework within which possible differences in the mode of performance of this life are possible. The first way, the naturally normal one, which for essential reasons must unconditionally take precedence, is that of straightforwardly living into the world-horizon toward whatever objects are given, in a normal unbroken constancy, in a synthetic unitariness running through all acts. This normal straightforward living toward objects means: all our interests have their goals in objects. The pre-given world is the horizon that encompasses all our goals, all our purposes, fleeting or enduring, in a constantly flowing manner.

But there can be yet another kind of waking life in the conscious-having of the world. It would lie in a transformation of thematic consciousness that breaks through the normality of merely going along. Let us direct our gaze to the fact that universally, that for us all, the world and the objects are not merely given in a mere having as substrates of their properties, but that they become conscious to us in subjective modes of appearance, modes of givenness, without our especially attending to this and while for the most part we have no inkling of it at all. If we now shape this into a new universal direction of interest, if we establish a consistent universal interest in the how of the modes of givenness — and for the *onta* themselves, not straightforwardly, but as objects in their how — we notice in this total turning of interest that not only a host of never before thematic types of individual details but of syntheses, in an inseparable synthetic totality, come to be our own, constantly produced through intentionally overreaching horizon-validities, mutually influencing one another in the form of confirming validations of existence or also invalidating cancellations and other modalizations. It is the hallmark of this synthetic totality that in it something previously completely unknown, never before beheld and grasped as a cognitive task, can become our own: the universal performing life in which the world — the world constantly existing for us in streaming nowness, the world constantly "pre-given" to us — comes about.

### § 39 The peculiarity of the transcendental epoché as total alteration of the natural life-attitude

How can the pre-givenness of the life-world become a universal theme of its own? Obviously only through a total alteration of the natural attitude — an alteration in which we no longer live as human beings of natural existence in the constant performance of validity of the pre-given world, but rather constantly refrain from this performance. Only so can we reach the transformed and novel theme "pre-givenness of the world as such": world purely and wholly exclusively as the and in the way that it has meaning and validity of being in our consciousness-life and in ever new shapes gains it. Only so can we study what the world as ground-validity of natural life is in all its purposes and doings, and correlatively what natural life and its subjectivity ultimately is — purely as the subjectivity that functions as validity-performing. The life of natural world-life that performs world-validity cannot be studied in the attitude of natural world-life. A total reversal is needed, a quite unique universal epoché.

### § 40 The difficulties of the genuine sense of performance of the total epoché. The temptation to misunderstand it as an abstention, to be achieved step by step, from all individual validities

The universality of the epoché with respect to the entire naturally normal life has in fact an incomparable peculiarity and initially its own questionabilities. It is not clear in advance how it is to be carried out. Here seductive wrong paths offer themselves — ways of understanding the execution of the epoché that certainly do not lead to the goal.

Husserl invites us to consider the manner of the naturally normal life: we move in a stream of ever new experiences, judgments, valuations, decisions. In each of these acts the I is directed toward objects of its surrounding world, occupied with them in this or that way. They are what is conscious in these acts themselves, sometimes simply as realities, sometimes in reality-modalities (as possible, doubtful, etc.). None of these acts and none of the validities enclosed in them is isolated; they necessarily imply in their intentions an infinite horizon of inactual, co-functioning validities in streaming mobility. The manifold acquisitions of earlier active life are not dead sedimentations; even the co-conscious but momentarily irrelevant, wholly unnoticed background (e.g. of the perceptual field) functions according to its implicit validities. Thus, by virtue of this constantly streaming horizonality, every validity straightforwardly performed in natural world-life always already presupposes validities, reaching back immediately or mediately into the one necessary underground of dark but occasionally available, reactivatable validities.

Husserl then describes an approach that does not work: trying to carry out the epoché piece by piece, first suspending this validity, then that one. Such a piecemeal procedure would never arrive at the universal epoché because each act of suspension already presupposes the rest of the world in its validity. The total epoché cannot be achieved as a result of a progressive stripping-away; it must be accomplished as a single total shift. The attempt to proceed stepwise leads only to an endless regress: each withheld judgment reveals further presupposed validities to be withheld.

### § 41 The genuine transcendental epoché makes possible the "transcendental reduction" — the discovery and exploration of the universal correlation between world and world-consciousness

The genuine performance of the epoché enables the transcendental reduction: the systematic discovery and exploration of the universal correlation between the world and consciousness-of-the-world. What was always already operative but never thematized — the constitutive life of consciousness in which the world has its meaning and validity — now comes into view as an infinite field of investigation. The world is not lost through the reduction but is retained in a new way: as *cogitatum* of *cogitationes*, as the intentional correlate of the total life of consciousness.

Husserl emphasizes that the transcendental reduction does not annihilate the world or declare it unreal. Rather, it transforms the world from a naively presupposed ground into a theme — it brings into focus precisely what the natural attitude takes for granted but never examines: the universal constitutive achievement through which the world has meaning and validity for us. The field that opens up is not empty: it is the whole of subjective life in its constitutive functioning, together with the whole world as its correlate. Nothing is lost; everything is gained in a new dimension of intelligibility. The transcendental reduction is thus the methodical access to what Husserl has been calling the "depth dimension" — the realm of anonymous subjective accomplishments that sustain the world as life-world.

### § 42 The task of the concrete preliminary sketch of the dimensions of the working problems by means of the discovery of the correlation

Husserl begins to sketch the vast terrain of problems opened up by the reduction. The universal correlation has many dimensions: the correlation between individual thing-experience and the manifold of its appearances; the correlation between the single experiencing subject and the intersubjective community of experiencing subjects; the correlation between the present experiential life and the sedimented history of meaning-formations. What we exclude as theme is everything about what things and realities of the world really are, their real being and real being-thus, all cognitions and determinations of true being. What interests us exclusively is the purely subjective: how it is that the world has, in the streaming modes of our consciousness-life, the meaning-of-being and the validity-of-being that it does. The motivation from the need to clarify the evident achievements of the positive sciences had originally required this theme. We have already detached ourselves from that motivation. How the theme can become an independent task, a field of working problems, requires deeper reflections.

### § 43 Characterization of a new way to the reduction in distinction from the "Cartesian way"

Husserl distinguishes his present approach from the "Cartesian way" into the transcendental reduction (the way through universal doubt, employed in the *Cartesian Meditations*). The Cartesian way has the advantage of brevity but the disadvantage that it reaches the transcendental ego "in one leap" without revealing the full richness of the constitutive problematic. It brings one very quickly to the apodictic ego, but this ego appears at first as empty — as a bare "I am" without content. The present way, through the life-world, has the advantage of gradually building up an understanding of what the transcendental reduction achieves by first exhibiting the life-world as the universal ground of meaning. The life-world path allows the philosopher to see concretely what kinds of constitutive problems await, before performing the final step of the reduction. It thus avoids the appearance of emptiness that plagued the Cartesian way and motivated its frequent misunderstanding as a merely methodological gimmick.

### § 44 The life-world as topic of a theoretical interest brought about by a universal epoché regarding the reality of life-worldly things

Husserl now attempts to introduce a preliminary form of the reduction through the life-world. We can adopt a universal epoché regarding the reality of life-worldly things — not suspending our belief in their existence, but redirecting our theoretical interest from the things themselves to their modes of givenness. This yields a consistently reflective thematization of the subjective correlates of all life-worldly objectivity.

### § 45 Beginnings of a concrete explication of the data of sensory intuition purely as such

Husserl offers initial concrete analyses of what shows itself when we attend purely to the subjective side. As a completely "disinterested" observer of the world — purely as the subjective-relative world — we hold a first naive survey, always intent not on investigating its being and being-thus, but on considering whatever counted and continues to count as existing and being-thus under the viewpoint of how it subjectively counts, in which appearance, etc.

For example: there are the respective individual things of experience. To perceive a thing, even when it is perceived as completely unchanged, is something highly manifold: seeing it, touching it, smelling it, hearing it, and in each I have something different. What is seen in seeing is in and for itself other than what is touched in touching. Yet I say: the same thing — different being self-evidently only the modes of its sensory presentations. If I keep purely within the sphere of seeing, there arise new differences: in the course of every normal seeing, which is after all a continuous process, each phase is itself already a seeing, but what is seen in each is actually different. The pure seeing-thing presents itself now from this "side" and now from that, and in them a continuous synthesis presents the surface for me. Each is a mode of presentation of it. Therein lies: while it is actually given, I mean more than it offers. I have certainty of being of this thing to which all sides simultaneously belong. In the continuous change of seeing, the seen side ceases to be really still seen, but it is "retained" and "taken together" with what was retained from earlier, and so I "get to know" the thing.

Similarly for proximity and distance, and for the full perception even in a first glimpse: perceiving, I constantly "mean" the thing with all sides, which are by no means given, not even in the form of intuitive pre-presentifications. The perception has a "horizon" belonging to its object. More exactly considered, what I ascribe to the thing itself — its seen colored figure — is in the change of near-far orientation yet again something manifoldly presenting itself: the change of perspectives. The perspectives of the figure and likewise of its color are different, yet each is in this new way a presentation of this figure, this color. In their course they function in a continuous or discrete synthesis of identification or rather of unification. This happens not as an external fusion; rather, as bearing "meaning" in each phase, they connect in a progressive enrichment and further-formation of meaning.

### § 46 The universal correlation-a priori

As soon as one begins, instead of being oriented toward life-worldly things and objects and seeking to cognize them as what they are, to inquire after the modes of their subjective givenness-ways, one enters a realm of ever more intricate and very remarkable exhibitions. We notice ordinarily nothing of all the subjective presentation-ways "of" things, but in reflection we recognize with astonishment that essential correlations prevail here, which are component parts of a further-reaching, a universal a priori. Immediately I am conscious of the existing thing, while I yet have from moment to moment changing the experience "presentation of," which, however, only becomes visible in reflection with its remarkable "of." Implied in the respective perception of the thing is a whole "horizon" of non-actual and yet co-functioning modes of appearance and syntheses of validity.

Every first description here is necessarily rough, and soon one stands before the riddles of this implication of non-actual manifolds of appearance, without which we would not have things or a world of experience given at all. And soon we stand before the difficulties of a concrete unfolding of this correlation-a priori. Husserl notes that the first breakthrough of this universal correlation-a priori (during the working-out of his *Logical Investigations*, approximately in the year 1898) shook him so deeply that from then on his entire life's work was governed by the task of a systematic elaboration of this correlation-a priori. This is one of the most important autobiographical remarks in the entire *Crisis*, anchoring the discovery of the correlation-a priori as the origin of the whole phenomenological project.

### § 47 Reference to further directions of research: the subjective basic phenomena of kinaestheses, validity-change, horizon-consciousness, and the communalization of experience

Husserl indicates further research directions. In perception, the systems of presentations "of" a thing are referred back to correlative manifolds of kinaesthetic courses, which have the character of "I do," "I move" (including "I hold still"). The kinaestheses belong to one's own body in a double-sidedness: inner kinaestheses and outer corporeal-real movements. Each body (*Leib*) demands special far-reaching descriptions of its own peculiarities in the way it presents itself in manifolds.

Another extraordinarily important thematic direction is designated by the phenomenon of validity-change — e.g., the change from being to semblance. In continuous perception a thing stands there for me in simple certainty of being of immediate presence — but only as long as the accompanying presentations run in the anticipated correlations. A hidden intentional "if-then" connection plays: in certain systematic co-sequences the presentations must run. The actual kinaestheses lie in a system of kinaesthetic possibility, to which the system of concordantly belonging possible sequences stands in correlation. This is the intentional background of every simple certainty of being of the presented thing. Occasionally, however, a break in this concordance occurs: being transforms into semblance, or merely into being-doubtful, being-merely-possible, being-probable. The semblance resolves through "correction," through alteration of the sense in which one had perceived the thing.

But not only at the individual thing: the individual is — in terms of consciousness — nothing for itself. Perception of a thing is its perception in a perceptual field. Every thing has not only an "inner horizon" (the system of possible further aspects) but an "outer horizon": as a thing in a thing-field, ultimately referring to the whole "world as perceptual world." This is the momentary perceptual field as a section "of" the world, of the universe of things of possible perceptions. Constantly in our — in my — waking life, the world is perceived in this way, constantly it streams onward in a unity of perceptual consciousness-life.

Husserl further directs attention to the intersubjective dimension. We are not isolated in our continuously streaming world-perceiving but stand in connection with other human beings. Each has their perceptions, their presentifications, their concordances, their devaluations of certainties into mere possibilities, doubts, questions, semblances. But in living-together, each can share in the life of the others. So the world is not only existing for isolated human beings but for the human community, through the communalization of the simply perceptual. In this communalization there constantly takes place also a validity-change in mutual correction. "The" thing itself is really that which nobody has as really seen, since it is rather constantly, for everyone, the unity of the openly endless manifold of changing own and foreign experiences and experience-things.

### § 48 All beings of every sense and every region as indices of their correlative systems of subjective experience

In this exclusive deepening into the manifolds of subjective modes of appearance in which the world is pre-given to us, the insight increasingly confronts us that what is at stake is not contingent factualities, but rather that no conceivable human being, however we might vary them in thought, could experience a world in other modes of givenness than those we have generally outlined. The naive self-evidence that everyone sees things and the world as they look for them concealed a great horizon of remarkable truths that in their peculiarity and systematic interconnection never entered the field of view of philosophy. Never before the first breakthrough of "transcendental phenomenology" in the *Logical Investigations* did the correlation of world and subjective modes of givenness arouse philosophical wonder, despite its audible announcement already in pre-Socratic philosophy and in the Sophists (though there only as a motive of skeptical argumentation). Everything stands in such correlation to its belonging and by no means merely sensory modes of givenness in possible experience, and everything has its modes of validity and its special ways of synthesis. Being of every concrete or abstract, real or ideal sense has its modes of self-givenness, on the side of the I its modes of intention in modes of validity, and belonging thereto its modes of subjective change in their syntheses of concordance and discordance, individual-subjective and intersubjective.

### § 49 Preliminary concept of transcendental constitution as "original formation of meaning." The exemplary narrowness of the analyses carried out; indication of further horizons

The concept of constitution as "original formation of meaning" (*ursprüngliche Sinnbildung*) is introduced as a preliminary title. All levels and strata through which the intentional syntheses, overreaching from subject to subject, are intertwined, form a universal unity of synthesis. Through it, the objective universe — the world as the concretely living given world (and the pre-given world for all possible praxis) — comes about. We speak in this respect of the "intersubjective constitution" of the world, thereby encompassing the total system of however hidden modes of givenness, as well as the I-like modes of validity. Through their systematic disclosure, the world that exists for us is made intelligible as a meaning-formation out of the elementary intentionalities. Intentionality is the title for the only real and genuine explaining, making-intelligible. To lead back to the intentional origins and unities of meaning-formation — that yields an intelligibility which (an ideal case, admittedly), once attained, would leave no meaningful question remaining.

What was treated more exemplarily was naturally only a beginning, initially only a beginning of the clarification of the perceptual world — and this itself, fully taken, is only a "stratum." World is spatiotemporal world; the orientation toward the perceptual world yields, with respect to the world, only the time-mode present, which itself refers horizontally to the time-modes past and future. For the meaning-formation of the past, recollection above all exercises the intentional function. But as deeper intentional analysis reveals, the standing now has a two-sided horizon: the continuum of retentions and the continuum of protentions. These first preliminary forms of temporalization and time hold themselves entirely in hiddenness. Here as elsewhere, Husserl notes, every reached "ground" in fact refers again to grounds, every opened horizon awakens new horizons, and yet the infinite whole in its infinity of streaming movement is directed toward a unity of meaning.

### § 50 First ordering of all working problems under the titles: ego — cogito — cogitatum

All problems can be initially ordered under the Cartesian titles: ego (the subject-pole and what belongs to its identity), cogito (the subjective as appearance in synthetic connection), and cogitatum (the object-poles — that which is unity in its appearances and toward which, through them, the intention of the I-pole proceeds). Although these titles are inseparable from one another, one must at times pursue each individually, holding order in the reverse direction from that suggested by the Cartesian approach. The first thing is the plainly given life-world, initially as it gives itself perceptually as "normal," simply, unbroken in pure certainty of being. With the establishment of the new direction of interest and thus of its strict epoché, it becomes a first intentional title, index, guiding thread for the inquiry back into the manifolds of modes of appearance and their intentional structures. A new direction of gaze, in the second level of reflection, leads to the I-pole and what belongs to its identity. Everything becomes complicated as soon as we consider that subjectivity is only in intersubjectivity what it is: constitutively functioning I. This means for the viewpoint "I" the new themes of the synthesis specifically belonging to I and other I, the I-You synthesis and likewise, but more complexly, the We-synthesis.

### § 51 The task of an "ontology of the life-world"

In all of this, a fixed typology prevails — and this is what makes scientificity, description, phenomenological-transcendental truth possible — a typology that, as already said, is an essential typology to be methodically grasped as a pure a priori. Here it is remarkable and philosophically very important that this also concerns the first of our titles: the life-world constituted as a unity through all relativities, the universe of life-worldly objects. This could actually, without any transcendental interest — in the "natural attitude" — have become the theme of a science of its own: an ontology of the life-world purely as experiential world. The life-world, which takes up all practical formations (including the objective sciences as cultural facts) without further ado, is admittedly in constant change of relativities referred to subjectivity. But however it changes and is corrected, it observes its essential-lawful typology, to which all life and all science whose "ground" it is remain bound. It thus also has an ontology to be drawn from pure evidence.

The meaning of an a priori science proper to this "ontology" stands in sharp contrast to that of the tradition. Modern philosophy in its objective sciences is guided by a constructive concept of a world true in-itself, one substructed at least with respect to nature in mathematical form. Its concept of an a priori science cannot therefore have the dignity of a genuine evidence — an essential insight drawn from direct self-giving (experiencing intuition). Within the transcendental epoché, the life-world transforms into the mere transcendental "phenomenon." It remains in its own essence what it was, but now proves to be, so to speak, merely a "component" in the concrete transcendental subjectivity, and correspondingly its a priori proves to be a "stratum" in the universal a priori of transcendentality. In the alternation of partial attitudes — that on the life-worldly phenomena serving as starting-point (as transcendental guiding thread) for the higher-level correlate-attitudes — the universal research-task of the transcendental reduction is realized.

### § 52 The emergence of paradoxical unintelligibilities. The need for a more radical self-reflection

The first survey of the pure correlation-problematic, which the shift from life in the natural interest in the world to that of the "disinterested" observer opened for us, has yielded — though in a certain naivety and therefore preliminary character — a wealth of obviously very strange cognitions. Now, for the purpose of methodical security, a reflection is needed concerning the ground of ultimate presuppositions in which this entire problematic is rooted. But we immediately encounter great difficulties, unexpected and initially insoluble paradoxes that call our entire enterprise into question — and this despite the evidences that offered themselves to us and that we cannot simply give up. The philosopher in the epoché from the outset lives in the paradox of having to regard the self-evident as questionable, as enigmatic, and of henceforth being able to have no other scientific theme than this: to transform the universal self-evidence of the being of the world — for him the greatest of all riddles — into an intelligibility.

### § 53 The paradox of human subjectivity: being a subject for the world and at the same time being an object in the world

A really serious difficulty now arises, which attacks our entire task-positing and the meaning of its results, and indeed compels a reshaping of both. By virtue of our present method of epoché, everything objective transformed into something subjective. But this obviously cannot mean that, through the epoché, existing world and human world-representation are juxtaposed and that, on the ground of the self-evidently really existing world, one inquires into the subjective, the psychic processes of human beings. Our scientificity is not that of psychologists. Through the radical epoché, every interest in reality or unreality of the world is put out of play.

The universal intersubjectivity into which all objectivity, all being whatsoever, dissolves, obviously cannot be any other than humanity, which is undeniably itself a partial stock of the world. How is a partial stock of the world, its human subjectivity, supposed to constitute the whole world — constitute it as its intentional formation? The subject-stock of the world devours, so to speak, the entire world and thereby itself. What a countersense! Or is it nonetheless a meaningfully resolvable, even a necessary paradox — necessarily arising from the constant tension between the power of the self-evidence of the natural objective attitude (the power of common sense) and the attitude of the "disinterested observer" that opposes it?

From the outset, the phenomenologist lives in the paradox of having to regard the self-evident as questionable, as enigmatic, and of henceforth being able to have no other scientific theme than this: to transform the universal self-evidence of the being of the world — for him the greatest of all riddles — into an intelligibility. The irresolvability of the paradox would mean that a really universal and radical epoché is altogether impossible to carry out — that is, in view of a science strictly bound to it.

### § 54 The resolution of the paradox

Husserl undertakes the resolution in a subsection titled "We as human beings and we as ultimately functioning-performing subjects." The question is: what about humanity as world-constituting subjectivity that is at the same time fitted into the world itself? In the naivety of our first procedure, we were interested in the ever-newly opening horizons of remarkable discoveries, held fast in the naturally first direction of gaze toward the correlation of object-pole and mode of givenness. The I came to speech as the theme of the uppermost level of reflection, but in the careful analytically-descriptive procedure it did not come to its full right. The depths of its being-in-function become pressing only late.

Are the transcendental subjects — the subjects functioning for world-constitution — the human beings? The epoché has made them into "phenomena." The philosopher in the epoché may not posit them as really existing — they are for him, as for all worldly beings, "validity-phenomena," indices for correlative constitutive investigations. And yet it is human beings and human communities that are the subjects whose intentional life constitutes the world. How is this possible?

The paradox resolves itself through the recognition that the ego that constitutes the world is not the empirical human being — a psychophysical entity among entities — but is rather the transcendental ego, which however is not a second ego standing behind the human one. It is the human ego itself seen from within the transcendental attitude: from the perspective of its constitutive functioning. What was anonymous — the constitutive life in which the world has its being-meaning — now becomes thematic. The human being "in" the world is the self-objectivation of transcendental subjectivity. The "paradox" dissolves once one grasps that being-a-subject-for-the-world and being-an-object-in-the-world are not two incompatible facts about two different entities but two aspects of the same subjectivity, distinguished only by the attitude in which they are grasped.

### § 55 The principled correction of our first approach through the way over the "life-world"

Husserl acknowledges that the first approach through the life-world requires principled correction. The life-world itself, as universal ground, must ultimately be understood as a constitutive achievement of transcendental subjectivity. This does not cancel the validity of the analyses carried out but deepens their meaning. The naively performed investigations of the correlation between life-world and its subjective modes of givenness retain their validity, but their ultimate sense is now recognized: they are not merely psychological observations about how human beings experience the world, but glimpses into the transcendental constitution through which the world as such achieves its meaning and validity.

Husserl draws the consequence: the path through the life-world leads us, not to a new empirical science (a "life-world science" standing alongside the other sciences), but to the threshold of transcendental phenomenology itself. The life-world functions as a guiding thread (*Leitfaden*) for constitutive analysis: its structures point beyond themselves to the constitutive achievements in which they originate. The "correction" consists in recognizing that the pre-given life-world, far from being an ultimate ground, is itself a constitutive formation — the most comprehensive and most fundamental one, but a formation nonetheless, requiring the transcendental reduction for its ultimate clarification.

### B. The way into phenomenological transcendental philosophy from psychology

### § 56 Characterization of the philosophical development after Kant, illuminating motives for a new approach through psychology

Husserl now introduces the second major pathway into transcendental phenomenology: through psychology. He characterizes the philosophical development after Kant — through Fichte, Schelling, Hegel, and the systems of German Idealism — as powerful but ultimately unsuccessful attempts at transcendental philosophy. They shared the conviction that the objective sciences, however they estimate themselves as sites of true method, are not yet seriously sciences — not cognitions from ultimate grounding. But the speculative idealists overleaped the concrete work of constitutive analysis and thereby fell into constructions that lacked evidence. The result was a reaction — the return to Kant, to positivism, to materialism — that abandoned the transcendental motive altogether. A new approach through a reformed psychology offers itself as a way to reach the transcendental domain while avoiding both the mythical constructions of speculative idealism and the naivety of objectivistic positivism.

### § 57 The fateful separation of transcendental philosophy and psychology

A fateful separation between transcendental philosophy and psychology characterized the entire post-Kantian development. Transcendental philosophy claimed to deal with a "transcendental" subjectivity entirely distinct from the "empirical" subjectivity studied by psychology. This separation is both necessary — the transcendental cannot be reduced to the empirical-natural — and dangerous, because it threatens to make the transcendental unintelligible and inaccessible. If the transcendental subject is not the human soul, what is it? If it is radically different from anything accessible in experience, how can we know anything about it? The separation, intended to preserve the uniqueness of the transcendental, instead produces the mythical constructions that plagued Kant and his successors. Husserl's project is to overcome this separation not by collapsing the transcendental into the psychological, but by showing how a properly reformed psychology naturally opens onto the transcendental dimension.

### § 58 Twinning and difference of psychology and transcendental philosophy. Psychology as the field of decisions

Psychology and transcendental philosophy are "twinned" (*verschwistert*) — they deal with the same subjectivity, only from different standpoints. The psychological attitude studies consciousness as a natural fact — as the psychic life of human beings in the world. The transcendental attitude studies consciousness as the constitutive source of all worldly meaning. The content of investigation is in a certain sense identical: the same intentional structures, the same correlations, the same syntheses. But the attitude is different, and this difference transforms the meaning of everything discovered. Psychology is the "field of decisions" because it is in psychology that the decisive turn must be made: only by carrying psychological investigation to its ultimate consequences does one arrive at the transcendental domain. The reformed psychology — purified of its naturalistic and physicalistic presuppositions — reveals itself, at its deepest level, to be transcendental phenomenology under another name.

### § 59 Analysis of the change of attitude from the psychological to the transcendental

Husserl describes the shift from the psychological attitude to the transcendental in closer detail. The psychologist studies consciousness as a real component of the real world — as something occurring within the spatiotemporal order. The transcendental philosopher studies consciousness as the source of all worldly meaning — the "how" through which the world achieves its validity. The shift does not require leaving psychology behind but deepening it: when the psychologist recognizes that the intentional life studied in psychology is not merely a natural fact but is the constitutive ground of all natural factuality, the psychological attitude passes over into the transcendental. This passage is not a leap into a different subject-matter but a change in the understanding of what psychology has been studying all along. What appeared as a regional fact — the psychic life of human beings — now reveals itself as the universal constitutive dimension.

### § 60 The ground of the failure of psychology: the dualistic and physicalistic presuppositions

The failure of psychology to achieve its proper task — the universal science of subjectivity — is rooted in the dualistic and physicalistic presuppositions inherited from the Galilean-Cartesian tradition. Psychology modeled itself on natural science and thereby lost access to the genuine character of psychic life: its intentionality, its constitutive function, its essential structures. By treating consciousness as a natural process causally determined by physical events, psychology made itself incapable of thematizing what is specifically psychic — the "meaning-bestowing" character of consciousness, its directedness-toward objects, its horizon-structure. The Lockean model of the soul as a "white paper" on which data come and go, and the subsequent empiricist-associationist tradition, represent the fullest expression of this failure. Husserl insists that the ground of the failure is not methodological incompetence but a fundamental philosophical error: the naturalization of consciousness.

### § 61 Psychology in the tension between the (objectivistic-philosophical) idea of science and the demands of its own subject-matter

Psychology finds itself in a structural tension between the objectivistic ideal of science (modeled on physics) and the demands of its own subject-matter (the intentional life of consciousness). If psychology attempts to be "scientific" in the objectivistic sense — seeking causal laws, quantifiable regularities, prediction and control — it systematically distorts the character of what it studies. Consciousness is not a natural process but a meaning-bestowing life, and its "laws" are not causal regularities but essential structures of intentionality. The resolution of this tension requires not an abandonment of scientificity but a transformation of the concept of science itself: a new scientificity appropriate to the intentional character of consciousness.

### § 62 Preliminary discussion of the countersense of the principled naturalization of consciousness

Husserl argues that the naturalization of consciousness — treating it as a natural process in the causal order of nature — is a countersense (*Widersinn*). Consciousness is not a natural event but the medium through which all natural being is given and has meaning. To naturalize consciousness is to treat the constituting as if it were one more instance of the constituted — to reduce the source of all worldly meaning to a worldly fact. This is not merely an error but a structural impossibility: one cannot account for the meaning of natural being by treating the meaning-giving consciousness itself as a piece of nature. The critique applies not only to crude materialism but to every form of psychophysical parallelism, epiphenomenalism, or functionalism that treats consciousness as causally embedded in nature.

### § 63 The questionability of the concepts "outer" and "inner experience"

The traditional distinction between "outer" experience (of physical objects) and "inner" experience (of psychic states) is critically examined. This distinction, inherited from the Cartesian-Lockean tradition, distorts the genuine character of experience. "Inner experience," modeled on outer experience, treats psychic life as a sequence of inner objects — data, impressions, ideas — to be observed, described, and causally explained. But the psychic is not given as a collection of inner objects; it is given as intentional life, as directedness-toward, as meaning-bestowing activity. The concept of "inner sense" or "inner perception" as Locke and the empiricist tradition understood it is a naturalistic distortion that treats the soul as an inner space filled with quasi-physical occurrences.

### § 64 The Cartesian dualism as the ground of the parallelization

The Cartesian dualism between *res extensa* and *res cogitans* is the historical ground for the parallel treatment of body and soul, nature and mind, physics and psychology. This parallelism is fundamentally misguided because it treats consciousness as a second kind of substance alongside bodily substance. In truth, consciousness has a fundamentally different mode of being from natural things: it is intentional, meaning-bestowing, self-constituting, horizon-structured. The parallelism between physics and psychology — the idea that psychology should study the psychic in the same way physics studies the physical — rests on the unexamined assumption that both domains have the same ontological structure. This assumption is precisely what Husserl's critique undermines.

### § 65 The examination of the right of an empirically grounded dualism through immersion in the factual course of experience

Husserl proposes to test the right of dualism not through speculative argument but through careful immersion in the actual course of experience. When we attend to what experience actually shows — without importing theoretical prejudgments from the natural-scientific or philosophical tradition — the rigid dualism dissolves. We do not find two separate domains (material things on one side, psychic processes on the other) but a single world of concretely experienced things, persons, and situations in which the "material" and the "psychic" are inseparably intertwined. The lived body is the prime instance: it is neither merely physical nor merely psychic but both at once, in a unity that precedes the dualistic division.

### § 66 The world of general experience; its regional structure

Husserl describes the regional structure of the world as given in general experience: material things, animate bodies, persons, cultural objects. Each region has its own essential structures, its own modes of givenness, its own types of evidence. The task of a reformed psychology is to investigate the subjective correlates of each regional type without importing the physicalistic schema of data-and-laws that distorts the genuine character of psychic life.

### § 67 Dualism of the experience-grounded abstractions. The historical continuation of the empiricist approach (from Hobbes to Wundt). Critique of the schema of "data" and "sense-data"

The empiricist tradition from Hobbes through Locke to Wundt perpetuated a distorted view of psychic life by treating it as composed of "data" — atomic sense-impressions that are then "associated." This data-model ignores the intentional character of consciousness and its constitutive achievements. The empiricist psychology never asked how it is that out of mere "data" a world of objects, persons, and meanings could arise. The question of the origin of objectivity out of subjectivity — which is the properly transcendental question — was blocked from the outset by the data-model. Husserl's critique extends to the entire tradition of associationist psychology and to the "sense-data" theories that still dominate epistemology. The fundamental error is always the same: treating consciousness as a collection of atomic contents rather than as an intentional, synthetic, constitutive life.

### § 68 The task of an explication of consciousness as intentional accomplishment

The proper task of a reformed psychology is the explication of consciousness as intentional accomplishment (*intentionale Leistung*). This means attending to consciousness not as a collection of data but as the structured, intentional, constitutive life in which objects, the world, and all meaning are formed. Every act of consciousness is an "intending," a "meaning," a "directing-itself-toward"; every object is the correlate of a system of intentional acts in which it is constituted. The task is to trace out these correlations concretely, exhibiting the "how" of constitution for each type of objectivity.

### § 69 The psychological basic method of the "phenomenological-psychological reduction"

Husserl introduces the phenomenological-psychological reduction as the basic method of the reformed psychology. This reduction brackets the natural attitude's straightforward positing of objects in order to attend purely to the intentional life of consciousness and its correlates. It parallels the transcendental reduction but remains within the psychological attitude: its theme is still the human soul, but the human soul now properly understood as intentional life. The first characteristic of this reduction involves grasping the intentional relatedness of consciousness — the fact that every experience is an experience "of" something — and the essential connection between act and object. This relatedness is not an external relation between two separately existing items but an internal structural feature of consciousness itself.

### § 70 The difficulties of "psychological abstraction." Paradoxes of the "intentional object"

The phenomenological-psychological reduction encounters characteristic difficulties. The paradox of the "intentional object" is central: the object as intended is not a real part of consciousness (the tree I perceive is not in my head), yet it is inseparable from consciousness (without the perceiving, there is no perceived-as-such). This paradox cannot be resolved by treating the intentional object as a "mental representation" standing between consciousness and the real thing, for this leads to the problems of the representative theory (how do I get from the representation to the thing?). The resolution lies in understanding intentionality correctly: consciousness does not contain objects but intends them — it reaches out to them through its intentional structure. The object "in" consciousness is the object as meant, as given, as constituted — not a copy or image of a reality lying elsewhere.

### § 71 The danger of misunderstanding the "universality" of the phenomenological-psychological reduction

There is a danger of misunderstanding the universality of the phenomenological-psychological reduction: of taking it as merely one more regional investigation alongside others (a study of "psychic facts" as one domain among many), when in fact it opens up the universal dimension of intentional life in which all domains are constituted. If the reduction is understood as merely regional, it remains a piece of positive science and never reaches the transcendental. But if its universality is recognized — if one sees that all objectivity, all worldly being, all meaning-formation refers back to the intentional life that the reduction discloses — then the psychological reduction already stands at the threshold of the transcendental.

### § 72 The relation of transcendental psychology to transcendental philosophy. The "parallelism" and the transition

Husserl now traces the decisive transition from phenomenological psychology to transcendental phenomenology. The reformed psychology, carried to its ultimate consequences, reveals that the psychic life it investigates is not merely a regional fact within the world but the constitutive source of all worldly meaning. At this point, psychological reflection passes over into transcendental reflection.

The "parallelism" between phenomenological psychology and transcendental phenomenology — the fact that their content is in a certain sense identical, differing only in the attitude in which it is grasped — is both the source of the greatest confusion and the key to the deepest understanding. The psychological reduction investigates intentional life as the psychic life of human beings in the world; the transcendental reduction investigates the same intentional life as the constitutive ground of the world. The content is the same; the meaning is transformed. This transformation is accomplished when the psychologist recognizes that the "world" presupposed as the ground of all psychological investigation is itself a constitutive achievement of the very intentional life being investigated. At that moment, the ground shifts: the world is no longer the unquestioned framework within which psychology operates, but becomes itself a "phenomenon" — a constitutive formation requiring investigation.

The transition from the one to the other is the most difficult and most important step in the whole of phenomenological philosophy. It is the step from the natural to the transcendental attitude, from positive science to transcendental science, from the study of beings-in-the-world to the study of the constitutive life through which there is a world at all. And yet it is not a leap into a different subject-matter but a deepening of the very same investigation that psychology had been conducting all along — only now freed from its naturalistic and objectivistic presuppositions and carried through to its ultimate ground.

### ⟨ § 73 Conclusion: ⟩ Philosophy as humanity's self-reflection, self-realization of reason

The appended Schlußwort, drawn from a later manuscript and placed here by the editor Walter Biemel, serves as a summation of the entire work's trajectory. The philosopher's task is universal science of the world — universal, definitive knowledge, a universe of truths-in-themselves about the world, the world in-itself. But how does it stand with this goal and its attainability? Can I begin with a truth — a definitive truth? Every experience of what is worldly is certain, but this certainty can be modalized — what was certain can become doubtful and dissolve into semblance.

Experiencing, living as an I, I am necessarily an I that has its You, its We and Your — the I of the personal pronouns. And equally necessarily, I am and we are, in I-like community, correlates of all that we address as worldly being. But always in such a way that the world is our common world, yet in individual cases I can come into contradiction with my others.

Husserl returns to the question of objectivity. The in-itself means the same as the objective. But never has the meaning of this task-positing and its presuppositions been seriously investigated in the manner of an ultimate responsibility. One has demanded of psychology the same objectivity as of physics, and precisely thereby a psychology in the full and proper sense has been rendered entirely impossible; for the soul, for subjectivity as individual and as socially historical, an objectivity after the manner of natural science is precisely a countersense.

This is the ultimate meaning of the reproach to be made against the philosophy of all times — with the exception of the philosophy of idealism, which admittedly failed in its method — that it never overcame the naturalistic objectivism that was from the beginning a very natural temptation. Only idealism in all its forms attempts to lay hold of subjectivity as subjectivity. But idealism was always too quick with its theories and could not free itself from hidden objectivistic presuppositions, or it leaped over the task of concretely and analytically questioning the actual subjectivity.

The life of the human being as personal is a constant becoming in a constant intentionality of development. What is becoming in this life is the person itself. Human personal life runs through stages of self-reflection and self-responsibility, from occasional acts up to the stage of universal self-reflection and self-responsibility — and up to the conscious grasping of the idea of autonomy, the idea of a resolve of the will to shape one's entire personal life into the synthetic unity of a life in universal self-responsibility. Philosophy as infinite task would thereby have come to its apodictic beginning.

Philosophy is nothing other than rationalism, through and through, but rationalism differentiated within itself according to the different stages of the movement of intention and fulfillment — the *ratio* in the constant movement of self-illumination, beginning from the first break-in of philosophy into humanity, whose inborn reason was previously still wholly in the state of closure, of nocturnal darkness. Greek philosophy in its initial stage designates the image of the dawn. The history of philosophy, seen from inside, is a struggling of the philosophical generations living and continuing to live in spiritual community — the bearers of this spiritual development — in the constant struggle of "awakened" reason to come to itself, to its self-understanding. Philosophy, science in all its forms, is rational — that is a tautology. But it is in all of them on the way to a higher rationality. Finally it discovers that this is an idea lying in the infinite and in fact necessarily on its way.

Reason is the specific thing of the human being. This life as personal is a constant becoming in a constant intentionality of development. The universally apodictically grounded and grounding science arises as the necessarily highest function of humanity — the function of enabling its development toward a personal and an all-encompassing humanitary autonomy. So philosophy is nothing other than rationalism, through and through, the *ratio* in the constant movement of self-illumination, beginning from the first break-in of philosophy into humanity.
