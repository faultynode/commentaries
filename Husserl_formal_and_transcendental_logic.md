# Husserl, *Formal and Transcendental Logic* — Introduction and Part I: Paragraph-by-Paragraph Analysis

---

## INTRODUCTION

Husserl opens by distinguishing what we today mean by "science" in a strict sense from older, looser usages. The philosophies of the pre-Platonic era, and analogous intellectual formations of other peoples and times, we call "sciences" only in a lax sense. They count for us only as precursors or preliminary stages of science proper. Science in a new, rigorous sense first arises from the Platonic founding of logic — logic conceived as a site for investigating the essential requirements of "genuine" knowing and "genuine" science, and thereby for establishing the norms according to which a science consciously aiming at thoroughgoing normative correctness could be built up, a science that consciously justifies its method and its theory. The intention behind this logical justification is entirely one of justification from pure principles. Platonic science thus refuses to be merely a naive exercise of theoretical interest. Every step it takes, it claims to justify in its genuineness, in its necessary validity, on principled grounds. The original sense of this project is that principled logical insight, drawn from the pure idea of possible cognition and possible method of cognition in general, should precede and practically guide the actually exercised method and factual scientific formation — not that the fact of some naively grown method and science should set itself up as a norm for legitimately shaping scientific work.

Husserl then identifies the historical origin of Plato's logic: it arose as a reaction against the universal science-denial of sophistical skepticism. If skepticism denied the principled possibility of something like "philosophy," like science at all, then Plato had to weigh and critically ground the principled possibility of such a thing. Since science as such was called into question, naturally no factual science could be presupposed. This drove Plato to the path of the pure idea. His dialectic — not read off from the factual sciences but shaping purely ideal, pure norms — his logic or science-theory, had the vocation of first making factual science possible and practically guiding it. And in fulfilling this vocation it really did help to create sciences in the emphatic sense, sciences consciously borne by the idea of logical science and striving to realize it: rigorous mathematics and natural science, whose further developments at higher stages are our modern sciences.

The next paragraph marks a dramatic turning point. The original relationship between logic and science has in modernity been peculiarly reversed. The sciences made themselves autonomous; without fully satisfying the spirit of critical self-justification, they developed highly differentiated methods whose fruitfulness was practically certain but whose accomplishment was not ultimately transparent. They developed these methods not in the naivety of the everyday person, but in a naivety of a higher order — a naivety that renounced justifying the method from pure principles, by recourse to the pure idea, according to ultimate a priori possibilities and necessities. Logic, which was originally the torchbearer of method and claimed to be the pure doctrine of principles of possible cognition and science, lost this historical vocation and fell far behind in its development. The great restructuring of the natural sciences of the seventeenth century was still determined by logical reflections on the nature and requirements of genuine knowledge of nature, on its principled goals and methods. These reflections situated themselves in the context of the efforts, so characteristic of the time, toward founding a new, true logic. Not only Galileo belongs here, but, as Husserl insists, also Descartes. The very title "Discourse on Method" is telling, and the "First Philosophy" of the *Meditations* is itself nothing other than expression of a completely radical and universal science-theory. So logic still goes ahead of the sciences in these modern beginnings.

But this essential relationship changes in the subsequent epoch — the epoch in which the sciences become autonomous special sciences that no longer care about logic, indeed almost contemptuously push it aside. Logic itself, in the most recent period, goes completely astray from its own sense and its inalienable task. Instead of pursuing the pure essential norms of science according to all its essential formations, in order thereby to give the sciences principled guidance and to make possible for them genuineness of method-formation and accountability at every step, logic is pleased to let itself be guided by the factual sciences — especially the much-admired natural sciences — in its ideal of science and in its problem-settings.

Husserl then suggests that what is at stake is perhaps a deeper and more consequential tragedy of modern scientific culture than what is usually lamented in scientific circles: that the series of special sciences has grown so large that no one can make full use of all this wealth. The deficiency of our scientific situation is a much more essential, a radically more fundamental one. It concerns not the collective unification and appropriation of results, but the principled rootedness of the sciences and their unification from these roots. It is a deficiency that would remain even if an extraordinary mnemotechnics and a pedagogy guided by it enabled an encyclopedic knowledge of everything theoretically established in all the sciences. Science in its specialized form has become a kind of theoretical technique that, like technique in the ordinary sense, rests far more on a "practical experience" grown in manifold and well-practiced practical activity (what is called in practice "intuition," practical tact and flair) than on insight into the ratio of the accomplished achievement.

With this, Husserl charges, modern science has abandoned the ideal of genuine science that had been alive in the sciences since Plato, and practically abandoned the radicalism of scientific self-responsibility. No longer is that radicalism the innermost driving force which constantly demands that no knowledge be accepted for which accountability cannot be given from originally first and perfectly transparent principles, behind which to inquire further has no sense. The actually becoming science might have been very imperfect in this regard. But the essential thing was that that radical demand guided a corresponding practical striving for perfection, and that thus logic retained the great function of investigating in essential generality the possible paths to ultimate principles, and so, through unfolding the essence of genuine science as such (its pure possibility), to give actual science norm and guidance. Nothing was further from it than the aim of a merely technical achievement, whose naivety stands in the starkest contrast to the achievement of a radical self-norming from principles.

Husserl deepens this point. What is principled — and this all the great thinkers from Plato onward have seen — gains its full force, its fullness of all-sided transparency, from the universality that inseparably binds all sciences as branches of a *sapientia universalis* (Descartes). The autonomous special sciences lack the understanding of the principled one-sidedness of their achievements; they lack the understanding that they first encompass the full sense of being of their respective domains only when they take off the methodical blinders that became unavoidable through exclusive focus on their particular domain — in other words, when they steer their investigations into the universality of being and its principled unity. Logic itself is complicit in this situation, because, instead of keeping its historical vocation firmly in view and working itself out as a pure and universal science-theory, it has itself become a special science. Its own purposive sense demanded that it reflectively make this purposive sense the theme of radical considerations and master the principally different strata of science-theoretical problems, with which the sequence of logical disciplines was prefigured in which alone the idea of a science-theory and science itself could be realized. But it has not done justice to this its own essential sense.

A paragraph of existential urgency follows. The present situation of the European sciences compels radical reflections. They have fundamentally lost the great faith in themselves, in their absolute significance. The modern human being of today does not see in science and in the new culture shaped by it — as the "modern" person of the Enlightenment epoch did — the self-objectification of human reason or the universal function that humanity created for itself to make possible a truly satisfying life, an individual and social life from practical reason. This great faith, once the substitute for religious faith — the faith that science leads to wisdom, to a truly rational self-knowledge, knowledge of the world and of God, and through this to an ever more perfectly shaped, truly livable life in "happiness," satisfaction, welfare, and so forth — has in any case lost its force in wide circles. People live in a world that has become incomprehensible, in which they ask in vain after the "what for," the once so undoubted sense acknowledged by both understanding and will.

Husserl then turns to what can be done. However critically and skeptically we may stand toward the historically grown scientific culture, we cannot simply abandon it just because we do not ultimately understand it and cannot direct it from such understanding — in other words, because we are unable to rationally explicate its sense, to determine its true scope within which we can ourselves responsibly justify it and realize it in ongoing work. If we are not satisfied with the joy of creating a theoretical technique, of inventing theories with which one can do so many useful things and win the admiration of the world — if genuine humanity and life in radical self-responsibility cannot be separated, and thus also scientific self-responsibility cannot be separated from the whole of the responsibilities of human life — then we must place ourselves above this whole life and this entire cultural tradition and, through radical reflections, seek for ourselves, individually and in community, the ultimate possibilities and necessities from which we can take a stand toward actualities in judging, valuing, and acting. Of course, we thereby gain only ultimately accountable generalities, "principles," while life consists of decisions of the "moment" that never has time for scientific rational grounding. But if science has made decisions from principled responsibility, these decisions can impress on life habitual norms as directions of will, as pre-sketched forms within which individual decisions must and can hold themselves, insofar as they have come to genuine appropriation. For a rational praxis, theory a priori can be only a limiting form; it can only erect fences whose transgression means nonsense or going astray.

Husserl briefly gestures toward the further problems this raises for self- and human education, then turns the reader back to the present concern: from our present situation in science and culture, to make clear to ourselves the necessity of a radical and universal reflection. These reflections, directed toward the possible sense and possible method of genuine science in general, are naturally directed first of all at what is common to all possible sciences by essential necessity. Corresponding reflections for particular groups of sciences and individual sciences would then have to follow.

The thought of conducting these reflections in the form of a critique of contemporary philosophical attempts is raised and immediately dismissed. In the confused state of current philosophy, with its literature swollen to immeasurable proportions yet so lacking in unity of method that there are almost as many philosophies as philosophers, this would be a completely hopeless undertaking. A universal reflection can, since the scientific situation has in fact become similar to what Descartes found in his youth, attempt the bold path of the Cartesian Meditations. In a radicalism that cannot be surpassed and is for that very reason philosophically exemplary, the idea of genuine science from absolute grounding — the old Platonic idea — is quite seriously renewed, and the question is asked about the first-in-itself ground that all cognition, and thus that of the positive sciences, already presupposes. The first attempt at such a most radical scientific grounding — that of Descartes himself — failed. The firm will to accept no cognition except from absolute justification is not yet enough for its realization; an absolutely good conscience, and especially an absolutely good intellectual conscience, is an infinite idea. But even the best possible conscience and a rational method of practical approximation to this idea is a theme of much greater and more difficult meditations than Descartes supposed. Unnoticed prejudices direct his meditations, so that, taken as a whole, they already lacked the power of conviction for his contemporaries. However great an effect his return to the *ego cogito* had on the whole of modern philosophy, the style of these meditations — from the absolute knowing subjectivity to undertake an absolute grounding of the sciences in their totality, or what for him was the same, an absolute grounding of philosophy — has never again been taken up, down to transcendental phenomenology.

Other paths of radically directed reflections are possible, and the present work attempts to blaze one — a path suggested precisely by the historical reference of the idea of genuine science back to logic as its norm — at least in its main pieces.

Husserl now turns to the logic that crystallized from the Platonic dialectic. Already with the Aristotelian Analytics, a firmly formed systematic theory was precipitated that defies millennia nearly as well as Euclid's geometry. He does not need to recall Kant's well-known judgment, which goes much too far in its assessment of the completeness of this logic; but every glance at the world philosophical literature and even at the chaos of modern logical attempts shows that "formal logic" has an insuperable force. Even through widely divergent presentations, indeed distorting caricatures, it asserts itself with an essentially identical core content as an inalienable possession. This formal logic, even though the specific sense of the "formal" may have remained unelicited, was in its sense the first historical attempt at a universal science-theory, a theory related to the essential conditions of possible science as such. Of course it maintains itself in a naturally motivated, indeed essentially grounded one-sidedness, while another, differently sided science-theoretical a priori was always touched upon but, with its depths hidden from natural thinking, remained for millennia inaccessible to systematic theoretical work, and indeed does not even come into its line of sight.

Husserl proposes to take what enters our experience as a firm spiritual formation — the core of theories that remains visible and firm through various modes of appropriation and interpretation — and venture to explicate its science-theoretical sense step by step, with constant reference to the drafts of old and new positive sciences to which this sense was and still is referred back. We thus presuppose the sciences, as logic itself does, on the basis of the "experience" that gives them to us. In this respect our procedure seems not radical at all; since the genuine sense of sciences as such — or what is the same, the essential possibility of their being as genuine and not merely putative sciences — is precisely what is in question. And the same holds for logic itself, which is supposed to be the science of science as such and in its theories is supposed to lay out precisely this essential possibility, or as a historically present discipline is supposed to have done so. Yet whether genuine or not, we have experience of sciences and logic as cultural formations given to us, which carry their meaning, their "sense" in themselves, since they are practical formations of the scientists and generations of scientists who built them up. As such, they have a purposive sense toward which there is constant striving and willing. Standing or entering into an empathetic community with the scientists, we can re-understand — and "reflect" upon ourselves.

Husserl here introduces a key methodological concept: *Besinnung*. Reflection (*Besinnung*) means nothing other than the attempt at the actual production of the sense "itself" that in mere meaning is meant, presupposed; or the attempt to carry over the "intending sense" (as it was called in the *Logical Investigations*), the sense vaguely "hovering before one" in the unclear aiming, into the fulfilled sense, the clear one, and thus to provide it with the evidence of clear possibility. Just this possibility is genuineness of sense, and thus the goal of reflective seeking and finding. Reflection, we can also say, is radically understood original sense-explication, which transposes sense in the mode of unclear meaning into sense in the mode of fullness of clarity or essential possibility, and first strives to carry it over.

Accordingly, for purposes of radical reflection we can let ourselves be guided by the empathetic experience of the sciences, taking them as products of achievement through which there runs a unity of aiming "meaning." Likewise we can let ourselves be guided by such experience of traditional logic in its relation to the experientially given sciences. Our aim is directed in the first instance at the genuine sense of a logic as science-theory, whose own task it would have to be to lay out clearly the genuine sense of science as such and to explicate it theoretically in that clarity. What we have in advance in the experiential glance is that "core" of formal logic, and in the correlative glance at the given sciences, that in them which this logic — allegedly — grasps as essential possibility and norm. From there, reflection proceeds at first in the one-sidedness conditioned by this reference-back and direction of glance, which determines the specific sense of the traditional logic as essentially "objective."

Radical reflection is *eo ipso* at the same time critique, which serves original clarification. This clarification has the character of a new sense-formation and not that of a mere filling-in of a pre-determined and articulated outline. Such a quite determinate sense-outline is always and essentially possible only as a secondary consequence of an already-gained clarity. When its living evidence has faded, its habitual achievement remains, with the possibility of an initially empty restitution, which then in the empty form contains the determinate sense-outline. This then carries with it the certainty of a possible clear restitution as repetition of the evidence. If, as for us, this case is not in question, then original reflection means at once closer determination of the merely vaguely indeterminate outline, removal of the prejudices stemming from associative overlappings, and cancellation of what conflicts with the reflective fulfillment — in a word, critique of genuineness and non-genuineness.

Husserl now gives a summary characterization of the aim and method of the work. It is an intentional explication of the genuine sense of formal logic. It proceeds from the theoretical formations that the survey of historical experience puts at hand — from what constitutes their traditional objective content — and places them back into the living intention of the logicians from which they sprang as sense-formations. And inseparably from this, it goes back to the intentionality of the scientists, from which the objective stocks of concrete scientific theory sprang — since the logician orients himself by the given sciences. The intentionality that comes alive in every genuine re-understanding is interrogated as to what it actually aims at. Reflective explication as critical clarification must give the answer.

In systematic execution, we will begin quite primitively and not immediately with consideration of the given logic; rather we will tie the first general distinctions to the word-meanings of *Logos* and to the question of how far theoretical themes are thereby designated. Through such "preparatory considerations" we gain a pre-understanding of what the historical logic directs its thematic interest toward, and thus the direction of view for our further sense-analyses.

Husserl previews the results. They lead, in Part I, first to a three-layering of the formal-logical basic concepts and thereby of the logical disciplines — not yet completely recognized in the *Logical Investigations* — which is of the greatest importance not only for the genuine understanding of the true sense of logic as a particular science but for the whole of philosophy. The grounding investigations, as necessarily going back to noetic intentionality (since the logical formations spring from categorial activity), are subjectively directed. Whether they are to be addressed as psychological and however they are to be characterized is entirely outside the question, given their initially merely subsidiary significance.

Connected with the demonstrated three-layering are further great problems: a radical clarification of the relationship of formal logic and formal mathematics, and the deeper (already carried out at a first stage in the *Logical Investigations*) justification of the inseparable unity of both in the idea of a formal *mathesis universalis*. Here the essential advance emerges: the final and, Husserl hopes, definitive clarification of the sense of pure formal mathematics (including formal syllogistics in a corresponding purification), the sense it has in the ruling intention of mathematicians: namely as a pure analytics of consistency (freedom from contradiction), in which the concept of truth remains outside the theme.

Again connected with this is the genuine sense of a formal ontology, whose concept was introduced in the *Logical Investigations* within the essentially fundamental distinction between formal and material (sachhaltige) ontology, or between the realms of an "analytic" and "synthetic" (material) a priori.

Husserl expresses confidence that these sense-clarifications carried out in Part I should be of lasting use to philosophers, as the named problems have worried and occupied him for decades.

For those interested in his more detailed presentations, Husserl adds that his original guiding problem for determining the sense of and demarcating a pure logic of "consistency" was a problem of evidence — namely the evidence of formal-mathematical sciences. He noticed that the evidence of formal-mathematical truths (including syllogistic ones) was a quite different one from that of other a priori truths: they require no concrete exemplary intuition of any objects or states of affairs to which they, albeit in empty-formal generality, relate. If it seemed self-evident that a science relating in this generality to everything and anything, to everything possible and conceivable, deserves the name of a formal ontology, then, if it were to be that in reality, the possibility of objectualities within its scope would have to be grounded in intuition. It is now easy, from the demarcation grounded in Part I of a pure "logic of consequence" or "consistency," to give a decisive answer to this question, even though the investigations carried out in the text do not themselves tie into this problem-setting.

In Part II, the subjective-logical becomes the main theme, always in connection with ongoing reflections on formal logic as a science-theory. The natural path from formal to transcendental logic is traced. The specter of psychologism appears right away, and the particular sense of the much-discussed battle against psychologism in Volume I of the *Logical Investigations* is clarified anew and with sharper focus. A series of presuppositions of logical cognition to which logical thematization points back is then uncovered, and gradually the insight is awakened that all subjectively directed sense-problems that are at issue for science and logic are not problems of natural human subjectivity (i.e., psychological problems) but problems of transcendental subjectivity, in the sense of transcendental phenomenology. In progressive deepening, the insight opens up that a truly philosophical logic — a science-theory that brings the essential possibility of genuine science in general to all-sided explication and can thereby guide the becoming of genuine science — can grow exclusively in the context of a transcendental phenomenology. The historical logic in its naive positivity, in its way of naively drawing evident truths straightforwardly, proves to be a kind of philosophical childishness. A self-standingly developed logic of ideal meaning-formations is just as un-philosophical as the positive sciences in general: it itself lacks the originative genuineness through which it could accomplish ultimate self-understanding and self-justification. The un-philosophical character of this positivity consists in nothing other than the fact that the sciences, through a failure to understand their own achievements as achievements of an intentionality that remains unthematic for them, are unable to clarify the genuine sense of being of their domains and of the concepts that grasp them.

Connected with the dogmatic naivety of an allegedly self-standing formal logic is the naivety of an externally appended, after-the-fact epistemology, raising and supposedly answering questions of origin and validity for it in a way that could no longer change anything about the absoluteness of the validity of objective logic. The true epistemology is the clarification of the "genuine" sense of the logical concepts and of logic itself — not a sense that already exists beforehand, but one first to be created by epistemology, first to be investigated in the horizons of its scope — but under the guidance of the previously merely intended sense. It is as with the positive sciences otherwise: once they are historically present, they are drafts, claims, and as such guiding threads for transcendental investigations, whose aim is to create them, the sciences, as genuine for the first time.

Through these investigations, the radical deficiency of the historical logic — especially that of modernity — will become apparent in ever new stages: that it could in no way do justice to the great tasks posed by the idea of science in the subjective direction, in regard to the judging, cognizing, researching thinking. The psychology of cognition since Locke has completely failed, through its absurd sensualism — already as a properly psychological investigation. But it has also failed, which is of special importance for a philosophical science-theory, because Locke and all later psychologizing logicians and epistemologists could not distinguish between psychological and transcendental investigation of cognition. Principled problems, appearing with the manifest intention toward a radical science-theory and thus as specifically philosophical, were pressed down to the level of an anthropological psychology, and moreover an empirical one. If we add that Kant's transcendental investigation of cognition had an aftereffect that remained far from all genuinely and concretely explicating analysis of cognition, the tremendous deficiency of modern objective science-theory emerges: that it could not even make understandable as a task the deepest clarification and grounding of the possibility of genuine sciences (and thereby of a true objectivity itself) from the universality of consciousness constituting objective sense in itself, let alone develop the method of solution and set it in motion.

Husserl now articulates the constitutional insight. What science has before it as its domain, given to theoretical work, is according to sense and being domain for the researchers from sources of their own consciousness-achievement; furthermore, what has become a finished result as theory of this domain is a result from an act-achievement that produces its entire sense and truth-sense. A theory can be "there" for us naively-straightforwardly from evidence, from repeated critical confirmation (a "real" theory), just as a thing from experience and experiential confirmation is in naive self-evidence existing reality for us. But thereby the being-for-us of the theory is not transcendentally understandable, just as little as the being-for-us of the thing is understandable in this respect — namely as constituted from sources of the individual subjectivity and intersubjectivity, as that which it is for us, for "everyone" — as the only thing that has sense for us. Every sense in which we philosophers ask about a sense of the world (the real one and whatever ideal one) presupposes the clarification of the transcendental origin and moves on the ground of transcendental science.

Husserl invokes Lotze's famous saying that the highest task of cognition is not merely to calculate the course of the world but to understand it, and claims this word, mutatis mutandis, for logic. It cannot suffice that logic, in the manner of the positive sciences, methodically shape objective theories and bring the forms of possible genuine theory to principles and norms. We must raise ourselves above the self-forgetfulness of the theoretician, who in theoretical accomplishing is devoted to the subject matters, theories, and methods, and knows nothing of the inwardness of his accomplishing, who lives in them but does not have this accomplishing life itself in his thematic gaze. Only through a principled clarification that descends into the depths of the cognition- and theory-accomplishing inwardness, of transcendental inwardness, does what is accomplished as genuine theory and genuine science become understandable. Only thereby does the true sense of that being also become understandable which science sought to work out as true being, as true nature, as true spiritual world, in its theories. Therefore: only a science that is transcendentally clarified and justified in the phenomenological sense can be ultimate science; only a transcendental-phenomenologically clarified world can be an ultimately understood world; only a transcendental logic can be an ultimate science-theory, an ultimate and deepest and most universal doctrine of principles and norms of all sciences.

If we grasp the idea of logic again as broadly, as generously, as it demands to be grasped according to its original intention, and animate it with the transcendental spirit, then we must say: what the modern sciences lack is the true logic that encompasses all science-theoretical problems and disciplines in the broadest yet principially unified sense, a logic that as a transcendental one illuminates the sciences with the deepest self-knowledge of cognition and makes them comprehensible in everything they do. This logic does not want to be a merely pure and formal logic, a *mathesis universalis* in the broadest Leibnizian sense — a logical ideal-science and yet only a "positive" science. Nor does it want to be a merely empirical technology for a sort of practically highly useful intellectual achievements called science. Rather, as the highest function of the purely theoretical interest working itself out, it wants to lay bare the system of transcendental principles that gives sciences their possible sense as genuine sciences.

How much the sciences need such a logic — how little they are capable, in naive positivity, of appearing as self-sufficient sciences — is shown by the dispute, lacking in no science however exact, about the true sense of its basic concepts. It is a symptom that they are truly in complete unclarity about their own sense. But of course only transcendental logic lets it be fully understood that the positive sciences can produce only a relative, one-sided rationality, that they leave over a complete irrationality on necessary counter-sides, and that through a mere systematic linking of all individual sciences a universal cognition of being in the highest sense can never arise, as the old philosophy originally strove for.

The final paragraphs of the Introduction concern the structure of the book. Part I has a certain completeness and closure that Part II, more stimulating than ultimately carrying out, necessarily lacks, since in it we are drawn into the enormous expanses of intentional phenomenology, which, even given what is already available of it, is by no means common property. Husserl is preparing a presentation of a series of very extensive investigations, going back many years, which are destined to supplement those of formal science-theory with the quite differently constituted ones of a material science-theory — as well as investigations that attempt to prepare and carry through the other possible paths of radical reflection, alongside the first, so to speak Cartesian path. He closes with warm thanks to Dr. Ludwig Landgrebe for his tireless assistance in the literary completion of the work.

## PREPARATORY CONSIDERATIONS

### §1. Point of Departure from the Meanings of the Word *Logos*: Speech, Thinking, What Is Thought

Husserl begins from the word *Logos*, from which the name "Logic" is derived. It has a great multiplicity of meanings arising through well-understandable transfers from the more original meanings of *legein* — "to lay together," "to lay out," then to lay out by means of the word, of speech.

In developed language, *logos* means sometimes word and speech itself, sometimes what is spoken about — the state of affairs under discussion — then also the thought generated by the speaker for purposes of communication or for himself, that is, the spiritual sense of the linguistic assertoric sentence, what is meant by the expression. Further, in some turns of phrase *logos* points also to the spiritual act itself: the asserting, claiming, or other thinking in which such a sense-content regarding the relevant objects or states of affairs is produced.

All these meanings of the word *logos*, especially where scientific interest is at play, take on an emphatic sense through the entry of the idea of a rational norm. *Logos* then means sometimes reason itself as capacity, then rational — that is, insightful or truth-directed — thinking. More specifically, *logos* also means the capacity to form legitimate concepts, and also this rational concept-formation as well as the correct concept itself.

Taking this manifold of visibly interconnected meanings of the word *logos* as guidance for forming a first idea of a science of *logos*, rich and connected themes for theoretical research and normative application open up. A natural course of research is easily found.

Tying into the second group of meanings, the theme of reason as the capacity for correct and insightfully justifiable thinking — and specially as scientific thinking — leads, via the general question of how transient acts of an I ground corresponding habitual capacities, immediately to the question of what kind of acts the relevant "rational" thinking-acts are. But before the specific character of this rationality can come up for consideration, the specific character of thinking itself must become the theme, prior to all distinction between rational and irrational.

We are led by the sense of talk of *logos* predominantly to asserting, to judging in the ordinary sense of the word, and thus to judgments as thoughts. But that does not encompass all "thinking" in the broadest sense. So we come back to thinking in the broadest sense as what is first to be considered. Since human thinking normally proceeds linguistically, and all activities of reason are bound to speech, since all critique — from which the rationally true is to emerge — employs language as intersubjective critique and always leads in its result to statements, not mere thinking-acts and thoughts but above all statements, expressed thoughts, come into question first. We are thus led back to the first group of meanings of the word *logos*. The research accordingly concerns three titles: speech, thinking, what is thought. Naturally the corresponding capacities must also become thematic.

### §2. The Ideality of the Linguistic. Exclusion of the Pertinent Problems

The three established titles are still very manifold and need further distinction and, because of the flowing unclarity of the words used, clarification. For the title "speech," Husserl notes a certain distinction we must not overlook. The spoken word, the actually uttered speech taken as a sensuous, specifically acoustic phenomenon, is distinguished from the word and the assertoric sentence itself, or from the sentence-sequence making up a larger speech. We speak, when not understood and repeating, of a repetition of the same words and sentences. In a treatise or a novel, every word, every sentence is something unique that is not multiplied through repeated, explanatory, or silent reading. Nor does it matter who reads aloud. The treatise itself (considered now merely grammatically by its word- and language-stock) is distinguished not only from the manifolds of explanatory reproduction but also from the manifolds of permanent documentation through paper and print or parchment and ink-writing. The one single linguistic stock is reproduced a thousandfold; we speak of the same book, with the same novel, the same treatise, and this sameness holds already in a purely linguistic respect.

Language as a system of habitual signs growing in a community, reshaping itself, persisting in the manner of tradition — a system through which, in contrast to other kinds of signs, an expressing of thoughts is carried out — presents its own problems. One of them is the ideality of language. Husserl characterizes this: language has the objectivity of objectualities of the so-called spiritual world or cultural world, not that of mere physical nature. As an objective spiritual formation, language has the same properties as spiritual formations otherwise: we distinguish from the thousands of reproductions of an engraving the engraving itself, and this engraving, the engraved picture itself, is seen through each reproduction and in each given in the same way as an identical ideal. On the other hand, only in the form of reproduction does it have existence in the real world. Similarly when we speak of the Kreutzer Sonata as opposed to its various reproductions. As much as it consists of tones, it is an ideal unity and its tones are no less so. They are not the physical tones or the tones of sensory acoustic perception. As the one sonata reproduces itself manifoldly in real reproductions, so each individual tone reproduces itself in the corresponding tones of reproduction. As the whole, so its part is an ideal that becomes real *hic et nunc* only in the manner of real particularization. The same holds for all linguistic formations, and this ideality is not merely one of what is expressed in them. Our findings concern the linguistic formations also as sense-filled speech, as concrete unities of linguistic body and expressed sense. But they concern them already with regard to the linguistic embodiment itself, which is a kind of spiritual embodiment. The word itself, the grammatical sentence itself, is an ideal unity that does not multiply with its thousandfold reproductions.

The principled discussion of the great problems concerning the clarification of the sense and constitution of objectualities of the spiritual world in all their fundamental formations, and among them language, forms a realm of its own. Here it need only be noted that language for the logician comes into question primarily only in its ideality, as the grammatically identical word, sentence, and sentence-connection — much as the theme of the aesthetician is the sonata itself, the picture itself, not the transient physical sound-complex or the physical picture-thing. This entire problem-group will be left out of account in the further investigations, which will be sufficiently justified from their own content and context.

### §3. Language as Expression of "Thinking." Thinking in the Broadest Sense as Sense-Constituting Experience

Husserl now turns to the second title: thinking, a word whose sense must be taken from the often-named connection "language and thinking." In that connection, the word has an enormously wide sense that, as it almost seems, encompasses the entire psychic life of the human being: for one also says, "in language the human being expresses his psychic life." But we must be more careful. Not all psychic life does the human being really "express" in language and can ever express through it. If common parlance says otherwise, this comes from the ambiguity of talk of "expressing" and the deficient clarification of the relations here obtaining. We can preliminarily bound this talk of "expressing" by noting that with every word and every word-connection ordered into a unity of speech, something is meant. More precisely, where speech runs in its natural function, really as speech in which "this and that is said," the practical intention of the speaker is manifestly not directed ultimately at the mere words, but "through" the words at their meaning; the words carry significative intentions, they serve as bridges to lead over to the meanings, to what is "meant" by them. This holds wherever speech is normally functioning and is real speech at all. A parrot does not truly speak. We also exclude lying speech, which means something other than what it says. To the unity of the speech corresponds a unity of meaning, and to the linguistic articulations and forms of the speech correspond articulations and formations of the meaning. But this meaning does not lie externally beside the words; rather, in speaking we continually perform a meaning, and in performing it the words mean something. Speaking is not merely sounding forth words but meaning, and — in a certain relation to the hearer — making known.

But as we may say, meaning is a mode of intending. And intending is not restricted to that meaning which is essentially bound up with linguistic expression. We understand, or can come to understand, the experience of a person and in empathy find, for example, the joy or the conviction without words and before all words. Let us take it a bit more broadly. When somebody just looks at something, perceives it, just remembers something, or phantasies it, that is already a "consciousness of" something, an "intending" of something, and thus in a certain, broadest, sense a "thinking." In every lived-experience of this type something is "thought" — the perceived thing, the remembered event, the phantasied centaur — and it is there as this, in this "how" of givenness. If we then say: language serves the expression of "thinking," we must take "thinking" in this broadest sense; only then does the expression "thinking" match the full range of what can be expressed.

At any rate, for logic this broadest concept of thinking is initially too broad. Among all the conscious experiences to which objects are given as objects, the assertive — judging — acts of positing are those that come first into question for the logician. It is the "predicative" thinking that he has immediately in view. The question of the essential delimitation of the "thinking" that is able to serve in the meaning-function then arises.

### §4. The Problem of the Essential Delimitation of the "Thinking" Qualified for the Meaning-Function

Not every lived experience, not even every intentional one, can serve as meaning. The pain I feel is certainly an experience, but it is not one in which something is "meant" in the logical sense. To be sure, the pain can become a theme: I can judge about it, wish it away, etc. But the lived pain itself is not a meaning-conferring experience. The question arises: what belongs to the essential type of lived experience that is qualified to function as meaning, as the "thinking" that makes up the intellectual core of linguistic expression? This question must eventually be answered within the framework of a universal theory of intentionality. Husserl leaves it open at this point as one that is not immediately required.

### §5. Preliminary Delimitation of Logic as an A Priori Science-Theory

The reflections so far show that a science of *logos* will be directed toward the forms of thinking-acts and of what is thought (the judgments, in the broadest sense), and above all toward the conditions under which thinking can achieve genuine knowledge. The idea of such a science is the idea of a logic. But what kind of science? All genuine science strives for universal and necessary knowledge. Logic, as a science of the essential conditions of genuine science, must be a priori: it must be an a priori science-theory. That is, it must investigate what belongs to the essence of science as such, to the essential forms of possible genuine theories, possible genuine cognitions. These essential structures hold for any possible science at all, regardless of its particular material content.

### §6. The Formal Character of Logic. Formal and Contingent A Priori

Husserl draws a crucial further distinction. Logic as an a priori science-theory has a specifically formal character. Its truths hold not merely for particular kinds of objects or particular domains but for objects as such, for any domain at all. The "formal" here means: determined purely by the forms of judgments, of concepts, of inferences — not by the material content of what is judged about. This formal a priori is distinguished from the contingent a priori, which pertains to particular material domains (e.g., the a priori of spatial figures, of color-relations). The formal a priori is distinguished by its universality: it is the a priori of the "something in general," of the empty form of objectuality as such. Logic, insofar as it is formal logic, operates exclusively at this level of formal universality.

### §7. The Normative and Practical Function of Logic

Logic has always been understood as having a normative function: it tells us how we ought to think in order to achieve genuine knowledge. But Husserl insists on distinguishing the normative function from the theoretical substance. An a priori science such as logic eo ipso constantly functions normatively and technologically, but it is not thereby a normative discipline in its essence; it is a science in the emphatic sense, an exercise of purely theoretical reason. A priori sciences function eo ipso constantly as normative-technological, but they are thereby sciences and not technologies. The attitude of the technologist (not the technician, but the one who designs a technology) is essentially different from that of the scientist. It is, even when he encounters scientific problems and solves them in a technological interest, a practical and not a theoretical attitude. His theorizing is here a means for an (extra-theoretical) practice. It makes no essential difference that here what is in question is not an individual practical instance but a general kind of practice. The same holds for mere norming taken by itself: the aim is to be practically useful to oneself or others, not to satisfy purely theoretical interests.

The distinction is, however, a relative one, since purely theoretical activity is itself activity, and as such stands under the formal rules of universal practical reason (ethical principles). But then the essential distinction remains that all sciences stand under the idea of an endlessly working-itself-out interest of theoretical reason, referred to the idea of an endlessly cooperating research-community united in activities and habitualities of theoretical reason. A life of individuals and many according to this idea can be compatible with the conviction that all theoretical results so gained in community, and infinite science itself, have a supra-theoretical function for humanity.

### §8. The Two-Sidedness of Logic; The Subjective and the Objective Direction of Its Thematic

Logic as the science of the logical in general, and in its highest, all-encompassing form as the science of science as such, is two-sidedly directed. Everywhere it concerns rational achievements in the double sense of the performing activities and habitualities, on the one hand, and the thereby accomplished and henceforth persisting results, on the other.

In the latter respect, the theme of logic is the manifold forms of judgment- and cognition-formations that grow for the cognizers during the execution of their thinking-activities, and in the special mode of "theme." The respective formations are what the thinker aims at as an enduring acquisition and what is also to serve as a means for gaining new such acquisitions. In their more highly constructed forms, these formations exceed the current sphere of consciousness-presence, but they remain pieces of a thematic "field," a proper realm of practical products. In the unity of a science, all such formations and the entire field of products sprung from the unity of a theoretical interest are all unitarily bound to a universal theory whose systematically ongoing elaboration is the communal goal of the scientists working together in open community.

All this objective has not only the fleeting existence of what appears in the thematic field as an actual formation and then passes away. It also has the being-sense of enduring continued validity, indeed of objective validity in a special sense, reaching beyond the actually cognizing subjectivity and its acts. It remains the identical in repetition, is recognized in the manner of something enduringly existent; in documented form it has objective existence, just as other objectualities of the cultural world. It is intersubjectively identifiable, enduring even when nobody thinks it.

The counter-direction of logical thematization is the subjective one. It goes to the deeply hidden subjective forms in which the theoretical "reason" accomplishes its achievements. First there is the question of reason in actuality — the intentionality running in living execution, in which those objective formations have their "origin." While the corresponding intentionality is in execution, while it runs as life accomplishing objectification, it is "unconscious" — meaning it makes thematic but is not itself thematic. It is hidden so long as it is not uncovered by reflection and thereby itself become a theme. Every objective-logical formation has its "subjective" correlate in its constitutive intentionalities, and essentially to every form of the formations corresponds a system of performing intentionality.

The two-sidedness of everything logical presents extraordinary difficulties for the interpretation of the right sense and right structuring of the correspondingly separating and interweaving problem-groups. One can say that it is because of them that logic, after millennia, has still not gotten onto the firm path of a truly rational development, that it has not become a science that would have advanced to a clear consciousness of its aims. Nearly everything concerning the fundamental sense of logic, its problems, its methods, is afflicted with incomprehensibilities from this murky source — from the objectivity arising from subjective achievement that has remained incomprehensible and never properly interrogated. Even the ideal objectivity of the logical formations and the a priori character of the logical doctrines pertaining to them are affected by this unclarity, since the ideal appears placed within the subjective sphere, as springing from it as a formation.

What we have said so far about logic is thus spoken in a preliminary overview and in an insight that must first be confirmed through more concrete explications and through understanding the historical motivations and the interpretations of logic that sprang from them.

### §9. The Straightforward Thematic of the "Objective" or "Positive" Sciences. The Idea of Two-Sided Sciences

The two-sidedness belonging to everything logical does not mean an equal ordering of the two sides. In simple devotion to the subject matters, thinking means creating a connected thematic field in which exclusively the respective thought-formations lie. Thinking itself — concretely understood as the intentionality in whose "synthesis" the thought-formations constitute themselves as "sense-unities" — first requires uncovering, which would take place in a new thinking.

The thinking of scientists is connected thinking — actually and habitually, individually and intersubjectively — through the unity of a theoretical interest. What grows in scientific thinking as judgment- and cognition-results forms an openly endless thematic field of its own — the ongoing unity of science as theory. This thematic field is transcended by subjectively turned reflections. In general, the scientist will not include a subjective thematic in the research. The geometer will not think of researching, alongside geometrical figures, also geometrical thinking. The subjective of thinking and of the thinking subjects themselves is not thereby drawn into the theme. So in all "objective" or "positive" sciences — what is usually meant when one speaks simply of sciences. This is because the idea of sciences with a consistently two-sided thematic — combining the theory of the scientific domain with a theory of the cognition of this theory — first broke through in modernity, and so unclearly that it first has to struggle for its peculiar sense and its right.

The positive sciences work themselves out exclusively on the level of theory — the theory to be shaped straightforwardly in a thematic direction toward the cognitive domain — in the ongoing categorial elaboration of the experience-objectualities of the domain, and in the systematic connection of the formations thereby gained into ever higher cognition-formations, into the openly endless and yet systematically unified structure of the scientific theory of the domain. This theoretical level closes off the scientific thematic, so much so that the positive sciences are consciously striving to grasp the concept of theoretical objectivity even more strictly, excluding as merely subjective much of what the prescientifically experiencing and thinking person finds as objective theme — as the natural scientist excludes the "sensory qualities."

### §10. Historical Psychology and the Subjectively Directed Thematic of the Sciences

While the positive sciences close themselves off from everything belonging to the merely subjective of experiencing and thinking, in order to satisfy the idea of the pure object as exclusive theme, there nevertheless appears in their circle a positive science of subjects: psychology, which makes the psychic, the specifically subjective, its main theme. Were this psychology in fact the science of everything subjective, it would stand in a remarkable correlation-relation to all sciences. All are subjective formations; all have their objective thematic from hidden achievements. Already their object-domains are pre-theoretically there for the researchers from subjective sources. Such a universal science of the subjective would compass everything conceivable as experienceable and theoretically true. It would be the science of the universal subjectivity in which everything that is truth, from an actual and possible life, would receive the sense of true being. Since this psychology itself would be a science, it would be reflectively self-referential; as a science of everything subjective it would also be the science of that subjective from whose sources it accomplishes its own achievements. This would repeat at infinite levels.

It is evident that no historically developed psychology, old or new, has ever satisfied this universality, let alone seriously thought it through as a problem. How far an anthropology and zoology, including a psychology and psychophysics, is qualified for those universal correlation-tasks with regard to all sciences and all that is for us — that can be called the great riddle of modernity, with its peculiar and ever-renewed attempts at transcendental philosophies, critiques of cognition, theories of cognition, doctrines of understanding and reason. We ourselves will have to carry out more precise considerations on this from our structural investigations into the idea of logic.

Here it matters only to sharply illuminate the contrast between the "straightforward" thematic of the positive sciences — determined by a given object-domain to which they devote themselves in exclusive interest — and the reflective thematic thereby excluded but now openly possible, namely with respect to the experiencing-cognizing-accomplishing subjectivity.

### §11. The Thematic Tendencies of the Traditional Logic

#### a) Logic Originally Directed at the Objective Theoretical Thought-Formations

From the consideration of the sciences we now turn to logic, which as science-theory oriented itself exemplarily by the sciences, and ask how the described essential relations of objective and subjective work themselves out in its historical development.

Self-evidently, from the beginning and really up to our own time, it had nothing other than objective, "positive" sciences before its eyes and as its guide, although at very different developmental stages. Accordingly, it could find as its first universal theme nothing other than the realm of thematic formations of scientific thinking with respect to whatever given objective domains — thus judgments with the "concepts" appearing in them, inferences, proofs, closed theories, with the belonging modalities and the normative differences of truth and falsity. To investigate all these actual and putative science-formations according to their form-typology and the conditions of possible truth interwoven with it was the task that first presented itself.

The predominantly cognition-practical interest of the logicians directed the gaze toward the acts of cognition. But what mattered was not an uncovering immersion into the constitutive intentionality that plays itself out hiddenedly in the cognizers, but only what — as in every willing aiming and action — goes on visibly in the consciousness-field together with the goal-setting and the realizing becoming of the results: "I strive for this and that, I produce it, it becomes willfully from my I." In this, the formations in their manifold contents and articulations and in their stages of intermediate formations are the properly describable, as against the uniform and always identical "I-am-aiming-at and I-am-producing."

The theoretical formations present themselves not as transient like the I-acts but as objects — as graspable, persistent, repeatedly identifiable objectualities accessible to consideration, analysis, and description — not much differently from those of outer experience. Only they are not passive pregivennesses but, as categorial, are given only through theoretical action. But then they are there, identifiable in repetition of the activity. So in every judging of one's own they can be taken as results, at all levels of complication. So the logician had standing objects as exemplary substrates for "ideations"; there arose the possibility for those "pure formalizations" through which the concepts of analytic-logical "forms" emerge. These forms were then first right something fixed and persistent, describable according to their elementary form-elements but also considered from operative viewpoints. There were ways of constructive form-variation, form-connection in iterative repeatability as open possibilities.

#### b) The Direction of Logic at Truth and the Thereby Conditioned Subjective Reflection on Insight

The orientation of logic was not at judgments in general, at putative knowledge, but ultimately at genuine knowledge and its typical formations. This inevitably brought about something like a subjective turn. One said: genuine knowledge, truth, is grasped in the activities of "reason," in insight, which, once exercised, is repeatable and also repeatable by every other rational being and remains as a spiritual possession. Propositions that are immediately insightful lead in insightful elementary inferences to propositions that thereby become insightful as consequent truths. A deductive theory, a genuine one, is a connection of elementary steps, built up out of steps of insight and so producing a unity of truth. In genuine science, nothing on the objective side appears in propositions and proposition-connections that has not acquired its "cognition-value," its validity-character of truth, from insights.

In such reflections, which manifestly determine the beginning of logic, talk of something subjective — above all of reason and insight — is inevitable. But however much this subjective talk presupposes a glance-turning toward the psychic, everything that comes to determination in science as a result lies purely on the objective side, and thus what the logician wants to thematically extract and treat as a theory of theory is only the objectively logical.

It is here especially to be noted that the "true," the "following from," the "consistent" acquired in insight appears as character and predicate at the judgment-formations themselves, thus on the objective side, and thus is a theme in the formal theories to be treated by a pure logic of meanings. Everything in the emphatic sense "logical," the "rational," has these characters as objective ones, and logic must expressly name them and investigate the conditions of their rightful belonging. Truth is the objective expression, insight and reason the subjective and correlative one. Every unitarily closed and simply set-up scientific statement has or claims to have this predicate of truth drawn from insight.

The frequent reflection on the subjective doing is common to the sciences with other fields of artful action. In the sense of the thought-formations themselves, as sprung from thinking-actions, lies the reference to the belonging acts. So one can describe the formations from the standpoint of the actor and his doing. Instead of saying *a − b + b = a*, one can say: subtract b from a and add b again, etc. But nothing essential is thereby gained; it is a reference to the rhythm of I-acts, but no genuine description of these acts is accomplished. Counting is producing numbers, subtracting is producing differences — one has the products in view, not a description of the producing I-activity beyond the empty "I count," "I infer." This does not mean there are no subjective analyses and descriptions, only that beyond the products and their subjective running-off, an intentional subjectivity is to be researched in which the becoming and become products constitute themselves as synthetic unities — a subjectivity that is not yet disclosed by the mere turn to the "I think."

#### c) Result: The Hybrid Character of the Historical Logic as a Theoretical and Normative-Practical Discipline

After all this, we understand why logic in its entire development up to the most recent time (so long as transcendental-philosophical motives did not radically affect it) had to have its essential thematic sphere in the field of theory, of the manifold judgment- and cognition-formations, and why the externally prominent thematic of the subjective thinking-actions had only a completely secondary character.

But we must not overlook the following. In describing the thematic tendencies of the traditional logic, as motivated by the guidance of the positive sciences, we had to dissect the intentional connections with a consciousness and sharpness still foreign to the logic itself. What we chiefly had our eye on — the objectivity of the theoretical formations as givennesses of a proper "experience" (the "categorial" one) — has by no means come to acceptance in the tradition and even today must still fight for its right. The naturally preferred normative and cognition-technical orientation of the logicians placed in the foreground the thinking as spiritual doing and thereby the real psychic, in which the irreal, the ideal thought-formation in each case appears. This doing, or the psychic subject as thinkingly active, was to be regulated. The pressing normative interest comprehensibly tended to cover over the ideal objectivity of the formations themselves and not to let a consciously pure theoretical thematic related to them arise. And yet the logician's work constantly had to do with these ideal formations, identifying them, bringing them under form-concepts, etc. They remained, however, thematically unseparated from the subjective.

What mattered for us now was to understand, through unfolding the most original sense-determining intentionality for logic, the essential character of the historical logic. Briefly summarized: first, to understand it in its hybrid character as a theoretical and normative-practical discipline, and in the thereby given hybrid thematic — on the one hand ideal meanings (categorial formations) and on the other thinking-actions and their normative regulation. Further, to understand that the theoretically graspable core in this hybridity, what lay in the historical syllogistic theories, was in essence nothing other, though not grasped in purity, than theory of theory, theory of the judgment- and cognition-formations of the ideal-objective field. What went beyond this in subjectively directed speech brought, as we showed, no essentially new content, but only self-evident subjective turns. Only very late — with the onset of psychologistic or anti-psychologistic transcendental philosophy — did really new and content-rich subjective investigations attach, under an unlucky star however, and in any case still struggling for their right sense.

We left these aside, in order to investigate, guided by our self-acquired insight about the purely objective thematic of logic and in connection with the original logical apophantics, the essential structures of an objective, apophantic logic — an "analytic," "formal" logic — and then the problems of its essential delimitation.

## Part I. The Structures and Scope of Objective Formal Logic

### A. The Path from Tradition to the Full Idea of Logic

### Chapter 1. Formal Logic as Apophantic Analytics

#### §12. The Discovery of the Idea of Pure Judgment-Form

Husserl traces the fundamental discovery that grounds the very possibility of formal logic: the recognition that judgments have a pure form distinguishable from their material content. When we formalize a judgment like "this house is red" into "S is p," replacing the material terms with variables, we arrive at a pure judgment-form. This discovery — implicitly Aristotelian — is what first makes possible a science of judgment-forms as such, a formal apophantic logic. The insight is that the form "S is p" is something identifiable across all particular instances, that it can be grasped as an ideal type, and that this grasping opens a domain of investigation: the domain of all possible pure forms of judgment. This is the first step toward the construction of formal logic. The discovery of the pure judgment-form is the discovery that there is a field of formal structures that can be studied independently of any particular subject matter.

#### §13. The Pure Morphology of Judgments as the First Formal-Logical Discipline

##### a) The Idea of the Morphology of Forms

The first discipline within formal logic is the pure morphology of judgments (*reine Formenlehre*) — a systematic inventory and theory of all possible judgment-forms. This morphology asks: what forms can judgments take? What are the basic forms and how can they be combined to produce compound forms? It is a pure theory of the syntactic possibilities of judgments, investigating which combinations of form-elements yield legitimate (meaningful) judgments and which yield mere nonsense.

##### b) The Generality of Judgment-Form; Basic Forms and Their Modifications

The generality of judgment-form means that any material whatever can be inserted into the formal positions. The basic forms — the categorical "S is p," the hypothetical "if p then q," the disjunctive, the conjunctive, and so on — admit of modifications: pluralization, negation, modalization (possibility, probability), nominalization, and so forth. These modifications are themselves governed by formal laws.

##### c) The Concept of Operation as a Guiding Concept of Form-Research

The concept of operation — the idea that complex judgment-forms are produced from simpler ones by definite formal operations (conjunction, disjunction, negation, nominalization, etc.) — serves as the guiding concept for the investigation of forms. This operational perspective reveals the constructive, iterative character of the morphological domain: from any given forms, always new forms can be produced through operations, in open infinity.

#### §14. The Logic of Consequence (Logic of Freedom from Contradiction) as the Second Level of Formal Logic

Beyond the pure morphology of forms, a second level of formal logic emerges: the logic of consequence, or the logic of freedom from contradiction (*Widerspruchslosigkeit*). While the morphology investigates which combinations of form-elements are grammatically possible (i.e., yield meaningful judgments at all as opposed to mere nonsense), the logic of consequence investigates which formally possible judgments are compatible with one another. Not every grammatically well-formed judgment-combination is consistent; some involve formal contradiction. The logic of consequence investigates the formal laws governing compatibility and derivability among judgments. It answers the question: given judgments of certain forms, what follows from them formally, and which combinations are formally excluded as contradictory? This is a layer deeper than the mere morphology, because it considers not just whether a judgment-form is meaningful but whether a combination of judgment-forms is non-contradictory.

#### §15. Truth-Logic and Consequence-Logic

A third and highest level within formal logic is then distinguishable: the logic of truth (*Wahrheitslogik*). While consequence-logic asks only about formal consistency — whether judgments can coexist without contradiction — truth-logic asks about the formal conditions of possible truth. Freedom from contradiction is a necessary but not sufficient condition for truth. A set of judgments can be formally consistent and yet none of them true. The logic of truth adds to the demands of consequence-logic the further demand of possible adequation to objectualities themselves. Husserl insists that the distinction between consequence-logic and truth-logic is not one that the tradition has drawn clearly, yet it is of fundamental importance for understanding the layered structure of formal logic.

#### §16. The Evidence-Differences Grounding the Level-Distinction of Apophantics. Evidence of Clarity and Evidence of Distinctness

##### a) The Modes of Execution of Judgment. Distinctness and Confusion

The level-distinction just established is grounded in phenomenological evidence-differences. Judgments can be executed in different modes. A judgment can be executed in the mode of distinctness (*Deutlichkeit*) — meaning that each of its syntactic members is articulated, each step of the judging is carried out step by step, so that the judgment-sense is fully unfolded. Or it can be executed in the mode of confusion (*Verworrenheit*) — where the judging runs through in a vague, unarticulated way, where the members are not distinctly differentiated. In the mode of distinctness, the judgment is carried through such that at every step the judging subject has before itself clearly the syntactic form: the subject, the predicate, the copula, the subordinate clauses, and so on. In the mode of confusion, the judging runs on without this articulation. This distinction between distinct and confused execution is not a matter of attention in the ordinary sense; it is a difference in the mode of intentional accomplishment.

##### b) Distinctness and Clarity

Distinctness must further be distinguished from clarity (*Klarheit*). A judgment can be carried out distinctly — every syntactic member articulated — and yet unclearly: the terms may remain empty, without intuitive filling. Clarity comes when the judgment is not merely distinctly articulated but also intuitively filled — when the objects and states of affairs meant in the judgment come to intuitive self-givenness. Distinctness without clarity is possible: I can distinctly judge "the sum of the angles of a triangle equals two right angles" without having any intuitive presentation of a triangle or its angles. Clarity without distinctness is also possible: I can have a vague intuitive sense of a state of affairs without articulately judging it.

##### c) Clarity of Self-Having and Clarity of Anticipation

Within clarity itself, a further distinction is drawn. There is the clarity of actual self-having (*Selbsthabe*) — the full intuitive presence of the judged state of affairs — and the clarity of anticipation — where the judging subject does not yet have the object or state of affairs in full intuitive presence but has a directed anticipation of it, a forward-reaching intention toward its possible self-givenness. This distinction is important because the evidence of the logic of consequence (distinctness) and the evidence of the logic of truth (clarity in the sense of self-having) are fundamentally different modes.

#### §17. The Essential Kind "Distinct Judgment" as the Theme of "Pure Analytics"

On the basis of the foregoing evidence-distinctions, Husserl can now say that pure analytics — the logic of consequence — has as its theme judgments purely as distinct judgments. Its concern is not with whether these judgments are true, not with whether the objects they intend actually exist, but only with whether, taken as distinct judgments, they are formally consistent. The essential kind "distinct judgment" forms a closed domain for investigation: the domain of judgments that can be distinctly executed as non-contradictory unities.

#### §18. The Fundamental Question of Pure Analytics

The fundamental question of pure analytics is: under what formal conditions can judgments be united into a non-contradictory whole? This is the question of the formal laws of consequence and compatibility. Given any judgments of certain pure forms, pure analytics asks what follows from them and with what they are compatible — purely on the basis of form, without any regard to the material content or to possible truth.

#### §19. Pure Analytics as the Foundation of the Formal Logic of Truth. Freedom from Contradiction as a Condition of Possible Truth

Pure analytics, though it does not itself concern truth, stands in an essential relation to the logic of truth. For freedom from contradiction is a necessary condition of possible truth. If judgments are formally contradictory, they cannot possibly all be true. Thus the laws of pure analytics — laws of formal consistency — are at the same time, indirectly, laws of possible truth. They are the first and most general necessary conditions for truth, though not sufficient conditions. The logic of truth builds upon pure analytics but adds the further dimension of possible adequation to the things themselves.

#### §20. The Logical Principles and Their Analogues in Pure Analytics

The traditional logical principles — the principle of non-contradiction and the principle of excluded middle — have analogues within pure analytics, but the analogy is not identity. The principle of non-contradiction in pure analytics says: a judgment and its negation cannot both be distinctly executed in a unified judgment-whole (they are incompatible in the mode of distinctness). This is weaker than the full logical principle, which says: a proposition and its negation cannot both be true. The analytic version speaks only of formal compatibility in distinctness, not of truth. Husserl carefully distinguishes these layers.

#### §21. The Evidence in the Coincidence of "the Same" Confused and Distinct Judgment. The Broadest Concept of Judgment

There is an evidence in the synthetic coincidence of a confused judgment and its distinct articulation as "the same" judgment. When we pass from the confused mode to the distinct mode of executing the same judgment, we experience an identifying synthesis: this is the same judgment, now made explicit. This evidence grounds the broadest concept of judgment as that which can be identified through such synthetic coincidence. A judgment in the broadest sense is anything that can be distinctly articulated and, in this articulation, recognized as the same as what was confusedly intended.

#### §22. The Domain-Concept of the Apophantic Morphology as Pure-Logical Grammar is the Judgment in the Broadest Sense

The domain-concept of the apophantic morphology — the first-level discipline of formal logic — is accordingly the judgment in this broadest sense. The morphology studies the pure forms of everything that can count as a judgment at all, including confused and pre-articulate intentions insofar as they are capable of being brought to distinct articulation. Its scope is thus the entire domain of what is syntactically formable.

### Chapter 2. Formal Apophantics and Formal Mathematics

#### §23. The Inner Unity of the Traditional Logic and the Problem of Its Relation to Formal Mathematics

##### a) The Conceptual Self-Enclosure of the Traditional Logic as Apophantic Analytics

The traditional logic as apophantic analytics was conceptually self-enclosed. It dealt with the forms of judgments, inferences, and proofs and had a definite and seemingly complete set of concepts and theories for doing so. It did not feel the need to step beyond the domain of judgment-forms.

##### b) The Emergence of the Idea of an Extended Analytics, Leibniz's *Mathesis Universalis*, and the Methodical-Technical Unification of Traditional Syllogistics and Formal Mathematics

With Leibniz emerges the idea of a broader analytics — a *mathesis universalis* — that would encompass not only the traditional syllogistic but also formal mathematics (arithmetic, algebra, analysis). The key idea is that formal mathematics, like formal logic, operates with pure forms and is governed by a priori formal laws. The question then arises: what is the relation between these two? Are they the same science or different sciences? Leibniz's vision of a *mathesis universalis* proposed their unification, and the subsequent development of mathematical logic and the logical calculus pursued this unification methodically-technically. But the philosophical clarification of this unity — of its sense and its justification — remained inadequate.

#### §24. The New Problem of a Formal Ontology. Characterization of the Inherited Formal Mathematics as Formal Ontology

Husserl raises the problem of formal ontology. While traditional logic deals with forms of judgments (apophantic forms), formal mathematics deals with forms of objects — with such concepts as set, number, order, magnitude, manifold, and so on. These are not judgment-forms but object-forms: they characterize the formal structure of possible objects as such. A formal ontology would be an a priori science of objects in general, determining what holds for objects purely in virtue of their formal-categorial structure. The inherited formal mathematics turns out, when properly understood, to be such a formal ontology — or at least to contain one.

#### §25. Thematic Distinctness and Yet Factual Togetherness of Formal Apophantics and Formal Ontology

Formal apophantics (the logic of judgment-forms) and formal ontology (the science of object-forms) are thematically distinct: the one studies forms of judgments, the other forms of objects. Yet they belong inseparably together. For every judgment is a judgment about objects, and the formal structure of judgments mirrors the formal structure of the objects judged about. The forms of objects (property, relation, set, number, etc.) appear within judgments as syntactic components. And conversely, the formal-ontological concepts derive from the syntactic operations of judging. There is a rigorous correlation between the two sides.

#### §26. The Historical Reasons for the Concealment of the Problem of the Unity of Formal Apophantics and Formal Mathematics

##### a) The Lack of the Concept of Pure Empty Form

One reason the unity of formal apophantics and formal mathematics was historically concealed is the lack of the concept of pure empty form. The tradition did not clearly grasp that both formal logic and formal mathematics operate with forms that are "empty" — purified of all material content — and that this emptiness is what unites them.

##### b) The Lack of Recognition of the Ideality of Apophantic Formations

Another reason is the failure to recognize the ideality of apophantic formations — the fact that judgments, propositions, and inferences are ideal objects, not real psychological events. Without this recognition, the systematic unity of logic and mathematics could not come into view.

##### c) Further Reasons, Especially the Lack of Genuine Origin-Research

Further reasons include the general absence of what Husserl calls genuine origin-research (*Ursprungsforschung*) — investigations into how the basic concepts of logic and mathematics arise from the constitutive intentional activities of consciousness.

##### d) Remark on Bolzano's Position Regarding Formal Ontology

Husserl adds a brief note on Bolzano, who anticipated some of these insights — particularly regarding the objectivity and ideality of propositions — but whose position regarding formal ontology was not sufficiently developed.

#### §27. The Introduction of the Idea of Formal Ontology in the *Logical Investigations*

##### a) The First Constitutive Investigations of Categorial Objectualities in the *Philosophy of Arithmetic*

Husserl traces the origin of his own concept of formal ontology back to his *Philosophy of Arithmetic*, where he first undertook constitutive investigations of categorial objectualities like sets and numbers, asking how such objects are constituted in the acts of collecting, counting, and ordering.

##### b) The Path of the *Prolegomena* from Formal Apophantics to Formal Ontology

In the *Prolegomena to Pure Logic* (the first volume of the *Logical Investigations*), Husserl traced the path from formal apophantics (the theory of judgment-forms) to formal ontology (the theory of object-forms), showing that a complete formal logic must encompass both sides and unify them in a single *mathesis universalis*.

### Chapter 3. Theory of Deductive Systems and Manifold-Theory

#### §28. The Highest Level of Formal Logic: the Theory of Deductive Systems, or Manifold-Theory

Beyond the lower levels of formal logic (morphology, consequence-logic, truth-logic), there is a highest level: the theory of deductive systems, or equivalently the theory of manifolds (*Mannigfaltigkeitslehre*). This level investigates not individual judgments or even individual inferences but entire deductive theories as wholes — the formal structures of possible complete scientific theories. Correlatively, it investigates the forms of possible object-domains (manifolds) that such theories could theorize about.

#### §29. The Formalizing Reduction of Nomological Sciences and the Manifold-Theory

If we take any nomological (law-governed, deductive) science and subject its theory to a formalizing reduction — replacing all material terms with variables — we obtain the pure form of that deductive system. The manifold-theory studies these pure forms in their generality: the forms of possible deductive systems and the forms of the manifolds they determine.

#### §30. The Manifold-Theory since Riemann

Husserl briefly situates the mathematical manifold-theory in its historical development since Riemann. The mathematical concept of a manifold — a multiply extended magnitude governed by certain structural laws — provides the mathematical implementation of the idea of a formal object-domain. Since Riemann, mathematicians have studied manifolds as formal structures abstractly, without reference to any particular realization.

#### §31. The Pregnant Concept of a Manifold, or of a "Deductive," "Nomological System," Clarified Through the Concept of "Definiteness"

The pregnant concept of a manifold is clarified through the concept of definiteness (*Definitheit*). A manifold is definite when its axiom-system completely determines all possible truths about the objects of the manifold: every meaningful proposition about the manifold's objects is decidable from the axioms. This concept of definiteness picks out a particularly important subclass of manifolds — those whose theory is complete in the logical sense.

#### §32. The Supreme Idea of a Manifold-Theory as a Universal Nomological Science of Manifold-Forms

The supreme idea of the manifold-theory is that of a universal nomological science of manifold-forms — a science that would investigate all possible forms of definite manifolds and the relations among these forms. This is the highest level of formal logic conceived as *mathesis universalis*.

#### §33. Actual Formal Mathematics and Mathematics of Game-Rules

Husserl distinguishes between actual formal mathematics — which, even when it does not explicitly attend to the question of possible application, retains in its sense a reference to possible objects — and a merely game-rule mathematics (*Spielregelmathematik*), which treats its symbols as counters in a formal game defined by arbitrary conventions. The philosophical logician cannot accept a mathematics that has torn itself free from the idea of possible application and become a mere intellectual game or, in the purely calculatory mathematics, a game of symbols that receive their sense through mere computational conventions.

#### §34. Complete Formal Mathematics is Identical with the Complete Logical Analytics

The fully developed formal mathematics is identical with the fully developed logical analytics. There is not a separate "formal logic" and a separate "formal mathematics" — when both are developed to their full extent, they turn out to be one and the same science: the complete *mathesis universalis*, the universal formal analytics of possible objects and possible truths about objects.

#### §35. Why in the Domain of the *Mathesis Universalis* as Universal Analytics Only Deductive Theory-Forms Can Become Thematic

##### a) Only Deductive Theory Has a Purely Analytic System-Form

Within the *mathesis universalis* as universal analytics, only deductive theories can become thematic. This is because only deductive theory has a purely analytic system-form: it is only in deductive theory that the entire content of the theory follows from its axioms by purely formal (analytic) derivation. Non-deductive theories involve material or synthetic elements that take them outside the formal domain.

##### b) The Problem: When Does a System of Propositions Have an Analytically Characterizable System-Form?

The correlative question is: under what conditions does a system of propositions have an analytically characterizable system-form? This is the question of when a body of knowledge can be cast in deductive form — a question that is itself formal-logical.

#### §36. Retrospect and Anticipation of the Further Tasks

Husserl reviews what has been accomplished. The three-layering of formal logic (morphology, consequence-logic, truth-logic) and its extension through the manifold-theory have been established. The unity of formal logic and formal mathematics in the *mathesis universalis* has been demonstrated. But a crucial problem remains: the relation between formal apophantics and formal ontology — the two "sides" of the *mathesis universalis* — has not yet been sufficiently clarified. This is the task of the next chapters.

### B. Phenomenological Clarification of the Two-Sidedness of Formal Logic as Formal Apophantics and Formal Ontology

### Chapter 4. Orientation Toward Objects and Orientation Toward Judgments

#### §37. The Question of the Relation of Formal Apophantics and Formal Ontology; the Insufficiency of the Previous Clarifications

Husserl returns to the demonstration that the formal-analytic disciplines are at once formal-ontological and apophantic. The theory-forms have, by their own sense, their correlates in objectual manifolds. The consistently pursued path from the side of propositions leads, by virtue of the sense-reference to objectualities lying in the propositions themselves, to a universal formal ontology. But is this essential double sense already sufficiently clear? Is it already clear what it means to be oriented toward judgments versus toward objectuality in formal generality? And is the sense of formal ontology versus material ontology transparent enough? These difficulties are what the next investigations must resolve.

#### §38. Judgment-Objects as Such and Syntactic Formations

Husserl poses the question: can a formal ontology be distinguished from an apophantic logic at all, even if only as its correlate, arising through a mere change of orientation from propositions to objects? Its domain is supposed to be the "formal region" of the object as such, determining objects in apodictically true propositions in this formal generality. But when we examine any exemplary case — this table here — and perform determinations in terms of properties, relations, pluralities, and so forth, and then try to determine it purely through "formal-ontological" concepts like object, property, relation, plurality, etc. (the modification-concepts of the something-in-general), are these anything other than "categorial" concepts, that is, concepts arising through abstractive regard to the syntactic forms in which the object is grasped through changing levels of syntactic actions — the judgment-actions? In formal analytics, the object is thought purely as object of possible judgments and of the judgment-forms accruing to it through them. The forming that takes place in judgment — from which also all mathematical concepts like set, number, series, magnitude, manifold arise — takes place not at the "transcendent" objects but at the objects as presented in the judgment itself. So: have we actually stepped beyond a formal judgment-theory? Do we not stand, with all formal distinctions of judgment-objectualities, still within it?

#### §39. Extension of the Concept of Judgment to All Formations of Syntactic Actions

Husserl acknowledges that in the preceding discussion, activities like collecting, counting, ordering, combining have been counted among the judgment-activities and their correlates among judgment-formations. But are they not indeed form-producing activities at various levels, and are they not represented in the ordinary (predicative) judgments by forms that no theory of judgment-forms may overlook? Just as property designates a form initially occurring unselfstandingly in the judgment, which "nominalized" yields the substrate-form property, so the plural arises in plural judging, and "nominalized" — transformed into an object in the distinguished sense of "object-about" — yields the set. These are "objectivating" (doxic) activities like the predicative ones; they have the same belief-modalities, they can be carried out on any substrates (something-in-general), and their formations are thus formal categories in the same way. In a truly complete apophantics, all forms of doxic "positings" and doxic "propositions" must occur — all those we address as formal-ontological. But one must notice this togetherness and not limit apophantic logic as if sets and set-theory, numbers and number-theory, were none of its concern.

#### §40. Formal Analytics as Thought-Game and Logical Analytics. The Reference to Possible Application Belongs to the Logical Sense of the Formal Mathesis

What has just been said gains a significant further turn when we take the cognition-interest into account. If we are within the horizon of a science, then every kind of doxic activity is necessarily thought as included in predicative activities directed toward cognition of the domain. One then does not collect and count for play or for other reasons, but in the interest of cognition of the domain, ultimately to cognize and predicatively determine the elements and unities as belonging to it. Therefore in logic, which has exclusively cognition-interests in view, only predicative judgments are spoken of — in which the pluralities, numbers, etc. appear as partial formations.

A mathematics developing as a specialty may not care about this — that it is logic and logical method, that it has a cognition-service to perform. It need not care that the reference to openly undetermined, ideally possible application belongs to its own formal-logical sense. But the philosophical logician must care. He cannot accept a mathematics thought *kata mēdemian symplokēn* — one that tears itself from the idea of possible application and becomes a clever thought-game or, in the merely calculatory mathematics, a game of symbols receiving their sense from mere computational conventions. As a logician he must see that formal mathematics is originally logical analytics and that to its own logical sense belongs a range of cognition-function — that of possible applications — which, in all their indeterminateness, still belong to the mathematical sense. And just thereby we stand in the apophantic judgment-sphere, to which all mathematical formations as component pieces are subordinated.

#### §41. The Difference Between Apophantic and Ontological Orientation and the Task of Clarifying It

Returning to the difficulty: the idea of the formal is firmly delimited by the doxic syntaxes that can all enter into the syntactic unity of an apophansis, a judgment in the ordinary logical sense. All cognition-objectualities as judgment-objectualities have a "categorial" form, a syntactic form stemming from *kategorein* and its syntactic actions. Formal logic determines objects in pure generality through this form. True also that nowhere else than in the judgment does the empty concept "something" appear. But is it thereby said that between apophantic logic and formal ontology there is no difference at all? Is it not essentially different to be thematically oriented toward judgments — and therein, toward the syntactic formations appearing with the sense of judgment-components — and to be oriented toward objects and their syntactic formations, which are indeed thematic in judging but in such a way that the judgments themselves and their components are not?

#### §42. The Solution of This Task

##### a) Judging is Not Directed at the Judgment but at the Thematic Objectuality

When we judge, we do not have the judgment itself before us as our theme. Judging, we are not directed at the judgment but at the "objects about which" (substrate-objects), at the predicates, at the relations, etc. The judgment that is being "passed" in the judging activity is something we have, but not as an object — not as a theme or judgment-substrate. A change of orientation is always possible in which we make our judgments, their components, connections and relations, the theme; but this happens in a new judging of the second level — a judging about judgments, in which judgments become objects of determination. Without this change of orientation, we could get no concept of judgment at all.

##### b) The Identity of the Thematic Object in the Change of Syntactic Operations

In the orientation toward objects, we carry out manifold operations in judging. Having judged "S is p," we can nominalize and judge: that S is p is regrettable, has its reasons, etc. The syntactic transformations do not change the fact that we are objectually directed. The relevant syntactic forms appear on the judgment-objectual, though we do not attribute them to it in the same way. "The same" state of affairs is, in different form, judged as "S is p" and "this, that S is p..." These identifying acts are syntheses connecting judgments to judgments of higher level, introducing the form "the same" into the meaning-content. It belongs to the essence of the objectual orientation that the judging constantly carries out identifications through the change of judgment-modes.

##### c) The Typology of Syntactic Object-Forms as That of the Modes of the Something

The objectual identified in such judgment-courses has, regarding its possible categorial formations, a quite determinate typology. It is the typology of the modes of the something-in-general: as "property," relation, state of affairs, plurality, singularity, series, order, etc. These are derivation-forms of the something, of the formal basic-category object, and they derive from it through the judging itself, through all the doxic activities that make up the wider sense of judgment. Property as a form originally arises in simple categorical judging, collection in collecting — not as a real psychic datum but as an intentional positional correlate. In the course of determining judging, it can take on various judgment-syntaxes and in the change of these be identified as the same property, state of affairs, collection, generality, etc.

##### d) The Double Function of Syntactic Operations

The syntactic operations function doubly: on the one hand as form-creating, producing objectualities of the various syntactic forms (the derivation-forms of the something-in-general); on the other hand as the changing syntaxes that such a categorial objectuality can take on, in whose change it remains identical. All syntactic modes function in both ways. And what was already important from another side: into the form "state of affairs" all syntactic forms can enter as component pieces belonging to it. On the other hand, the state of affairs can receive from the outside, from further-reaching syntactic activities, a changing functional form that is not attributed to it itself.

##### e) Connection of Judging in the Unity of the Self-Determining Substrate-Objectuality. Constitution of Its Determining "Concept"

The progressive judging has connection through the unity of a "theme" in the first, most emphatic sense — the substrate-objectuality whose determination is constantly and ultimately aimed at. Judging and judging again, the judger acquires for this objectuality what it is like, individually or generally; he acquires states of affairs, in which it comports itself thus and so — always new categorial objectualities into which the substrate-objectuality enters judgmentally. The judgment-process can progress as a unity into infinity. All the manifold particular formations that the judger actively acquires have categorial connection in the identity of the substrate-objectuality and constitute for it progressively the determining concept that accrues to it from all these judging achievements — the respective "what it, all told, is thereby" — an ever-progressing, ever-reshaping concept. In the course of judgment-actions, convictions do not merely expand but may also be struck out as "false," with others entering as the "correct" ones. Ideas and idea-differences also belong to the unity of the constituting achievement — such as the idea of "nature as it simply is" versus nature as judged according to the hitherto achieved judgment-achievement.

##### f) The Categorial Formations Arising in Determining as Habitual and Intersubjective Possession

The categorial formations are not merely objectualities during and while the judging action takes place. In their own being-sense lies transcendence. What the judger has shaped in his actual thinking, he possesses as an enduring spiritual acquisition: the actual validity transforms into a habitual one. What was once posited in validity through genuinely constituting activity can resurface in memory, and not merely as having been in validity but as still valid. But this resurfacing includes the certainty of being able to reactivate the constitutive process, to restore it in its continued validity. Only so can progressive judgment-processes, including those to be resumed after interruptions, become possible.

Nothing essentially changes when we also take into account the communalization of judging. The categorial objectualities are then those constituted in the judging community. Not "nature for me" but "nature for us," "for us natural scientists," "for the natural science of our time."

##### g) Objectuality Already Given to Thinking versus Categorial Thought-Objectuality — Illustrated by Nature

One must not recur to the nature given before all thinking through mere experience. Nature as a judgment-formation, especially as a natural-scientific cognition-formation, will of course have beneath it nature as an experience-formation, as a unity of actual and possible experience. But the beneath-it is at the same time an in-it. Only the experience taken up into the judging itself determines the judgment-sense, and for the judger as such, only the nature that is categorially formed in judging is valid. We do call the unity of universal experience "nature" and say it is and has its properties "before" our judging. But only from our judging and for possible judgers does it a priori have the "being" and the "it is how it is." Only when we synthetically pass from simple judging on the basis of experience to making the experiencing itself and its achievements a judgment-theme can we originally know that experiencing already carries "implicitly" the being-sense of nature — the same sense that thinking explicates.

All this is nothing less than a piece of argumentative idealism, and not drawn from any speculating "epistemology" or standpoint-philosophy, but simple reflection and demonstration.

#### §43. The Analytics as Formal Science-Theory is Formal Ontology and as Such Objectually Directed

What holds for nature and for natural science holds for all domains and their sciences. It also concerns analytic logic as formal science-theory. Its theme is the categorial objectualities in general according to their pure forms, grasped under the form-concepts arising through formalization, with the material "cores" left in indeterminate generality. This "abstraction" from material content means, correlatively, that the concept-formation of logic follows exclusively the categorial syntaxes. The analytics as formal science-theory is, like the sciences themselves, ontically directed, and by virtue of its a priori generality, ontological. It is formal ontology. Its a priori truths say what holds for objects in general, for object-domains in general in formal generality — in what forms they can at all be — naturally in a judgment-wise manner, since objects in general "are" only in a judgment-wise way and are in categorial forms, for the same reason.

#### §44. Turn from Analytics as Formal Ontology to Analytics as Formal Apophantics

##### a) Thematic Reorientation from Object-Domains to Judgments in the Sense of Logic

After reaching this point, the question arises: has not the very opposite of what we wanted to show been made evident? We wanted to show the two-foldness of the idea of logic as formal ontology and formal apophantics. But it seems we have only the one. All objectualities with which we are ever engaged, with all their formal-ontological formations, everything of which we ever say it is, are seemingly nothing other than "judgments" in our extended sense — "propositions" that have become in the positing activities, their actual and then habitual correlates. Is that something other than categorial objectuality?

To justify the announced position, we must show that, over against the thematic orientation in which we had moved, a thematic turn is always possible, according to which not the respective object-domain lies in the thematic field, but as something other and well-distinguished, what we call the judgments and their components.

##### b) Phenomenological Clarification of This Reorientation

###### α) The Orientation of the Naively-Straightforwardly Judging Person

In the preceding presentation, we had placed ourselves into the judging persons and asked exclusively what was "there" for them as such: the substrate-objects or domains, the categorial objectualities accruing to them through judging. Judging is always believing something, having something as existing. This does not exclude that in the further course of judging this being-validity may not hold up. The being "modalizes" into the doubtful, questionable, possible, probable, or even the null. The judgment-connection is called consistent so long as such things do not happen.

###### β) In the Critical Orientation of the One Wanting to Cognize, Putative Objectuality and Actual Objectuality Separate

The judger does not merely posit in being-validity but occasionally, already in everyday judging, cognition-interests in the distinguished sense arise: interests in securing "confirmation," needs to convince oneself "at the things themselves," "how they really are." The categorial formations that were previously simply existing objectualities are now to be confirmed, recognized as truly and really existing, through passage to evidence, to "categorial intuition." Thereby, for the judger, the putative objectualities as putative — purely as what was posited in the judging-actions — separate from the corresponding "true" or "actual" objectualities. In the general case of natural judging, the judger leaves the objectuality in validity during the confirmation process, and in the evidently intuited object "itself" says: the object really is, really has such properties. In transition, an identifying coincidence takes place between what was previously already in belief and what is now given in the fulfilling belief. But the intention toward confirmation can also be "disappointed." Then what was previously simply existing becomes "the previously merely putative."

###### γ) The Orientation of the Scientist; the Putative as Such is the Object of His Cognition-Critique

The scientist lives in professional consistency in the "theoretical interest." His professional judging is everywhere dominated by cognition-intentions. To cognize his domain in the strict sense means for him: to accept no other judgments as scientific results than those that have shown their "correctness," their truth, through adequation to the things themselves. Not that the scientist does not judge at all before such self-having of the "things" — but all such judgments are taken as only preliminary, the categorial objectualities in them as only preliminarily valid, as merely putative. Through them the cognition-intention goes to the things themselves, to their self-givenness or evidence.

There is a further difference. The everyday person sees whether it is really so and is satisfied. The scientist knows that evidence has its degrees of clarity and can also be deceptive evidence. So putative and genuine evidence separate. His judgments are to be confirmed through genuine, most complete evidence and only then admitted into the resultant stock of science as theory. This determines a particular judging behavior of the scientist — a judging in zigzag: first steering straightforwardly toward self-givenness, then going back in the manner of critique to the preliminary results, but the critique itself must again be taken into critique. The scientist is guided by the idea of a perfect or progressively perfectible evidence with the correlate of an achievable or approximable true being.

#### §45. The Judgment in the Sense of Apophantic Logic

The necessary passage through the critical orientation that every scientific judgment must undergo makes it so that the scientist constantly has placed over against one another: objectuality simply — as being for him in straightforward judging — and putative objectuality as such, putative consequence, putative determination, etc. With this putative as such, the mere correlate of "meaning" (linguistically also often called meaning, *doxa*), we have grasped what in traditional logic is called judgment (apophansis) and is the theme of apophantic logic. Logic as science-theory always looked to the preceding beginnings or far-developed drafts of the sciences and understood reason and rational achievement by what manifested itself in these drafts. Accordingly, for logic the judgment-sphere purely as such had to set itself off and become a proper thematic field.

Logic constituted itself from the start as a science that wanted to serve that critique which creates genuine science. It thus followed the orientation of the criticizer, who does not straightforwardly judge but judges about judgments. Only mediately was it directed at the being itself, immediately at judgments as meanings of being. As "formal logic," which was traditionally meant and developed entirely as judgment-logic, its theme was those judgment-formations that are essential-lawful conditions of possible adequation to the being itself.

#### §46. Truth and Falsity as Results of Critique. Double Sense of Truth and Evidence

The end-result of critique is — ideally speaking — "truth" or "falsity." This truth means correct, critically confirmed judgment, confirmed through adequation to the corresponding categorial objectualities "themselves." From this adequation and from reflection on the bare meaning and its coincidence with the meaning in fullness, there arises the concept of that correctness which constitutes the one, critical concept of truth — the one according to which the judgment is true or false. Alongside this critical truth-concept of judgment-correctness, we have a second truth-concept: reality — truly or actually existing — as the correlate of self-giving evidence. And the word "evidence" too takes on a double sense: alongside original self-having of true or actual being, also the property of the judgment (as putative categorial objectuality) of being adequate to a corresponding actuality. The correlative double sense of evidence and truth also means a correlative double sense of formal logic itself.

### Chapter 5. Apophantics as Sense-Theory and Truth-Logic

#### §47. From the Orientation of Traditional Logic at the Critical Attitude of Science Follows Its Apophantic Orientation

The results of the preceding considerations about the judging-ways of the sciences and the intentionality ruling in them serve to advance the structural understanding of the logical idea. Logic from its origin wanted to be science-theory. It looked to the drafts of the sciences and understood reason by what these drafts manifested. Accordingly, the judgment-sphere had to set itself off for logic as a proper thematic field. Logic constituted itself as a science serving that critique that creates genuine science. In doing so, it followed the orientation of the criticizer, who judges about judgments. Understandably, formal logic as apophantic was constituted with the predicative judgment as its main thematic concept.

#### §48. Judgments as Mere Meanings Belong to the Region of Senses. Phenomenological Characterization of the Orientation Toward Senses

Judgments are themes — this means, meanings as such are objects of a proper region, forming a self-enclosed object-field. Every judging is objectually directed. But every judging can essentially be transformed into a judging of the second level, in which not what was straightforwardly judged — the objectuality that was, for the judger, existing — is posited, but in a reflection the judged as such. Not only a substrate-object, a property, a state of affairs can be objectual, but also a putative substrate as putative, a putative property as putative, etc. These are objectualities that designate a proper region. The possible passage from judgment to judgment-meaning repeats at arbitrary levels — it is an iterative process. But evidently we here come back to an ultimate distinction: between objectualities that are meanings and those that are not. Meaning and sense are the same region. Instead of meaning we can also say sense, and from statements speak of their significations. The region of senses is thus designated. There are, for predicative judgments, state-of-affairs-senses; for substrate-objects, objectual senses in the narrower sense; and so on.

Sense-explication can be evident, but it need not be, and it can also deceive. The senses are, as objects, just as "transcendent" relative to their acts and subjects as objects that are not senses. A sense that is called "judgment S is p" can never be identified with the straightforwardly judged "S is p" and the state of affairs to be extracted from it by nominalization. In the passage to being-evidence, the actual being of the judgment as a sense does not suffer if S does not exist or S is not p. The judgment is then incorrect, but as a judgment it is something existing in the sense-region.

#### §49. The Double Sense of Judgment (Proposition)

A glance back at the correlation of judging and judgment: the double sense must be expressly highlighted. Judging in the broadest sense is doxic "positing," and what is posited is the "proposition." The predicative judgment posits the predicative proposition. It is what is judged. But is the proposition or judgment what apophantic logic understands by this title? What is judged in a judging is the judged, the judgingly meant categorial objectuality. Only in a judging of the second level does the proposition in the sense of logic — the proposition as sense, the putative categorial objectuality as such — become an object. In every judging its sense "lies," but the sense becomes an object only through the reflective second-level judging. In straightforward judging, the respective meaning is conscious but is merely a "passage" of the cognition-aiming, which terminates in the corresponding categorial objectuality itself and not in the mere judgment.

#### §50. The Extension of the Concept of Sense to the Entire Positional Sphere and the Extension of Formal Logic by a Formal Axiology and Practice

What has been said for judging and judgment-sense holds for the entire positional consciousness-sphere. Every cogito has its cogitatum. The cogito can mean "I perceive," "I remember," "I expect" (which belongs to the doxic sphere though not to predicative determining thinking); but also "I exercise evaluating activities" in pleasure or displeasure, in hoping or fearing, or in willing activities, etc. Each such cogito admits of a reflection under change of orientation onto its cogitatum qua cogitatum, its "intentional objectuality as such." All "acts" in a distinguished sense — all intentional experiences that perform "positings" (positions, theses, stances) — stand under a critique of "reason," and to each kind of such positings belongs a proper evidence, which can essentially be converted into a doxic evidence. All extra-doxic senses can enter into the doxic sphere through a doxic thematization. This opens the possibility of extending the idea of formal logic by a formal axiology and practice — a formal logic of values, of goods.

#### §51. Pure Consequence-Logic as Pure Sense-Theory. The Articulation into Consequence-Logic and Truth-Logic Holds Also for the Manifold-Theory as the Highest Level of Logic

Pure formal analytics, taken as a self-enclosed systematic theory whose thematic sphere is exclusively the senses, results when we combine the clarification of the judgment-concept as predicative objectual sense with the earlier findings. A pure systematic theory of the region of senses in the strict sense is the analytics only for the two sub-layers: the pure morphology of senses (or meanings) and the pure analytics of consistency. The latter, founded in the former, forms a self-enclosed logical discipline in unity with the former, and thus understood is the universal and pure science of apophantic senses. Excluded from its theme are all truth-questions.

This layering must hold also for the theory of manifolds. The *mathesis universalis* in its entirety is analytics of possible categorialies. The manifold-theory has no compelling reason to include in its theme questions of possible truth for its theory-forms or possible reality of manifolds under its formal ideas. The mathematician as such need not care whether there really are manifolds in concrete "actuality." He can — as "pure" mathematician — conceive his concepts so that their extension does not co-intend such possibilities at all.

#### §52. Properly Logical and Extra-Logical *Mathesis Pura*. The "Mathematics of the Mathematicians"

With this, the essential piece of the properly logical, science-theoretical sense is lost for the mathematics that has been reduced to pure consistency-analytics. A whole science can be established that investigates and wants to investigate nothing further than the universal realm of pure apophantic senses. With the consistent exclusion of truth-questions, it turns out that one has not really lost anything of this entire logical *mathesis* but still has it entirely as "purely" formal mathematics. This purity in the thematic restriction to objectual senses in their own essentiality can also be exercised in a certain sense unconsciously — namely, when the mathematician omits ever posing questions of possible reality of manifolds. So it is understandable that for a pure formal mathematics there can be no other cognition-worries than those of "consistency," of immediate or mediate analytic consequence and inconsistency — to which obviously all questions of mathematical "existence" belong.

It is different for the logician, who, scientifically-theoretically oriented, will not easily think of performing this reduction to an analytics of pure senses and will thus gain mathematics only as an expanded logic, essentially related to possible object-domains and theories. Philosophically, the fullest consciousness is necessary here. It must be seen that formal mathematics in the sense of that reduced purity has its own right, and at the same time it is a great advance of philosophical insight that this restricting reduction has its essential sense as a science dealing with nothing other than apophantic senses according to their own-essential a priori, and that thereby finally the proper sense of "formal mathematics" of the mathematicians — where all properly logical intention remains absent — is principially clarified. Here lies the only legitimate distinction between formal logic and mere formal mathematics.

#### §53. Illustrations from the Example of Euclidean Manifold

Husserl illustrates. The manifold-theory presents itself to the logician as a science of the a priori constructible form-types of possible manifolds (or deductive sciences) that have possible truth. The reduction to purity yields the form (still that of "Euclidean manifold") as that of a system of possible propositions (judgments) purely as senses — such that they form a self-enclosed system of pure consequence ("consistency"): the Euclidean form in its extension no longer has deductive sciences related to possibly existing domains, but consistent judgment-systems. As the correlate of a possible systematic theory, we have a possible manifold — not of objects simply but of putative objects as such, objectual senses, substrate-senses suited to function consistently as substrates of predications in a judgment-system.

#### §54. Concluding Determination of the Relation Between Formal Logic and Formal Ontology

##### a) The Problem

The idea of the *mathesis universalis* as a science of apophantic senses of all categorial levels had been, for complete purity, separated from all logical interests. These are now put back into play. Mathematics stands again within the science-theory, exercising functions of critique. The connection-laws between consistency and truth are stated, and the introduction of truth-concepts is accordingly permitted. The laws of consequence and consistency become laws of possible material truth.

The question is then posed: is formal logic accordingly to be regarded as formal ontology? And why does this not hold for the analytics of mere consistency, even though it too is related to judgments in general and thereby to the something-in-general?

##### b) The Correlative Double Sense of Formal Logic

To speak straightforwardly of objects in general means normally to speak of them as actualities or possibilities. If we call formal ontology an a priori science of objects in general, this means: of possible objects purely as such. The pure mathematics of consistency does not deserve the name of formal ontology. It is an ontology of pure judgments as senses — an ontology of the forms of consistent, and in that sense possible, senses: possible in the evidence of distinctness. Every possible object has its corresponding object-sense. But the possibility of a sense-form contains in and of itself nothing about the possibility of objects of a corresponding sense.

As soon as we draw this in and set the two possibilities in relation — asking about possible correctness — we stand in logic proper, and the whole mathematics of judgments as senses takes on a formal-ontological significance. And yet we may not without further ado address it as formal ontology. Formal analytics as formal logic of possible truth is critically oriented. Its theme is the totality of judgments under the viewpoint of possible adequation. So long as judgments are the exclusive themes, we are not yet properly formal-ontologically oriented. But just as in the sciences the critical orientation toward judgments is only a means to serve the primary interest in the things themselves and how they truly are, so too for logic that does not lose sight of its science-theoretical vocation. In its ultimate sense, it is not pure formal apophantic logic but formal-ontological. The deep and task-appropriate sense of formal analytics is to be the science of the possible categorial forms in which substrate-objectualities can truly be.

Categorially formed objectuality is not an apophantic concept but an ontological one. Yet it is the essence of such objectuality to be nothing other than fulfilled judgment of corresponding sense-form. The evident coincidence of the judgment-meaning with the actuality "itself" shows that in the truly existing objectuality the judgment-meaning lies, only saturated with cognition-fullness. Hence the double sense of evidence, paralleling the double sense of judgment. In one sense, evidence is truly existing state of affairs in the mode of self-givenness. In the second, correlative sense, evidence is self-givenness of the correctness of the judgment-meaning. As cognizing-judging beings, we have no other objectualities than categorially formed ones. Truly existing nature, truly existing sociality or culture, etc. — this has no other sense than that of a certain categorial objectuality.

The correlative double sense of evidence and truth means a correlative double sense of formal logic. From the traditional orientation toward judgments as apophantic meanings, we gain an apophantic logic, which fully expanded reaches the *mathesis universalis*. If we prefer the orientation toward possible categorial objectualities themselves and their forms, we from the start and consistently pursue a formal-ontological logic, which will nonetheless for methodological reasons need to make judgment-senses into objects, albeit only as means, while the ultimate aim is the objects.

##### c) The Idea of Formal Ontology Separable from the Idea of Science-Theory

After this investigation, the double sense of logic and the two orientations may be considered fully clarified. Such clarifications are fairly indifferent for the mathematician. But the logician who does not miss them or declares them indifferent is in any case no philosopher, since they concern principled structural insights into formal logic.

Finally, formal ontology can also be directly posed as a task from the start, without starting from the idea of a science-theory. Its question is then: what can be said within the empty-region object-in-general? The syntactic formations are available for producing from given objects always new categorialies. One will also come upon the difference between possible formations that yield merely distinct meanings but, as contradictory, cannot lead to possible objects themselves, etc. Obviously the whole formal *mathesis* then arises. Afterward, we can always make clear to ourselves the science-theoretical significance of this ontology, since every science aims for its domain at "true being," at categorialies whose forms, if it is genuine science, must stand among the formal-ontologically possible ones.

## Part II. From Formal to Transcendental Logic

### Chapter 1. Psychologism and the Transcendental Grounding of Logic

#### §55. Whether with the Development of Logic as Objective-Formal the Idea of Even a Merely Formal Science-Theory Has Already Been Satisfied

Husserl opens Part II by taking stock. In Part I, we unfolded the sense prefigured for the traditional formal logic by the Aristotelian Analytics. It presented itself as a completely self-enclosed science. We clarified the sharp essential delimitation of its domain and likewise the layer-disciplines bound together a priori within it through founding-relations. We also learned to understand its correlative and thereby equivalent thematic as formal apophantics and formal ontology, which permits speaking of one logic, only to be treated in two orientations.

It might now seem that we are finished with this logic as philosophers and can leave its theoretical elaboration to mathematicians. It might seem that any remaining tasks for the logician could only involve an extension of the logical idea. The guiding idea from the start was that of a universal a priori science-theory, treating the formal-a priori of all sciences as such. At minimum, form in the sense of analytic or "formal" logic is of this kind: every science produces categorial formations and stands under the essential laws of their form.

The question now arises whether this analytic-formal science-theory fills out the idea of a science-theory at all, or whether it must be supplemented by a material science-theory. The peculiarity of analytics is that it makes the "cores" (the "cognition-matters") binding judgments and cognitions to particular object-spheres into arbitrary ones, mere modes of the something-in-general. If we let content flow back into these emptied cores, perhaps a material a priori of universal science-theoretical significance might be gained.

If we define the concept of the analytic a priori through the fully developed formal analytics, what is now in question is a new "synthetic" a priori — a material, universal a priori that binds all material-a priori special domains into a totality. In other words: is not everything that exists, thought as concretely determined, essentially something within a universe of being, a "world"? Does not every material a priori belong to a universal a priori that pre-delineates the a priori material form for a possible universe of beings? It seems we should now steer toward a material, genuine ontology to supplement the merely analytic-formal one.

However natural this thought-train is, Husserl insists we may not yet follow this new guiding idea. For the situation is not that we are already finished with formal analytics — we as philosophical logicians, not mathematical technicians, seriously minded to satisfy the purposive idea of an analytic-formal science-theory. Has this been perfectly accomplished by our previous investigations?

#### §56. The Reproach of Psychologism Against Every Subjectively Directed Consideration of the Logical Formations

Husserl ties back to the demand for two-sidedness of logical research set up in the Preparatory Considerations, a demand not yet adequately clarified regarding the sense of the subjectively directed thematic. The sense and right of this subjectively directed logical thematic — which, to say it right away, will not claim the significance of a separate logical discipline to be set off from the objectively-ideally directed analytics — are now in question.

At the entrance stands the specter of psychologism. Against the demand for subjectively directed logical research, one raises the objection — citing the first volume of the *Logical Investigations* — that its achievement was supposed to be the radical extirpation of everything psychological from the theme of logic. The dominant empiricism (historically an anti-Platonism) was blind to the peculiar objectivity of all ideal formations; everywhere it psychologistically reinterprets them as the respective psychic actualities and habitualities. So too those objectualities — propositions, judgments, truths, inferences, proofs, theories, and the categorial objectualities formed within them — that make up the thematic domain of logic. The judgments of which logic speaks in its laws are not judgment-experiences (the judging); the truths are not evidence-experiences; the proofs not the subjective-psychic proving.

Just as number-theory does not have the experiences of collecting and counting as its domain but the numbers, so syllogistics does not have the psychic experiences of judging and inferring to do with. Thus it seems that for logic, as for every other objective science, every subjectively directed thematic is excluded — it belongs to psychology, not to logic.

Husserl notes that shortly after the *Logical Investigations* appeared, the objection was raised that the phenomenological investigations of Volume II constituted a relapse into psychologism. Remarkably, the *Prolegomena* were regarded as an outright overcoming of psychologism, without noticing that there was never talk of psychologism as such (a universal epistemological error) but of a psychologism of a quite particular sense: the psychologization of the irreal meaning-formations that are the theme of logic. Husserl confesses he himself had not entirely overcome the unclarity about the problem of epistemological psychologism at that time, though the phenomenological investigations of Volume II opened the necessary approaches to posing and radically overcoming the problem of transcendental psychologism.

It is therefore very necessary to go more closely into the particular problem of psychologism that the *Prolegomena* had treated, giving it a purer form and placing it in more general contexts that lead us to clarify the necessary sense of a "two-sidedly" researching, genuinely philosophical logic. For our main aim is to show that a logic straightforwardly directed at its proper thematic sphere and exclusively busy with its cognition remains stuck in a naivety that bars it from the philosophical advantage of radical self-understanding and principled self-justification — the advantage of the most perfect scientificity, which philosophy exists to fulfill.

#### §57. Logical Psychologism and Logical Idealism

##### a) The Motives for This Psychologism

We spoke earlier of the difficulty of separating the psychic formations making up the thematic domain of logic from psychological subjectivity: regarding judgments, sets, numbers, etc. as something other than psychic occurrences in the judging human being. What originally grows in judging activity appears member by member in the consciousness-field of the judger. It is nothing foreign to the psychic, nothing like a physical process. Rather, in the psychic activity running as a consciousness-experience itself, unseparated from it and not outside but inside, the judgment-members and whole judgment-formations appear. The English empiricists, misled, do not even make a distinction between the judging experience and the formation shaping itself memberwise "within" it. So it seems that all logical data are real occurrences of the psychological sphere.

The main thing is the equating of judgment-formations (and all similar formations of reason-acts) with phenomena of inner experience. This equation is grounded by their "inner" appearing in the act-consciousness itself. So concepts, judgments, inferences, proofs, theories would be psychic occurrences and logic, as J. St. Mill said, "a part or branch of psychology." In this seemingly so compelling view lies logical psychologism.

##### b) The Ideality of the Logical Formations as Irreal Occurrence in the Logical-Psychic Sphere

Against this we say: it is an original evidence that judgments, inferences, etc. formed in repeated acts, identical or similar, are not merely alike but numerically identical the same. Their "occurrence" in the consciousness-domain is manifold. The respective forming thinking-processes are temporally separate, individually different. Not so the thoughts thought in thinking. They are not real, not spatial objects, but irreal spiritual formations whose peculiar essence excludes spatial extension, original locality, and mobility. Like other spiritual formations, they allow physical embodiment — here through sensory word-signs — and so gain a secondary spatial existence. Every kind of irreality has modes of possible participation in reality. But this changes nothing about the principled separation between the real and the irreal. Deeper clarifications are indispensable here. Through study and parallelization of the evidences of the real and the irreal, the general likeness of objectualities — as objectualities — will become understandable.

#### §58. The Evidence of Ideal Objects Analogous to That of Individual Objects

The evidence of irreal, ideal objects is in its accomplishment entirely analogous to that of ordinary experience, to which alone — without any ground other than a prejudice — the accomplishment of original objectification is attributed. The identity of an ideal and thereby its objectuality is to be "seen" in the same originality as the identity of an ordinary experience-object. In repeated experiences, the consciousness of the same comes about in their synthesis. This original identifiability belongs as an essential correlate to the sense of every object of experience in the emphatic sense. Likewise, an irreal object has its identifiability on the basis of its proper modes of self-grasping and self-having. In its accomplishment this is really something like "experience," only that such an object is not individuated from a temporality originally belonging to it.

The possibility of deception belongs to the evidence of experience without canceling its fundamental character and achievement. The consciousness-dissolution of a deception is itself a kind of evidence. This holds for every evidence or "experience" in the extended sense. Even an evidence claiming apodicticity can reveal itself as deception and yet presupposes a similar evidence at which it "shatters."

#### §59. Generalities on Evidence as Self-Giving

The constant offense that may be felt at this presentation lies only in the usual fundamentally wrong interpretation of evidence, owing to the complete lack of a serious phenomenological analysis of the accomplishment running commonly through all its forms. The concept of evidence is understood in the sense of an absolute apodicticity, attributed — quite incomprehensibly — to a single experience torn from the concrete essential-unitary context of subjective experiencing. Evidence is seen as an absolute criterion of truth, which would actually eliminate not only all outer but also inner evidence. If one then resorts to so-called evidence-feelings — unable to interpret evidence as a functioning intentionality — the achievement of truth attributed to evidence itself becomes a miracle, indeed a countersense.

Evidence designates the intentional accomplishment of self-giving. More precisely, it is the general distinguished form of "intentionality," of "consciousness of something," in which the objectual conscious in it is conscious in the mode of self-grasping, self-seeing, of consciousness-wise being-with-it-itself. The primordial mode of self-giving is perception. An intentionally modified and more complexly built mode is clear recollection. Evidence has various originality-modes.

Our general characterization of evidence is not set up as a new "theory" or appealing "interpretation" to be tested. Rather, it is an evidence of higher level to be gained through phenomenological unfolding of every experience and every genuinely exercised "insight." This in turn can only be interpreted and understood through a third-level evidence, and so in infinitum. Only in seeing can I bring out what properly lies in a seeing; I must carry out a seeing explication of the proper essence of such seeing.

Every self-giving consciousness can ground right, correctness for another consciousness, for a merely unclear or confused meaning — in the form of synthetic adequation to the "things themselves." The self-givings, the acts creating evident right, are creative original-institutions of right, of truth as correctness — because they are originally constituting, originally instituting sense and being for the respective objectualities.

#### §60. The Fundamental Lawfulness of Intentionality and the Universal Function of Evidence

Every consciousness of something belongs a priori in an openly endless manifold of possible consciousness-modes synthetically joinable to one consciousness as consciousness of "the same." To this manifold belong essentially also the modes of a manifold evidence-consciousness. Evidence is thus a universal mode of intentionality related to the entire consciousness-life; through it, consciousness has a universal teleological structure, an orientation toward "reason" and even a pervasive tendency thereto — toward the demonstrating of correctness and the cancellation of incorrectness.

Not only in regard to this universal teleological function is evidence a theme for far-reaching investigations. They concern already the general evidence as a single experience, the originality-modes of evidence and their functions, and the various regions and categories of objectualities themselves. Category of objectuality and category of evidence are correlates. To every fundamental kind of objectualities belongs a fundamental kind of "experience," of evidence, and a corresponding evidence-style in the possible heightening of the perfection of self-having.

There arises the great task of investigating all these modes of evidence, of making understandable the highly complicated accomplishments joining together in synthetic consistency, in which the respective objectuality shows itself now less, now more perfectly. To discourse about evidence "from above" yields nothing here. And to cling to the tradition that reduces evidence to apodictic, absolutely doubtless, and self-enclosed insight — that means to block understanding of all scientific achievement.

#### §61. Evidence in General in the Function of All Objects, Real or Irreal, as Synthetic Unities

Returning to the irreal objectualities, particularly those of the analytic-logical sphere: for them the self-giving or right-giving evidences are the corresponding "experiences," and they have the essential property of all experiences or evidences: in repetition, in the synthesis of different experiences of the same, they make visible a numerically identical and not merely alike object. If one substitutes for the ideal objectualities the temporal occurrences of consciousness-life in which they "appear," one would consistently have to do the same with the data of experience. The physical objects too appear "in the consciousness-field," not otherwise than ideal objects — as intentional unities in the flow of manifold appearance-modes. One must bring the consciousness-manifolds to view in phenomenological reflection, structurally dissect them, follow them in synthetic transitions, ask about the intentional role or function, and make understandable how the transcendent object consists within the sight-sphere of synthetic experience itself — as the identity-pole immanent to the individual experiences and yet transcendent in the identity surpassing them.

One must place at the center of all principled reflections the great and much-neglected self-evidence that something like an object originally draws its peculiar ontic sense only from the experience-processes in which it is characterized as consciousness-having in the mode "it itself" — as self-appearing. Experience is the original institution of the for-us-being of objects and of their objectual sense. This holds equally for irreal objects. Everywhere, also for outer experience: evident self-giving is to be characterized as a process of constitution, a self-forming of the experience-object.

#### §62. The Ideality of All Kinds of Objectualities vis-à-vis Constituting Consciousness. The Positivistic Misinterpretation of Nature as a Kind of Psychologism

In the sense of every experienceable object, even a physical one, lies a certain ideality — over against the manifold psychic processes. This is the general ideality of all intentional unities over against the manifolds constituting them. Therein consists the "transcendence" of all kinds of objectualities over against the consciousness of them. We thus find an exact analogue of the psychologistic interpretation of the logical and all other irrealities in positivism (the Machian philosophy, the philosophy of "as if"), which reduces things to empirically regulated complexes of psychic data ("sensations"). It is not only a false doctrine, blind to phenomenological essential states of affairs, but also countersensical in that it fails to see how fictions themselves have their mode of being, their mode of evidence, their way of being unities of manifolds.

#### §63. Originally Producing Activity as the Self-Giving of the Logical Formations and the Sense of Talk of Their Production

We have often spoken of a producing of logical formations in consciousness. This talk requires a warning against misunderstanding. Where we otherwise speak of producing, we refer to a real sphere — a bringing-forth of real things or processes. In our case, we have irreal objects before us, given in real psychic processes. Judging is also acting, only not principally a treating of something real. But this acting has from the beginning and in all its level-formations exclusively the irreal in its thematic sphere: in judging, something irreal is intentionally constituted. In the active formation of new judgments from already given ones, we are seriously productively active. What is "treated" here are not realities. Yet they are producible goals, ends and means, and they are what they are only "from" original production. But this does not mean they are what they are only in and during the original production. Their mode of givenness from such original activity is nothing other than their proper kind of "perception." Or what is the same: this originally acquiring activity is the "evidence" for these idealities. This evidencing consciousness-activity — here a spontaneous activity difficult to research — is the "original constitution," more precisely the original institution of the ideal objectualities of the logical kind.

#### §64. The Being-Priority of the Real over the Irreal Objects

Husserl closes this investigation by noting that some fierce opposition arises from a misunderstanding. For us it was only a matter of the right of the broadest sense "object-in-general" and correlatively of the most general sense of evidence as self-giving. In another respect than that of the legitimate subsumption of ideas under the concept of object and thereby of substrate of possible predications, there is by no means an equal ordering between real and ideal objectualities. Reality has a being-priority over every irreality, since all irrealities are essentially referred back to actual or possible reality. To survey these relations on all sides and bring the total connection of everything actually and possibly existent to systematic cognition leads to the highest philosophical problems — those of a universal ontology.

#### §65. A More General Concept of Psychologism

The extraordinary broadening and radicalization of the refutation of logical psychologism has also brought us an extreme generalization of the idea of psychologism — of a quite particular, though not the only, sense. It is to be characterized by the fact that some kind of objectualities to be made evident — or even all kinds, as in Hume's philosophy — are psychologized because they constitute themselves in consciousness, building up their being-sense in and for subjectivity. They are "psychologized" means: their objectual sense, their sense as a kind of objects of peculiar essence, is negated in favor of the subjective experiences, the data in immanent or psychological temporality.

#### §66. Psychologistic and Phenomenological Idealism. Analytic and Transcendental Critique of Cognition

This psychologism, so generally and even intentionally ambiguously grasped, is the fundamental character of every bad "idealism" (like Berkeley's or Hume's). One must not confuse it with phenomenological idealism, which receives its fundamentally different new sense precisely through the radical critique of that psychologism on the basis of a phenomenological clarification of evidence.

Every "seeing," every identification in "evidence," has its own right; likewise every self-enclosed realm of possible "experience" as a domain of a science. To each belongs a secondary thematic sphere, the sphere of its critique: a critique of cognition in a first sense, related to the ideal cognition-results and, in the subjective direction, to the corresponding ideal acts. Through this critique, which we can call analytic critique of cognition, every science gains its relation to analytics.

But every science has a third thematic sphere, also one of critique, but of a differently turned kind. This concerns the constituting subjectivity belonging to every domain. Over against the critique of the obvious pregivennesses, actions, and results, we here have to do with a quite differently constituted critique of cognition — that of the constitutive origin-sources of their positional sense and right: the critique of the (whether psychologically or transcendentally grasped) "reason," or as we can say in contrast to the analytic critique of cognition, the transcendental critique of cognition.

#### §67. The Objection of Psychologism as Misunderstanding of the Necessary Logical Function of the Transcendental Critique of Cognition

The objection of psychologism was directed against the *Logical Investigations*: that they combated psychologism in the *Prolegomena* and yet in Volume II passed over into investigations of phenomenological subjectivity. Such "descriptive psychological" investigations of cognitive psychology were said to be psychologistic transgressions of a pure logic.

In the sense of such critique lies the separation of science and reason-critique, granting science its own existence in its own right and conceiving reason-critique as a novel higher-dignity science that does not disturb the rightful self-being of the sciences. Pure (analytic) logic is taken as an absolute norm, and reason-critical questions must not enter into the concretion of logical consciousness-life.

Against this, Husserl insists that the battle against logical psychologism had no other purpose than the highly important one of making visible the peculiar domain of analytic logic in its purity and ideal distinctness. Its domain — its thematic field in the first and main sense — does not exclude that secondarily, in service of the cognition of the domain, what does not belong to the domain but stands in essential connection with it also becomes thematic.

And should not something similar hold for the entire field of intentional acts, of appearance-modes, of whatever consciousness-modes in which the scientific domain is pregiven for the judger? Should there not here be a field of a critique necessary for all sciences — a transcendental critique — necessary if they are to be genuine sciences at all? General science-theory is eo ipso general theory of the critique proper essentially to genuine science as such.

#### §68. Preview of the Further Tasks

The matter to be clarified is complicated because logic itself is a science and would itself need such critique, and because it is the science that is supposed to treat, in a universality concerning all sciences at once, of those subjective critical investigations necessary for all sciences. Both do not without further ado coincide. Our next task is to direct the further investigations at the clarification of the subjective investigations demanded for analytics and their necessity. The investigations required in further consequence for shaping the idea of a formal into that of a real and finally an absolute ontology will lead us to the still outstanding clarification of the genuine sense of a transcendental psychologism.

### Chapter 2. Initial Questions of the Transcendental-Logical Problematic: The Problems of Basic Concepts

#### §69. The Logical Formations Given in Straightforward Evidence. The Task of the Reflective Thematization of This Evidence

Despite the distortions of the analytic sphere, analytic logic has long been available, even in highly developed form regarding the formal-mathematical disciplines. There cannot have been a lack of evidence in the formation of the logical categories. Yet this evidence is nothing less than exemplary. It — evidence in general — is to be reflectively considered, analyzed, reshaped, purified, and improved.

In a straightforward evidence, the logical formations and their general forms are initially given — that is the necessary first. But now demanded is the thematic reflection on this evidence, on the previously unthematically and naively straightforwardly performed formative activity. We interrogate the thereby awakened evidence as to what it aimed at and what it acquired, and in higher-level evidence we identify and fix the possible variations of otherwise imperceptible thematic fluctuations and distinguish the associated aimings and realizations — the shifting logical concept-formations.

#### §70. The Sense of the Demanded Clarifications as Constitutive Origin-Research

##### a) Shifting of Intentional Aimings and Equivocation

What is at stake is not coined equivocations to be resolved by attending to words and word-meanings, but inner shiftings of intentionality and its accomplishment, connected and demanded by essential connections. As their consequence, the linguistic equivocation arises, which can only be resolved through that reflective interrogation of the intentional aimings and the original constitution of the formations accomplished in their execution.

The entire investigations we carried out to clarify the logical domain illustrate this. Their indispensability for a seriously scientific logic is beyond doubt. They were thoroughly subjective-phenomenologically directed: they concerned the contrasting of three attitudes in judging, the demonstration of three evidences, three ways of empty pre-intention and fulfillment, and accordingly originally separating concepts of judgment. The concept-shifting and equivocation here occurred in the thinking of logicians not from accidental but from essential reasons and had to remain hidden because it itself belonged to the thematic unity of their "straightforward" thinking.

The shifting is at the same time overlapping and coincidence: the judgment-unity of the lower level simultaneously enters the higher level in identification, while the new element of the higher level (distinctness, or the fullness of evidence) must be grasped as its own predicate.

##### b) Clarification of the Basic Concepts to be Distinguished as Uncovering of the Hidden Subjective Formation-Method and as Critique of the Same

All these investigations have the character of fundamental investigations of the uncovering and critique of the original logical method. The basic concepts are always already familiar and freely available, produced and reproduced in renewed evidence. But this naively exercised "method" is not yet a genuine method. The constitutive research is only initially such reflection and ongoing uncovering of the factually and "unconsciously" exercised method. In its progress, it is "critique" — active fulfillment on the basis of the systematic separation of the intentional directions interwoven in the unity of synthesis. Such critique is creative constitution of the relevant objectualities in the unity of consistent self-givenness and creation of their essences and essential concepts. Every constitutive analysis is creative in this respect; the creatively acquired constitutive unities are norms, and their creative acquisition is itself thematically become method and as such norm for future habitual methodical practice.

#### §71. Foundation-Problems of the Sciences and Constitutive Origin-Research. Logic Called to Leadership

This already casts light on the much-discussed foundation-problems not only of mathematics but of all objective sciences. Sciences that have paradoxes, that operate with basic concepts not created from the work of origin-clarification and critique, are not sciences at all but merely theoretical techniques despite all ingenious achievement.

The basic-concept creation is thus a literally fundamental achievement for all sciences. Above all for logic, which is called to be the principled method for them all and to encompass all their special methods in the a priori of method in general. Only in a scientific life that bows to the radicalism of this research is genuine science possible.

#### §72. The Subjective Structures as an A Priori Correlative to the Objective. Transition to a New Level of Critique

The subjective structures in question are not contingently psychologically related to the corresponding concepts of objective logical theory. They designate an a priori correlative to the objective. To every operative law of the morphology there corresponds a priori a subjective lawfulness regarding the constituting subjectivity. The basic concepts are the supreme concepts of the logical domain itself and its layering. With their shaping, a first critique of the necessarily first logic-straightforwardly is accomplished. But is this first critique already a full and complete critique?

### Chapter 3. The Idealizing Presuppositions of Logic and Their Constitutive Critique

#### §73. Idealizing Presuppositions of Mathematical Analytics as Themes of Constitutive Critique. The Ideal Identity of Judgment-Formations as a Constitutive Problem

Pure mathematical analytics presupposes the ideal identity of judgments. Logic refers not to givennesses in merely actual evidence but to the enduring formations, to the ever-re-activatable and identifiable, as objectualities henceforth at hand. Logic with its formal generalities and laws presupposes judgments whose being-in-itself in identity is established. It presupposes what is self-evident to every thinker: that the identity of my judgment-meanings can always be made certain across all pauses of my thinking-actuality.

The traditional logic and naively straightforwardly theorizing mathematics do not worry about this. They presuppose that identity properly runs through the thinking. But so long as the naively exercised method remains anonymous and its intentional achievement is not thematically clarified, we have no right to let that pretended right stand. If it is an ideal that the norm-giving logic presupposes, we stand before the dilemma: either logic operates with a universal fiction and is not norm-giving, or the ideal is a genuine basic norm inseparably belonging to the possibility of genuine science.

#### §74. The Idealities of the And-So-Forth, of Constructive Infinities, and Their Subjective Correlate

Not even all idealizations playing a universal role in pure analytics have been considered. Husserl calls attention to the basic form of the "and so forth," of iterative "infinity," which has its subjective correlate in the "one can always again." It is an obvious idealization, since de facto nobody can always again. But it plays its sense-determining role everywhere in logic. Mathematics is the realm of infinite constructions, a realm of ideal existences, not only "finite" senses but also constructive infinities. The problem of subjective constitutive origins repeats itself here.

#### §75. The Analytic Law of Contradiction and Its Subjective Turn

In pure consequence-logic we have to do with laws of consequence and contradiction. Objectively: every contradicting judgment is "excluded" by the judgment it contradicts. Every judgment that is an analytic consequence is therein "included." Subjectively: whoever has a judgment-meaning and, explicating it, sees an analytic consequence, judges the consequence not only factually but "cannot do otherwise." The general evidence of analytic consequence together with the attempt to negate it shows objectively the general impossibility of this unity and, subjectively, the impossibility of believing-in-judging for any judger at all in the evidence of distinctness.

These subjective turns merely indicate the real lawfulness lying behind them, which emerges in the actual uncovering of the correlative subjective structures. All these evidences with their belonging essential structures must be unfolded as co-functioning in the subjective and hidden "methodology" of the intentional constitution of the various ideal unities and connections binding the morphology with the consequence-theory into the unity of mathematical analytics.

#### §76. Transition to the Subjective Problematic of the Truth-Logic

We have so far spoken of analytics in the narrower sense of the "pure" *mathesis universalis*. It gains no new disciplines but only the specifically logical function when it afterward includes truth-concepts in its theme. The pure mathematical analytics then passes over into an analytic science-theory proper, or equivalently into a "formal ontology."

More carefully considered, such a science-theory is very little suited to accomplish what it is destined for — namely, to offer norms for the essential possibility of genuine sciences. The question arises: how does it bring this "true" into its formal generalities at all? At deeper penetration, we encounter not just similar but new and much further-reaching presuppositions and difficulties than those we have had occasion to learn of so far. Our investigation has a given starting point in the concept of truth and in the "logical principles" axiomatically explicating it.

#### §77. The Idealizing Presuppositions Contained in the Principle of Contradiction and the Principle of Excluded Middle

The principle of contradiction expresses the general impossibility that contradictory judgments are together true (or false). The principle of excluded middle contains the presupposition that every judgment can be brought to adequation. Every judgment is to be brought "to its things themselves" and directed according to them, either in positive or negative adequation. This is an idealization for which no evidence has ever been sought. We know that very few judgments anyone can de facto intuitively demonstrate; yet it is supposed to be a priori evident that there can be no non-evident judgments that could not "in themselves" be made evident.

The double-principle says simply: every judgment is one of the two, true or false. It contains no subjective word like "evidence," though truth and falsity originally draw sense and right from evidence. A judgment is not sometimes true and sometimes false but true or false once and for all. The principle of identity (A is A) can be given this sense: if A is true, it is true once and for all. Furthermore, the "everyone" must be introduced: the same judgment is the same not just for me but for everyone. All logical principles claim to hold once and for all and for everyone.

#### §78. The Conversion of the Laws of *Modus Ponens* and *Tollens* into Subjective Evidence-Laws

For the pure consequence-principle we gain as a law: the possibility of the distinctness-evidence of the analytic ground-judgment necessarily entails the possibility of the same evidence for the consequent-judgment. The new element in the conversion of the corresponding law of truth-logic is that, in carrying out the syntactic (categorial) actions of the ground-judging at the originality of the "things themselves" (on the basis of "experience"), the same possibility of material evidence must also hold for the judgment-actions of the consequence.

#### §79. The Presuppositions of Truth and Falsity In Themselves and of the Decidability of All Judgments

Truth and falsity are predicates of judgments, but not proprietary, not "constitutive marks" of them. One cannot simply "see" them on judgments. Having judgments self-given is not having one or the other of these predicates self-given. It cannot even be said that a claim to truth is essential to judgments. In their own essence, judgments have nothing of a claim to truth and falsity, but each can take into itself the practical intention toward confirmation. According to the origin-sense of apophantic logic, every judgment is thought from the start as a claim to be confirmed, and accordingly every truth as a decision to be gained. For the logician in the stance of positivity, a basic conviction is always already in advance: that of truth in itself and falsity in itself. Every judgment is in itself decided; its predicate of truth or falsity "belongs" to its essence — though, as shown above, it is not a constitutive mark of any judgment as a judgment. This is very remarkable.

#### §80. The Evidence of the Truth-Presupposition and the Task of Its Critique

We have de facto cognition, evidence, and truth achieved in it. We have de facto had undecided judgments, questioned them, and presupposed that they were decidable positively or negatively. When we as logicians established the principle of contradiction and excluded middle — did this not happen on the basis of an essential generalization from such exemplary individual cases, an apodictically evident generalization? Against this there is nothing to object. The self-evident certainties of life include that truths in themselves exist, that one can seek and find them. We do not want to give up these self-evidences; they have the rank of evidences. But this must not prevent us from subjecting them to a critique, questioning them according to their peculiar sense and "scope." Judgment-evidences can have presuppositions — not hypotheses but presuppositions included in the evidence of the material underpinnings and co-founding the truths and falsities.

The necessity and significance of this deepest critique of achievement will become much more visible in what follows. And understandable will also become why the question "what is truth?" is not a mere play-question of a dialectic between skeptical negativism and logical absolutism but a tremendous work-problem resting on its own ground of objectivity and pointing to highly comprehensive investigations.

#### §81. Formulation of Further Problems

The problematic sense of the "true for everyone" connected with the "true once and for all" has been touched upon but by no means fully uncovered. A further problematic arises through the relation of predicative truth to objects-about and finally to "ultimate substrates," to objects of possible "experience." These objects are "objective" in the sense of traditional logic; experience is eo ipso objective experience, truth eo ipso objective truth. The sense of this being of objects is usually thought in the sense of an absolute being, to which the relation to the cognizing subjectivity is inessential. Absolute being has its correlate in an absolute truth. To all this we have the corresponding evidence-problems.

### Chapter 4. Reduction of the Evidence-Critique of the Logical Principles to the Evidence-Critique of Experience

#### §82. The Reduction of Judgments to Ultimate Judgments. The Categorial Primal Modifications of the Something and the Ur-Substrate Individual

Every actual and possible judgment, if we follow its syntaxes, leads back to ultimate cores that contain no syntaxes. Every judgment reduces to ultimate substrates, to absolute subjects (no longer nominalized predicates), to ultimate predicates, ultimate generalities, ultimate relations. For the *mathesis universalis* as formal mathematics these ultimates hold no special interest. Quite otherwise for truth-logic; for ultimate substrate-objects are individuals, of which much is to be said in formal truth and to which ultimately all truth is referred back.

#### §83. Parallel Reduction of Truths. Reference-Back of All Truths to a World of Individuals

To the reduction of judgments to ultimate judgments with ultimate sense corresponds a reduction of truths from higher-level truths to those of the lowest level — truths directly related to their things and thing-spheres, related to individual objects. It is an a priori that every conceivable judgment ultimately has individual object-reference and thereby has reference to a real universe, to a "world." All generality ultimately refers back to individuals.

#### §84. Staircase of Evidences; the First-in-Itself Is That of Experience. The Emphatic Concept of Experience

The staircase of judgments and their judgment-senses is followed by that of evidences, and the first-in-themselves truths and evidences must be the individual ones. Individuals are given through experience, experience in the first and most emphatic sense — defined as direct relation to the individual.

#### §85. The Genuine Tasks of the So-Called Judgment-Theory. The Sense-Genesis of Judgments as Guiding Thread to the Seeking of the Level-Order of Evidences

The judgments as senses have a sense-genesis. Every sense-formation can be questioned according to its essentially belonging sense-history. This fundamental fact, in its universality encompassing the entire intentional life, determines the proper sense of intentional analysis as uncovering of intentional implications. Not only the open or finished sense but the implicated sense constantly has something to say, and it especially co-functions in the making-evident of the logical principles. Without clarity about all this, we cannot really have the logical principles at our disposal.

#### §86. The Evidence of Pre-Predicative Experience as the First-in-Itself Theme of the Transcendental Judgment-Theory. The Experience-Judgment as the Judgment of Origin

The lowest level to which we come, led by sense-genesis, leads to individual judgments and thus to individual evidences of the simplest form: the simple experience-judgments. A proposition from the general theory of consciousness says that consciousness of the mode of self-giving for every kind of objectualities precedes genetically all other consciousness-modes related to them. Accordingly, the first-in-itself judgment-theory is the theory of evident judgments, and the first-in-itself within it is the genetic reduction of predicative evidences to non-predicative evidence called experience.

At this beginning lies the systematic place to discover, from the judgment, that certainty and certainty-modalities, intending intention and fulfillment, identical being and identical sense, evident self-having, being-truth, truth as sense-correctness — that all this is not exclusively peculiar to the predicative sphere but already belongs to the intentionality of experience. The experience-judgment — the judgment of origin — provides the ur-ground. From its syntaxes, the higher syntactic genesis goes upward.

#### §87. Transition to the Higher-Level Evidences. The Question of the Relevance of the Cores for the Evidence of Material and Formal Generalities

There is a fundamental difference in the manner of essential generalizations carried out in the sense of the material a priori versus the formal a priori. In the former, we draw from the individual eigenwesentliche contents; in formalizing generalization, every individual is to be emptied into a something-in-general. The evidence of analytically a priori laws does not need determinate individual intuitions but only whatever examples of categorialies. Yet the sense-reference of all categorial meanings to the individual cannot be indifferent for the sense and possible evidence of the analytic laws. Without uncovering the judgment-genesis, even in the evidence of formal generalization, the cores are not wholly irrelevant.

#### §88. The Implied Presupposition of the Analytic Law of Contradiction: Every Judgment Is to Be Brought to Distinctness-Evidence

The logical principles contain a basic presupposition: an unclarified presupposition must have entered restrictingly into the judgment-concept of consequence-logic, so that only in this silently presupposed restriction does the judgment stand under the lawful conditions of genuine executability.

#### §89. The Possibility of Distinctness-Evidence

##### a) Sense as Judgment and as "Judgment-Content." Ideal Existence of the Judgment Presupposes Ideal Existence of the Judgment-Content

The concept of sense has an essential double sense for the judgment-sphere. As sense of a statement, one can understand the respective judgment, but also, when the assertor passes from simple certainty to presuming, doubting, affirming or denying the same "S is p," a "judgment-content" as a common element emerges, the identical what of the judgment persisting through the change of being-modality. The possible unity of such a judgment-content, as a positable unity, is tied to conditions. Mere unified grammatical understandability — pure grammatical meaningfulness — is not yet the meaningfulness that logical analytics presupposes.

The unified executability of the judgment-content lies before the executability of the judgment itself and is its condition. The ideal "existence" of the judgment-content is presupposition of the ideal "existence" of the judgment.

##### b) The Ideal Existence of the Judgment-Content Is Tied to the Conditions of the Unity of Possible Experience

The clarification lies in intentional genesis. Before all judging lies a universal ground of experience, always presupposed as a consistent unity of possible experience. In this consistency, everything has something to do with everything materially. Every original judging in its content and every coherently progressing judging has connection through the connection of the things in the synthetic unity of experience on whose ground it stands. The syntactic stuffs of non-intuitive judgments can from the grounds of their being- and sense-genesis not be completely freely variable. A priori, the syntactic stuffs of each possible judgment have intentional relatedness to the unity of a possible experience. The formal-logical consideration says nothing about this in its objective orientation, but every formal connection of S and p presupposes in concealment that the S, p, etc. materially "have something to do with one another."

#### §90. Application to the Principles of Truth-Logic: They Hold Only for Judgments with Material Sense-Unity

Logic obviously does not have in mind judgments of the materially senseless kind (e.g., "the angle-sum of a triangle equals the color red"). If the logical principles were to refer to judgments in general, they would not be tenable — certainly not the principle of excluded middle. For all materially "senseless" judgments break through its validity. The principles are unconditionally valid for all judgments whose cores belong together sense-wise. For these judgments it is a priori given that they relate to a unified experience-ground. The "third" is not excluded for the wider realm of judgments that includes materially senseless ones; it consists in the fact that judgments with predicates having no sense-related bearing on the subject are, in their senselessness, "above" truth and falsity.

#### §91. Transition to New Questions

An intentional judgment-theory is necessary, and it must be deeply developed, in order even originally to understand what the proper and pure sense of the logical principles is. But what is gained for the clarification of the idea of truth is not more than the demonstration of the necessity of an "epistemological" preparatory work. Judgment-evidence "gives" truth in the sense of judgment-correctness. Experience, as a pre-predicative evidence, gives "realities." But how does evidence stand to truth? Not as simply as the conversion made it appear.

### Chapter 5. The Subjective Grounding of Logic as a Transcendental-Philosophical Problem

#### §92. Clarification of the Sense of the Positivity of Objective Logic

##### a) The Relatedness of the Historical Logic to a Real World

Logic as formal science-theory had in its unforgettable beginning in the Platonic dialectic the principled theme of the possibility of science and of what is as such. In the changed situation of later times, logic took the form of a formal apophantic critique of pregiven science, for which existing objects and an existing world were established in advance. True being in general, predicative truth and theory in general, and the possibility of advancing to this through experience and theoretical cognition were never-considered self-evidences. Logic is logic for a world thought as pregiven.

##### b) The Naive Presupposition of a World Ranks Logic Among the Positive Sciences

The formal logic of a possible world thereby ranks itself among the positive sciences: for all of them the world is an in-advance unquestioned fact.

#### §93. The Insufficiency of the Attempts at Critique of Experience Since Descartes

##### a) The Naive Presupposition of the Validity of Objective Logic

Descartes' attempted reform of all sciences required a critique of experience that leads to the result that experience lacks absolute evidence. The naive presupposition of the world is to be suspended and all objective cognition grounded on the sole apodictic givenness: the ego cogito. This was the beginning of all modern transcendental philosophy.

##### b) The Missing of the Transcendental Sense of the Cartesian Reduction to the Ego

Already in Descartes, through an absolute evidence the Ego is fixed as a first bit of the world (mens sive animus, substantia cogitans), and the rest of the world is to be inferred by logical procedure. Descartes operates with a naive a priori heritage, with the naive presupposition of ontological and logical evidences for the treatment of the transcendental thematic. He misses the properly transcendental sense of the Ego he discovered.

##### c) The Grounding of Logic Leads into the Universal Problem of Transcendental Phenomenology

The grounding of logic leads into the universal problem of transcendental phenomenology.

### Chapter 6. Transcendental Phenomenology and Intentional Psychology. The Problem of Transcendental Psychologism

#### §94. All Beings Constituted in Consciousness-Subjectivity

Everything that is for us draws its being-sense, with all its determinations, from constitutive consciousness-achievements. The transcendental question concerns how objectualities of every kind constitute themselves in consciousness.

#### §95. Necessity of Beginning from One's Own Subjectivity

The beginning must be from one's own subjectivity: the ego must take itself as the starting-point of transcendental reflection.

#### §96. The Transcendental Problematic of Intersubjectivity and the Intersubjective World

The transcendental problematic of intersubjectivity encompasses how the experience of others constitutes itself, how intersubjective objectivity arises, and how the transcendental solipsism that seems to threaten is only apparent.

#### §97. The Method of Uncovering Consciousness-Constitution in Its Universal Philosophical Significance

The method of uncovering constitution is itself a universal philosophical method.

#### §98. The Constitutive Investigations as A Priori

The constitutive investigations are a priori: they concern essential structures, not factual contingencies.

#### §99. Psychological and Transcendental Subjectivity. The Problem of Transcendental Psychologism

The relationship between psychological and transcendental subjectivity is clarified. The problem of transcendental psychologism consists in the confusion of the two. A pure psychology of intentionality and a transcendental phenomenology are parallel in content but fundamentally different in sense: the one takes the human being as part of the world, the other suspends the world-thesis. The overcoming of transcendental psychologism is the decisive step toward genuine transcendental philosophy.

#### §100. Historical-Critical Remarks on the Development of Transcendental Philosophy and Especially on the Transcendental Problematic of Formal Logic

Husserl provides an extensive historical-critical discussion tracing the development from Hume through Kant and beyond, showing how the transcendental problematic of formal logic was never adequately posed. Hume did not raise the transcendental problem of the constitution of ideal objectualities. Kant, reactively dependent on Hume, did not put transcendental questions to formal logic, treating it as self-sufficient in its aprioricity. The anti-Platonism of the tradition — its refusal to recognize the objecthood of the ideal — has been the greatest obstacle to understanding the sense, problematic, and method of genuine transcendental philosophy. Only after the establishment of the ideality of logical formations could the transcendental problem of logic be posed, and it was in fact from this direction that phenomenology arose historically.

### Chapter 7. Objective Logic and Phenomenology of Reason

#### §101. The Subjective Grounding of Logic as Transcendental Phenomenology of Reason

The subjective grounding of logic is transcendental phenomenology of reason. This is not an alien appendage to logic but its deepest self-clarification and self-justification.

#### §102. The World-Relatedness of the Inherited Logic and the Question of the Character of the "Ultimate" Logic Norming Its Own Transcendental Clarification

The inherited logic is related to a real world — its truths and its a priori are world-related. The question arises about the character of the "ultimate" logic that must norm the transcendental clarification itself.

#### §103. Absolute Grounding of Cognition Is Possible Only in the Universal Science of Transcendental Subjectivity as the Only Absolute Being

Absolute grounding of cognition is possible only in the universal science of transcendental subjectivity. The world is not an absolute being; it constitutes itself in transcendental subjectivity. Only transcendental subjectivity "is" in the absolute sense, and only from it can ultimate, absolute cognition-grounding proceed.

#### §104. Transcendental Phenomenology as the Self-Explication of Transcendental Subjectivity

Transcendental phenomenology is the self-explication of transcendental subjectivity. It does not impose a foreign framework but lets subjectivity lay out what it finds in itself.

#### §105. Preparations for the Conclusion of the Transcendental Critique of Logic. The Usual Evidence-Theories Misled by the Presupposition of Absolute Truth

The usual evidence-theories are misled by the presupposition of absolute truth — truth as something that exists absolutely, independent of all subjective constituting. This presupposition must be subjected to critique. The idea of an absolute truth existing in itself, to which evidence must conform as a mere external indicator, misconstrues the relationship. Truth is not something existing beyond evidence to which evidence must somehow find its way; rather, truth is itself an achievement-concept of evidencing consciousness.

#### §106. Further on the Critique of the Presupposition of Absolute Truth and of the Dogmatic Theories of Evidence

The critique deepens. The idea of an absolute truth for an absolute world — a world "in itself" to which our experience is merely an imperfect approximation — involves an idealizing presupposition that has never been subjected to constitutive critique. The correlative idea of an absolute evidence fully and definitively grasping this absolute truth is likewise an idealization. Evidence always has its horizons, its incompleteness, its openness to correction. The dogmatic theories of evidence, which make it either an absolute criterion or dismiss it as a mere feeling, both fail to grasp its actual phenomenological character as a functioning intentionality with its own teleological structure.

#### §107. Preliminary Sketch of a Transcendental Theory of Evidence as Intentional Achievement

##### a) The Evidence of Outer (Sensory) Experience

Outer experience is always perspectival, one-sided, never giving its object in absolute completeness. It is essentially a process of ever-further determination, never completed but always pointing beyond itself. Its evidence is genuine — it is self-giving — but it is always imperfect, always a starting-point for further experience that may confirm or disconfirm. This character of outer experience is not a defect to be lamented but an essential structural feature to be understood.

##### b) The Evidence of "Inner" Experience

Inner experience — the experience of one's own psychic life — has its own essential structure. Although often regarded as the paradigm of perfect evidence, it too is essentially imperfect. Even the so-called immanent perception gives only a starting-point; the full objectification of a psychic datum requires the synthetic accomplishments of retention, protention, and recollection.

##### c) Hyletic Data and Intentional Functions. The Evidence of the Immanent Time-Data

The most fundamental level of evidence concerns the immanent time-data and the functioning of the primal time-consciousness. Here we reach the deepest constitutive level: the consciousness of internal time, in which all other evidences are ultimately founded. The analysis of time-consciousness reveals the most basic structures of constitution.

##### d) Evidence as an A Priori Structural Form of Consciousness

Evidence is an a priori structural form of consciousness. Consciousness is essentially constituting; its life is a constant striving toward self-giving, toward fullness. Self-giving as fulfillment, confirmation, confirmation, cancellation, falsity, practical missing, etc. — these are all a priori structural forms belonging to the unity of a life, and the clarifying investigation of this unity is the immense theme of phenomenology.

## Concluding Word

Husserl summarizes: the path from the traditional logic to the transcendental has been attempted in this work — to the transcendental, which is not a second logic but only the radical and concrete logic itself, growing in phenomenological method. More precisely, as such transcendental logic we had in view only the traditionally delimited analytic logic, which by virtue of its empty-formal generality spans all being- and object- and cognition-spheres. Nevertheless, compelled to outline the sense and breadth of transcendental research, we have gained in advance also an understanding for the "logics" of other sense to be grounded — material science-theories — of which the highest and most comprehensive would be the logic of absolute science, the logic of transcendental-phenomenological philosophy itself.

Under the title logic, equivalent to ontology, fall also all material-a priori disciplines to be grounded — disciplines of the one mundane ontology, first to be grounded straightforwardly in transcendental "naive" positivity. It unfolds the universal a priori of a purely possible world in general, which as eidos must concretely arise through the method of eidetic variation from the factually given world. From this thought proceed the levels of the great problematic of a radically grounded world-logic. As the ground-level functions the "transcendental aesthetic" in a new sense (related to but not identical with the Kantian), treating the eidetic problem of a possible world as a world of "pure experience," the eidetic description of the universal a priori without which in mere experience and before the categorial actions, objects could not unitarily appear and a unity of nature or world could not constitute itself as passive synthetic unity.

Upon this is layered the logos of objective worldly being and science in the "higher" sense, researching under ideas of "strict" being and strict truth and forming corresponding "exact" theories. The whole "exact" physics operates with "idealities," underlaying the actually experienced nature with a nature as idea, as regulative ideal norm, as its logos in a higher sense. For a radical self-understanding and a transcendental critique of "exact" natural cognition, tremendous problems lie here.

Thus we have only delimited the essence of a formal science-theory and led it over to its transcendental form, while the full idea of a science-theory, a logic, an ontology has gained only its framework and points to future presentations.

## Appendix I: Syntactic Forms and Syntactic Stuffs, Core-Forms and Core-Stuffs

Husserl introduces this appendix as a deepening of the insight into the essence of judgment-form. The distinction between syntactic forms and syntactic stuffs, used throughout the main text, will now be more fully elucidated and supplemented by related distinctions. All of this belongs to the pure-logical morphology ("pure-logical grammar"), and wherever grammatical expressions like "predication," "proposition," and the like are used, only the relevant meaning-formations are meant. Under the title "syntaxe," what is at issue is a descriptive demonstration of previously unexplored essential structures of the judgment-sphere, whose relevance for the grammarian is self-evident. Husserl notes that this appendix derives essentially from his Göttingen formal-logical lectures, in the final version from the winter semester 1910/11.

### §1. Articulation of Predicative Judgments

Taking predications of the simplest categorical form "A is b," each articulates into two parts, with a caesura: "A — is b," the substrate-member (that about which something is predicated) and what is predicated of it. Articulation does not mean partitioning into separable pieces; at minimum the predicate-member is not independently detachable. That the same holds for the subject-member will soon become apparent.

A more complex case: the hypothetical judgment "if A is b, then C is d." It too articulates sharply into two parts, with a caesura: the antecedent and the consequent. Each member again articulates. The hypothetical antecedent and likewise the consequent present themselves as "modifications" of a simple categorical proposition, carrying within themselves the caesura between the modified subject-member and its modified predicate-member. A may itself contain further articulations, such as attributive annexes. So a unified proposition can be more or less richly articulated, and not all members need stand on the same level. In every proposition, however, we arrive at ultimate articulations and members. All members in this sense are under all circumstances unselfstanding: they are what they are in the whole, and different wholes can have equal members but not the same member.

If we say "A is b" and continue "A is c," the same object A is meant, but in a different how, and this how itself belongs to the meaning (not the meaning-act), to the meant as such. In the two propositions we have at the corresponding places something different of the same content A, and this differently formed. The form "the same" appears implicitly in the second proposition, establishing a connection that differently forms both. We stand in the unity of a total proposition: "A is b and the same is c."

### §2. The Thing-Relatedness in Judgments

At the members within the concrete predicative meaning-unity, and also at the whole judgment or proposition, two kinds of moments are to be distinguished. Every self-standing proposition relates to some things and something pertaining to them. In the judged proposition a state of affairs is "meant." This relation to objectualities — its thing-relatedness — is tied to certain of its moments, which we shall call the stuffs, while it is concretely possible as a meaning-relation to objectual only through other moments, the moments of form.

Under the question of how the proposition as a whole achieves objectual relation, we note that we always find parts that themselves have objectual relation. This holds of all members, and if they are articulated, of their members down to the last. With these we have the ultimately thing-related types of part-meanings under the viewpoint of articulation: subject-meanings relating to substrate-objects, part-meanings relating to properties, and again to relations. On the other side, parts stand out that, like "is," "or," "because," etc., represent meaning-moments essential to propositions but that in themselves harbor nothing of thing-relatedness — though through their function in the proposition they participate in its thing-relatedness.

### §3. Pure Forms and Stuffs

From here arises, as opposed to articulation, a remarkable and totally different kind of "division" of every predicative meaning. On one side we can step by step abstract out the completely unselfstanding, completely abstract moments that lack thing-relatedness from themselves: they are called moments of pure form. Then at each member and ultimately at each final member there remains a core-content — again something completely abstract, but precisely what gives the member its thing-relatedness. In this regard we speak of stuffish moments. An example: taking proposition-subjects like "the paper," "the centaur," and thinking other propositions where "the same" expressions stand in modified function (grammatically declining), an identical element of thing-relatedness stands out, maintaining the relation to the same — to paper, to centaur — through all changes of form. We arrive at two limit-concepts: "pure forms" and "pure stuffs." Both belong necessarily to the concretion. The forming is not an activity performed on pre-given stuffs — which would presuppose the countersense that one could have stuffs by themselves.

### §4. Lower and Higher Forms. Their Sense-Relation to One Another

Forms distinguish themselves into lower and higher-level forms: forms belonging to the lowest members and those encompassing already formed members, bringing them to higher-level concretions. By their sense, the higher-level forms are referred to the lower ones and function in this back-reference for thing-relatedness. The proposition as a whole has whole-forms and through them has unified relation to the whole state of affairs. This thing-relatedness is a founded one, since it already presupposes the thing-relatedness of the members. But also in another way meaning-relations of the forms to one another show up, with corresponding differences between immediate and mediate thing-relatedness. Through the form, a thing-related member sometimes receives a thing-relatedness reaching beyond itself, related to that lying in another member.

### §5. The Self-Enclosed Functional Unity of the Self-Standing Apophansis. Separation of the Whole-Forms into Copulation and Conjunction

Forms are of different kinds. In the meaning-whole of the proposition they stand in the self-enclosed unity of one function; "proposition" in formal generality co-expresses this functional unity. Distinctions in the manner of the connection-form emerge. On one hand we have connection-forms like "and" and "or" — the conjunctive in the extended sense. They connect and create categorial unity, but in their own sense nothing lies of the relation to the predicating, the "apophantic" judgment. They neither themselves establish such a categorial unity nor do they point back to one. On the other hand we have the "connection"-mode that constitutes the specific unity-form of a predicative proposition: the form of the copula — the is-form in its various shapes. It is the functional form that brings the members, shaping for them their member-form, together into members of the propositional whole, so that the whole-form is abstractively to be lifted off as their connection-form.

### §6. Transition into the Broadest Categorial Sphere

#### a) Universality of the Distinguished Connection-Forms

The conjunctive connection-forms can in fact take on something of copulation for external reasons — associative-apperceptive, or because we conjunctively connect judgments themselves. The distinguished connection-modes, the copulative and the non-copulative, have the same universality as modes of connective forming of categorial objectualities into new ones.

#### b) Extension of the Distinctions Connected with Articulation to the Entire Categorial Sphere

What was said about articulation, always only looking at apophantic judgment-formations, fits with only slight modifications all "syntactic" formations (numbers, combinations, etc.). Here too we have reduction to ultimate articulations and a construction of categorial wholes from last members, either at the same level or in arbitrary hierarchical stages.

#### c) The Extended Categorial Proposition-Concept as Opposed to That of the Old Apophantic Analytics

All these formations stand under the broadest concept of "proposition" as the analytic formation — called "proposition" not as a correlate of copulations but of positings, of a sense-content of categorial form. The traditional apophantic logic considers under the title "judgment" exclusively, first, categorical propositions (including existential) in all doxic modalities, and second, all conjunctive and other formations from categorical propositions destined to produce unity of predicative theory.

### §7. Syntactic Forms, Syntactic Stuffs, Syntaxe

With respect to the unity-form of a judgment and the forming that its members must correlatively have, significant distinctions arise. We separate subject-form and form of the predicate, and within the latter, property-predicate and relation-predicate. Additionally, the same stuffish content appears once in subject-form and another time in object-form within the relative predicate — these are pure forms belonging immediately to the unified functional form of predication. In view of the pure total form of the apophantic unity in its encompassing of the pure special forms belonging to it, it is the unity of the syntaxe, through which the remaining identical stuffs are syntactically formed. Subject-form, object-form, etc. are syntactic forms. The stuffs — syntactic stuffs — are moments of the judgment that stand out through abstraction from those functional forms: e.g., the identical substantive or the identical "adjective" regardless of which syntactic position it occupies.

### §8. Syntagma and Member. Self-Standing Judgments as Syntagma, Likewise Judgments in the Extended Sense

Taking the syntactic stuffs in their forms — concretely united — we call this unity the syntagma. It is nothing other than the unity of the member in the proposition, which is formed stuff. Different members can have the same form but different stuffs, and different form but the same stuff. This law holds for members of however complicated predication, and also for self-standing propositions themselves — since every proposition can essentially be modified into a syntactic member of higher-level predications. Every whole proposition is thus itself a syntagma, unity of syntactic stuff in syntactic form. Judgments in the extended sense — all categorial meanings whatever — are syntagma and stand under the lawfulnesses of the structure that this word indicates.

### §9. "Judgment-Content" as Syntactic Stuff of the Judgment as Syntagma

When a complex proposition has separable pieces, each piece in the whole is given as a syntactically formed piece, a member. If the piece is made self-standing, it is not the member as it was that becomes self-standing; rather a self-standing proposition of the same "judgment-content" is produced. The being-self-standing-for-itself is itself a syntactic form. In the change of functions in which "the same" proposition takes the various forms of antecedent, member of a disjunction, etc., there emerges as the identical the same "proposition-matter" or "judgment-matter" — in the sense of the same predicative syntactic stuff that takes the various syntactic forms. This applies in formal generality. Husserl notes that from here, returning to §89a of the main text, a deepening results through the more radical concept of judgment-matter: the possibility of distinctness-evidence hangs in a deepest sense on the total syntactic stuff of the judgment.

### §10. Levels of Syntactic Forming

The number of syntactic forms is limited compared to the infinity of identical syntactic stuffs. The syntactic forms sort themselves by levels: certain forms (e.g., subject and predicate) appear on all levels of composition; other forms (like the hypothetical antecedent and consequent) already require syntactically articulated stuffs. Within a total member, forms can appear that differ from the syntactic forms of its subordinate members.

### §11. Non-Syntactic Forms and Stuffs — Demonstrated Within the Pure Syntactic Stuffs

The concepts of form and stuff so far treated referred to syntagma — syntactic forms were forms of proposition-members and propositions themselves. The stuffs entering into them, upon closer analysis, also have a forming, but one of a totally different kind. Forms of a wholly new style are presupposed in the final stuffs. Going down the natural staircase of articulation to the last, no-longer-articulable members: their syntactic stuffs are pure stuffs, free of syntactic forms — e.g., substantives like "paper," "human," abstracted from subject-form, object-form, etc.; adjectives like "white," "round." Comparing different such pure or final syntactic stuffs, we notice that despite their difference they can share something identically in common. Comparing "paper" and "human," an essential generality of form emerges — something of "substantival" form. Likewise "adjectival" form, and the form of "relativum." In a single form infinitely many contents can be grasped. We arrive at a limited group of entirely novel, no longer syntactic forms: the pure-grammatical categories of substantivity, and of adjectivity as propertiness and relationality.

### §12. The Core-Formation with Core-Stuff and Core-Form

In place of the syntagma now appears a different kind of unity of stuff and form: the respective substantive itself, predicate and relativum itself, as enclosed in the syntactic stuff. Each must essentially enclose such a unity, so that we have reached a deeper structure of predication — one lying in all its syntaxes, specifically in the syntactic stuffs. We call this unity the core-formation (*Kerngebilde*).

Comparing core-formations of different categories — "similarity" and "similar," "redness" and "red" — we see that in each pair, core-formations of different category share an essential moment in common. "Redness" and "red" have "contentual" commonality in the different core-form. We arrive at the core-stuff (*Kernstoff*) of the respective core-formation, and its correlate, the core-form (*Kernform*), which forms the core into one of the determinate categories.

### §13. The Preference of the Substantival Category. Substantivization

Every adjective and relativum has opposed to it a corresponding substantivum — the "substantivized" adjective and relative. But there is no adjectivization of arbitrary substantives. Substantives like "similarity" or "redness" give themselves sense-wise as "modifications" with a secondary sense pointing back to the original, non-substantival one. This is connected with an essential possibility of syntactic transformation of the relevant propositions.

### §14. Transition to the Complications

We have gained a reduction to the ultimate "elements" in the sphere of predicative meanings — the stuffs in the most ultimate sense, which have no more meaning-forms and underlie all formings of different kinds and levels. At these last elements the last forms appear: core-forms. The treatment of higher complications still yields important results: the structural distinction in the last syntactic stuffs holds for all syntactic stuffs whatsoever, and every categorial formation that does not already have "nominal" form can be "nominalized," and it is the syntactic total stuff that receives a "substantival" form in the extended sense.

### §15. The Concept of "Terminus" in the Traditional Formal Logic

The traditional logic has worked out virtually nothing of these distinctions. The concept of core-stuff essentially coincides with what the traditional logic in a completely vague way calls a terminus. The use of termini fits the traditional syllogistic: in a syllogism, "mortal" and "mortals" count as the same terminus. It is not the syntactic stuff that is meant but the core-stuff, what remains identical when the core-form changes. The word "concept" (*Begriff*) is often used instead of terminus, though it has multiple ambiguities. The concept of the terminus does not restrict itself to last core-stuffs: analytics leaves it open in its proposition-forms whether the termini are substantivized categorial formations or not.

## Appendix II: On the Phenomenological Constitution of the Judgment. The Originally Active Judging and Its Secondary Modifications

### §1. Active as Self-Producing Judging as Opposed to Its Secondary Modifications

Active judging is a producing of "thought-objects," categorial formations. To its essence belongs the possibility of progressing in a hierarchical construction, ideally in an iteration in infinitum. Active judging is the original form in which alone the putative categorial objectuality comes to genuine and proper production — in which alone the "judgment" comes to original self-givenness. All other modes of givenness of the same judgment are characterized in themselves as intentional modifications of the actively producing, original mode. This is a special case of the essential-lawful priority of originality, which holds for every passive or active object-constitution.

### §2. From the General Theory of Intentionality

#### a) Original Consciousness and Intentional Modification. Static Intentional Explication. Explication of the "Meaning" and of the Meant "Itself." The Manifold of Possible Consciousness-Modes of the Same

One and the same object can a priori be conscious in very different consciousness-modes (perception, recollection, empty consciousness, etc.); among them the "experiencing," original mode has a priority. Intentional modification has the peculiarity that it in itself points back to the non-modified. From any non-original givenness-mode, the consciousness-subject can strive toward the original and make clear the objectual sense. The fulfilling clarification is carried out in a synthetic transition in which the non-originally conscious gives itself as the same as the originally ("in experience") conscious. Every intentional givenness-mode can be "statically" explicated in this way.

Where the original form is one producing in synthetic activity, a double intentionality and self-giving are intertwined: the activity producing the judgment itself (merely as judgment) originally, and the activity of original formation (making-evident) of the categorial objectuality itself. This holds in the widest generality for every intentionality.

Every consciousness stands essentially in a belonging consciousness-manifold, in a synthetic open infinity of possible consciousness-modes of the same — a manifold with its teleological center in possible "experience." This remains essentially open to the counter-possibility of disappointing cancellation.

#### b) Intentional Explication of Genesis. Genetic as Well as Static Originality of the Experiencing Givenness-Mode. "Original Institution" of the "Apperception" for Every Object-Category

While static analysis, led by the unity of the intended object, strives from the unclear givenness-mode toward the clear, genetic intentional analysis is directed at the whole concrete context in which each consciousness and its intentional object stand. The immanent unity of the temporality of life comes into question, in which life has its "history."

The original form of consciousness — "experience" in the broadest sense — is not only statically but also genetically preferred. For every fundamental kind of objectualities, no non-original consciousness-mode is essentially possible unless the corresponding original consciousness-mode has previously occurred in the synthetic unity of immanent temporality as the genetically "originally instituting" one. This does not mean we can never have in non-original mode an objectuality we have not previously experienced as the same; but the type of the experience has been instituted in a prior original-instituting genesis. This holds essentially for every object-category in the broadest sense.

Every original givenness-mode has a double genetic aftereffect: first, in the form of possible remembering reproductions via retentions; second, the "apperceptive" aftereffect, according to which in a similar new situation what is already constitutively present is apperceived in a similar way.

#### c) The Time-Form of Intentional Genesis and Its Constitution. Retentional Modification. Sedimentation in the Background of Non-Prominence (Unconsciousness)

The universal essential form of intentional genesis is the constitution of immanent temporality: every consciousness-experience, in the streaming change of typically modified givenness-modes within a living present, receives its identical time-position and then enduringly retains it. At every experience occurring in the primal mode of immanent presentness, a "retentional" consciousness attaches in unalterable necessity as an original modification, through which the primal mode "presently given" passes in continuous synthesis into the modified form of the same "just-having-been." Each modification functions according to the same lawfulness as a relative primal mode for a new modification, and so continuously onward. Each modification points back to its absolute primal mode. The continuous retentional self-modification goes to an essential limit: a graduality of prominence goes hand in hand with the modification, and this has its limit where the previously prominent flows into the general background — the so-called "unconscious," which is nothing less than a phenomenological nothing but itself a limit-mode of consciousness.

### §3. The Non-Original Givenness-Modes of the Judgment

#### a) The Retentional, as the First-in-Itself Form of "Secondary Sensibility." The Livingly Self-Transforming Constitution of a Many-Membered Judgment

Over against the originally producing givenness-mode, the retentional is the first-in-itself non-original mode. The original action transforms in retentional continuity into a secondary form that is no longer activity, a passive form — a form of "secondary sensibility." Through the continuous identity-synthesis, the passive consciousness is consciousness of the same that was previously in active originality constituted. The acquisition endures initially only during the living progress of the retentional modification up to the limit of non-prominence. This kind of retention alone makes possible progressive judgment-processes as living further-formation and connection of categorial meanings to the unity of ever-new and higher-level judgments.

#### b) Passive Recollection and Its Constitutive Achievement for the Judgment as Enduring Unity

When we speak of every categorial activity leading to an enduring acquisition, more is normally meant than what occurs during living retention. Every judging leads to a judgment-result that is henceforth a permanent "result" over which the judger can freely dispose at any time. Here the general essential lawfulnesses of passive genesis come into question — the essential laws of "association" and associative constitution, including the formation of "apperceptions." A proposition, a proof, a number-formation can for associative reasons long after the disappearance of the original production recur in the givenness-mode of a memory-occurrence and, though in this mode, participate in new original judgment-actions. The "result" of the earlier originality is taken up again and from it something new created, without the taking-up involving a repetition of the activity.

#### c) Surfacing as Apperceptive Occurrence, an Analogue of the Occurrence of Passive Recollection

Formations can also occur to us that are analogues of memory-occurrences but not themselves memory-occurrences — formations we never originally actively produced, but that arise by analogy with ones we did produce. This is understandable from the intentionality of association and its governing essential laws. Especially where signs and expressions are involved, the associatively wakening perceptive and the apperceptively surfacing become unitarily thematic, producing a two-sided-unitary object-constitution: "through" the sensibly given sign the thematically preferring gaze goes to the signified.

### §4. The Essential Possibilities of Activating the Passive Givenness-Modes

In all secondary givenness-modes — retentional, properly remembered, and apperceptive — we have "modifications" that phenomenologically point back to original activity. It is also to be noted that with such pointings-back is included the consciousness of a freedom, a practical possibility, of producing the givenness-mode of original activity. If the restoration succeeds, a synthesis of fulfillingly-identifying coincidence necessarily enters — the consciousness of return from the passively meant to the meant itself. Instead of leaving it at the passive resurfacing of a judgment, one can genuinely reactivate it, re-produce it, produce it anew in renewed and genuine activity. To every other modification-mode of occurrence belongs the consciousness of a capacity for genuine activation.

### §5. The Fundamental Forms of Originally Producing Judging and of Judging in General

Originally producing ("explicit") judging can be (1) "through and through" original activity — every partial categorial objectuality in the unity of the formed and further-formed categorial objectuality has been originally produced in the active liveliness of the judging process, so the resulting supreme whole has through and through originality of self-givenness. (2) The other, ordinary case is that judgment-activity ties back to old judgment-acquisitions, to passive and modifiedly resurfacing categorial objectualities, that "well-known" propositions are reused, that substrate-objects already carry their rich determination-contents from earlier determining judgments as sedimentations and are passively taken up. Explicit judging occurs insofar as new formations are carried out in a certain relative originality, but on the ground of "old" formations. We must also think of "apperceptive occurrences": very commonly we judge on the basis of judgment-apperceptions, of passively but indirectly — on the basis of our earlier similar formations — coming to us, occurring to us, categorial thoughts.

Between the two extremes — the thoroughly confused, entirely inexplicit judgments and the perfectly distinct, fully explicit ones (produced originally in all categorial components) — lie all the otherwise explicitly executed judgments that work over inherited stocks: the cases of incomplete distinctness.

### §6. On Indistinct Linguistic Judging and Its Function

In linguistic judging, language with its articulated prominences and the prominences of its meaning-indications plays a great role. Every simple sign indicates a meaning associatively. The combination of words into the unity of speech is the unity of an associatively originated apperception. Also in the voluntary formation of grammatical sentences we can follow the habitual style of sense-formation, letting new formations arise from elements and formations of typically familiar form without at all actually carrying out categorial actions. So unnoticed material countersense, senselessness of unification, or analytic countersense can come about. Unity of "judgment" comes about, but it is "confused," inexplicit, "improper" judging. It is an associative passivity that carries within itself, in the manner of intentional implication, a spontaneous activity transformed into passive sensibility, pointing to it as activatable.

Precisely thereby it also has important functions within the framework of reason, which only in active producing yields categorial evidence of every kind. Because association only indirectly indicates and anticipates but does not itself give, "blind" merely associatively originated judging stands before questions of the "existence" of the indicated judgment itself and further of the categorial objectualities themselves. The mathematician who on the basis of the configuration and sequence of formulas anticipates a new theorem and a proof for it — guided by association — has, as he well knows, not yet found real cognition; he strives for the explicit action, which is his proper rational activity — as much as the associatively indicating one necessarily remains to pre-indicate goals and paths for his rational practice.

### §7. Advantage of Retentional and Recollective Confusion over the Apperceptive: Secondary Evidence in Confusion

A significant difference of these confused judgment-modes emerges over against the inactivity of retentions and recollections, as much as these too are accessible and in need of justification through making-proper. For as little as they are originally self-giving and thus genuine evidences, they have the significance of secondary derivations of evidence in which, as a critique of cognition shows, though indirectly, something of evidence still remains. Without them there would be no science. If living retention were worthless, no thinking-result would come about at all. Reproductive recollections too have not only evidence as clear recollections — the evidence of the experience of the past, imperfect but essentially perfectible in the manner of approximation to an ideal limit — but also a secondary evidence as still-unclear recollections. Without this, every possible justification of science's trust that it is a stock of enduring cognition-acquisitions, as reactivatable evidences at any time, would be missing.

## Appendix III: On the Idea of a "Logic of Mere Freedom from Contradiction" or "Logic of Mere Consequence"

### §1. The Goal of Formal Freedom from Contradiction and Formal Consequence. Broader and Narrower Versions of These Concepts

The traditional formal logic was already long characterized as a logic of mere freedom from contradiction or a mere consequence-logic. Husserl's demonstration that it is to be defined as a "pure analytics" and to be exactly delimited essentially vindicates those old characterizations, though also giving them a genuine and purified sense. However, the way the expressions "freedom from contradiction" (also "compatibility") and "consequence" were sometimes used may occasion misunderstandings, as Professor O. Becker pointed out.

The old logic was called logic of (formal) freedom from contradiction, though it was not concerned solely with questions of formal compossibility of judgments. Questions of analytically necessary following, of syllogistic consequence, were its main theme. Still the old designation had a good sense. In its normative orientation, the question was: how can we in our judgments, before all entry into their material themes, first of all avoid falling into "contradictions" conditioned by mere form? And how find the relevant formal law-norms? Since every negation of a formal necessary consequence is a contradiction, the entire formal consequence-logic, that of analytic necessities, falls under the viewpoint of freedom from contradiction.

But the universal question of the essential forms and norms of a universe of freedom from contradiction especially and necessarily leads to the question of the essential forms of analytic necessities, according to which in given judgments others are included. The universal form-lawfulness of freedom from contradiction thus encompasses that of inferential consequence; formal logic of freedom from contradiction is also formal consequence-logic.

Conversely, the whole logic can be referred to consequence in an entirely broad sense. I remain "consequent" so long as I hold to my judgments; I am formally-inconsequent if I recognize that my later judgment contradicts my earlier one. Judgments form a system of "consequence" when they go together at "careful examination" into the unity of a connected judgment within which none contradicts another. The analytics as universal essential lawfulness of possible formal freedom from contradiction is also analytics as essential lawfulness of possible formal "consequence."

The fundamentally essential thing in the doctrine presented in the main text is that compatibility, contradiction, consequence of every sense in question, as they function in the entire formal analytics, can and then must be precisely characterized in a pure sense that retains nothing of a relation to truth and falsity of the judgments. The pure analytics has judgments purely as judgments in question, the judgment-relations concerning genuine executability and non-executability — but nothing of whether this has relevance for possible truth.

### §2. Reference-Back of the Systematic and Radical Construction of a Pure Analytics to the Theory of Syntaxes

If we grasp the universal task of analytics as investigating the essential laws of judgment-form that are conditions of possibility for any judgment to be a "properly existing" judgment — an explicitly executable one — in the sense of distinctness-evidence, then we must go along with the lawfulness of the syntaxe and its substructure. We must take as starting point the syntactic articulation with the belonging distinctions of syntactic forms and stuffs, ask for the "primitive" or primal forms and their primal articulation, further for the equally primitive syntactic connection-modes.

Laws of analytic existence belong here — first the law of analytic primitivity: primitive forms are a priori "existent," i.e. properly executable. Every modalization preserves this existence in and for itself, though not without further ado in connections. Mere conjunction without any copulation yields new forms of possible existence. Every connection of forms through commonality of a terminus has the significance of a copulative connection belonging to it. Altogether, in-themselves-possible judgments syntactically inserted do not yet yield a whole that is possible with respect to its total syntaxe. Everywhere it depends on the interweaving through copulations when the possibility of coexistence (compossibility on the basis of pure form) can become questionable.

We can say that unity from copulation defines a quite distinguished judgment-concept — precisely the one that traditional logic exclusively has in view, leaving "connectionless" judgment-conjunctions out of consideration. In examining the syntactic complexions of this copulative sphere, one naturally encounters in the most general way all analytic necessities and the contradictions that are their reverse side.

A pure analytics, as opposed to the "pure-grammatical" morphology that raises no questions of genuine executability, can be called a higher morphology — that of possible explicitly executable judgments — with the correlate of the morphology of negatively-executable judgments, the contradictory ones. Pure analytics is a science that systematically seeks the primal forms of judgments to be judged in genuine and complete activity, the "primal operations" of their possible syntactic modifications, their original modes of connecting (copulative, conjunctive) combination. From this it has to pursue, under the guidance of the pure-grammatical iterations of form-building, the gradually resulting possibilities of form-building of "genuine" judgments and so to master legislatively the whole system of judgment-possibility of the distinctness-sphere.

### §3. The Characterization of Analytic Judgments as Merely "Cognition-Elucidating" and as "Tautologies"

Looking at the peculiarity of analytics regarding the role that the "termini" play: in constitutive respect, the syntaxes designate the noematic correlates of the specific judgment-activities. The cores point to the fact that judgment-action always already presupposes pre-givennesses — which ultimately lead back to pre-predicative experience of the individual. Analytics leaves this thematic out of question with the open indeterminacy of its termini. Its formal distinctness-evidence concerns only the genuineness of the syntactic formation, while the termini remain free-floating as to their origin and possibility. In material exemplification or application, the thematic interest does not enter into the material termini but, keeping them only in identity, concerns itself purely with the syntaxes.

"Analytic judging" is thus also to be characterized as analytic in the sense Kant sought with the words "mere cognition-elucidation" as opposed to cognition-extension. Cognition is not "enriched" — in all analytic doing one remains with what one already "had" in judgment or cognition; everything analytically arising is "included" therein. One has "always the same," the same things, the same states-of-affairs. What is inferred is already there, standing in total or partial identity with the presuppositions. This determines the concept-formation and teaching of "tautology" in recent logistic, under which every closed analytic connection falls.

### §4. Remarks on Tautology in the Sense of Logistic. By O. Becker. (On §§14–18 of the Main Text.)

Professor O. Becker provides supplementary remarks. According to logistic, a tautology can be conceived as the negation of a contradiction, and vice versa. From this "definition" follows the purely analytic character of the designated tautologies. They are self-sufficient consequence-systems needing no premises lying outside them.

Leaving the domain of pure analytics and considering possible truth and falsity: every contradiction a limine excludes questions of adequation — it is falsity a limine. Correspondingly, every tautology a limine excludes questions of adequation — it is truth a limine.

If from judgments p₁, p₂, ... pₙ one forms through logical operations the complex form P(p₁, p₂ ... pₙ), then P is a tautology (or contradiction) if and only if P is true (or false) regardless of whether p₁, p₂, ... pₙ are true or false. The question of adequation of the judgment-senses to any formal-ontological or material states of affairs is without relevance here.

These definitions can also be set up in the purely analytic sphere, strictly without any use of a truth- or falsity-concept: "P is a tautology (or contradiction)" means "P is compatible (or incompatible) with every logical product arising from p₁ · p₂ · ... · pₙ by replacing any pᵢ by its negation."

Just as truth is a predicate that can attach only to a distinct (non-contradictory) judgment, falsity is a predicate that can attach only to a non-tautological judgment — one not already self-evident in the mere sphere of distinctness. Just as the inconsistency of "partial senses" in a complex judgment excludes truth, the "self-consistency" (tautological structure) excludes falsity, both a limine. Open to both possible truth and possible falsity are only the consistent but not "self-consistent," the distinct but not "self-distinct" judgments.

The negations of tautologies are contradictions and vice versa. In the whole tautological-contradictory judgment-sphere, the principle of excluded middle holds — which as is well known in the purely analytic sphere does not hold in general. This rests on the fact that, as soon as the idea of possible truth or falsity is added, the decidability-question for truth and falsity of a judgment of the tautological-contradictory sphere is a limine solved in the positive sense.
